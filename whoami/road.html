Forest Road

Had we been walking for about ten minutes?

The uneven climb beneath my feet suddenly leveled out, and I found myself lifting my head.

Stretching out before me was a forest road—far gentler and flatter than I had expected.

—Wait… it’s this straight here…?
I thought.

A strange mix of anticlimax and quiet relief spread through me.
The suffocating pressure from my backpack that had weighed down on my back just moments ago seemed to ease, as if it had suddenly lifted.

On both sides of the white gravel path, the greenery still held a trace of dimness, resting quietly in the morning air.

—I wish the whole trail stayed like this…

If only there were moments like this down in the world below—times when all you had to do was keep walking forward like this…
I couldn’t help but think that.

Lately, no matter whether I turn on the TV or open my phone, it’s always the same—nothing but grim news.
All over the world, anger and hatred spill over, and somewhere, there’s always a war or a conflict happening.
They’re supposed to be stories from distant countries, and yet all that seeps through the screen is a slow, suffocating weight.

Japan isn’t an exception either.
You keep hearing things like how relations with neighboring countries are worsening, or how people there are being brainwashed through anti-Japan education—words like that are thrown around as if they were completely normal.
Even domestic news is filled with talk of “crisis” and “threat,” stirring up anxiety, and if you take a look at the internet, it’s full of people lumping together entire nations they’ve never even seen, speaking about them with hostility.

But when I look at all that, something about it starts to feel… strange.

If people in neighboring countries are taught to hate Japan through government influence,
and then Japanese people respond with that same kind of hatred,
then in the end…
aren’t we just doing the exact same thing
as the ones we claim to dislike?

Just like in Japan,
there are good people,
and there are selfish people—

in those neighboring countries too,
surely,
there must be both
good people,
and bad people.

To judge an entire country
based only on a few voices,
or a single incident—
no matter how you think about it,
it doesn’t make sense.

And yet,
anger and fear take over first,
and even something that simple,
something that obvious,
starts to slip out of sight.

Amid all that, even in Japan, there were moves toward military expansion, and laws being put in place that would punish acts like damaging the national flag.
It felt as though more and more people were beginning to accept and align themselves with that direction.

When I walked through the city, I could sometimes sense that same atmosphere—quietly seeping into everyday conversations and even into the things people said online.

To be honest, I couldn’t really tell who was telling the truth, or what was actually right.
All I knew was that the voices filled with anger were the loudest, and it felt like the space to pause and think things through was being steadily worn away.

Fragments of those news reports suddenly overlapped with something my grandfather used to say.

My grandfather, who lived in Fujisawa, had been a social studies teacher at a public high school.
Maybe because I was such a grandpa’s kid, I used to talk with him about all kinds of things ever since I was little.

There was a time we talked about something like this.

My grandfather looked up from his newspaper and said,

“Just because you’re Japanese,
it doesn’t mean you’re some fundamentally different kind of being from people in other countries.”

The words came so suddenly that
I found myself stopping my chopsticks.

“People, no matter where they’re from, eat when they’re hungry,
and when it rains, they look for shelter.
That’s all there is to it.”

“But still…”
I said after thinking for a moment.

“Countries aren’t something you can just cross so easily, right?”

My grandfather didn’t answer right away.
He lifted his teacup, took a sip, and then said,

“When you think you have to live within the boundaries of a country,
it starts to feel suffocating.”

As he said that,
he glanced quietly out the window.

“But the things people need in order to live
aren’t really that many.”

“Anywhere in the world, anyone is still human.
A man and a woman can have a child.
If their blood types match,
even people from different countries can share blood and save a life.”

At the time,
I remember thinking,
“That’s just idealistic.”

“Humans, as long as they have food and a place to sleep,
can manage to live almost anywhere.”

“Then what about war?”
I said, my tone growing a little stronger.

“If the other side attacks, we have no choice but to fight, right?”

My grandfather didn’t scold me,
nor did he reject what I said.
He simply spoke, quietly.

“People say things like, ‘it’s okay to go to war.’
But in the end,
that’s the same as saying it’s okay to kill someone.”

I couldn’t say anything.

“You can always come up with reasons afterward—
that the other side was wrong,
or that it couldn’t be helped.
But for the ones who died,
none of those words ever reach them.”

After a short pause, he continued,

“It’s a strange thing—
sometimes people say it’s acceptable to kill others,
and yet they also say you must never take your own life.”

At the time,
I didn’t understand how those ideas connected.

“That contradiction…
I’ve never really been able to make sense of it myself.”

With that, my grandfather gave a small shrug.

“If life is really something so light,
then it would mean being killed is acceptable too.
And if that’s the case…
where are we supposed to place the meaning of being born?”

Back then,
all I could think was,
“He’s talking about something complicated.”

—I only began to understand what he meant much later,
after I started being driven by numbers and evaluations.

There was another time we talked about something like this.

“Do you think it’s strange that there are people who dislike their own country’s flag?”

When my grandfather asked me that and I nodded, he gave a slightly troubled smile and said,

“Then why do you think there are people who come to dislike it?”

I couldn’t answer.
After a brief pause, he continued,

“That flag… for some people,
it represents how this country was led into war,
and how so many lives were lost.

There are people who feel that way.”

“As a teacher,
I always carried one thought with me—
never to send my students to war again.”

This country is a democracy, with fundamental human rights—freedom of thought, freedom of religion, and freedom of the press are all supposed to be protected.

And yet, my grandfather said, if the government starts telling people what they should or shouldn’t do regarding the national flag, there is always a reason behind it.
“People who like the flag are free to like it,” he said.
“But don’t you think it’s strange to expect even those who don’t like it to feel the same way?

Feelings like liking or disliking something—
they’re supposed to come naturally, from within.
They’re not something that can simply be decided for you.”

“When people are told to respect the flag,” he said,
“before long, it can start to mean something more.

Be proud of this country.
Value it.
And eventually… be ready to give your life to protect it.

Sometimes, that’s what it turns into.”

“But Sho,” he went on,
“what do you think this ‘country’ is that we’re supposed to protect?”

Land can be bought by foreigners, if they have the money.
Nature can be destroyed, again and again, in the name of profit.
Traditions fade away if there’s no one left to carry them on.
And the people—the citizens—can be cast aside all too easily,
under low wages, long working hours, and rising prices.

“In the end,” he said quietly,
“there are times when the only things truly being protected
are the government… and a small number of wealthy people.”

He would tell stories like that many times, often mixing in light jokes and a touch of irony.

“For example,” my grandfather once said.
“It’s like the ocean.”

“Plankton that cause red tides—
as long as there are nutrients, they keep multiplying without limit.
The more they grow, the more they seek out food,
until eventually, they begin competing with one another.”

“Some of them even produce toxins.
When they grow too numerous,
the sea turns red and murky, fish begin to die,
and in the end, even those that produced the toxins
die off together—from their own poison.”

“If they end up destroying each other over food,
then humans and plankton…
aren’t all that different, are they?”

My grandfather let out a small, almost playful laugh.

Then, as if suddenly changing the subject, he said something like this.

“You know, Japan is often called ‘the country where the sun rises first,’
but if you look at it from the International Date Line,
there are plenty of places where morning comes earlier than it does in Japan.”

“Pride isn’t something someone else decides for you.
It’s something you think about for yourself—and choose to carry.”

And then, as if to drive the point home, he told an almost ridiculous story.

“Imagine this,” he said.
“If your company president suddenly declared,
‘This shirt is my god,’
and then said,
‘Anyone who doesn’t praise it is guilty,
anyone who doesn’t salute it will be fined,
and anyone who stains it will be thrown in jail,’
what would you think, Sho?”

“If you were told to place your hand over your heart,
to devote your life
to protecting that shirt—
would you really call that faith?”

At the end, my grandfather shrugged lightly, almost teasingly, and added,

“By forcing people to protect the form,
you make them stop thinking.
And that… is the easiest kind of control.”

Lately, watching the way things have been going,
I can’t help but wonder if what my grandfather said might have been right after all—

I find myself thinking that as I walk.

—Maybe that’s why.

Why this time, walking through the forest like this,
feels like I’ve been gently separated from reality,
as if I’ve stepped into a different world.


“Hey, Saku, look over there… see that spot where there’s still snow?
That’s where we’re heading—that’s today’s goal.”

Takizawa-san’s voice came back to me, remembering how he pointed toward the mountains from inside the car.

—There’s no way it stays flat like this forever… I thought.


Dense green forest stretched along both sides of the trail,
and the stiff soles of my Caravan hiking boots added an awkward rhythm to my stride as I walked over the gravel path.

Once we entered the forest road, the path widened,
and the slope eased.
Because of that, our single-file line naturally began to loosen,
and before I knew it, people drifted into small groups with those they felt closest to.

“…Hey, by the way, why is convenience store iced coffee so damn good?” Yohei said beside me.

“Now? You’re bringing that up now?” I said.

It was stupid. But somehow, it was exactly the kind of conversation I needed.

“Actually, you know, the secret to good iced coffee is the extraction method,”
Tsuruoka cut in, catching up from behind and joining our conversation.
“Dark-roasted beans, brewed slowly… that’s how you get that perfect flavor.”

For some reason, I felt like I wanted to get away.

“Whoa, whoa, Tsuruoka-san, spare us the coffee lecture,”
Yohei said with a laugh.
“We’re beginners here, man. That kind of deep talk’s a bit much for us.”

—Does he just not want to be alone? He’s talking a lot more than usual, I thought.

Up ahead, Mari-senpai and Yabe-san were talking.

“Yabe-chi, did you get a decent night’s sleep?”

“Four hours. Not bad.”

Yabe-san answered after a brief pause, her expression unchanged.

“I see,” Mari-senpai said with a smile.
For just a moment, the corners of Yabe-san’s mouth softened slightly.

As they walked, Yabe-san suddenly muttered,
“You’re good at small talk.”

“Was that a compliment?” Mari-senpai widened her eyes, then quickly let out a soft laugh.

“…Probably,” Yabe-san replied.

Takizawa-san, who usually chats easily with everyone, was surprisingly walking alone at the front, fiddling with his GPS.

Every time I think back on what my grandfather said, something stirs faintly in my chest.

In my head, I know his words make sense.
And yet, the real world seems to run on a completely different logic.

I was always taught that war is wrong, that life is more important than anything.
But once I stepped into society, that same “war” was spoken of as if it were just another matter of “the economy,” “growth,” or “livelihood.”

It’s not even a question of which is right—
but which one is “real.”

Without finding an answer,
I now find myself standing right in the middle of that reality.


The company I work for, Sansei Electronic Precision, is a private business,
and whether it makes a profit directly affects our lives.

Because of that, there are a lot of people at work who think in terms like,
“Japan first,” or that there’s no point unless you win the competition for money.

And if you support the government, you get policies that benefit companies in return—
that kind of talk had, before I knew it, become something people discussed as if it were completely normal.

Our company also deals with products that could be adapted for military use,
so Tsuruoka-san sometimes says things like,
“Honestly, if a war broke out, demand would go up and our pay might too,”
in a half-joking tone.

Hearing him say that with a laugh,
I could never quite tell whether he meant it or not.

Being close to my grandfather, I always find myself recalling his words at times like this.

“Competition, you see,
is meant to drive progress.

As long as there’s not enough to go around—
as long as supply can’t keep up with demand—
competition serves a purpose.

People try to create something better,
and society moves forward because of that.

But, Sho…
once supply begins to meet demand,
and there’s still more left over,
things start to change.

Now, instead of creating to advance,
people have to create new demand just to keep things going.

And that’s when—
war can sometimes be used.”

“War, you see, can also be started to create demand.
When things stop selling, they’re destroyed.
And to destroy them… people are killed.”

“Even if a war breaks out somewhere far away,
and Japan isn’t directly involved,
there are still people who profit by making weapons.
That’s the kind of world we live in.”

“International affairs, you know,
can sometimes feel like a scripted match—
like pro wrestling—
where someone is always set up to win.”

“If we’re already facing a declining birthrate,
what happens if young people start throwing their lives away?
All that means… is fewer people left to cherish.”


“Tell me—
which do you think truly cares about this country?

The ones who sacrifice their fellow citizens
to protect their own gain or pride
or the ones who try to get along with other countries,
and live by helping one another here at home?”

My grandfather gave a small, almost troubled smile, and said,

“You see, in competition…
people trip each other up,
push one another down,
and hurt each other—

and yet, they call that ‘bonds’… or ‘unity.’”

He paused for a moment, tilting his head slightly.

“…That’s something
I’ve never quite been able to understand.”

Then he continued quietly,

“I think… real bonds
are something you can stand in together,
without having to trample anyone else.”

“Humans aren’t creatures that destroy themselves
by fighting over resources.

We’re beings who can think—
who can hold back our selfish impulses and pride,
and choose to live together.”

“Sho, remember this.
Before being Japanese…
what matters is whether you can remain human.”

As I recalled my grandfather’s words,
another line of thought began to form in my mind.

We’re supposed to compete in order to move forward—
and yet, I had just heard that war is sometimes needed
to create demand.

But that didn’t make sense.

War is destruction.
Not progress.

Progress means moving forward.
Destroying things is stepping backward.

If we’re moving backward,
then calling it “progress” has to be wrong somewhere.

If things stop selling,
that only means this world already has enough.

Instead of fighting because there isn’t enough,
we’re destroying things because there’s too much—
can that really be right?

If there’s enough,
then we should just share it,
and use it together.

Isn’t it really that simple?

A fork leading up into the forest appeared on my left.

A short set of steps led upward,
and on a tree just above, a wooden sign read “Yari Onsen.”

Takizawa-san stopped, turned back, and pointed at the fork.

“This is where we’ll meet again on the way down.
We’ll come back along that path on the left,
so make sure you remember it.
Stay sharp—don’t let your guard down.”

His voice was calm as always.

As I stared at the sign,
I found myself imagining how tough the descent might be,
and a faint tightness crept into my chest.

Takizawa-san works in the general affairs department and is around forty years old.

He’s fairly tall,
and his solid, well-built frame is probably the result of years of mountain training.

His slightly tanned skin and well-defined features,
along with his short, neatly trimmed straight hair,
give him the look of an athlete.

The way his fingers absentmindedly fiddle with his GPS
seems like a habit he’s picked up over time.

Well… it’s probably also a sign that he’s always calmly assessing the situation.

With me, he says things like,
“Hey, Saku, you nervous? You’ll be fine, don’t worry!”

And to begin with,
he was the one who half-forced me into joining this hiking club.

Takizawa-san is the founder of this group—
and its leader.

He talks easily with everyone,
calling me “Saku-chan,” Yohei “Yo-chan,” and Tsuruoka “Tsuru-san.”
There’s a strong sense of trust in the way he does that.

Right beside me,
I could hear the steady rhythm of Yohei kicking small stones as he walked.

As we came around a bend in the forest road,
the sky suddenly opened up before us.

Through a break in the trees,
I could see two large triangular peaks standing side by side against the blue sky in the distance.

The two shapes seemed to connect in a gentle downward curve,
like a giant bird spreading its wings.

Across the gray rock faces of the mountains,
ridge lines ran like streaks of lightning,
and the streams flowing down from them branched out intricately like the limbs of a tree.

I didn’t even know their names,
and yet, a quiet sense grew inside me—

“Wow, look, look! It’s so beautiful!
…Doesn’t it look like a postcard? …And that shape—like twin peaks!”
Mari-senpai called out as she gazed at the mountains.

“Whoa, if we’re getting a view like this right from the start,
my hype’s already through the roof!
At this point, we could just head back and call it a day!” Yohei said.

Everyone burst out laughing at that.

—Yeah, yeah… here we go again.
I let out a polite laugh along with the others.

“Hey, are you stupid? We didn’t come all this way just to turn back.
What are you, an idiot?” Tsuruoka-san said.

…Something about that felt slightly off.

“…You’re letting the scenery win.”
Yabe muttered quietly beside me.

—That was kind of cool just now, I thought.

“That one on the left is Mount Hakuba.
That’s where we’re heading.”
Takizawa said, pointing toward the mountain on the left.

“The peak on the right is Korenge.
And that dip in the middle—that’s the border of three regions.
Once we cross that, we’re in Toyama.”
he explained.

Everyone started snapping photos,
each taking in the view in their own way.

“Wait… we’re climbing all the way up there? …That’s insane…”
I muttered without thinking.

But maybe it was the view—
the tension I’d felt earlier had already begun to fade.

Everyone stopped and pulled out their cameras,
snapping photos one after another.

Yabe, too, silently pressed the shutter.

“Oh man, this is picture-perfect!” Yohei said.

“This angle’s got more depth.”
Even Takizawa, who had been leading the way, started taking photos.

Seeing everyone else shooting,
I felt like I had to take some too.

I took off my pack,
flipped open the top lid,
and pulled out the camera from its small case.

Pushing aside my windbreaker,
I unzipped it,
and the lens—faintly reflecting the light—peeked out.

I aimed it at the mountains and pressed the shutter.

Just in case that one didn’t come out right, I took another.

And since it looked beautiful from this angle too—another one.

Before I knew it,
I was taking the same shot over and over again.

“Hey, hey… if you keep firing off shots like that,
you’re gonna run out of memory before we even reach the summit…”
Tsuruoka-san said from behind.

“Ah, it’s fine, it’s fine.
Memories are at their best right in the moment, you know?”
Takizawa-san said, glancing back at us.

“Alright, no point standing around taking photos forever.
Let’s get moving.”
Takizawa-san said, raising one hand lightly as he glanced back,
then started walking off at a brisk pace.

—Wait, seriously…?
You were just the one saying “it’s fine, it’s fine” and snapping away like crazy…
I thought.

At his words,
everyone reluctantly put their cameras away
and started walking again, a bit scattered.

I slipped mine into my pants pocket
so I could take it out again quickly if needed,
and followed after them.

The pocket bulged into a square shape,
and it felt like it was getting in the way every time I lifted my leg.

My grandfather is a member of a teachers’ union.

There’s a union at my company too,
but even though they’re both unions,
it feels like what mine stands for is quite different
from what my grandfather’s believes in.

I spoke a little hesitantly,
averting my gaze as I did.

“…Hey, Grandpa.
The union at my company…
it doesn’t seem to be completely against military expansion.”

“And even about the pacifist constitution,
there are people who say
‘maybe it should be changed to fit the times…’”

I paused for a moment,
as if searching for the right words.

“When I’m at work,
it kind of…
sometimes sounds like the more ‘realistic’ view.”

“And me…
I’m starting to not really know anymore
what’s actually right…”

When I confessed that,
my grandfather fell silent for a moment,
then gently set his teacup down.

“…Sho.
If a war were to break out,
who do you think would guarantee
that your company would be safe?”

“Companies that make things
that could be used for military purposes…
from the enemy’s point of view,
they’re not ‘something to protect’—
they’re ‘targets to be taken out first.’”

“Factories, warehouses…
they won’t care
whether people are inside or not.”

Lowering his voice slightly, he went on.

“Even if you think you’re getting closer to war
to protect your company…
war doesn’t let you choose
what gets protected first.”

“For the sake of the country, for the sake of the company—
the ones who step forward because of those words
are the ones who face danger first.”

He gave a faint, bitter smile.

“‘If we become stronger, we can protect ourselves’—
that way of thinking
is the easiest path into war.”

My grandfather traced the rim of his teacup as he continued.

“Sho, when people hear ‘union,’
it quickly turns into talk about right and left,
but really, it starts from something much simpler.”

“You’re weak on your own,
so you raise your voice together with others—
that’s all it is.”

He paused for a moment, then went on.

“In private companies,
if the business does well,
there’s a chance salaries will go up too.
So naturally,
more people start to value the company doing well.”

“Some call that ‘right-leaning,’
but when your livelihood depends on it,
it’s not such a strange way to think.”

He looked out at the sky beyond the veranda.

“On the other hand,
public service isn’t about making profit.
It runs on taxes,
and the people it serves aren’t ‘customers,’
but society itself.”

“So instead of thinking only about their own pay,
it becomes easier to think about
how everyone can live.
That’s probably why it looks ‘left-leaning.’”

He gave a soft smile.

“But you know, Sho…
right or left—
they probably both begin
from the same place:
wanting to protect yourself and the people around you.”

“When you stand in different places,
you see different views.
And when the view changes,
the words sound different too.”

He lowered his voice slightly.

“What’s dangerous is
turning one side’s way of thinking
into an ‘answer’ you don’t have to question.”

“A union isn’t a tool for fighting—
it’s a place for talking.
That’s what I believe.”

“More than right or left,
what matters is
whose voices are actually being heard.
If you don’t lose sight of that,
even when the winds change,
you won’t be carried away so easily.”

At the end, he gave a small shrug.

“Like the Shonan wind.
You don’t have to fight it,
but if you leave everything to it,
you never know where you’ll end up.”

“Hey, Saku.” Yohei called out.
With his Nagoya-tinged voice, he casually dropped a cheerful hand on my shoulder.

“Man, nature really is the best, huh?
Places like this… it’s like it actually washes your mind clean or something!”

Bright red jacket.
Flashy bandana.
Loud-colored shoes and sunglasses.

Walking along looking like he’d just come back from a music festival—
that was Onishi Yohei, my coworker from the same year.

He lived alone, while I still lived with my parents,
but we were close enough to drop by each other’s places whenever.

I played guitar, he played bass,
so we were always talking about starting a band together.

There were nights when we’d ride our scooters out
to a quiet neighborhood park after everyone had gone to sleep,
and just talk about our dreams until morning.

Everyone called him “Yo-chan.”
Wherever he went,
he had this way of lighting up the mood.

He had some experience with hiking,
but this was only his second time joining this hiking club.
Even so,
he already fit right in—
probably because of how naturally friendly he was.

“So, Saku, what’ve you been listening to lately?
Me, I’m still all about the old-school rock.
The kind we’d call in Nagoya—
‘straight-up awesome.’”

He changed the subject like that all the time.
He’d just as easily veer into dirty jokes,
but deep down,
he cared about people more than anyone—
and before you knew it,
he’d have everyone around him smiling.

I don’t mind Western music either.

“I’ve been really into ‘Intuition’ by TNT lately.
That melody just hits me, you know?
And the riff—it’s so good, it really gets me pumped.” I said.

When I talk with Yohei,
the way I refer to myself naturally shifts from “boku” to “ore.”

“Oh yeah, I get that! That song’s seriously a classic.
It’s not super popular,
but anyone who knows it probably loves it.”

“Right? And not just TNT—
I also think ‘Summertime Girls’ by Y&T is great.”

“Oh man, yeah—and the vocalist from White Lion was amazing too.
I still can’t forget how powerful they were live.”

We got so caught up talking about music
that a gap had opened up between us and the group ahead.
Even Tsuruoka-san, who’d been behind us,
was now up in front.

“Hey, Sho—falling behind again?” Tsuruoka-san called out as he turned around.
“Didn’t you get chewed out at work for missing a deadline?
Come on, learn your lesson already.”

Mari-senpai and Yabe-san both turned to look at me.

—Seriously… you’re bringing that up here?
Yeah, I messed up, sure…
Back then the client was furious,
and even my boss had to apologize for me…
And now you’re bringing it up?
Doesn’t that just kill the whole mountain vibe?

Even thinking all that, though…

“…S-sorry.”
I ended up saying it anyway.

“Whoa, Tsuruoka-san—first the coffee lecture,
now a public shaming?
Man, this mountain’s scary.”
Yohei said with a wry smile.

“Talking about work out here…
you do realize you’re killing the mood, right?”
Yabe-san said.

“Yeah… we all came here to refresh ourselves.
Bringing up work isn’t exactly the vibe.”
Mari-senpai added, and that was a small relief.

“…Tch.”
I thought I heard Tsuruoka-san click his tongue under his breath.

Right beside me,
Yabe-san and Mari-senpai exchanged a glance,
and for a moment,
the air felt just a little tense.

I suddenly remembered a rumor I’d heard somewhere before.

A few years back,
a product from the manufacturing division Tsuruoka-san was in charge of
had caused trouble in an overseas deal.

When it came to who was responsible,
people in the company started pointing fingers—
saying it was bad design,
or that it had been used incorrectly on site.

In the end,
it was Yabe-san, who had been handling the client response,
who had to step forward and apologize.

And on top of that,
the internal report apparently blamed it on operational error at the site,
which meant the responsibility was effectively pushed onto Yabe-san’s team.

During a company meeting after that,
Yabe-san finally snapped.

“You people always do this—
you run from responsibility.”

That’s what I heard she said,
raising her voice as she directly called Tsuruoka-san out by name.

Tsuruoka-san tried to argue back at first,
but in the face of Yabe-san’s anger,
he was eventually overwhelmed…
and fell silent.

Within the company,
the story of “Tsuruoka of all people being silenced”
became something of a legend.

I don’t know how much of it is actually true,
but somehow…
the heaviness in the air right now
felt connected to that story.

A forest road cut across the mountainside to the left, covered in deep green.
Below to the right, a river ran through the trees.

Crunch… crunch…

As our footsteps pressed into the gravel path,
a faint rushing sound began to mix in from ahead.

At first, I thought it was just the wind.
But with each step forward,
the sound grew clearer—
until it became unmistakably the sound of flowing water,
and soon, through the gaps in the trees,
a stream came into view,
its surface catching the light and scattering it in bright flashes.

“Stream up ahead,” Takizawa-san called out,
stopping and turning back to us.
“Watch your footing—you might get a little wet.”

The moment we passed through the trees,
the view suddenly opened up.

Clear water rushed between two lush green slopes on either side.
Above it,
a wooden footbridge stretched across in a zigzag shape.

The morning sun lit up the surface of the bridge,
and the damp wood reflected the light with a soft glow.

“Wow… what is this place… it’s beautiful!”
Mari-senpai let out another breath of admiration.

Ahead of us,
Hakuba-dake and Mount Korenge—
the same majestic peaks we’d been seeing—
stood tall,
with the ridge connecting them clearly in view.

—What is this feeling…?

A breeze flowing down from the mountains crossed the stream
and gently brushed against my cheek.

In front of me was a weathered wooden bridge,
its surface faded to a dry brownish gray,
stretching over the river—
beyond it, endless green and the deep blue of the sky.

The sound of water running beneath my feet
somehow calmed me in a way I couldn’t quite explain.

I stepped onto the bridge.

Such a simple act—
and yet it felt like this journey,
which had been nothing more than a hike,
was slowly turning into an adventure.

—I should take a picture too.

Thinking that,
I pulled out the camera I’d tucked into my pocket earlier.

With the camera no longer weighing down my pocket,
my legs felt just a little lighter.

“All right, let’s cross! Watch your step—it might be slippery!”
Takizawa-san called out as he started across the bridge over the stream.

“Saku-chan, could you take a picture of me crossing?
Make sure you get the mountains in the background too.”
Maybe she’d noticed me pulling out my camera—
Mari-senpai turned back toward me as she said it.

“Y-yes! Got it!”
I stammered, feeling a little embarrassed at myself,
but at the same time…
if she was asking me to take her picture,
that meant I’d get one of her, too.
For some reason, that made me happy.

Following Takizawa-san,
Mari-senpai stepped onto the bridge.
Just as I was about to raise my camera—

“Well, look at that. Got yourself picked, huh?”
Tsuruoka-san said, nudging my shoulder.

—That’s none of your business.
I kept a polite smile on my face,
but that’s what I was thinking inside.

Click…

On the bridge, Mari-senpai flashed a peace sign,
and I pressed the shutter.

At the sound,
she gave a slightly shy smile.

Behind her,
the mountains stretched out in a bold ridgeline,
under a wide blue sky.

A gentle breeze drifted across the bridge,
brushing lightly through my hair.

It was a moment that felt endlessly calm,
endlessly peaceful.

“Hey—there’s a line forming behind you…”
Yabe-san called out to Mari-senpai,
who was still posing on the bridge.

—She didn’t have to say that…
I thought to myself.

“Sorry, sorry.”
Mari-senpai said lightly,
raising a hand without a hint of embarrassment as she moved on,
and Yabe-san stepped onto the bridge after her.

“Alright! Now I’m getting seriously pumped!”
A voice came from behind.
When I turned around,
Yohei had both arms raised in the air.

“This crystal-clear stream! These mountains! And me!
Now that’s the perfect combination!”
he declared proudly.

“…Just try not to get so pumped
that the bridge gives out.”

Yabe-san muttered dryly over her shoulder
as she crossed,
and Mari-senpai, already on the other side,
let out a soft laugh.

I stepped onto the wooden bridge as well.

Shhh—

Below me, clear water rushed by,
throwing up white splashes as it flowed.

The planks creaked under my weight,
and the sound of the river seemed to pass right through me.

The rushing water slipped beneath my feet
and faded away behind me as I crossed.

On the other side,
Takizawa-san, Mari-senpai, and Yabe-san
were already waiting,
and I joined them as we waited for the remaining two.

“Whoa, whoa, whoa—okay, okay!”
Yohei said, pretending to lose his balance as he made his way across.

“Hey, don’t do that on the bridge—it’s dangerous…”
I said with a laugh.

“Relax, Saku-chan, I’ve got this!”
Yohei shot back.

Behind him,
Tsuruoka-san started crossing.

—I'd rather not get involvbed with him.
I kept a smile on my face,
but that was what I was thinking inside.

“Alright, let’s move!”
Takizawa-san’s voice rang out,
cutting across Tsuruoka-san,
who was still carefully making his way over the bridge.

—Serves you right.
I thought to myself,
seeing how Takizawa-san was about to move on
without even giving him time to catch up.

“…So you’re not going to wait for him…?”
Yabe-san murmured softly with a faint smile,
glancing at me for just a moment before turning her gaze forward again.

I couldn’t tell what she was thinking—
her profile gave nothing away.

After crossing the stream,
the ground beneath my feet returned to the familiar crunch of gravel along the forest road.

My damp soles pressed into the dry stones,
the gritty sound echoing pleasantly in my ears.

The trees closed in overhead once more,
filtering the sunlight into soft patches that fell across the path.
A cool breeze slipped gently over my skin.

The sound of the stream faded behind us,
replaced by footsteps and birdsong filling the air.

At the front was Takizawa-san,
just behind him Mari-senpai walked along, quietly humming to herself,
and Yabe-san occasionally muttered things like,
“Where did I put my water bottle again…?”
Yohei was behind me.

I glanced back,
pretending—at least to myself—that I was being considerate of Tsuruoka-san,
who had been left a little behind.

There was a small distance between us now.
Having just crossed the bridge,
he had started walking a bit faster to catch up.

I wasn’t trying to suck up to him,
but I put on a polite smile
and quietly turned forward again.

I found myself wondering
whether that smile I’d made for him
had looked unnatural.

“Man, that stream was nice.
Places like that really reset your mood.
…We’ll be hitting the climb soon, so get ready!”
Takizawa-san called out, turning slightly as he spoke.

“Okay—looking forward to it,”
Mari-senpai replied.

“Yeah, I’ll keep up,”
I said.

“Not gonna lose!”
Yohei added from behind.

Ahead of me,
Yabe-san muttered something under her breath,
but I couldn’t quite catch what she said.

Crunch, crunch—step… step…
shh, shh, step-step-step…

Our footsteps overlapped along the forest road,
blending into a steady rhythm.

Bzzzzzz—

Somewhere close on the left,
near enough that I could probably find it if I looked,
a cicada droned on.

“Hehe, but those stream photos are totally Instagram-worthy, right?
Saku-chan, you got a good shot, didn’t you?
It was a great composition.
Will you send it to my phone later?”
Mari-senpai said,
leaning slightly out from behind Yabe-san as she turned to me.

—Wait…!?
If she wants me to send it,
does that mean she’s going to give me her number?

Could it be… she likes me?
Like—what if she asks me out or something…?

“Y-yeah… I think it came out okay,”
I said.

—Probably…
I added silently.

“It was… nice.
I mean, you looked like you were really enjoying it.”
The words just slipped out.

Something about what I’d said felt a little off.

“Hey, hold up, Sho.
You’ve been saying ‘senpai, senpai’ a bit too much, haven’t you?
Man, you’ve already crossed the ridge in your head.”
Yohei teased from behind.

“‘Ridge,’ huh? Trying to be clever?
…We’re still on a forest road.”
Yabe-san said,
barely turning her head to the left as she spoke.

She moved less than most people would
when addressing someone behind her.

“You guys are getting carried away.
…Don’t start whining when you burn out.
…Though, I guess I’ll pull you along if it comes to that.”
Tsuruoka-san called out from the back.

“No thanks.”
Yabe-san replied,
still facing forward,
as if she were talking to herself.

I almost burst out laughing.

—Whoa… she actually said it.
She really did.

I thought to myself.

Yabe-san was still hard to figure out,
but the fact that she could say something like that—even to Tsuruoka-san—
made her seem strangely strong.

And yet,
there was still something about her that kept people at a distance—
like she didn’t really want anyone getting too close.

“Alright, we’ve got a climb coming up ahead.
…Let’s push on until the next break.”
Takizawa-san said,
turning back toward us from the front.
