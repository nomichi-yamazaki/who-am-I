Departure from the Shelter

Once everyone had finished getting ready, Takizawa-san turned back to look at us all.
“Let’s go in the same order as before. Tsuruoka-san in front, Sho next, then Yabe-san, Mari, Yohei, and I’ll bring up the rear. Alright, let’s head out.”
“Yeah,” Tsuruoka-san answered shortly, then stepped onto the mountain trail fading into the white mist.
The silver bundle on his back swayed as he took the lead.
I started walking after him, following the line of his back.
Behind us, the sound of the shelter door closing echoed softly, and that pocket of warmth sank away behind us.
Ahead of us lay nothing but a white world, and the trail of footprints continuing through it.

The moment we left the emergency shelter behind, we stepped straight back into the white fog.
On both sides of the trail, clusters of low alpine plants dotted the ground, and green ropes had been strung up to mark the boundaries so no one would trample them.
At my feet, rocks of all shapes and sizes lay scattered everywhere, and every time I stepped on one, the sole of my boot tilted, forcing my ankle this way and that and making it hard to walk.
Maybe because we had only just taken a break, my body still felt light.
I wasn’t tired yet, and I was able to keep moving while focusing on Tsuruoka-san’s backpack ahead of me.
On top of that dark green pack, something wrapped in a silver aluminum sheet and tied down with light blue rope still stood out strangely, glinting faintly through the white fog.
Behind me, Yabe-san was following at a slight distance.
I was grateful it didn’t feel like she was pushing me from behind.
Maybe she was walking in a way that took the feelings of the person in front of her into account.
Thinking that, I felt my heart grow just a little lighter.
From somewhere deep in the white fog behind us, Takizawa-san’s voice rang out.
“Hey! Everybody still here? …Good. Keep calling out to each other as we go, alright? It’s easy to get lost in this fog!”
Even as I heard him, I found myself looking at the green ropes and muttering inwardly that with these here, getting lost would be the harder thing to do.
As we kept walking, though, my legs gradually started to feel heavier.
The ease I’d felt earlier began to fade, and my breathing quickened just a little.
I tried to tell myself I was still fine, but my body was slowly starting to make its complaints known.
Maybe it was because I wanted to distract myself from the fatigue, or maybe because whenever she snapped back at Tsuruoka-san it somehow made her feel like she was on my side, but for some reason I suddenly felt like talking to her.
As we walked, I finally worked up the nerve to call back to Yabe-san behind me.
“Yabe-san, the things you say always hit the mark, don’t they?”
“What’s that supposed to mean, Sho? That Yabe-san’s always right whenever she says bad things about me?”
Unexpectedly, it was Tsuruoka-san walking ahead who answered my words.
“No, that’s not what I meant…”
Even though his tone sounded joking, I hurriedly stumbled out a reply.
“…You sure do talk a lot of nonsense too,” Yabe-san said from behind me with a small laugh.
—I’m usually not even that talkative…
I argued back silently in my own head.
And yet whenever I actually opened my mouth, somehow I always ended up saying something awkward.
Was that what Yabe-san had meant?
The thought gave my chest a small sharp sting.
Maybe because of that, my steps gradually began to feel even heavier.
The backpack on my shoulders suddenly felt like it was made of lead.
My breathing turned shallow and fast, and deep inside my chest, heat began to build.
The ease I’d felt only moments ago had vanished somewhere, and inside my head I was already whining that this was getting rough.

As we kept climbing through the fog, before long I began to hear the sound of clear water trickling down between the rocks.
Suddenly, the silvery bundle wrapped in aluminum sheet ahead of me—its outline wavering softly in the white mist—came to a stop, and I realized Tsuruoka-san had halted.
Then, almost at once, out of the thick white fog right in front of me, a water source suddenly appeared, spilling from between the rocks.
Crystal-clear water slid down the rock face like a tiny waterfall, sparkling as it caught the pale light in the mist.
I stopped without thinking, captivated by how pure it looked.
At the edge of the spring, Tsuruoka-san, still wearing his pack, peered into the gap between the rocks while getting a cup ready.
“Let’s take a moment and hydrate here,” Tsuruoka-san said.
Out of the fog behind me, Yabe-san slowly came into view, followed by Mari-senpai emerging beside her.
“Wow, what a beautiful water spot…” Mari-senpai said.
“It really has a nice atmosphere,” Yabe-san added as she slipped one arm out of her shoulder strap and prepared her cup.
“…Mm—ahh, that’s good,” Tsuruoka-san said after bringing a cupful of water to his lips.
Yohei’s figure appeared through the white mist.
“Oh, a water spot,” he said.
I scooped some up with my hand and took a sip too.
As the icy water slid down my throat, it felt as if life was slowly returning to every part of my body, easing just a little of the fatigue that had built up.
“Mmm… ahh, delicious,” I said as I swallowed.
Yabe-san quietly drank as well.
At last Takizawa-san emerged from beyond the mist, stopping as if to make sure everyone was there.
“Good, everyone made it safely. Guess I’ll have some water too,” he said as he took off his pack.
“It’s so cold and refreshing,” Mari-senpai said, lifting her cup to her lips.
“This is the best—blows the fatigue right away,” Yohei said, lowering his cup from his mouth.
The sight of the spring before me, and the reassuring way my companions kept appearing one after another out of the fog, made the tension finally leave my shoulders.
In that white, misty world, the little spring shone like a tiny oasis.

“Well then, let’s keep going.” Tsuruoka-san said with a satisfied smile as he put away his cup.
—Already? I thought, letting out a small breath before I could stop myself.
The silvery bundle wrapped in aluminum sheet on top of Tsuruoka-san’s dark green pack began to sway again, and I started walking after his back once more.
The rounded bulge of water pooled beneath the aluminum sheet covering the bundle seemed slightly larger than before.
The ice inside must be melting that much more.
The swollen pocket of water trapped inside the wrapping sloshed from side to side in rhythm with Tsuruoka-san’s steps.
The relief of the cold water on my throat lasted only a moment, because once we started moving again, my legs suddenly felt as heavy as lead.
My throat was refreshed, yet the fatigue clinging to the rest of my body only seemed to grow thicker.
As I stared at the green ropes stretched between the alpine plants on both sides of the trail, a fear began to take hold of my chest: What if I can’t keep walking like this?
The trail was still an uneven path hemmed in by those green ropes, white rocks of all sizes scattered everywhere.
Step by careful step, I chose where to place my feet and kept moving forward.
From behind me, I could faintly hear Yabe-san’s voice.
“If the fog wasn’t here, this whole area would be a flower field…”
—I see.
It wasn’t as if I was especially interested in alpine flowers, but somehow that still felt a little disappointing.
That said, instead, we had something precious swaying on top of Tsuruoka-san’s pack—something that might make all of us famous overnight.
From somewhere behind Yabe-san came Mari-senpai’s calm voice as well.
“When things settle down, let’s come back and do it properly next time.”
Those words steadied my heart, if only a little.
Just hearing everyone’s voices reminded me that they were all still with us.
But little by little, the fatigue in my legs kept building.
The rocks were never the same size, forcing me to adjust my stride every single step, draining my strength.
Tsuruoka-san kept walking ahead of me.
—Takizawa-san said we’d rest every hour, but when is Tsuruoka-san going to stop? We’re not really going all the way without another break, are we…?
The moment that thought entered my mind, anxiety seeped in, and the exhaustion only seemed to grow worse.
My body felt heavy.
My knees wouldn’t come forward.
My breathing turned ragged, as if someone had stuffed stones deep inside my chest.
Was I really going to end up unable to move at all?
—Maybe I’m done for… I thought.
Then, just then, I noticed something when I looked ahead.
Perhaps because of the weight on his back, Tsuruoka-san’s pace had slowed a little.
I can catch up.
That single realization alone made my heart feel suddenly lighter.
I remembered what Mari-senpai had said.
“As long as you keep putting one foot forward, that means your destination is getting closer.”
And Yabe-san’s words too:
“You’re just putting one foot in front of the other.”
That’s right. I’d told myself the same thing before…
“I’m just here to enjoy the climb…”
Repeating those words over and over in my head, I quietly kept climbing, stepping over each rock one by one.
As I did, my mind gradually began to settle.
—Before I knew it, strangely enough, my steps had become light again.
The fatigue vanished, leaving behind almost nothing at all.
I was simply putting one foot in front of the other, and it felt as though I could keep walking forever.
Maybe this was that state I’d once heard about before—the so-called climber’s high.
The thought drifted across my mind.

Before long, a staircase made of wooden beams appeared, and we climbed it one step at a time.
Following the back of Tsuruoka-san, with the silvery bundle swaying atop his dark green pack, I made my way up the wooden steps.
When the staircase ended, the trail turned back into a rough path of jagged white stones, and after a little while, the upward trail that had been visible through the white fog suddenly disappeared as we emerged into a broad, rocky open area.
“Hey, everyone—we’ve made it to the ridge…” Tsuruoka-san said, stopping and turning around where he stood.
“Haa…” I let out a breath as I stepped onto the ridge myself.
—Ah, he’s right. If it weren’t for the fog, the view here would probably be amazing… I thought.
“Haa… thank goodness,” I heard Mari-senpai’s voice behind me.
“We did it! Just a little farther now, senpai!” came Yohei’s voice from farther back.
“Oh yeah, just one more push,” Takizawa-san’s voice followed as well.
As everyone emerged one after another from the fog, Tsuruoka-san started walking again, and I fell in behind him once more.

Out of the white fog, a small white trail sign came into view.
Mounted atop a square wooden post were two signboards: one shaped like a long, horizontally stretched hexagon, and another like a stretched home-plate shape laid on its side.
In black letters on a white background, the direction we had come from read “← Sarukura / Descent”; to the left it read “← Mt. Shakushi / Hakuba-Yarigatake”; and to the right, “Mt. Hakuba / Hakuba Grand Lodge →.”
The lettering had bled slightly after years of wind and rain, and with the chipped corners, the whole sign had the unmistakable feel of something handmade.
Beneath the words, the estimated walking times were carved in small lettering, and at the tips of the arrows there were traces of moss and tiny scratches.
There was even a faint dab of red paint so small you might miss it if you didn’t look closely.
Inside one of the tiny holes, ants busily crawled back and forth, while the sign’s shadow swayed gently over the rocks and grass.

All at once, the fog suddenly swept away before my eyes, and the view opened wide.
Blue sky appeared overhead, and out of the white mist emerged Yabe-san, Mari-senpai, Yohei, Takizawa-san, and the broad ridge stretching around us.
Before us, a sea of clouds spread out in white beneath our feet.
As I looked around, Mt. Hakuba’s summit appeared from the white clouds sliding sideways as if the whole world were turning.
When I turned around, the sharp peaks of Mt. Shakushi and Hakuba-Yarigatake stood behind us, looking as though they were floating above the clouds.
—Whoa… amazing!
The sheer power, the grandeur, the overwhelming presence of nature struck straight into my heart.
“Oh! You can even see Tsurugi-dake!” Takizawa-san said, pointing into the distance, where a triangular mountain rose quietly beyond the white clouds.
The majestic sight stole my breath away.
The fog was still moving fast, and the very next moment the view might be swallowed in white again.
But in that brief instant, I forgot all the fatigue, the weight of my pack, everything—there was nothing I could do but stare at the breathtaking scene before me.
That magnificent view didn’t last long, and before long everything around us was wrapped once more in white cloud.
—So this is what it means to be inside the clouds, I thought suddenly.
When I looked at the back of my hand, I saw that the fine hairs there were covered in countless tiny round droplets.
When I wiped my face with my palm, it was soaked even though it wasn’t raining at all, almost as if I had just washed my face.

Tsurouka-san’s back began to sway again, and I started walking after him, my eyes fixed on the silver-wrapped bundle strapped atop his dark green pack.
I didn’t feel tired anymore.
Maybe it was because we were on the ridge now, where the trail had widened and everything felt open and good—I felt like I could keep walking forever.
All around us, the world was wrapped in white gas.
Each time the clouds shifted, a faint *whump*—like something large flapping overhead—echoed above us, while far away a long *whooo...* of wind rushed through the valleys.
The damp air clung to my skin, and the crunch of stone beneath my boots, mixed with the swaying sound of our packs, formed a steady rhythm.
The clouds around us were moving violently, and the white curtain that had been covering the trail ahead only moments before suddenly swirled upward to the upper left, as if caught in a giant invisible current.
In that instant, everything around us turned faintly golden and bright, and a gentle warmth seeped across my back.
Without thinking, I turned around.
There, at the edge of the white clouds against the blue sky, the sun peeked through.
The sharp glare made me narrow my eyes at once.
—The sun’s out.

When I turned forward again, I could clearly see Tsurouka-san ahead of me, carrying his dark green pack with the silver-glinting bundle wrapped in aluminum sheet and wound round and round with pale blue rope strapped on top, as well as the scenery around us where alpine plants grew thickly.
Our boots crunched as we moved along the slope covered in yellow-green grass and mountain flowers.
White stones lay scattered everywhere, and the trail wound on in a slow curve.
Then, at the edge of my vision, I suddenly sensed a hint of color and turned my eyes toward it.
—Huh?
A ring of rainbow colors was floating inside the towering wall of white cloud.
It was a strange sight, almost impossible to believe.
Fantastical, dreamlike, unbelievably beautiful.
“Wait—? There’s… some kind of round rainbow!” I said.
Maybe the reason I instinctively turned sideways wasn’t to tell Tsurouka-san in front of me, but everyone behind me.
“Ohhh, you can see a Brocken spectre,” Takizawa-san’s voice came from behind, and when I turned around, I could clearly make out everyone standing along the ridge.
For some reason, just seeing Mari-senpai there made me feel relieved.
—A Brocken spectre?
The unfamiliar words threw me for a moment.
I had no idea something like that even existed.
Yabe-san, who had been right behind me, stopped without a word.
“Saku-chan, you’re lucky. It’s not something you get to see very often,” Mari-senpai said from behind Yabe-san, and I smiled at her.
—Wow… so I’m lucky.
Just thinking that made a warm feeling spread quietly in my chest.
We all stopped and stared at the rainbow ring together.
Then Yabe-san spoke softly.
“The one standing in the center of the rainbow is always yourself.”
She said that, but I didn’t really understand what she meant.
As I was still thinking about it, Mari-senpai stepped beside me and explained, “The one in the center of the rainbow is different for each person looking at it. What you’re seeing is the rainbow forming around your own shadow, Saku-chan. Other people can’t see the Brocken spectre with you in the center.”
—Ohhh… I see. That’s fascinating.
I tried raising my hand and stretching it out to the side, and sure enough, the shadow inside the cloud moved exactly the same way.
—So that’s how it works.
Mari-senpai was standing beside me, so next to my shadow in the center of the rainbow ring stood her shadow too.
—It feels like we’re being blessed together.
The thought made the warmth in my chest grow even stronger.
Then, just as I was savoring that feeling, another shadow drifted softly into view, turning the three figures inside the Brocken spectre into three shadows.
Tsurouka-san had come up from behind us.
—Come on… don’t ruin this.
I grumbled inwardly before I could stop myself.
“Whoa, it moved!” Youhei said, raising both arms and making a circle over his head.
But I couldn’t see his shadow inside the Brocken spectre at all.
The shadow in the clouds only responded to my own movements.
It was strange, fun, and somehow mystical.
“The Brocken spectre you see from an airplane window is different,” Yabe-san said. “The airplane’s shadow sits in the center of a perfectly round rainbow.”
—I thought she was quiet, but she actually talks quite a bit.
—So it really is a complete circle… it’s just that we can’t see the bottom from here.
That was what I thought.
“Oh right, Yabetchi used to be in Overseas Operations, didn’t you? Must be nice…” Mari-senpai said.
“Not really. It’s hot, and half the time you can’t understand the language,” Yabe-san replied.
“Hm, is that so? I’ll make sure to let them know,” Mari-senpai said.
“Oops… right, you’re in HR, Mari. But if you’re envying my department, doesn’t that mean you’re dissatisfied with your own?” Yabe-san shot back.
“Hehe… who knows?” Mari-senpai laughed it off.
“Want to trade places?” Yabe-san asked.
“That might actually be nice,” Mari-senpai said.
“Then next time you’ll be the one complaining it’s too hot, and I’ll be saying Overseas Operations sounds nice,” Yabe-san said.
“Hehe, maybe so…” Mari-senpai laughed.
We all waved our hands and struck silly poses at the rainbow ring, laughing together.
Then, in the next instant, a dark shadow descended from above across the wall of cloud where the Brocken spectre shimmered, like a curtain falling over a screen.
Before long it swallowed the rainbow completely, and once again everything was shut away behind white gas.
“Aww… it’s gone,” Mari-senpai said wistfully.
“What a waste,” Youhei added.
I wanted to keep looking at it a little longer too, but ahead of us Tsurouka-san’s pack began to sway once more, and my feet moved forward almost on their own.

Crunch, crunch—
with each step sounding beneath my boots, I started walking again, once more following that back ahead of me.
Through the white gas, I could make out patches of yellow-green grass growing here and there.
Between them stretched a broad trail littered with white stones of every size.
To either side, there was no ground higher than where we were walking now.
Ahead of me, blurred in the white gas, the silver bundle atop the dark green pack swayed back and forth.
