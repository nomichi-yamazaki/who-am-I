Sarukura Trailhead

The sky was blue.
I took a deep breath.

The morning forest was quiet and clear.
Somewhere, a bird chirped—chirp, chirp, chirp—softly, as if announcing the quiet beginning of a new day in the mountains.

“Sho! Is your pack heavy? It’s pretty rough for beginners, you know! …But hey, the weather’s great today—I’m looking forward to it!” Yohei said in his Nagoya dialect.

“Rough,” huh… so he means it’s going to be tough, right? …Yeah, probably, I thought.

The crisp morning air filled my lungs, tightening my cheeks as I breathed in.
A faint ache twisted in my stomach—nerves.

I couldn’t shake off small, nagging worries.
What if I needed to use the bathroom along the way?
What if I couldn’t keep up with everyone… especially people like Mari-senpai-an older colleague I'd met a few times at companiy events-, who had always been so kind to me, and Yabe-san?-the polete "-san"honorific we use in Japanese.-

“…Can I really keep up? Will I make it all the way to the top? What if I burn out halfway…?” I muttered.

“Oh, nervous?” Takizawa said lightly. “You’ll be fine. Anyone can make it if they pace themselves.”

“Don’t start whining already,” Tsuruoka-san said, flicking open his Zippo.

Click. A small flame flared to life.

Each time he drew in smoke, a dry, bitter smell—like burning grass—drifted past my nose.

—Ugh… it’s smoky…

I instinctively turned away, covering my nose.
A cold sweat crept across my back. I wanted to breathe in that crisp morning air again, but before I knew it, the smoke had already mixed into it—like it had stolen the freshness away.

“Cough… cough…”

“It’s your first time, right? That’s normal,” Mari-senpai said gently.

“Huh? What’s this? You two seem pretty close. You know each other?” Tsuruoka-san said, smoke drifting from his mouth. When he finished speaking, he let out a thin stream of smoke through his nose.
“Yeah, we’ve met a few times at company events, right, Saku-chan?”-using the familiar Japaniese suffix"-chan"-called out cheerfully.- Mari-senpai said with a smile.

“Y-yeah…”

“Phew…”
The smoke Tsuruoka exhaled lingered in the air around us. Combined with his presence, it felt oddly oppressive. It was just a cigarette—but somehow, the atmosphere had shifted.

“Well, everyone’s nervous at first,” Takizawa-san said with a calm smile. “We’ll take it slow today. I’m planning to stop about once an hour. No point pushing too hard and burning out. Drink water before you get thirsty—and if your laces are loose, fix them now.”

Ahead of me, a dense green forest stretched out.
To my right, two buildings stood side by side.
The one on the right was larger, with a red roof and a row of windows along its side.
The one on the left was slightly smaller—Sarukura-so, built from dark brown wooden planks, with a row of pillars supporting the overhanging roof at the front.
Gravel covered the ground beneath my feet.
On the far left, a slope overgrown with pale green grass dropped down to a parking lot below.

My breath wasn’t quite visible yet, but the cool air clung to my rolled-up sleeves, sending a faint chill across my skin and tightening my senses just a little.

Back in Nagoya, it had still been too hot to sleep without air conditioning.
But here in Sarukura, even with a fleece on, the air around my neck felt slightly cold.
Without thinking, I tugged at my sleeve and tensed my shoulders.

—Had September really come this far already?
It felt as if time were flowing completely differently here than in the world below.

The unfamiliar weight of my pack slowly dug into my shoulders.
The air around me was chilly, yet I could feel a faint warmth where the pack pressed against my back.

My feet were completely encased in hiking boots made of synthetic leather and nylon mesh.
They weren’t sweaty, but something about them felt… restrictive.

My ankles were held firmly in place, and when I walked, there was none of the lightness or freedom I was used to.
It felt like I was entrusting my feet to something designed purely for walking.

I glanced down at my toes.

The smoky green boots sat heavily at my feet, almost bulky in their presence.
The black rubber parts gleamed faintly in the reflected sunlight.

—I’m really not used to these… What if I get blisters?

The stiff soles didn’t follow the slope of the ground.
Even standing still, it felt like part of my foot wasn’t fully touching anything.

Thick socks and rigid insoles wrapped around my feet, dulling their shape, making it hard to grasp the sensation of walking.

I tried moving them slightly.
Right. Left.

They wouldn’t bend the way I wanted.
It felt as if my feet were locked into a frame.

Even though I could hear my friends laughing behind me, my attention was fixed on my feet.

The tension—and this strange discomfort—slowly began to creep into my mind.

My feet hadn’t even started moving yet, but my thoughts were already trying to walk ahead, driven by nervousness.

And yet, in this space filled with voices flying back and forth, there was laughter—there was something ordinary.

A little ahead of me, Takizawa stood with his arms crossed.

He kept fiddling with the GPS button with his thumb, folding and refolding the map—how many times had he done that already?

He nudged small pebbles with the heel of his hiking boot, silently waiting for everyone to finish getting ready.

He’d said we’d be fine, and he didn’t show it on his face, but… somehow, I couldn’t help thinking there was a slow, heavy weight settling in his stomach.

Taking a group up a mountain wasn’t something you could take lightly.

From beside him, I heard Mari-senpai’s soft laughter.

“Hey, Yo-chan, calm down!”

She was always smiling. Kind to everyone.

Even now, she lightly nudged Yohei with her elbow as he reapplied his lip balm and adjusted his bandana.

She’d probably noticed I was nervous—but she chose not to bring it up.

Instead, she’d glance at my pack and later say something like, “Is your load balanced okay?”

Sometimes, that kindness felt a little frightening.

“Alright! Everyone—this is it! This is the vibe!” Yohei called out.

His flashy T-shirt peeked out from beneath his red outer layer, catching my eye along with the sunlight.

I couldn’t see his eyes behind his sunglasses, but I was sure he was smiling.

On his back was a black pack with strips of rainbow-colored tape—just a little unusual.

Off to the side, Tsuruoka-san was silently adjusting the length of his trekking poles on his own.

He didn’t say anything.
But his expression looked like he had something to complain about.

He pulled his cap back on, wearing it backwards again, and glanced sideways at Takizawa as if he wanted to say something.

Our eyes almost met—but he quickly looked away.

A little farther off, in the shade of a tree, stood Yabe-san.

As always, she had her mouth covered with a black neck gaiter.

She idly twirled a small branch between her fingers, and I couldn’t tell whether she was looking at me or not.

But that movement alone felt strangely quiet—almost out of place.

A cool breeze began to blow.

My back suddenly felt cold, and this time the chill brushed across my skin like a shiver.

The sky, shifting from pale violet to a faint pink, cast a soft light over the eastern mountain range.

Dawn had already passed. The sky was clear, and the stars faded one by one.

Around me, my companions were already starting to come alive.

Takizawa spread out the map once more, tracing the route with his finger.

Mari-senpai adjusted her gloves again, holding down her hat as the wind threatened to carry it away.

Yohei kicked pebbles along the ground, cracking jokes and filling the air with laughter.

Yabe-san twirled a branch between her fingers.
Each of them moved with a mix of anticipation and quiet tension for the journey about to begin.

Tsuruoka-san suddenly pulled a small white portable ashtray from his pack pocket.

When he pressed lightly on both sides, the center puffed up and the opening spread open.

He dropped the finished cigarette inside. With a small click, the flame went out.

I rolled up the sleeve of my fleece once more and gripped the straps of my pack tightly.

—Here we go…

I took a slow, deep breath.

In the quiet morning air, time seemed to drift by slowly.
