Confrontation

Dust drifted through the beam of light pouring in from the hole.
My nose started to itch, and I nearly sneezed.
I pinched my nose shut and barely managed to smother it.
My fingers were probably pitch-black with dust by now, so my nose was probably black too.
Even though I couldn’t actually see myself, I felt as if my entire body had been contaminated with grime.
Mixed in with the smell of gasoline and burnt wood, a sour odor occasionally drifted up through the air — probably from whatever Naoki-kun had vomited onto the floor below.
At first, the noise rising from below sounded like nothing more than distant conversation.
But before long, the texture of it changed.
— Angry voices.
— Heavy sighs.
— The scrape of chairs against the floor.
I carefully peered down through the jagged hole in the ceiling once more.
I looked toward the reception area where everyone had gathered.
Now that I’d turned sideways instead of looking straight down, I could see the room from an angle. The people below were no longer just scattered shapes — I could make out parts of their faces and tell who was sitting where.
In reality, I was lying flat on my stomach across the beam, my head hanging slightly to the left near the hole as I peered downward.
If you considered the dining hall’s right side to be the window side and the left side the hallway side, then at the table nearest the windows sat the two women in their twenties, shoulders pressed tightly together, clutching each other’s hands anxiously.
Further beyond them sat the lone mountaineer in his sixties — the same man who had pointed a knife at me and driven me into the storeroom. He sat there silently with his arms folded.
At the front-center table, lodge owner Okubo-san and Aya Morishita-san sat with their backs toward me. Facing them across the table were Ren Kobayashi-san and Sho Suzuki-san.
At the next table behind them on this side sat, from right to left, Dr. Uchida — who had tried to treat Naoki-kun — Takamura-san, still grieving Akane-san, Mari-senpai, and Takizawa-san.
Across from them sat, from right to left, Yabe-san, Yohei, and Tsuruoka-san.
At the hallway-side table nearest the front sat the parent and child.
At the table behind them sat the athletic guy and the glasses-and-jeans guy.
And gathered between the tables occupied by the lodge staff and the parent-and-child pair were the campers who had come in from outside.
From where I was looking, the seventy-year-old man stood closest to me, slightly off to the left. Behind and to his right stood one of the three college guys—the playful one. Farther behind him was the chubby, round-glasses guy from the trio. Off farther to the left stood the sturdily built, short-haired guy. Beyond them stood the woman of the female-led couple, with her partner slightly behind and to her right. — What are they arguing about?
I held my breath and listened.
“Why the hell did you let people who might be monsters into the lodge?!”
Tsuruoka-san’s roar exploded through the dining hall.
— Ah… so they’re fighting about letting the campers inside…
Tsuruoka-san always ends up at the center of every conflict, I thought.
Off to the left, the pair of men in their thirties nodded to each other and raised their voices.
“Yeah, seriously! Bringing in people who might be monsters is only gonna make things worse!”
Aya Morishita-san stood up, desperately trying to keep her voice calm.
“Please calm down… we’re only doing what we believe is safest for everyone. There’s a storm outside. If we don’t let them in, people could die!”
Her voice trembled slightly.
I felt as if even the crawlspace itself could hear that trembling.
Lodge owner Okubo-san spoke next in a heavy voice.
“It’s our duty to protect climbers. Whether someone seems suspicious or not doesn’t matter.”
Ren Kobayashi-san slowly shook his head.
“It’s difficult to judge something like this… but there’s a storm raging outside. These people are human beings. If something happened to them, who would take responsibility? …Just because there’s danger doesn’t mean we can shut everyone out.”
“The wrong decision here could cost lives later,” Sho Suzuki-san said, following Ren-san’s words — the same man who had defended me earlier.
“We have to stay cautious… but turning people away without mercy isn’t the answer either.”
“Being careful is necessary. But casting people out should be the very last resort,” Suzuki-san continued.
“We’re supposed to protect people. I don’t want anyone getting hurt.”
…And yet, even after that, the suspicion hanging over the room refused to disappear.

“Do the people in this lodge think we’re suspicious or something?!”
The woman from the female-led couple cried out, her voice shaking with tears.
“Why the hell are we being treated like monsters?! We were just out climbing!”
The man who looked to be in his seventies — one of the campers who had come in from outside — slammed his hand against the table as he shouted.
“What the hell is even going on here?! At least explain something! From our side, it just feels like we’re being suspected for no reason!”
said the sturdily built, short-haired guy from the trio of college students.
Tsuruoka-san barked over all of them, crushing their protests beneath his voice.
“Those monsters outside can take human form. We already know that. Ain’t it obvious? One of ’em slipped in among you people and’s trying to fool us!”
For a moment, the entire room fell silent.
Only the sound of the storm outside hammered against the wooden walls.
Then the chaos exploded again.
“We’re desperate too, damn it! Of course we’re scared of letting in people who might be monsters!”
Tsuruoka-san shouted.
“You keep talking about protecting everyone, but you’re just making things more dangerous!”
the athletic guy snapped in agreement with Tsuruoka-san.
Even though he’d been treating Tsuruoka-san like an enemy earlier…
“Anybody suspicious oughta be thrown straight into the storeroom,”
Tsuruoka-san declared.
“Yeah. That’d be safer.”
“Please do that.”
“Everyone who came from outside should be locked up.”
“I agree.”
It was exactly the same as when they’d locked me in the storeroom.
People didn’t want truth.
They wanted reassurance.
Everyone wanted someone they could turn into the villain so they could feel safe themselves.
It really was just like the recent waves of anti-immigrant hysteria, the growing hostility toward neighboring countries, the rapid swing toward fear-driven nationalism… like a witch hunt.
…Only in this case, it was a hunt for those things.
That was what I found myself thinking.
“Cough… cough… cough…”
The woman from the female-led couple began coughing violently.
“Hey! Don’t start coughing over there! What if it spreads?!”
Tsuruoka-san roared.
A child’s crying suddenly rang out through the dining hall.
Not quiet sobbing — screaming.
“Shut that kid up already!”
the athletic guy yelled.
“What do you expect us to do?!”
the father shouted back, panic cracking through his voice.
Near the windows, the two women in their twenties finally broke down crying, covering their faces with their hands.
“Why the hell did we have to get dragged into something like this?!”
said the one who’d been obsessed with that movie actor.
“I think we should’ve organized the situation better before letting anyone inside,”
Takizawa-san said calmly, his voice managing to suppress the uproar, if only slightly.
“But we couldn’t exactly leave people outside to die either. This is a mountain… and there’s a storm out there…”
Mari-senpai shot back, almost as if arguing against Takizawa-san.
“Everyone, please calm down. Let’s organize the information first before we argue any further,”
said Dr. Uchida — the female doctor who had grown close to Yabe-san — as she stood up.
Beside her, Takamura-san quietly looked toward the campers who had arrived later and muttered:
“…Akane… Did any of you… happen to see Akane outside…?”
The atmosphere in the dining hall froze solid.
All because of that single sentence.
Up in the darkness of the crawlspace, my heart gave a violent thud.
— Akane-san was supposed to be dead.
And yet… if someone really had seen her outside, then that would mean whoever saw her might already have been turned into one of those things too.
The voices… the sounds… everything seemed to dissolve into the darkness around me.
The light rising from below suddenly looked alive — flickering like some breathing creature.

I lay sprawled across the dusty beam in the darkness of the crawlspace, straddling it with my body pressed flat against the wood.
The cramped space above and below me was crowded with thick square support pillars rising in every direction.
I gripped the beam tightly with my right hand while my left clung to one of the metal rods suspending the ceiling below, supporting my weight as I shifted my upper body to the left and peered down through the charred, jagged hole burned into the lattice-like ceiling.
Warm air drifted upward from the dining hall below, so I wasn’t cold, but the fact that I was dressed in nothing more than a T-shirt on top, boxer shorts and half-pants below, with bare feet exposed to the dust and darkness, made me feel unbearably vulnerable.
Along with the warmth came the smell of burning and the sour stench that was probably coming from whatever Naoki-kun had vomited onto the floor below.
The odor stung my nose.
Every now and then, the structure let out a sharp creak.
The entire building seemed to shudder around me.
Each time, panic shot through my chest and my grip tightened instinctively around the beam, terrified that the ceiling might collapse beneath my weight.
But when I stopped to think about it, the truth was obvious.
A storm was raging outside.
The violent wind battering the lodge was probably what made the structure groan.
Gooooo…
Shhhhhh…
Zashh… zashh…
Clatter-clatter-clang…
If I listened carefully, I could hear the distant sound of rain and wind slamming against the building from somewhere out in the darkness beyond the walls.
The radio wouldn’t connect.
Cell phones were useless too.
We were stranded high in the mountains, nearly three thousand meters above sea level.
Outside lay nothing but violent wind, pounding rain, and endless blackness.
There was nowhere to run.
