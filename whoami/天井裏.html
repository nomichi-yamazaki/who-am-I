Crawl Space

—I wonder if Mari-senpai is okay…
…What about Yohei? Takizawa-san?
…Not that I care about Tsuruoka-san…
No. I shouldn’t think like that.
Tsuruoka-san is still a human being too…
Right now, everyone in this lodge is in danger.
Uchida-san, the doctor… Takamura-san, who had lost Akane-san…
The lodge owner I’d joked around with about Bon Curry…
Suzuki Sho-san, who I’d first thought was kind of an asshole because he kept complaining, but who had helped me build the weapons and had even defended me earlier, saying I wasn’t one of the “Somethings”…
Kobayashi Ren-san, the mountain photographer…
And Morishita Aya-san, who looked a little like Mei Nagano…

Keeping myself small, I stared blankly down the narrow passageway between the shelves in the storage room.
Canned food. Dried supplies. Old cooking utensils.
Just imagining that one of those strange “Somethings” might be hiding even in a place like this made it impossible to sit still.
—I have to warn Mari-senpai somehow…
The thought of Mari-senpai becoming something else… no. I couldn’t bear it.
With that fear circling through my head, I slowly looked around the storage room again.
Shelves stacked with cardboard boxes and oversized cookware.
Then I looked up.
A wooden ceiling.
And in the corner where the ceiling met the wall, there was a square opening framed in aluminum with a metal trim around it.
An access panel.
—That’s it. The crawl space.
Back when I worked at Sansei Denshi Seiki, I’d crawled through spaces like that searching for electrical insulation faults.
Dust. Heat. Itchy insulation fibers. The low electrical hum of wires running through the darkness.
I could still remember how suffocating those spaces felt.
If this lodge was built the same way, I might be able to crawl through the space above the ceiling all the way to the dining hall.
And if I could do that, maybe I could warn Mari-senpai.
It was the only option I had.
If I opened that access panel, I could get into the crawl space.
The shelves on both sides nearly reached the ceiling, and if I climbed onto the top shelf, I should be able to reach it easily enough.
To pry the panel open, I’d need a flat tool.
At the bottom of the shelf to my front right, there had been what looked like a blue metallic toolbox.
Kneeling down, I pulled it toward me and undid the rusted latch.
Inside were sealing tape, pliers, an adjustable wrench, water-pump pliers, a Phillips screwdriver… and a flathead screwdriver.
“…There.”
I picked it up carefully with my fingertips.
The metal felt cold.
This should be enough to force the panel open.
I’d also need some kind of light.
Then I remembered — when they shoved me into this storage room, I’d seen a flashlight mounted on the wall to the left.
I had no idea whether the batteries still worked, but it was better than nothing.
Slowly exhaling, I whispered to myself.
—I have to go. I have to warn Mari-senpai.
If I crawl through the ceiling space, I should be able to reach the dining hall.
And if I can get my voice to them from up there… maybe there’s still time.
Standing in front of the shelves, I tightened my grip around the screwdriver in my hand.

I slipped the flathead screwdriver into the pocket of my shorts.
Taking the flashlight from its holder on the wall, I shoved it into the other pocket.
I kicked off the slippers at my feet, then peeled off my socks as well.
Honestly, I hated the thought of getting filthy… but slipping and falling would be far worse.
Placing my hands into the narrow gaps between the cardboard boxes, I began climbing the three-tier shelf.
I could feel the hard wooden edges digging into my toes as I climbed higher.
The higher I went, the warmer the air near the ceiling became, brushing heat against my face.
Reaching the access panel, I braced myself carefully against the edge of the shelf with my hips and feet, holding the panel shut with my left hand while pressing the tip of the flathead screwdriver against the screw fastened into the aluminum trim.
As I twisted the screw, the latch released.
The panel gave way to gravity and quietly swung downward.
I jerked my head aside to avoid being hit as it dropped open.
The moment the opening appeared, a strange smell drifted down from the crawl space above.
Dust. Old wood.
And mixed faintly among them… gasoline. Something burned.
Naoki-kun…
I recognized the smell instantly.
A wave of hesitation passed through my chest.
I didn’t want to crawl into that dust-filled space.
But if I had to, there were spare clothes down here.
This was for Mari-senpai.
Steeling myself, I pushed my head up into the opening.

The four corners of the access opening were suspended from above by thin metal rods.
In the darkness,somewhere deeper off to the right, I could make out a faint light.
Beyond the beam immediately to my left was a concrete wall, and behind it, I could picture the generator room.
The place where Suzuki Sho-san and I had built the weapons…
Just thinking about it made my body tense.
The dim yellow light from the storage room below only reached this far.
Everything beyond that was swallowed in darkness.
In a situation like this, peering into a crawl space felt like the kind of thing that might reveal something staring back at you, and a chill crawled up my spine before I could stop it.
When I switched on the flashlight, the narrow but sprawling space above the ceiling emerged faintly in its yellow beam.
Some distance off to the right, a spiderweb stretched between the beams glimmered softly in the light.
For some reason, I found myself thinking that even up here in the mountains, spiders still lived their lives.
Above me spread the underside of the second floor.
Thick beams ran overhead, and between them, rows of narrow wooden joists had been laid side by side at even intervals, each piece roughly 45 by 60 millimeters in cross-section, with the longer side set vertically.
A little higher above the opening, thick beams stretched one after another into the distance at regular intervals, like the rungs of a giant ladder laid across the ceiling.
On top of them stood vertical posts supporting the larger crossbeams above.
And here and there, thick support pillars rose through the darkness, probably running all the way from the first floor to the second.
Beneath the beams, at the same height as the access opening, ceiling panels made from thin wooden battens arranged in a lattice framework with boards fastened underneath were suspended by metal rods and brackets attached both to the undersides of the beams and to the 45-by-60-millimeter wooden members lined up at narrow, even intervals above them.
Judging from the structure, those suspended sections didn’t look very strong.
I definitely shouldn’t put my weight on them.
Back when I was doing electrical inspection work above office ceilings, I’d once stepped straight through a ceiling panel.
I could still remember the scream from the person below.
Well… if a human leg suddenly burst through the ceiling above you, anyone would scream.
Luckily, this crawl space had enough room between the sturdier beams for a person to crawl forward along the tops of them.
The place where faint light was shining up from below — probably where Naoki-kun had been burned — lay beyond three crossbeams from the access opening.
If I crawled forward along the beam beneath me, then shifted right using the third crossbeam, I should be able to reach it.
Even so, thick layers of dust had piled up on top of the beams, enough to make me hesitate before climbing in.
By the time I got out of here, I’d probably be covered head to toe in black dust.
But that didn’t matter right now.
I had to tell Mari-senpai that the people who were supposed to have been attacked were still inside the lodge.
Across the beams, gray flat electrical cables for the lighting system snaked everywhere in tangled, careless loops.
Some sections bulged upward so badly they had slipped halfway off the beams altogether.
I always thought the same thing whenever I saw work like this:
people carefully organized wiring anywhere customers could see it, but above ceilings, everything became sloppy.
Tiny spiderwebs trembled in the stale air, tangled with dust and shimmering faintly in the flashlight beam.
Here and there, joint boxes where the cables branched off had been mounted haphazardly, making the crude construction feel even more exposed.
Carefully, I placed the flashlight on the edge of the opening, gripped both sides with my hands, and lightly kicked off from the shelf below to lift myself upward with my arms.
The moment I did, the structure creaked sharply, and the four metal rods suspending the opening frame began wobbling violently.
My heart pounded at the thought that the entire ceiling might collapse under my weight.
If I put too much pressure near the suspended section, the ceiling panels could give way.
I’d have to spread my weight carefully across both hands and both feet so no single point took too much strain.
Dragging myself forward on all fours, I slowly pulled my body into the opening.
The ceiling panels groaned and creaked beneath me.
Every sound made me tense, certain the hanging rods were about to snap free and send the whole ceiling crashing down.
I reached my left hand toward the beam beside me.
The rough grit of thick dust scraped against my palm.
Carefully, I dragged myself forward and shifted onto the beam.
Thankfully, there was just enough height above it for me to crawl on all fours.
Little by little, I pushed the flashlight ahead of me as I crawled along the beam toward the faint light shining up from below.
Scrrrape…
Scrrrape…
Even trying to stay quiet, I couldn’t keep the noise from escaping.
My nose started to itch, and my throat felt strangely ticklish.
Probably because of all the dust I was stirring up.
The beam itself was thick enough, but just inches to either side lay the weak ceiling panels.
One wrong move and I could punch straight through them.
My heart pounded so hard it made my stomach ache.
Just above my head was the underside of the second floor.
The thick crossbeams overhead looked close enough to smash my skull against if I lifted my head too quickly.
Each time I crawled forward on my knees, the rough wood scraped against the skin exposed beneath my shorts, dust clinging to me until it felt like my legs were turning black.
I tested the edge of the beam carefully with my fingertips, holding my breath whenever I felt even the slightest flex beneath my weight.
Down to my right, flat gray F-cables disappeared into openings within the lattice framework below.
There were probably lighting fixtures mounted underneath the ceiling panels.
The uneven circular holes where the cables passed through glowed faintly with light leaking up from below.
I carefully climbed over one of the F-cables stretched across the beam.
Every movement demanded concentration — one careless snag could yank the cable loose and make a loud noise.
Again, I slid the flashlight farther ahead and crawled toward its beam.
A thick pillar loomed up in front of me.
I’d have to get around it to keep going.
I pushed the flashlight past the pillar first, then stretched my right foot sideways onto the adjoining beam.
Next came my right hand.
Slowly, carefully, I shifted my weight across, moved my left hand after it, and finally dragged my remaining left leg over.
I reached my left hand onto the beam beyond the pillar, followed with my right, and pulled myself forward inch by inch, alternating my hands as I crawled.
My left foot crossed past the pillar, then my right followed, carrying me onward toward the faint glow ahead to my right.
Spiderwebs clung to my hands and arms, leaving prickling strands against my skin.
Dust coated my sleeves and the hems of my shorts, and every breath drew fine powder into my nose and mouth.
The edges of the beams brushing my shoulders carried the smell of old wood mixed faintly with something scorched.
Every careful movement forward meant banging my hands against sharp beam corners and scraping my elbows raw.
My palms and knees grew black with dust, slick with sweat and grime.
Fear and tension quickened my breathing, but I kept moving forward step by step for Mari-senpai’s sake, painfully aware of every sensation — the rough beams beneath my hands, the cold wood against my skin, the gritty dust clinging to me, and the constant terror that the ceiling might give way beneath me at any second.
In the darkness, the only things grounding me in reality were my dust-covered hands and knees as they pushed me onward above the dining hall.
I slid the flashlight ahead again and crawled past another pillar.
—Ah…!
My heart nearly stopped.
As I pushed one knee forward along the beam, it slipped off the edge for a split second, and my body lurched sideways in panic.
Scrrrape…
Scrrrape…
No matter how carefully I crawled, I couldn’t completely silence the noise.
The dining hall should be directly beneath me now.
—Could they hear this from below?
—What if they realized I was up here?
My heart hammered painfully in my chest.
Keeping my knees bent and both hands gripping the beam, I crawled forward as carefully as I could.
Dust worked its way between my fingers, rasping softly every time my palms shifted.
My knees and elbows had darkened with grime as well, and the hems of my clothes had become frayed and stiff with dust clinging to the fabric.
Ahead and to my right, the narrow shaft of yellow light leaking up from below drew closer.
Dust drifting through that beam of light looked almost like tiny floating stars.
The burnt smell grew steadily stronger, and instinctively I knew the light marked the place where Naoki-kun had been burned.
From the third pillar, I stretched my right hand sideways and shifted onto the crossbeam beside it.
As I rotated my body sideways across the beam, the shaft of light that had been ahead to my right slowly shifted toward my left.
Pulling my legs after me, I moved my left hand across as well, then crawled forward hand over hand while sliding my left foot onto the beam and dragging my right after it.
The light was almost directly ahead on my left now.
After crawling a little farther, the hole where the light shone through came level with my head.
When I glanced down through it, I caught a glimpse of the floor below, and my heart lurched at the thought of falling through.
Voices drifted up from beneath me.
People arguing.
But the opening was too small to reveal more than a narrow patch of the room.
Could I widen it somehow?
Carefully, I pressed against the edge of the tiny opening with my left hand.
Crack… rip…
Brittle wood and dust broke away with a harsh tearing sound, widening the hole — and my heart jumped violently in panic.
At the same moment, the voices below stopped.
They couldn’t see me.
They absolutely couldn’t.
I jerked backward instinctively.
For a second I was certain I’d been discovered, and I stopped breathing altogether.
Dust tickled my nose.
A small cough escaped before I could stop it.
Panic surged through me again.
Had they heard that?
I stayed perfectly still.
After a few agonizing seconds, the voices below slowly rose again into uneasy chatter.
Relief washed through me.
The area around the hole must have been weakened and charred by the heat.
Thanks to that, the opening had widened to roughly the size of a human head.
When I carefully brought my face closer, part of the dining hall was still blocked by one of the support pillars below, but I could now see a surprisingly wide section of the room.
Directly beneath me lay the blackened floor, the corner of a table, and fragments of broken ceiling scattered across the ground.
Further ahead to the right, something Naoki-kun had vomited still stained the floor.
Someone had wondered earlier whether that stuff might attack people too…
There had been hair and even eyeballs mixed in with it.
For one horrible moment, I found myself imagining that what lay down there might actually have been Naoki-kun’s real body.
