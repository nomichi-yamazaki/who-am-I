名簿の人々

From inside the opening, I cautiously peered out.
The long rectangular plywood panel Suzuki-san had removed was lying on the floor.
The wooden boards, slick with mucus, reflected the pale yellow light and glistened wetly.
There were signs that something had passed through here.
But for now, nothing seemed to be moving.
Carefully, I eased my head a little farther out of the opening.
My field of view widened.
The corridor to either side.
The sleeping platforms.
The far wall.
There was no sign of them anywhere.
I edged forward a little more.
Now I could see the raised sleeping platform across from me.
The bedding that should have been neatly folded lay disturbed.
A creak echoed from somewhere behind me.
My heart lurched.
Something had attacked from above earlier.
That meant I had to check the beams too.
One by one, my eyes followed the beams, the timbers above them, and the shadows cast by the support posts.
Nothing moved.
Very slowly, some of the tension left my shoulders.
Then I looked around again.
And saw it.
The guest register.
And a pen.
They were lying on the sleeping platform only a few feet from where I had been hiding.
The edges of the paper had absorbed moisture and begun to curl.
Suzuki Shou-san hadn't even had time to take it with him when he fled.
For some reason, I felt as though I shouldn't be there.
A sense of unease stirred deep in my chest.
I placed the register on the floor of the corridor and crouched down, bracing both hands against the boards.
Carefully, I lowered my right foot into the opening and searched for the beam with my toes.
Tap.
Dry wood met my foot.
I steadied my breathing.
Then lowered myself onto my stomach and slowly slipped back into the crawl space.
My shoulders cleared the edge.
Then my back.
The air immediately felt colder.
Stretching out an arm, I reached for the guest register I had left behind.
My fingertips caught it.
The edge of the paper scraped softly against the floor.
Then I carefully pulled it into the crawl space and eased myself farther back.
After crawling backward several dozen centimeters, the light shining up through the opening fell across the register.
I stared at the closed guest register lying in that dim glow.
Its white cardboard cover was stained brown in places.
Along the left edge, spiral creases ran vertically where it had been opened countless times over the years.
The corners, made from layers of thin paper pressed together, had begun to peel apart.
Time itself seemed to have soaked into them.
A black cord threaded through gold eyelets held all the pages firmly together.
Written across the cover in black marker were the words:
Guest Register
Had the lodge owner made it?
Or had it been someone else?
The lodge owner.
Naoki-kun.
Tanaka-san.
Kobayashi Ren-san.
Suzuki Shou-san.
Morishita Aya-san...
Their voices came back to me.
The sound of their laughter echoed faintly in my memory.
Okubo-san, excitedly talking with me about Bon Curry in the dining hall filled with the scent of wood.
The lodge owner's face, with the small mole beside his nose, smiling as if he were slightly self-conscious about it.
Something inside my chest creaked quietly.

I held the guest register up to the yellow light spilling in from outside and slowly turned it in my hands.
The faint rustle of paper echoed in the darkness.
Across the worn cardboard cover, the words Guest Register had been scrawled in black marker.
I opened it.
Inside, rows of names written in pencil and ballpoint pen filled the page.
Written in the order they had arrived, the names stretched down the paper.
And now, in the dim yellow light, they seemed to tremble silently before me.

I flipped through the pages, searching for the date we had written down.
One page.
Then another.
Each time my fingers brushed the edge of a sheet, I felt its faint roughness and caught the lingering smell of ink.
Every page was filled with names.
People who had once lived, laughed, and spent time in this mountain lodge.
Each name seemed like a fossil from a time that would never return.

My finger stopped at a page where there was still space left in the date column.
For some reason, the light seemed softer there.
I rested a hand on the page and let out a slow, quiet breath.

Names written in blue ink stood out from the others in the margin—Tsuyoshi Okubo, Misaki Tanaka, Naoki Takahashi.
A line had been drawn through each of them.
Kobayashi Ren-san, the mountain photographer... He was gone too.
I let out a slow breath and lowered the tip of the pen to Kobayashi Ren-san's name.
As I drew the line through it, the stroke felt unbearably heavy, as though it were making one more terrible truth permanent.
Faces drifted through my mind in fragments.
One after another.
Tsuyoshi Okubo.
Short, graying hair.
A rough stubble beard.
Deep lines carved into his sun-darkened face.
And the large mole beside his nose.
The man who had looked at the aluminum sheet I'd spread out at reception and awkwardly asked,
"You picked it up from the snowfield...? And you're saying it's a fossil inside?"
At dinner, when I told him,
"The original Bon Curry was delicious!"
that intimidating face of his had suddenly broken into a smile.
His eyes had lit up as he launched into an enthusiastic discussion.
"You can't skip Bon Curry at a mountain lodge!"
He had seemed stubborn at first.
But once you talked to him, he was surprisingly warm and good-humored.
Even now, I could still feel the warmth of that Bon Curry conversation we shared in the dining hall that night.
When he opened that door, we trusted him.
And this was where his story ended.

Misaki Tanaka.
When she was put in charge of reception, she had been the kind of person who immediately took an interest in the young woman working in the lodge shop.
When Aya-chan mentioned that she was studying environmental science at university, Tanaka-san had smiled and replied that she taught geography.
That gentle warmth had stood out amid the bustle around the front desk.
She was also the one who had cut away the tendril clinging to Naoki-kun's cheek in the washroom.
"Naoki-kun! Run!"
I could still hear her voice echoing in the back of my mind.
The last time I saw her, she had collapsed as though her body were melting away.
But when we went back later, she was gone.
Gone without a trace.
I still couldn't believe that those hands—that voice—had vanished from this world.

Naoki Takahashi.
A young man who wore a black Adidas tracksuit and whose face softened into an almost puppy-like grin whenever he smiled.
I could still see the excitement in his eyes at reception when he'd said,
"Whoa... what's it like? I wanna see it."
The mischievous look peeking out from beneath his untidy bangs.
The kind of person who would crack jokes one moment and instinctively lend a hand the next whenever someone needed help.
Back then, he hadn't hesitated for even a second.
"I'll give you a hand!"
With that, he'd stepped down into the dirt-floored entryway in nothing but his sandals and reached for the thing wrapped in the aluminum sheet.
Later, when that thing began moving in the washroom, Naoki-kun was the first to rush toward it.
"H-Hey... are you serious...? Where the hell did that come from?"
Then the tendril attached itself to his cheek.
And all I could do was watch as his cries for help grew weaker and weaker.
I still remembered the way he laughed through his tears and muttered,
"...Ahh... so I'm gonna get cooked after all..."
Even as his skin began to melt away, it felt as though the real Naoki-kun was still there somewhere beneath it all.
When he called out Aya-chan's name, there was still something unmistakably human in his voice.
"Ahh... Aya-chan... look what happened to me..."
That shaky plea still tightened something deep inside my chest.
His smile.
His voice.
The warmth of his presence.
All of it was sinking further and further into memory.
If only I'd told him to stop.
If only I'd said something.
The name Naoki Takahashi seemed to tremble on the page before me.

Ren Kobayashi.
A quiet man who introduced himself as a mountain photographer.
Whenever he lifted a camera to his eye, a look of complete focus would come over him. Yet he was always courteous, always considerate of the people around him.
I still remembered the slightly embarrassed smile he gave when he asked Suzuki Sho-san,
"Sho-chan, could you hold my camera for me?"
while helping carry the thing to the drying room.
During the chaos in the washroom, he pulled Takamura-san to safety, called out to people as they fled, and when necessary spoke hard truths no one else wanted to say.
His calm, steady voice had helped hold together what little order we had left.
Which was why, when I saw that eye where his ear should have been...
something inside me broke.

I held down the edge of the page with my fingertips and looked inside the grid.
Across the top row, written neatly from left to right, were the headings:
Date. Meals. Name. Address. Phone Number.
The ruled lines looked as though they had been drawn with an old ruler.
In places they had faded, and traces of blue ballpoint ink overlapped them again and again.
Beneath the Meals heading, every entry was divided into Breakfast and Dinner.
Some boxes bore careful circles.
Others rough, hurried ones.
Some had no marks at all.
In the first entry below the headings, the date read 9/22.
To the right was a name.
Tsubasa Iwase.
Both Breakfast and Dinner had been circled.
Below them were an address in Hikarigaoka, Nerima Ward, Tokyo, and a phone number.
The blue ink had bled slightly into the paper, which had swollen from years of damp mountain air.
Beneath the entry, a single horizontal line had been drawn in blue pen.
The color was faint, but the intention behind it was unmistakable.
It marked the end of one day and the beginning of another.
The guests staying on September 23—today—began below that line.
I stopped breathing for a moment.
My fingertips trembled slightly against the rough paper as my gaze slowly drifted downward.
And kept going.

And then I saw the two names that had been crossed out.
Reiko Takamura.
Akane Ogawa.
A dull ache stirred somewhere deep in my chest.
The pen strokes trembled slightly.
Even the circle beside Akane's meal entry had been drawn twice, as though she'd rushed it.
That was just like her.
I could almost picture her laughing as she hurriedly filled out the form.
Reiko Takamura... Nagaramachi, Gifu City, Gifu Prefecture...
Akane Ogawa... Nakacho, Kakamigahara City, Gifu Prefecture...
"I'm from Gifu too. Well, more or less nearby."
Reiko-san had said that after hearing Tsuruoka-san's dialect.
She had been a calm, graceful woman, someone who reminded me of a young Wakako Sakai.
Akane-san, on the other hand, had been smaller, livelier, always full of energy.
Even when Reiko-san gently scolded her for getting carried away, the two of them had seemed more like sisters than friends.
I remembered meeting them at Hakubajiri.
The two of them had lifted their cameras at the same time and exclaimed,
"The mountains are gorgeous!"
Later, when Takizawa-san said tent camping sounded cool, Akane-san had laughed.
"It just means heavier packs."
And Reiko-san had smiled and added,
"But what we're really looking forward to is cooking our own meals up there and watching the stars."
When Yabe-san mentioned blueberries, Akane-san had immediately replied,
"I love blueberries."
The two of them had laughed together.
Then the conversation had turned to hot springs.
After a moment's thought, Reiko-san had said,
"We're planning on Happo, but depending on how things go, we might consider that too."
Those quiet conversations still lingered somewhere in my ears.
So they were the first guests to arrive today...
Of course they were.
Even at Hakubajiri, they'd arrived after us but started climbing before we did.
While we had been digging that thing out of the snowfield.
Then the washroom came back to me.
Akane-san had gone pale.
"Reiko... what do we do...? This is seriously bad...!"
I couldn't move.
The flickering fluorescent lights tore their shadows apart.
Beyond the mirror, the black mass pulsed.
The sound of running water stopped.
Only Akane-san's frightened voice remained.
"What is this!? I seriously can't—this is too much!!"
I could still hear the tremor in her voice.
Then the black tendrils covered her.
And crushed her head.
"Akane!! Akane!!"
Reiko-san's scream still echoed in my memory.
Back at the lodge, she had collapsed in tears in front of Akane-san's backpack.
A small beige teddy-bear keychain swayed from it—the same one I had noticed earlier at Hakubajiri.
Looking at that little bear, I remember realizing that its owner was gone.
And feeling something tighten painfully inside my chest.
Later, when Uchida-san was about to examine Naoki-kun, Reiko-san had straightened herself and said,
"I'll help. I used to be a nurse."
Her voice had sounded as though she were trying to convince herself.
And afterward...
"Akane... she used to be a nursery teacher. And now... this..."
No one had asked.
Yet the tall, graceful woman said it anyway.
As though she wanted everyone to know that the small, cheerful young woman who was no longer there had mattered.
Had lived.
Had been loved.
Then came the dining hall.
When Akane-san appeared again in her original form, Reiko-san had nearly lost her mind trying to let her inside.
Kobayashi-san and Suzuki Sho-san had to stop her.
And later, when the three newcomers began to transform...
The head of the chubby man with glasses split apart.
Akane-san's face emerged from within.
Its neck stretched impossibly long.
It swallowed the hand of the man in his sixties.
Then his face changed too.
The back of his head became Akane-san's face.
And it attacked Reiko-san while she stood frozen before it.
I traced their names with my finger.
For a moment, it felt as though a trace of their warmth still lingered in the ink.
...I'm sorry.
The words dried up in my throat before they could become a voice.
Outside, the sound of the rain suddenly felt very far away.

Beneath Akane-san's entry was another name.
"Saito Masaru... who was that again?"
I frowned at the unfamiliar name and let my eyes drift farther down the page.
There, written in blue ballpoint pen, were two more names—Yoko Kishida, crossed out with a line, and Osamu Yagi, marked with a question mark.
Something stirred in the depths of my memory.
Right... those two...
They had stayed in the room at the far end of the lodge, on the left side.
A middle-aged man and woman whose movements seemed strangely in sync as they organized their belongings.
The woman wore a gentle smile, yet there had always been something tense hidden behind her eyes.
The man was quiet and reserved, but he seemed to anticipate her needs effortlessly—handing her a towel, carrying part of her load, adjusting his pace to hers.
It was as if he understood her movements before she even made them.
There was something about the distance between them that I couldn't quite explain.
They didn't seem like lovers, or husband and wife, and certainly not siblings—
yet there was a familiarity between them, as though they had grown deeply accustomed to each other's presence.
For a brief moment, the word affair had crossed my mind.
But I hadn't thought much more about it.
Even so, for some reason, the image of the two of them had stayed with me.
At dinner, they had sat together at the same table.
Kishida-san, wearing a red softshell jacket, had tossed out lighthearted jokes that brightened the atmosphere around her.
Yagi-san had accepted them with a slightly embarrassed smile,
pouring tea into her cup or picking up chopsticks she had dropped.
Those small gestures had stirred something inside me for reasons I couldn't explain.
—What had brought them here?
What burdens had they carried with them into these mountains?
Then the scene in the washroom returned to me.
Beneath the flickering fluorescent lights—
"What is that!? Move it! Somebody move it out of the way!"
The voice echoed in my head.
I had never imagined seeing Kishida-san's face, usually so composed, twisted by such raw terror.
Then another memory surfaced.
Yagi-san appearing afterward, searching for her.
"Excuse me—have you seen Kishida-san? A middle-aged woman... strong-willed..."
That voice.
The moment he was told what had happened to her, the color drained from his face.
"...No... that's not possible... That can't be right!"
He kept repeating it as he tried to run outside.
We had to hold him back.
The strange distance that had always existed between them—
in that moment, it felt as though some invisible thread connecting them had been severed.
Their names sat side by side on the page.
The handwriting remained perfectly still.
Yet somewhere deep inside me, those two names still seemed to be screaming.

Ayako Uchida.
The moment I saw her name, something tightened deep in my chest.
She was a doctor.
Calm, reserved, and never one to waste words—but there was strength behind everything she said.
The first time I saw her was in the lounge.
Tsuruoka-san kept trying to draw her into conversation, and though she answered politely with a strained smile, she never let him push too far.
Then, at a pause in the conversation, she simply said,
"I'm sorry... I just remembered something. I think I'll go rest for a bit."
With a slight bow, she rose and headed upstairs.
I remember thinking there was something dignified about the way she walked away.
At dinner, she came down the stairs with Yabe-san, chatting as though they had known each other for years.
When Yabe-san said,
"He's a bit off, you know—just barges right into people's personal space without a second thought,"
she covered her mouth and laughed softly.
"We've got someone like that where I work too."
It was hard to believe they had only met a few hours earlier.
Their smiles looked completely natural.
But after what happened in the washroom, after everyone fled back to the lodge and saw the tendril attached to Naoki-kun's cheek, that smile vanished.
"Don't move. Right now, leaving it alone is the safest thing we can do."
Her hands were trembling.
Even so, she refused to give up.
Beside her, Takamura-san checked Naoki-kun's condition while the two of them desperately tried to find some way to help him as his body continued to change.
Silent screams.
Ragged breathing.
Faces drained of color.
Even then, she never ran.
...She was with us only a little while ago.
Right up until the moment Kobayashi Ren-san and that old man in beige—the one who had threatened me with a knife—changed.
Please... be alive.
Please let me hear her calm voice again.
Let her tell us to stay calm.
Even now, part of me is still listening for her voice somewhere in the darkness.

Through a gap in the crawlspace floor, I stared at the two names written in the register.
Osawa Takuma, Motosōjamachi, Maebashi City, Gunma Prefecture.
Nitta Shinpei, Egimachi, Takasaki City, Gunma Prefecture.
Osawa Takuma’s name had already been struck through with a blue line.
So that meant Osawa was the athletic-looking guy from the two men in their thirties.
And because their names were written together, Nitta Shinpei must have been the glasses-and-jeans guy.
So that accent had been from Gunma.
I remembered the two of them wandering over from the lounge when we first unfolded the aluminum sheet and showed the black fetus-like thing to the lodge staff.
“Whoa—what the hell is that...? Wait, what happened here?”
Neither of them had anything to do with it, yet they pushed their way into the middle of everything, grinning with curiosity.
Then, when it came time to carry the thing to the drying room, they suddenly lost interest.
“Well then... guess we should head back to our room.”
“Yeah, let’s do that.”
Yet later that night, they were at it again.
I could still picture them sneaking into the drying room, peeling back the aluminum sheet, and taking photographs of the black fetus with their phones.
The image had never left my mind.
I remembered seeing them stretched out near the edge of the communal sleeping room on the way back to my place for the night.
Right around there...
Yes—the very area where I was hiding now.
Even after the chaos in the washroom, they kept pressing people for answers, demanding to know what had happened.
In the dining hall, they only added to the confusion.
And then Osawa—the athletic one—had been taken.
I swallowed hard.
Just a little while ago...
I had seen a pair of jeans attached to the back of that monster.
Nobody else in a mountain lodge like this would have been wearing jeans.
The glasses-and-jeans guy...
There was no mistake.
Nitta Shinpei was gone too.
Slowly, I picked up the pen.
I drew a straight line through the name “Nitta Shinpei.”
The faint smell of ink drifted up from the page.
Then, in a voice too quiet for anyone else to hear, I whispered:
“...So they're both gone now.”

From the narrow beam of light, my eyes moved to the next row of the register.
The names written there were Shota Oishi, Yuma Kikuchi, and Kengo Sato.
All three had been crossed out.
When I had said the first ones to start changing in the dining hall were that trio, Sho Suzuki-san had drawn lines through their names.
So these had to be them—the three young guys. The short-haired one, the playful one, and the chubby guy with round glasses.
I remembered them.
When we first arrived at the lodge, they had been gathered around the cooking area, chatting noisily among themselves.
The playful one in the orange quick-dry shirt. The sturdily built short-haired guy. The chubby young man with the round glasses.
I could almost hear the clatter of mugs and smell the coffee drifting through the room.
The slim playful one joking about pronunciation, the short-haired guy immediately arguing back with some overly logical correction, and the round-glasses guy quietly explaining the details.
Their easy rhythm had been youth itself.
I remembered seeing them again outside the drying room after dinner.
Alongside the two men in their thirties, the two women in their twenties, and the father and child, they had crowded around the black fetus-like thing, staring at it with open curiosity.
And the moment there was any chance they might be asked to help, they slipped away without hesitation.
That too had felt unmistakably like something college students would do.
I remembered when they came into the dining hall after the chaos in the washroom.
Soaked by the storm, they had stumbled inside looking desperate.
"Please, help us! Our tent got blown away in the storm!" the playful one had shouted.
The short-haired guy had shrugged helplessly beside him, while the chubby one followed close behind, shivering from the cold.
Even now I could remember the smell of wet clothes, rainwater, and damp fabric rising from them.
Just getting inside must have felt like salvation.
But something tugged at the back of my mind.
Something wasn't right.
Akane-san's face surfaced in my thoughts.
By the time those three had knocked on the door... had one of them already been replaced?
No.
The memory came rushing back.
Akane-san—the thing wearing Akane-san's face—had attacked them after appearing outside the dining hall window.
The desperate smiles and pleading voices from that stormy night were now tangled together with something dark and unnatural.
I let out a slow breath and looked back down at the register.
Tracing the crossed-out names with my fingertip, I remembered their laughter, their panic, and the tragedy that followed.
"...Yeah... that's right..."
"They're gone too."
My grip tightened around the register.
The memories remained vivid and painfully alive.
But the names on the page were cold and silent.

In the next row of the register were two names sharing the same surname: Makoto Iwasaki and Yota Iwasaki.
My pen stopped.
I drew in a slow breath.
...They had to be the father and son.
The two from the cooking area, the ones surrounded by the smell of garlic and sizzling oil.
When I closed my eyes, I could still hear the crackle of food frying in the pan.
"Hey, don't let it burn—keep stirring."
"Aye aye! Chef coming through!"
The father's calm voice and the boy's playful reply echoed back and forth.
For that brief moment, the mountain lodge had felt less like a shelter and more like a family kitchen.
Yota's face had crinkled into a grin.
And around Makoto-san's eyes, the same smile lines had appeared.
"TV is TV. We're using fire in the mountains. Be careful."
I still remembered how gentle his voice had sounded when he said it.
Even Mari-senpai's faint smile came back to me.
"They seem close," she had whispered.
...And yet the last thing that father and son saw was this nightmare.
Inside the dining hall, Makoto-san had pulled his son close, trying to shield him from the chaos around them.
"Papa... I'm scared..."
Even now, that trembling voice lingered somewhere deep in my ears.
Makoto-san had stepped in front of his son as if his own body could protect him.
And then—
When I looked down from the ceiling above—
I watched the two of them disappear together into the shadows of those writhing tendrils.
My hand trembled.
I tried to draw the line, but my arm refused to move.
Even so... I had to do it.
Makoto Iwasaki.
Yota Iwasaki.
Slowly, quietly, I drew a single line through both names.

Beneath them was another name.
Hiroshi Saeki.
A line had already been drawn through it.
Earlier, when Sho Suzuki-san had been marking down the people who had been taken in the washroom, he had crossed out that name as well.
Tanaka-san. Naoki-kun. Akane-san. Kishida-san.
And one more—the camping man in his forties with the thinning hair, glasses, and slightly jutting lower lip.
So that had been Hiroshi Saeki.
I remembered him standing some distance away, silently watching us as we unearthed that black fetus-like thing beneath the Great Snow Valley. He had barely moved, holding his backpack against his chest, simply watching us.
As if he were trying to confirm something.
Even now, I could still feel that gaze from behind those glasses lingering somewhere in my memory.
When we arrived at the lodge, he was in the cooking area, standing quietly by himself.
While the three young guys loudly joked around with their ridiculous "I can do it!" routine, he had simply smiled to himself.
At first glance, he seemed gentle and easygoing.
And yet...
I couldn't shake the feeling that there had been something strangely detached behind that smile.
Something almost like an observer watching from the outside.
After dinner, when the athletic-looking guy and the glasses-and-jeans guy peeled back the aluminum sheet in the drying room to sneak a look inside, Saeki-san had been there too.
Standing slightly apart from the others, peering over from a distance.
I had almost spoken to him.
But for some reason, I hadn't.
Why?
Even then, there had been a faint sense of unease somewhere deep inside me.
And then—
The washroom.
Together with Kishida-san, the strong-willed middle-aged woman, he had been attacked by one of those thing.
One moment they were there.
The next, they had vanished into the darkness.
...Hiroshi Saeki.
The single line drawn through his name seemed to carry the full weight of a life that had truly existed.
A life that had disappeared that night.

I let my gaze drift down to the next entry.
Akari Fujikawa.
And beneath her name, Saya Miura.
Faint smudges marked the paper above them, as if someone had brushed the page with their fingertips.
Back at the reception desk, when the two women came down the stairs, the one who kept talking about her favorite movie character had called the other Saya. So that must have meant her name was Akari Fujikawa.
They had been with me only moments ago.
The sound of their laughter still echoed in my ears.
“Excuse meee! Are you all okay?”
I remembered the two of them climbing up across the snowfield.
There had been something bright about them, as though they belonged to the white world of snow and sunlight itself.
I could still picture the curious expressions on their faces as they watched us dig up that black fetus-like thing.
When I saw them at the lodge, they had been smiling too.
Colorful tights. A pink cardigan.
Talking about movies. Wondering aloud if they should climb Mount Fuji someday.
So carefree. So ordinary.
Who could have imagined it would end like this?
I remembered them sitting by the window in the lounge, scrolling through their phones.
I remembered them laughing together against the wall during dinner.
And now their voices were gone.
Not a trace remained.
From the crawlspace above the dining hall, I had seen them clinging to each other and crying.
The image felt as fresh as if it had happened only seconds ago.
“Why is this happening to us...?”
That cry still lingered in my ears.
“If those things can imitate people, what's stopping them from getting to the second floor too?”
“You really are Saya... right?”
After those words, I never saw either of them again.
And Akari...
Her head had been fused together with that man in his sixties and the glasses-and-jeans guy as they descended the stairs.
I drew a line through the name Akari Fujikawa.
But Saya...
Had she managed to escape?
Had Kobayashi-san gotten to her after he changed?
Or had that beige-clad man in his sixties found her?
I couldn't tell.
I hadn't been able to identify any clothing.
...Please be alive.
Even as I thought that, I picked up the pen and placed a small question mark beside Saya Miura's name.
A dull ache spread through my chest.

Below that...
"So it was Kajiwara..."
I traced the letters on the page with my finger and murmured quietly.
The name Suzuki-san had been about to tell me earlier—the man who had pulled a knife on me and locked me in the storage room.
There was no doubt about it.
Genzo Kajiwara.
I still couldn't believe he was the same man with that calm, measured voice.
"What are you all doing?"
His voice drifted across the white silence of the Great Snow Valley.
When I turned around, I saw a man in his sixties standing out on the snowfield.
Short graying hair. Glasses. Kind-looking eyes.
And yet there had been something unfathomably quiet about him.
"Well, seems we stumbled onto a bit of a discovery," Tsuruoka-san had replied with a grin.
Kajiwara-san gave a small nod.
"I see. But if you push yourselves in a place like that, it could cost you your lives, so please be very careful."
The warning had sounded gentle.
Yet now, looking back, it felt almost prophetic.
At the time, though, I simply kept digging, trying to ignore the uneasiness crawling beneath my skin.
Later, after we'd checked in, I heard footsteps descending the stairs.
Khaki trekking pants. A beige fleece jacket.
Everything about him blended into the same muted colors, as though he were part of the mountain itself.
The moment I saw his face, I recognized the man from the snowfield.
But he never noticed us.
He simply turned away and walked toward the washroom.
