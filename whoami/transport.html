Transport

“Alright then… shall we all carry it together?” Tsuruoka-san said.
“Okay, let’s move it then. Murase-san, Yabe-san—could you stay here and keep an eye on our packs?” Takizawa-san said.
“Well then… guess we should head back to our room,” the athletic guy who had barged in said.
“Yeah, let’s do that,” the man with glasses replied.
—They didn’t want to get roped into helping, so they’re making a run for it.
I thought.
“I’ll help carry it. …Hey, Sho-chan, could you hold my camera for me?” Kobayashi Ren-san said to the receptionist who had complained to me.
“Yeah, sure. …Mind if I transfer the photos to a computer?” the receptionist replied.
“Go ahead,” Ren-san said, handing the camera over to Suzuki Sho-san at the front desk.
“Just don’t go posting anything, alright?” Tsuruoka-san added firmly.
“Yes, understood,” the receptionist said.
“Alright, I’ll head back to the shop. …Aya-chan, thanks,” said Tanaka-san, the high school teacher with the gentle, intelligent expression and ponytail.
“Okay, I’ll get back to work too,” said the girl in the red apron—Aya-chan, the one who looked like Nagano Mei—as she turned and walked toward the back.
“Tanaka-san, sorry about earlier,” Suzuki Sho-san, the receptionist who had complained to me, said as he headed back toward the front desk.
Tsuruoka-san draped the spread-out aluminum sheet back over the black, fetus-like form.
The silver surface glistened faintly, damp with beads of water.
“Alright—here we go!” Takizawa-san’s voice rang out.
“Let’s do it,” Yohei answered.
“Okay,” I replied as well.
Takizawa-san, Tsuruoka-san, Yohei, myself, and Kobayashi Ren-san gathered around the mass, now wrapped in the silver sheet.
Moving to the side, Tsuruoka-san held the sheet in place with one hand and said, “Easy now—don’t put too much force into it.”
“Whoa… it’s kinda slippery. Careful,” Yohei muttered.
“Yeah,” I answered instinctively.
Standing beside us in his slippers, Kobayashi Ren-san lightly touched it with his fingertips.
“Whoa… it’s cold. It’s wet under the aluminum,” he said.
Water seeped little by little from the gaps in the sheet, forming small puddles on the dirt floor.
I steadied it with my left hand and slid my right hand underneath, taking hold of the bundled mass.
Even through the aluminum, the cold of the ice pressed into my palm.
It was chilling—its surface felt faintly rough.
“Whoa… my hand—it feels like I’m touching the ice directly,” I let out without thinking.
“Don’t worry about it. Just don’t let it slip,” Tsuruoka-san replied calmly.
“On three, we lift!” Takizawa-san called out.
“Yeah, got it,” said Tsuruoka-san.
“Alright, let’s go,” said Yohei.
“I’m ready,” said Kobayashi Ren-san.
“Okay,” I said.
“Here we go—one, two!” Takizawa-san shouted.
“Ugh—!” “Heave—!” “Hup!” “There!”
Tsuruoka-san, Yohei, I, and Kobayashi Ren-san answered as we carefully lifted the aluminum-wrapped mass.
Even through the sheet, its weight pressed heavily into my hands.
The cold of the ice, the strange softness beneath it, and that faint, slick sensation—
for a moment, I had to fight the urge to pull my hand away.
“Alright… we’ve got the balance,” Takizawa-san said, checking the load.
“Yeah… I think we’re good,” I replied quietly.
“Okay, let’s set it down on the floor once.”
At Takizawa-san’s words, we carefully lowered the aluminum-wrapped mass onto the floor for a moment.
Then Takizawa-san, Tsuruoka-san, Yohei, and I slipped off our sandals and stepped up onto the wooden floor. Kobayashi-san, meanwhile, climbed up just as he was, still wearing the slippers he had used on the earthen floor.
It felt like some unspoken privilege of the people who worked here.
The four of us headed over to the shoe shelves, took a pair of green slippers, and slipped them on.
Once again, we gathered around the aluminum-wrapped mass, lifted it together with a call, and began walking down the corridor.
Thick square pillars lined the left side at regular intervals, their shadows swaying faintly in the dim light.
As we approached the intersection, it was the same spot where those two men in their thirties had come out earlier.
Glancing to the right, I saw that the rooms closest to us had no doors—just wooden lattice frames on either side, through which the interiors were partially visible.
On the left, the floor was raised and covered with tatami mats. On the right, tables and chairs were arranged in neat rows.
Farther back on the left was a solid wooden sliding door with no windows, while on the right was a glass-paneled sliding door framed with lattice. Straight ahead stood another heavy door, similar to the entrance of the lodge.
As we passed through the intersection, a fire extinguisher was mounted on the right wall, and beyond it, posters of the surrounding mountains and detailed maps of the trails and lodge were plastered across the surface.
Even as my eyes drifted from one thing to another, the weight of the thing in my arms never left my awareness.
The athletic one and the guy with glasses went clattering up the stairs on the left, their footsteps echoing sharply.
At the end of the corridor, a heavy sliding door with a glass panel framed in lattice—just like the entrance—stood in our way.
When we reached it and pulled it open with a rattling sound, a draft of air flowed in—strangely warm for a place this high in the mountains.
The building ahead came into view.
It was a long, low structure, its exterior covered in metal panels painted to resemble brown wood grain.
It showed signs of age, the kind that told you it had stood here for years, enduring the mountain’s seasons.
At the front was an entrance with a sliding door—latticed, with frosted glass set between the wooden frames. Above it hung a sign that read: Toilet / Washroom.
The door was left open, and from where we stood, I could see a long sink along the right wall inside, its surface covered in stainless steel, stretching from front to back.
On the left side, there were doors at both the front and the back—one probably for men, the other for women.
Straight ahead, a frosted window filtered the dim light.
Beyond the washroom building, slightly to the left, was the entrance to the drying room, built in the same style.
Between the corridor we stood on and the washroom building, the ground dropped down a level. Concrete had been laid there, with wooden slats placed on top.
From this building, a simple wooden ramp had been set up to descend to that lower level.
Lining both sides of the passage between the lodge and the washroom building were square wooden posts, supporting beams overhead. A slanted roof made of pale blue translucent plastic and corrugated panels stretched across them.
At the edge of the roof, gray plastic gutters ran along, guiding away rain and melting snow.
“Watch the step there—don’t trip,” Takizawa-san called out as he held his side of the aluminum-wrapped mass.
“Yeah,” we all answered.
“The drying room was to the left, right?” Takizawa-san asked, glancing toward Kobayashi-san.
“That’s right,” he replied.
We went down the ramp one by one.
As soon as I stepped onto it, the wooden plank gave a small clack beneath my weight.
Still holding the thing wrapped in aluminum, I carefully made my way down.
My slipper caught on the edge of the ramp for a split second, almost slipping off, and a chill ran through me.
Five of us, carrying the covered mass, stepped down onto the slatted walkway between the main building and the washroom structure, then turned left.
I tightened my grip and walked along the wooden slats, placing each step carefully as we headed toward the drying room.
Each footstep echoed with a hollow clatter beneath us.
Over Takizawa-san’s shoulder, I caught sight of the drying room entrance ahead.
It was a raised doorway with a sliding door—latticed, with clear glass set between the frames.
Behind us, Kobayashi Ren-san walked in silence, his eyes slightly narrowed, as if he were trying to peer through the aluminum sheet.
At last, we reached the door of the drying room.
Grrr… rattle…
As the sliding door opened, a wave of heat rushed out at us.
A large table stood in the center, and our eyes were naturally drawn to it.
The drying room was enclosed by wooden plank walls and a matching ceiling. On the floor, wooden slats had been laid over concrete.
From ropes stretched between the beams hung colorful rainwear and empty hangers, swaying slightly. The air was thick with a mix of scents—the dry smell of wood, the sour dampness of half-dried clothes, the odor of wet leather and rubber, and faintly, something like stale, humid drainage.
On both the entrance side and the outer wall, windows with clear glass set in wooden lattice frames were fitted into place.
Droplets clung to the glass, silently telling of the moisture trapped inside.
On the table in the center, several pairs of work gloves and nylon gloves lay scattered without order.
The five of us, breathing heavily, checked where to set down the thing wrapped in aluminum.
Still carrying it, we stepped inside.
Each step on the slats produced a faint creak beneath our feet.
“We’ll set it here,” Takizawa-san said.
At his cue, the five of us moved together and carefully lowered the thing onto the table.
The aluminum sheet gave a soft rustling sound, dissolving into the tense air.
I straightened my back and looked up at the door of the drying room.
That was it—for now.
We let out a quiet breath of relief.
I opened my hand, trying to shake off the lingering numbness in my arms.
But no matter what, my eyes wouldn’t leave the bundle.
“Thank you,” I said, joining in.
“Thanks a lot,” Yohei added.
“It’s nothing,” Kobayashi Ren-san replied, narrowing his eyes slightly, a faint, almost shy smile forming at the corners of his mouth.
“Just don’t go posting those photos, alright? I’m asking you,” Tsuruoka-san said, pressing the point.
“Yes, of course,” Kobayashi Ren-san answered.
