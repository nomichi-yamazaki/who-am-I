Hakuba Grand Lodge

“Ohhh, we made it! The hut’s right here!”
Suddenly I heard Tsurouka-san’s voice from just ahead of me, and for a moment I felt almost caught off guard.
—That was kind of anticlimactic.
That was what I found myself thinking.
Out of the white gas, a reddish-brown roof and walls of dark horizontal planks appeared abruptly, standing there all alone.
The windows, perhaps no longer in use, had been boarded shut, and beneath them were piles of things that had clearly been carried up here by human hands, making the front of the hut look a little cluttered.
—Phew… we’re here…
The tension slipped from my shoulders, and it felt as though the unease lodged deep in my chest was slowly melting away.
Even though I had been sure not long ago that I was at my limit, strangely enough it still felt like there was strength left in my legs.
Behind me, the voices of the others rose one after another.
“Ahh… thank goodness,” came Mari-senpai’s voice.
“It’s true! We finally made it to the hut!” said Youhei.
“Ohhh, now we can finally catch our breath,” Takizawa-san added.
Yabe-san emerged from the white gas without saying anything, but for the brief instant our eyes met, I thought I saw the corner of her mouth soften into the faintest smile.

The single-story hut had the shape of a pentagon, like a home plate stretched sideways, and its small windows had been sealed with several rows of horizontal boards.
Various things had been left scattered in front of it in a disorderly way.
The dark brown wooden walls were faded in places, with patches where the color had peeled away, giving the whole hut the worn feel of age.
The walls themselves were made of long horizontal boards layered like steps, each upper plank overlapping the one below it.
It was probably designed that way to keep rain from getting inside.
Closest to the right front of the hut stood a large square box-shaped object sitting on a wooden pallet, covered with a blue tarp and bound round and round with striped rope.
Farther behind it sat an orange plastic fuel tank resting on a stand made from green-painted steel beams with L-shaped cross sections.
To its left was a door with a square pane of frosted glass set into the top.
Beside it stood a narrow little shed with a small reddish-brown sheet-metal gabled awning, its two sides slanting down from the center, probably once used to house a tall propane gas cylinder.
The propane tank itself was gone, and instead a pipe protruded from the wall, its black rubber hose coiled up neatly and wrapped over and over with blue vinyl tape.
To the left of the propane shed stood two upright metal drums, one blue and one orange, with a thick wooden plank laid across them both.
A rusted shovel leaned in the narrow gap between the propane shed and the drums.
Next to them sat a yellow plastic beer crate.
Between the beer crate and the left edge of the building, a tracked cargo carrier was parked along the wall.
It looked like an ordinary handcart, except it had an engine and caterpillar tracks fitted underneath, with switches and a brake lever attached to the handle for steering.
At the hut’s far left corner stood a small white sign.
Alongside a red arrow pointing deeper in, the black letters read: Reception.
For some reason, that sign made my chest stir ever so slightly, as if it were guiding us toward a place beyond that I still didn’t know.
Ahead of me, Tsurouka-san walked in the direction of the arrow, and I followed after him.

The edge of the hut slipped past behind me to the right.
The front of the building narrowed into the mist, tapering away until its far end vanished completely into the white fog.
Crunch, crunch—the sound of my boots on the stones dissolved into the haze.
Now and then, the wind whistled sharply along the mountainside, brushing past my ears as the cold air stung my cheeks.
The windows lined up along the visible stretch of the building on the right were all boarded over in exactly the same way.
It looked almost like an abandoned shack, and a ripple of unease spread through my chest.
—What if this hut isn’t even in use anymore?
If that’s true, does that mean we still have to keep walking to the real mountain lodge?
The thought made the weight on my back suddenly feel heavier.

At the front corner of the building, a water pipe rose out of the ground, its end bent into an L-shape with an old faucet attached, and beneath it lay a green hose coiled into tight loops.
Beside it sat a single bucket with its white paint peeling away.
When the wind picked up for a moment, the end of the hose trembled faintly, making a dry rustling sound.
Farther along, beneath another boarded window, a bench missing one of its legs had been left leaning crooked against the wall.
When I glanced back to the left, I could make out red, blue, yellow, orange, and yellow-green tents standing dimly in the white gas, their colors bleeding softly into the fog.
—Ah… if tents are pitched here, then I guess this really is the right place.
I found myself wondering if the tent of those two women we’d met at Hakubajiri—the ones who said they were traversing all the way toward Mt. Karamatsu—might be somewhere among them.
To my right was the decaying building; to my left, the colorful tents that hinted at human presence.
Passing through that contrast, I kept moving forward, my boots crunching steadily over the stones.

Before long, the right side of the building, its length seeming to narrow with perspective, slowly began to emerge from the white gas.
A shadow like an entrance took shape in the wall.
As we drew closer, a small gabled awning emerged from the mist, jutting out above what should have been the doorway.
But that too had been boarded over with plywood.
A scrap of paper, swollen and warped from the rain, had been pasted onto it, the dirty letters reading: NO ENTRY.
Above the doorway, only a blackened outline remained where a signboard had once been mounted.
—So it really does look like a hut that’s no longer in use…
The thought only deepened my unease.
Even if I still felt like I could keep walking, being made to expect rest at last only to be forced onward would be brutal.
Keeping my eyes on the silver mass wrapped in aluminum sheet swaying atop the dark green pack ahead, I moved farther on, and beyond the sealed entrance I noticed several windows that still looked as if they were in use.
—Ah, those windows aren’t boarded up…
Does that mean this place is still being used?
Then, as if pushing the gas aside, an even larger two-story building began to emerge farther beyond.
—Ah, so the one beside us is the old wing, and the one ahead must be the building still in use.
The outer wall of the building ahead was painted a lighter brown than the old wing we were passing now, and the contrast with the dark, shuttered structure beside me brought a small sense of relief back to my chest.
We passed in front of the sealed entrance to the old wing.
Little by little, the scene ahead began to reveal itself through the white gas.
In front of me, Tsuruoka-san’s back swayed as he walked, the silver-wrapped mass still strapped atop his dark green pack.

Lively voices drifted over from somewhere ahead to the right.
Just knowing we weren’t the only ones here made me feel a little more at ease.
Beneath the windows of the old wing on the right, a faded blue tarp lay folded on top of a wooden pallet, weighed down by a single large stone.
We passed by it.
Off to the left, half-veiled in the white gas, a young man and woman were pitching a tent.
“Get yourself together,” the woman’s voice carried faintly on the wind.
—Those two… they were the pair taking pictures of us while we were digging that thing out from on top of Tsuruoka-san’s pack…
Even they didn’t seem to be paying any attention to us now.

Ahead to the right, the new two-story lodge was drawing closer.
It had a red roof and walls built from horizontal timber planks in a lighter brown than the old wing.
At the very front was a wooden sliding door.
Above it, panes of glass divided by a three-by-three wooden lattice were set into the frame, and with one side left open, I could catch a glimpse of the inside.
A red bench sat beneath the dark brown sash window just beyond the doorway.
The near edge of the new lodge approached from the right front.
Through the half-open sliding door and the window beside it, I caught another brief glimpse inside.
In the cooking area, three young men huddled shoulder to shoulder, chatting animatedly over the soft hiss of a camping stove.
The faint clatter of mugs touching the tabletop reached my ears, and the rich aroma of coffee drifted all the way out to us on the wind.
On the table in front of them, thermoses, half-used cups, snack wrappers, a partially eaten pack of rice balls, and steaming mugs lay scattered in easy disorder, quietly telling of the messy, lived-in rhythm of the three of them.

“Isn’t the pronunciation of can kind of hard?” said a lanky guy with a slight hunch, his brown hair tied back roughly, wearing an orange quick-dry T-shirt under a gray jacket, his tone loose and playful.
“Yeah, I thought so too,” said a sturdily built guy with cropped hair and sun-darkened skin, a navy fleece hanging from his shoulders. “When I asked a foreigner, they told me can is ‘can,’ and can’t is ‘can’t,’ but when you actually listen, nobody really says can’t like that.”
They looked about college age, I thought as I walked past.
There was one other man in the room, maybe in his forties, and he wore the expression of someone completely uninvolved as he focused on his cooking.
He had the look of a veteran climber—balding from his forehead to the crown, glasses on, his lower lip jutting out slightly.
—Ah… that’s the guy who was watching while we were digging that thing up, then just walked off without saying anything…
He wore a subdued gray shirt with khaki climbing pants.
Steam rose from the pot set over the camping stove in front of him, the movement of it strangely in sync with his own steady motions.
“The other day, I said I can do it to mean I could do something, and they thought I meant I couldn’t,” the playful guy said.
—That balding, bespectacled middle-aged guy is acting like this has nothing to do with him, but he’s definitely listening to the college guys talk… I thought.
“When you mean can, apparently you pronounce it more like ken,” said the third guy, average-built and slightly chubby, wearing round glasses, his short black hair tousled with bedhead beneath a light gray softshell jacket.
“No way… then when you can do something, should I say I ken do it?” the playful guy said.
“Yeah. If you say I can do it, people might take it as can’t,” Round Glasses said.
“Then if you just nod or shake your head while saying I can do it, wouldn’t it work either way?” the sturdily built guy said, and the three of them burst into loud laughter.
I didn’t miss the way the middle-aged man, who had been pretending not to care, let the corners of his mouth loosen.
—Knew it. He was listening after all, I thought.
“That’d be a problem over the phone…” the playful guy muttered.
“Heh…” Behind me, I heard Yabe-san give a small laugh.
It was the kind of laugh that sounded just a little mocking.
Why had she laughed?

The row of brown sash windows lining the building on the right gradually slipped behind us.
When I glanced up, I saw the same windows continuing along the second floor.
Then, through the white gas, what looked like the entrance to the building came into view ahead on the right.
The doorway had the same kind of sliding door as before, and above it a long signboard made from a naturally shaped piece of driftwood, cut flat like a plank, was mounted horizontally. Carved into it in black letters were the words Hakuba Grand Lodge.
Even the katakana had a brushstroke quality to it, which somehow made it a little amusing.
“We’re here,” Tsuruoka-san said, glancing back over his shoulder before sliding the entrance door open with a rattling garagara.
—Yes. We made it, I thought.
Tsuruoka-san quietly stepped into the still air inside.
For a moment, I hesitated, wondering if I should follow him in.
My feet almost moved on their own, but then I remembered that Takizawa-san was the leader, and decided it would be better to follow after him instead.
Peering in from the entrance of the mountain lodge, I saw that the concrete at my feet was dark and damp.
One step up from there was a wooden floor, where Tsuruoka-san had already lowered his large pack and sat down as if loosening the strain from his shoulders.
Across the top of the dark green pack lay that bulky bundle, wrapped in silver emergency foil and bound tightly with a pale blue climbing rope.
He bent down and began casually undoing his shoelaces, slipping off his boots without a second thought.

I stopped in front of the doorway and turned back the way we had come.
—Right… Yabe-san’s in the overseas business division, so she can speak English…
So that’s why she laughed when those three were talking about can… Just as that thought crossed my mind—
“If you’re not coming in, please close the door.” A low voice came from inside the lodge.
When I turned around, a man off to the left had bent his body so he could look past the angle and see me clearly.
“Ah, sorry…” I hurriedly slid the open door shut.
The wooden frame rattled loudly as it closed.
“...Seriously, complaining over something like that…” I muttered under my breath, too quietly for anyone to hear.
—Back at Hakubajiri, I hadn’t complained even when Yohei left the toilet door hanging open. What a petty little guy, I thought.

From behind me, still outside in the white gas, the voices of the others came one after another.
“We made it…” Yabe-san said softly.
She emerged from the white gas and stopped beside me.
“Ahh, finally,” Mari-senpai breathed in relief.
She appeared from the mist as well and came to a stop beside Yabe-san.
“Yes! We’re finally here!” Yohei’s excited voice rang out through the fog.
He came up to where I was standing.
“Where’s Tsuru-san?” Takizawa-san asked as he emerged last from the white gas.
“He’s already inside,” I answered.
“Heh, that’s so like Tsuruoka-san,” Mari-senpai said with a laugh.
“Seriously,” Yabe-san added.
“Well then, shall we head in? Afternoon,” Takizawa-san said as he slid the door open and stepped inside.
I hesitated for a moment, then glanced back and waited for Mari-senpai and Yohei.
“Come on, Saku-chan, get in,” Mari-senpai said, giving my back a gentle push.
I managed to force out a small “Hello,” and stepped inside after them.
