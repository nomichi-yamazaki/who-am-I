The Price of "Something"

Three years had passed since the incident, and life seemed to have settled back into something resembling normal.
The word Nanika had all but disappeared from the news.
The nationwide purge once known as the "Nanika Hunt" had been carried out under government orders.
Those believed to have replaced humans and blended into society were taken to secure facilities, where they were kept under strict supervision and studied.
And when the research was finally complete, the answer turned out to be almost disappointingly simple.
Nanika were—
colonies of slime mold–like unicellular organisms. They exchanged information through tiny membrane-bound messenger particles, much like exosomes.
They had no intelligence, no will of their own.
But when they experienced stress—fear above all—their survival instinct drove them to mutate and multiply.
They had no will.
No purpose.
They simply sought a place to survive, using the bodies of other living creatures as their shelter.
They replaced human bodies, settled inside the host's brain, and copied its memories and knowledge, behaving as though they were the original person.
Learning that brought back something Tsuruoka-san had said ten years earlier, inside the mountain lodge dining hall.
“It’s obvious, ain’t it? It was gonna attack everyone in here and turn us all into its own kind.”
Even back then, something about those words had never sat right with me.
Now, at last, I understood why.
We hadn't been terrified of Nanika themselves.
We were terrified because we didn't know what they were.
We didn't know who—or what—they were.
We didn't know what they wanted.
So we convinced ourselves we had no choice but to attack.
But once we understood...
Nanika were no longer monsters to fear.
They were simply living things trying to survive.
Back when we'd first discovered that black fetus in the Great Snow Valley, Tsuruoka-san had said something like, “The world runs on competition.”
Yet when that same black fetus began to mutate in the lodge washroom, he let out a strange scream and became the first one to run.
Yabe-san had watched him go and muttered, "The ones who act the toughest are usually the first to break,"
Later, when I spoke to Tsuruoka-san through the ceiling while everyone was barricaded inside the staff room... he'd already soiled himself.
That was when I began wondering if people competed because they were afraid—because fear was stronger than they were.
And then there was Nanika.
Whenever they felt fear, they began to divide and attack people.
The researchers said they possessed no intelligence whatsoever.
Which meant they weren't acting out of hatred...
only out of instinct.
If that was true...
then wasn't human fear born from the very same place?
Perhaps our own competitiveness came from the fear of seeing our pride wounded—a fear rooted in nothing more than our instinct to survive.
Looking back now, I finally understood why the delinquents at the industrial high school I attended in Aichi smashed windows.
It wasn't because they were fearless.
It was because they were frightened.
Because they felt weak, they had to make themselves look strong.
And the boys who bullied me...
perhaps they were frightened of those same delinquents.
Perhaps they, too, needed someone weaker than themselves to feel safe.
Desire gives birth to competition.
Competition breeds fear.
Fear becomes hatred.
Hatred becomes anger.
And anger gives birth to conflict.
If all of it ultimately grows out of the instinct to survive... then maybe competing with one another is, in some ways, simply part of being human.

But human beings are supposed to possess reason.
Reason should give us the strength to endure fear—
to keep our pride and selfishness in check.
Ever since I was a child,
I'd never liked the idea
that the world had already been decided for us
the moment we were born.
Wasn't that simply someone else's way of seeing the world?
If so, then I had every right
to think for myself...
and to build a different one.
That's how I'd always tried to live.
So when Nanika's true nature was finally revealed,
I found myself thinking something.
One person walks to the right,
and everyone else follows.
One person turns left,
and the crowd flows left with them.
Someone cries, "She's a witch!"
and before long, everyone is shouting the same thing,
and the witch hunt begins.
The Nanika hunts had been no different.
Instead of thinking for themselves,
people surrendered their judgment to the crowd.
And it struck me then...
wasn't that, in its own way,
the same as Nanika?
Living through someone else's mind,
without thinking for yourself—
driven not by reason,
but by nothing more than fear
and the instinct to survive.

Nanika replicated human DNA with near-perfect accuracy.
There was only one difference.
A single genetic region—
one unique sequence that allowed them to reshape their bodies at will.
In theory, if that sequence could be removed,
a Nanika would become nothing more than an ordinary human.
That was where CRISPR Injection came in.
CRISPR is a gene-editing technology capable of cutting DNA at precisely targeted locations.
Like a pair of molecular scissors, it snips away only the sequence that needs to be removed.
Injected into the body, the CRISPR treatment sought out the mutation gene unique to the Nanika and excised it with remarkable precision.
The results were exactly as the researchers had hoped.
Those who underwent the treatment lost the ability to mutate,
becoming, both genetically and physiologically,
indistinguishable from ordinary human beings once again.

When I learned the truth, I couldn't shake one strange thought.
What if the Nanika had never really been our enemy at all?
They simply reacted to fear,
changed,
and multiplied.
They had no desire to attack,
no ambition to rule anyone.
All they had ever tried to do
was survive by existing alongside us.
Maybe it was we humans who had attacked them,
simply because we feared what we didn't understand.
And I found myself wondering...
perhaps enemies don't truly exist at all.
Perhaps they're something our own hearts create.
Once the mutation gene had been removed through CRISPR,
the Nanika hadn't lost anything that truly mattered.
If anything,
they had simply returned to a way of living alongside us.
When two lives can coexist,
both can survive.
It was such a simple answer...
and yet humanity had forgotten it, again and again, throughout history.
Among all the other human species that disappeared,
only Homo sapiens endured.
Not because we had sharper teeth,
or greater strength,
but because we learned to cooperate,
to share,
and to find common ground instead of endless conflict.
In a society where life's necessities are already abundant,
no one should have to live in constant fear of survival.
But the moment money—something that was meant to be nothing more than a tool for distributing resources—became concentrated in too few hands,
fear began to grow.
What if it's taken away?
What if losing means I can't survive?
Fear gives birth to competition,
and competition slowly convinces us
that selfishness and exclusion are somehow justified.

Human beings are, by nature, creatures of reason.
We have the capacity to restrain our pride,
to keep our selfish desires in check,
and to find common ground with one another.
What keeps us from doing so,
perhaps,
is our inability to overcome fear.
Every person in the world is the same species—Homo sapiens.
Even if we're fortunate,
our lives amount to little more than a hundred years.
For such a brief moment,
why spend it hating one another,
or fighting over what we can take?
There are kind people in Japan.
There are cruel people too.
The same is true of every neighboring country,
and every nation on Earth.
To say, "They're bad because they're from there,"
is to mistake the tiny view through the eye of a needle for the whole world.
What we need
is a broader heart—
one with room enough to embrace those who are different from us.
To overcome fear,
and to choose coexistence
instead of taking from one another.
Perhaps that was what the Nanika had been showing us all along,
simply by the way they existed.
A Nanika could become a human...
or a monster.
And perhaps we humans are no different.
By holding fast to reason,
we can keep our pride and selfishness in check
and remain truly human.
Or we can surrender to our desires,
and become monsters ourselves.
Sometimes I can't help wondering...
maybe the Nanika came to this planet
not to conquer us,
but to make us take a long, hard look at ourselves.

There was one more thing I couldn't stop thinking about.
It was what happened at the very end,
back at that mountain lodge.
When Yabe-san and I decided
that we couldn't let this nightmare reach the world below,
I tried to burn the lodge down.
To be honest...
I wasn't afraid.
I might have died.
I might have been arrested.
I had no idea what I stood to lose.
And yet...
none of it seemed to matter anymore.
What was it that had stopped mattering?
No...
it was everything.
The narrower our world becomes,
the more easily we're hurt.
The more things we cling to,
the more things we're desperate to protect,
the smaller our capacity to endure becomes.
And from that narrowness,
fear is born.
But the moment everything stopped mattering...
I stopped being vulnerable to fear.
It wasn't that I no longer had anything worth protecting.
I had simply stopped being chained by the need to protect it.
Maybe it was nothing more than extraordinary strength born of desperation.
Maybe it was what climbers call a climber's high.
Or perhaps...
it was something people would call enlightenment.
Whatever it was,
I know one thing for certain.
In that moment,
fear no longer ruled me.
If people could gradually broaden their horizons,
little by little,
perhaps we'd become harder to wound.
Our capacity to accept would grow,
and we'd learn to embrace the differences between us.
Coexistence isn't some distant ideal.
It's simply what emerges naturally
once fear begins to loosen its grip.
If humanity hopes to keep moving forward,
then overcoming fear may be the most important challenge of all.
And perhaps...
one of the strongest ways to overcome fear
is to learn to tell ourselves,
"Maybe it doesn't matter as much as I think."
Ever since that day,
since the moment everything stopped mattering at that mountain lodge,
I've found myself thinking that more and more often.

I found myself thinking...
Animals,
birds,
fish,
even insects...
they've carried life forward for countless generations
without ever inventing something called money.
Maybe...
life was always meant to be much simpler than we've made it.
Instead of fighting over work,
perhaps we could share it,
producing what we need,
along with enough to prepare for tomorrow.
Money would still have its place—
simply as a tool for exchanging those things.
Not something to be hoarded by a fortunate few,
but something that could circulate fairly
among everyone who is alive.
Perhaps that alone would free us
from spending our lives chasing money,
or racing against time itself.
People might once again have the time
to raise children,
without feeling they had to sacrifice everything else.
We wouldn't need endless production,
and perhaps the forests,
the oceans,
and the sky
could finally begin to heal.
No one would have to lose
just so someone else could win.
There would be no reason to destroy
what generations had worked so hard to build through war,
or to keep wounding one another in the process.
And yet...
humanity still struggles to let go
of the fear that there won't be enough.
So even in a world that already has more than enough,
we go on risking our lives
in competitions that never truly end.

In the end,
the Nanika came to be seen as something that could be cured.
The campaign to eradicate them accelerated,
driving the species to the very brink of extinction.
Everyone believed
that was the end of the story.
But the final handful of patients kept under observation
turned everything upside down.
Every disease they had carried before being parasitized...
had vanished.
Terminal cancer.
ALS.
Diabetes.
Advanced dementia.
Inherited genetic disorders.
Every one of them...
gone without a trace.
In hindsight, it made perfect sense.
The original body had been replaced entirely
by Nanika cells.
As research continued,
another extraordinary discovery emerged.
Nanika cells possessed the ability
to transform into virtually any type of human cell.
Neurons.
Muscle tissue.
Internal organs.
All without triggering immune rejection.
For regenerative medicine,
they were nothing short of the ideal building material for life itself.
From that day on,
the language began to change.
"Nanika hunts"
became
"Nanika recovery operations."
"Extermination"
became
"Protection."
Governments and corporations quietly began competing
to secure what few Nanika remained.
The beings people had once feared and despised...
had become resources worth fortunes.
Someone joked one day,
"Looks like we humans haven't changed at all."
The moment an enemy proves useful,
it becomes a treasure.
As they watched the dark mass of cells
floating inside a culture tank beyond the laboratory glass,
no one could bring themselves to argue.
The true nature of the Nanika
had finally been understood.
But...
the true nature of humanity
remained an unsolved mystery.
