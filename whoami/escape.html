Escape

I cranked the timer on the Sunpot heater as far as it would go, then spun around and burst out through the steel door.
The rain had almost stopped.
Not long ago, a T-shirt and shorts had felt warm enough in the muggy air.
Now, even with all the layers I was wearing, the cold bit deep into me.
Leaning against the outer wall were Yabe-san's pack and my own.
I looked at hers.
When I had given my rainwear to Morishita Aya-san, Yabe-san had said,
*"Just wear somebody else's. There should be one in your room, right?... They're not going to need them anymore."*
Those words drifted back into my mind.
I guess Yabe-san wouldn't be needing this pack anymore.
The thought made something deep inside my chest ache.
Without saying a word, I shouldered my own pack.
And then—
I simply started running back the way we had come.

The sound of rushing water was everywhere around me.
From the rooftops, from cracks in the rocks, from beneath the ground itself.
It was as though the mountain was still catching its breath.
Amid that endless murmur, I could hear the crunch of my own hiking boots on the stones.
In the end, I was alone.
With that thought in mind, I turned toward the mountain trail.
I rounded the shadow of the steel door to the left and followed the side of the building.
With every step, the sound of running water seemed to trail after me.
I could sense unseen streams crossing the path beneath my feet.
The rain had almost stopped.
When I looked up, I saw that the pitch-black clouds had begun to tear apart, revealing a deep indigo sky beyond them.
That indigo sky seemed faintly illuminated, as though dawn were beginning to stir behind it.
It looked like the typhoon had finally passed its peak.
Yet the endless rushing of water continued without pause.
Above me on the left was the dining hall window.
Only a dim yellow light remained inside, and nothing moved behind the glass.
—This was the place.
The very window where Akane-san had desperately pounded, begging to be let in.
Before the memory could take hold, I ran.
I ran.
Forcing my heavy hiking boots to tear themselves free from the ground with every stride.
I passed the entrance to the lodge.
The waterproof sheet they had used to carry out Naoki-kun's burned body still lay spread beside it.
Hurry... hurry...
If I didn't, the whole place would explode.
My mind kept urging me onward.
My heart pounded so hard it felt like my stomach might leap out through my throat.
I ran past the lounge and beyond the cooking area.
The memories surfaced on their own.
The place where the playful college guy, the sturdily built short-haired one, and the chubby, round-glasses one had laughed together about "I Can Do It."
The seat where the man, balding from his forehead to the crown, glasses on, his lower lip jutting out slightly, had pretended not to listen while secretly eavesdropping on their conversation.
And then—the old wing.
The window of the communal sleeping room.
That was where my best friend, Yohei, had changed.
Something clenched tightly inside my chest.
Everyone...
Everyone was gone...
The entrance to the old wing had been boarded shut with wooden planks.
The space beside it lay in darkness, with only a faint yellow glow slipping through the gaps between the boards.
I need to get my headlamp ready somewhere...
The thought crossed my mind for an instant.
But this building could explode and burst into flames at any moment.
I had to get away from it as quickly as possible.
The moment I stepped out from the shelter of the building, a powerful gust of wind slammed into me, hurling the last lingering raindrops against my body.
The endless rushing sound never stopped.
Neither quite the wind nor the remnants of the rain,
it alone seemed unnaturally loud in my ears.
Ahead of me was nothing but darkness.
I could barely see.
I had no choice but to feel my way forward with my feet, and that inevitably slowed me down.
Still, I remembered the path we had taken on the way here.
If I kept moving away from the lodge, there should be a fork ahead.
If I turned left there, I would reach the trail leading down the mountain.
I had to get farther away.
The lodge was going to explode.
Panic welled up inside me.
I stumbled over a rock, my body pitching forward.
Nearly falling, I instinctively dropped onto all fours.
The sound of water covering the ground suddenly seemed much closer.
I could no longer tell where the path ended and the streams began.
Water flowing over the stones washed across my hands.
Feeling my way along the ground with both palms,
I...
Moved farther and farther away from the mountain lodge.

Suddenly, a low roaring sound rose behind me,
followed almost immediately by a heavy, booming impact.
BOOM!
The next instant,
a blast of pressure slammed into my back,
as if someone had kicked me hard from behind.
At the same time—
it felt as though countless voices, men and women alike, had spread across the night sky.
A mass of cries that were neither screams nor moans
swelled in an instant,
then vanished as though the darkness itself had swallowed them whole.
...Mari-senpai too...
The thought made my chest tighten painfully.
The boom didn't come only once.
It echoed again and again through the surrounding mountains.
It felt as though the sound had carried far beyond this mountain,
all the way to the towns in the distance.
Then,
a yellow glow spread across my back.
My shadow fell sharply onto the ground before me.
A moment later, the heat arrived.
A wave of warmth pressed down on me from behind.
Somewhere back there, the lodge continued to burn.
Whoooosh...
Whoooosh...
The flames, whipped by the wind,
let out a low roar that seemed to scrape against the very air itself.
Without thinking, I looked up at the sky.
The underside of the black clouds had turned yellow in the firelight,
swaying eerily above me.
Realizing that everything around me had grown a little brighter,
I pushed myself back to my feet.
Looking down, I saw only scattered rocks beneath me.
At least this had to be the trail.
Even so,
the shadows cast by the larger boulders remained as black as spilled ink.
As I started walking again,
my own shadow stretched out ahead of me.
The farther I moved from the mountain lodge,
the longer it grew,
dancing and swaying in time with the flickering fire behind me.
I could see this far, at least.
But beyond that lay only darkness.
I couldn't make anything out,
though there should have been mountains all around me in the open night.
I just hoped I hadn't wandered onto the edge of some unseen cliff.
After surviving this much,
I didn't want it all to end with a single misstep into the abyss.
I should get my headlamp ready while I still had some light.
I set my pack down and unzipped the lid compartment.
Taking out my headlamp, I strapped it onto my head.
I pressed the small rubber button on the side.
Click.
A circle of white LED light sprang to life,
revealing a mountain trail littered with jagged stones and scattered boulders of every size.
Beyond the reach of the beam,
the darkness on either side remained as deep as ever.
What if something suddenly leapt out from that darkness?
The thought crossed my mind,
and a small shiver ran through me.
I found myself sweeping the light around,
to the left,
to the right,
and even behind me.
...Nothing.
Nobody was there.
Instead,
somewhere in the darkness at my feet,
I heard the soft sound of running water.
Saaah...
The remnants of the rain must have been threading their way
through cracks in the rocks and across the mountain trail.
The night should have been quiet,
yet that sound alone seemed unnaturally loud.
Far beyond it,
the roar of the flames still hadn't faded.
Whoooosh...
The fire was still burning.
Another gust of wind swept over me.
The rainwear Yōhei had left behind
flapped noisily against my body.
Yōhei too...
Mari-senpai too...
They're gone now.
The moment that thought crossed my mind,
a quiet but unmistakable pain settled deep within my chest.

The roaring of the burning mountain lodge gradually faded into the distance behind me.
The moment that beast-like bellow—the sound of fire devouring the air itself—finally disappeared, the world felt as though it had fallen into a vacuum.
This was the place.
The arrangement of the rocks beneath my feet and the way the wind moved through here felt familiar.
This had to be where we'd seen the Brocken spectre.
Standing above the sea of clouds,
my shadow had swayed within a ring of rainbow-colored light.
There had been no fear then—only exhilaration.
For a fleeting moment, it had even felt as though the world itself were blessing me.
"Saku-chan, you're lucky.
It's not something you get to see very often."
Mari-senpai's voice came back to me with perfect clarity.
Back then, she had been smiling.
Everyone had still been alive.
Something tightened painfully deep inside my chest.
When I drew a breath, the cold air stabbed at my lungs.
They're all gone now.
My feet nearly stopped moving.
If I stopped here,
I knew I might never be able to move again.
This place would pin me down together with all those memories.
...No.
I have to keep going down.
I have to return to everyday life.
No matter how faded it may seem now,
I have to return to the place where people still live.
I forced my legs forward,
one deliberate step at a time.
Then, almost without thinking, I looked up at the sky.
The heavy black clouds had torn apart,
revealing an unimaginable sea of stars beyond them.
There were so many that they stole my breath away.
And there, hanging above them all,
was an almost perfectly round white moon.
The waxing moon cast a gentle glow along the edges of the clouds.
...So that was it.
The reason the sky above the clouds had looked faintly bright earlier
had been the moonlight all along.
Beneath the black clouds still hanging over the mountain lodge,
the flames continued to flicker.
Their crimson glow reflected against the clouds,
giving the night sky an eerie brightness.
I looked back.
The orange flicker beneath the dark clouds was growing smaller and smaller.
And with it,
the sound of the fire—
that distant, roaring whoosh—
was being stretched thinner and thinner by the wind,
fading farther away with every step.
It no longer had the strength to push against my back.
All that remained
was the sense that somewhere beyond the mountains,
something was still being destroyed.
Everyone... in there...
I shook the thought away and faced forward once more.
The cold white beam of my headlamp illuminated the trail with mechanical indifference.
Then,
from the darkness at my feet,
came the soft sound of running water.
Saaah...
The rainwater left behind by the storm crossed the mountain path,
threading its way through cracks in the rocks in countless narrow streams.
Somewhere farther down the slope,
another sound joined it—
a powerful rush of falling water.
Whooosh...
An unseen mountain stream must have been gathering everything the storm had left behind.
And closer at hand,
countless little trickles overlapped one another,
their quiet murmurs coming from every direction.
From here.
From there.
It felt as though the entire mountain were breathing out water.
The roar of the flames that had filled my ears only moments before
had already sunk beneath the sound of the flowing streams.
Stone and stone.
Shadow and shadow.
For now,
that was enough.
