潜伏

I was wrapped up in the futon in my own room, in my own home.
Outside my window, the night lay silent. The lights of the town shimmered softly in the darkness.
After I lost consciousness in the crevasse, I was found by another group of climbers. The staff at the Hakuba-jiri mountain lodge contacted the rescue team, and they brought me to safety.
The next time I opened my eyes, I was lying in a hospital bed.
My father and mother were there to visit me...
When I was discharged, they took the long train ride home with me.
The moment I saw my house, a wave of relief washed over me.
I was alive. I had escaped that mountain lodge with no way out and made it back home.
But that relief was soon swept away by the ache stirring deep inside my chest.
Mari-senpai... Yabe-san... Yohei... Takizawa-san... Suzuki Sho-san... They were all gone now.
The lodge owner... Uchida-san... Takamura-san... and everyone else whose lives had become entwined with that nightmare drifted through my mind.
Their smiling faces... their faces twisted in terror... the desperate way they fought to protect one another...
Every memory tightened its grip around my heart until even breathing became painful.
Beneath the covers, my hands were clenched so tightly that my fingers began to ache.
The moment I closed my eyes, the lodge's corridors, the shadows stretched across the floor, the feel of those tentacles, and the echoes of terrified screams all came rushing back.
The line between dreams and reality still wavered, pulling my thoughts into a whirlpool of confusion.
Even so, the softness of my futon, the quiet of my room, and the simple fact that I was finally safe brought me a small measure of comfort.
I took a slow, deep breath... and let it out.
Closing my eyes, I curled up beneath the covers and sank into their warmth.
Gradually, the images and voices that had been swirling through my mind drifted farther and farther away, until only the quiet rhythm of my breathing remained.
The pain in my chest never completely faded, but drowsiness slowly weighed down my eyelids.
Feeling the warmth of my own body beneath the covers... I had no choice but to surrender to sleep.
Mari-senpai was smiling.
So were Yabe-san, Yohei, Takizawa-san, and Tsuruoka-san.
Their smiles were gentle... warm... as though time itself had come to a standstill.
And yet, something about the scene felt wrong.
I couldn't see it.
But I could feel something slowly drawing closer.
One of those things...
It had no clear shape, no shadow I could make out. Yet somehow, I could sense it creeping toward me—from beneath my feet, from beyond the walls—slowly... relentlessly.
I wanted to run.
I wanted to get away.
But my body felt as heavy as lead, refusing to move.
I have to protect everyone...!
Only my desperate cry echoed inside my mind. My body remained frozen, while fear tightened its grip around my heart.
Then, from somewhere deep within the silence, I heard a faint whisper...
"You too... You too..."
The voice belonged to no one.
And yet... somehow, I knew it was calling my name.
My chest tightened. My breathing grew uneven. Sweat trickled down my forehead, my palms turned cold and damp, and my whole body began to tremble.
Then... I woke up.
Birds were singing.
Soft morning light streamed into the room.
I was in my own bedroom.
But the sense of relief vanished in an instant.
In my dream... everyone had been alive.
In reality... they were all gone.
My chest tightened.
I couldn't catch my breath.
I made my way toward the bathroom, my legs threatening to give out beneath me.
Something tickled deep inside my nose.
When I looked into the mirror, it almost seemed as if my nose hair was twitching... twitch... twitch...
...What?
A chill ran through me as I leaned closer for a better look.
Something black, flat, and slender slowly emerged from one of my nostrils, swaying back and forth as though searching for something.
My heart lurched inside my chest.
A flood of memories crashed through my mind.
The lodge corridor... Yohei collapsing... Mari-senpai's cries...
Countless tentacles engulfing Takizawa-san.
The pale yellow fluid streaming from Tsuruoka-san's eyes.
Mari-senpai's eyes... They looked as though she believed in me until the very end.
But the shadow spreading at my feet told a different story.
No one could be saved.
"No... It wasn't a dream...!"
I squeezed my eyes shut and braced myself against the sink, barely daring to breathe.
But my mind only spiraled deeper into chaos.
Dreams and reality bled into one another until I could no longer tell where one ended and the other began.
I looked at my reflection.
Out of the corner of my eye, something black writhed.
Everything around me felt as though it might crumble at the slightest touch.
Every breath stabbed at my chest.
Even with my eyes closed, the pounding of my heart echoed in my ears like relentless tinnitus.
My sense of self began to dissolve.
The world around me twisted out of shape.
"You too... You too..."
The whisper came again.
It belonged to no one... and to everyone.
Fear.
Loneliness.
Despair.
They surged through every part of my body.
The instant I looked into the mirror again, something moved—without question—at the edge of my vision.
I had no idea what it was.
The only thing I knew... was that I was no longer the person I used to be.
My breathing grew ragged.
My throat tightened.
My whole body trembled.
In the mirror before me, countless shadows stared back at me.
I wanted to scream... but no sound would come.
All that remained was the pounding of my heart... and the whisper echoing deep inside my ears.
Then I felt something deep in my throat.
As though something were forcing its way up from within me.
A violent wave of nausea struck, and I lurched over the sink.
An instant later, countless black, glistening tendrils spilled from my mouth, spreading across the basin until they covered it completely.
I jerked my head up and looked into the mirror.
The black mass spread across its surface in an instant.
Then... nothing remained.
"Somebody... please tell me..."
"What am I supposed to do...?"

Back in the mountain lodge, there were moments when I found a strange sense of peace by telling myself that the terror would end when I died.
Then I saw what I had become... and realized that the horror might continue even after death.
But then another thought came to me.
If I could still feel fear... didn't that mean I was still alive?
That's what I began to wonder.
Perhaps fear itself was born from the instinct to survive...

I sat alone in my room, trembling.
I never knew when someone might discover what I had become.
If anyone realized I was one of those things, violence would find me in an instant.
It dawned on me that human beings were far more terrifying than one of those things.
Whenever one of those things felt fear, its survival instinct drove it to mutate and divide.
In other words, fear gave birth to violence... and violence only spread the infection of one of those things even further.
By the time people finally realized that truth, the hunt for one of those things had already spread everywhere.
If someone looked at my blood test results and decided, "You're no longer human," I knew the violence would begin without hesitation.

I suddenly remembered a conversation I once had with my grandfather about organized crime.
"The people who need love the most..."
"...are often the ones pushed the farthest away."
"And before they even realize it..."
"...they end up becoming part of the very society that has forgotten how to care."
He told me there was something deeply ironic about that.
"It's not simply that bad people make the world worse."
"It's the way the world goes wrong..."
"...that keeps creating more people who lose their way."
Back then, his words had reminded me of an infection.
The harder people tried to drive away what frightened them...
...the farther that fear seemed to spread.
What was happening across the city now was no different.
Fear gave birth to violence.
Violence created more of one of those things.
And one of those things only stirred even greater fear in people.
Watching that vicious cycle unfold, I finally understood.
This wasn't a war.
Fear itself...
...was the only thing multiplying.

Human beings were far more terrifying than one of those things.
One of those things simply began to mutate and divide out of a basic instinct to survive whenever it felt fear.
Human beings, on the other hand, chose to eliminate one another when fear took hold.
Fear gave birth to violence, and violence spread the infection of one of those things.
The bitter irony was that my very existence invited fear and violence into human society.
As fear of one of those things spread from person to person,
it looked strikingly similar to the way one of those things reacted to fear—mutating, dividing,
and multiplying in the blink of an eye.
There was only one difference.
One of those things multiplied by instinct.
Human beings did the same thing,
only draped in words like "justice" and "security."
The city had become suffocating under constant surveillance.
Countless security cameras watched from every street corner, while drones patrolled the skies day and night.
Organizations with names like the Crisis Management Center and the Counter–One of Those Things Task Force
were established one after another.
Oddly enough, the very first debate wasn't about countermeasures or relief efforts.
It was about what to call them.
On the evening news, a panel of so-called experts discussed the issue.
"Nanika."
"Them."
"Inhumans."
"That Thing."
The suggestions were tossed around almost casually.
But "Inhumans" was rejected for being discriminatory.
"Them" was considered too emotional.
"That Thing" was deemed too vague for official use.
In the end, "Nanika" was the name that remained.
A word that meant nothing.
Vague enough to explain everything,
belonging to no one,
yet convenient enough to place on anyone.
And so,
the official designation for this life-form
became the all-purpose term, "Nanika,"
perfectly suited for government reports and committee documents alike.
The moment it was given a name,
it ceased to be merely a phenomenon.
It became something to be managed.
As I watched the broadcast, I couldn't help but think...
Perhaps giving it a name made people feel a little safer.
But at the same time,
perhaps it also turned it into something they no longer felt the need to understand.
Billboards displayed messages like "Reporting suspicious behavior is mandatory" and "Don't forget your monthly blood test."
Outwardly, people went about their lives as though nothing had changed.
But their eyes were always searching, never letting even the slightest irregularity escape their notice.
Rumors spread through the city.
"If you refuse to kneel before the flag of the Nanika Countermeasures Headquarters, they'll assume you're Nanika."
Refuse... and you'd be imprisoned.
Demand they prove what you were... and they'd torture you until you did.
People told those stories with a tone that hovered somewhere between a joke and a warning.
Inside the blood-testing centers, the cold, mechanical hum of the machines echoed through the halls.
People waited their turn, letting out quiet sighs before silently offering an arm.
No one spoke.
No one met anyone else's eyes.
Yet the silence itself was saturated with fear.
"Report anyone showing abnormal behavior immediately."
The words on the sign echoed through my mind again and again.
Every step I took was a gamble with my life.
Just glancing at a passerby was enough to make my heart pound.
Is he...?
A brief hesitation.
A single breath drawn just a little too quickly.
That alone was enough to make me shrink back and hold my breath.
One mistake, and the surveillance data would be transmitted to the central database in an instant.
Then the hunt for Nanika would begin.
All across the city, human fear was hunting down others like me.

Watching it spread, I couldn't shake the feeling that I'd seen it all before.

Back at the mountain lodge that day, as everyone gathered in the dining hall,
the suspicion that I might be Nanika
had spread from one person to the next like a cough.
Someone became the target of outrage on the Internet,
and anger and righteousness spread in waves.
Voices calling for immigrants to be driven out
multiplied alongside people's growing anxiety.
The way society suddenly seemed to lurch to the right.
The way criticism of Japan would suddenly erupt in a neighboring country,
only to be answered
by an equally rapid surge of hostility toward that country within Japan.
The pattern was always the same.
Fear gave birth to more fear.
Suspicion bred more suspicion.
And the image of an "enemy"
grew on its own inside people's minds.
Just as Nanika sensed fear,
mutated,
divided,
and spread,
fear within human society also changed its shape,
passing from one person to another like an infection.
Watching the hunt for Nanika spread across the country,
I realized
it was frighteningly similar
to every one of those things.

The numbers were scrolling across the giant screens again today.
"Nanika apprehended this month: +18% compared to last month."
Beside them, applause erupted from the speakers as a smiling man appeared on the screen.
Beneath his image was the title:
"Community Safety Contributor."
He stood tall, gripping the microphone with unmistakable pride.
A few passersby stopped to look up at the screen.
"Impressive."
"Our district can't afford to fall behind."
The voices were quiet,
yet unmistakably real.
Before anyone realized it,
the hunt for Nanika had become a competition.
It reminded me of the witch hunts of centuries past.
Public shaming, denunciations, righteous outrage...
they all wore the face of justice.
But beneath it all lay the same desire:
to reassure yourself that you were still on the safe side.
If someone else became the outsider,
then you could still believe you were human.
If you could condemn someone else,
you didn't have to confront your own weakness... or your own misery.
At the heart of paranoia
was the fear of having one's self-worth wounded.
So people searched for someone they could condemn,
and wrapped their hands around stones,
calling it justice.
Who found the most?
Who reported them first?
Who protected the city better than anyone else?
But the expressions on their faces held more than the smile of justice.
Behind their eyes
was desperation.
Fear.
The desperate thought that...
"I might be next."
Blending into the crowd, I found myself thinking:
This wasn't justice running wild.
Nor was it the corruption of good intentions.
It was nothing more than the instinct to survive.
Who was human...
and who was Nanika?
I no longer knew.
Who was right,
and who was wrong?
I no longer knew that, either.
The moment that thought crossed my mind,
that feeling came rushing back.
Back at the mountain lodge,
the more people gathered together,
the more they began to suspect
that Nanika was hiding among them.
And then...
the scene inside the lodge's dining hall returned to me with startling clarity.
Standing in a circle
were the people who had become Nanika.
A faint light glowed from each of their foreheads.
From it extended slick, glistening tendrils.
The tendrils connected to one another,
quivering like a single giant nerve.
Then...
someone's thoughts flowed into the circle.
—Four people in the staff room.
—Around ten on the second floor.
—Several more in the old wing.
They weren't voices.
There was no emotion behind them.
Only information,
passed from one to another
with quiet, mechanical efficiency.
Where people had fled.
How many were hiding.
Where the surviving humans still were.
It was a report.
And at the same time,
a process of selection.
Back then,
I feared something even more than being discovered by the monsters.
It was watching those who had once been human
reduce other human beings
to nothing more than data.
A chill ran down my spine.
There was no point in hiding.
There was no point in running.
Someone was always watching.
And someone would always report it.
—They're here.
—They're still alive.
What had begun inside that mountain lodge
wasn't simply an infection.
It was the beginning
of surveillance...
and reporting.
Every glance that met another,
every sound of breathing,
every movement of a fingertip,
began to look like evidence.
It was no different in the real world.
In the midst of competition, people gradually lost the ability to trust one another,
until suspicion became the only way they knew to protect themselves.
Paranoia wasn't born because something was out there.
It existed because...
fear demanded it.
When human beings gave in to fear,
they fled,
they hid,
and in the end,
they chose to eliminate.
If you found the enemy before anyone else,
if you reported them before anyone else,
you could convince yourself you were safe.
And so the competition began.
Fear fed more fear,
and righteousness raced ahead of what was truly right.
Just as Nanika divided in response to fear,
human beings also divided themselves into roles born of fear.
Those who watched.
Those who reported.
Those who hunted.
At the center of it all
was a wish so simple it was almost heartbreaking:
to survive.
Holding my breath,
I kept walking.
So that I would never become part of that competition.
And so that I wouldn't become its next target.

Yesterday, I witnessed that fear with my own eyes.
In a narrow alley, a blood-soaked Nanika was pinned to the ground by people in protective suits and bound in steel restraints. The screams echoed through the streets, yet no one came to help.
They weren't using capture nets alone.
Protective suits that covered every inch of their bodies.
Electrified batons glowing at the tips.
Equipment designed to stop Nanika without ever touching them.
The moment I saw it, my mind returned to the mountain lodge.
Back then, we'd talked about protecting ourselves with rain gear,
and Sho Suzuki-san and I had built an improvised electric spear powered by a generator.
At the time, it had simply been a desperate idea born of necessity.
But the methods I was seeing now...
felt strangely familiar.
Perhaps Sho-san and I hadn't been so wrong after all.
I held my breath and pressed myself against the wall.
If I moved now, I'd be next.
The eyes of people consumed by fear were already searching for another target.
In addition to mandatory blood tests, people were now required to receive the Nanika Vaccine.
The government called it "the injection of hope," promoting it relentlessly on television and giant screens throughout the city.
"Take it, and you'll be protected from infection."
"Take it, and you'll finally sleep in peace."
Believing those promises, people stood in line and silently held out their arms.
Reality, however, told a different story.
Even after receiving the vaccine, people continued to mutate.
Some even disappeared only days after the injection.
And still, people believed.
Because they couldn't go on living if they didn't.
I couldn't take it.
If someone like me—a Nanika—received that vaccine, my body might simply collapse.
So I remained silent,
trembling alone in the corner of my room.
Whispers spread quietly through the city.
"The vaccine isn't meant to stop the infection."
"It's meant to quietly dispose of those who might be Nanika."
Anyone who dared say such things was gone the next day.
All that remained were rumors...
and lists of unexplained deaths.
"It's just another conspiracy theory," people laughed.
They laughed because believing they were safe was easier than facing the truth.
Yet the evening news showed another person collapsing moments after receiving the injection.
No one questioned it.
Fear had already taken hold,
and silence had become everyone's choice.
Looking out the window, I found myself wondering...
Had those who feared Nanika already become Nanika themselves?
They obeyed fear.
They never questioned orders.
They watched their neighbors.
They disposed of one another.
Perhaps that had been the first invisible disease Nanika had ever spread among humanity.
Becoming Nanika had given me power.
The ability to transform without limit.
A body that would never die.
But it had taken away my freedom.
If I failed to hide what I was,
my ordinary life would be drowned in blood.
If I made even a single mistake,
human fear would bare its fangs and hunt me down.
If, instead, I surrendered to fear and allowed my mutations to spread,
I would unleash Nanika upon society myself.
Even so...
I had to survive.
Holding my breath.
Burying my heart.
Trembling alone in the corner of my room.
Nanika was never the true horror.
Human fear...
was the key that kept me imprisoned.
A society built on surveillance.
Mandatory testing.
People driven by fear.
All of it bound me,
stealing away my freedom.
And yet...
I was still alive.
That alone
was my only hope.
