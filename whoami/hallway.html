Hallway

A pillar stands in the middle of the corridor, lined with others stretching toward the back.
Ahead to the right is the space beneath the stairs.
To the left, a wall covered with climbing route maps, with a fire extinguisher farther down.
Right in front of me, people are gathered together.
My gaze drops to the lower right— the young man in the black Adidas tracksuit is down on the floor, bracing himself on his hips and elbows, his head lifted awkwardly.
On his cheek, the severed tendril is still writhing.
Mari-senpai stands in front of me, still gripping my arm.
A faint, sweet scent drifts in the air—her perfume.
Yabe-san and Yohei are there, but neither Tsuruoka-san nor Takizawa-san can be seen.
My legs tremble violently, strength draining from my hips.
Just above my knees, along the inner sides, the muscles twitch uncontrollably.

“What happened…? What the hell happened…?”
A voice comes from farther down the corridor.
…It’s that guy—the athletic-type who was taking photos of that thing without permission…

“Sho, you okay?” Yohei says.
“I’m fine.” I answer shortly, then turn to Mari-senpai. “Thank you… You saved my life…”
I can feel my voice trembling.
“What are you saying something like that for?” Mari-senpai frowns slightly and lets go of my arm.
…It’s a good thing Mari-senpai was the one who pulled me.
Way better than being pulled by Kobayashi-san.
Some part of my mind, pretending to stay calm, is thinking something like that.

“Hey—close it! Hurry! …Lock it! …Now!” The lodge owner’s voice rings out.
With a rattling sound, Kobayashi Ren-san pulls the lodge door shut.
Clank.
He grabs the key—with its narrow brass shaft—and twists it over and over, forcing it in.
A low, grinding creak—metal biting into the wooden frame.
…There are still locks like this?
The thought crosses my mind, completely out of place.
When I was a kid, the front door of our old house in Yokohama had the same kind of lock.
I remember spinning it for fun, and my mother scolding me— “You’ll break it if you keep turning it like that.”

“Uh, excuse me… that scream just now—whose was it? It was a woman, right? …No? Wasn’t it?”
A voice drifts in from farther down the corridor.
…It’s one of them—the glasses-and-jeans guy who’d been taking photos of that thing without permission.

Everyone there—Mari-senpai, Yabe-san, Yohei, the young man in the black tracksuit with the writhing fragment of a tendril still stuck to his cheek, the receptionist named Sho who had complained to me, the mountain photographer Kobayashi Ren-san, and the slender, beautiful woman who resembled Sakai Wakako, now without her partner…
Every single one of them wears a rigid expression. Even the smallest movements are stiff, unnatural.
Mari-senpai—and the woman who has lost her companion—are trembling.
Of course, I am too. A tight, crushing weight grips my stomach. Inside my head, it’s like darkness has settled— I still can’t make sense of anything.
I don’t want to exist in this place— and yet, here I am.
My chest feels tight. It’s hard to breathe.
At the very back, the two who had been taking photos of that thing without permission—the athletic-type and the glasses-and-jeans guy—are there, making noise.
Only those two seem strangely unaffected, as if none of this has anything to do with them.
“Tanaka-sensei… Tanaka-sensei…” The receptionist named Sho, the one who had complained to me, leans against the wall, pressing his arms and forehead into it as he hangs his head.

"What—what the hell happened?!" the athletic guy’s voice bursts out from the back.

"Where are Tsurouka-san and Takizawa-san?" I ask, breathless.
"They ran," Yabe-san says.
"Men… can be surprisingly weak," Mari-senpai murmurs, almost under her breath.
"The ones who act the toughest are usually the first to break," Yabe-san adds quietly.
Hearing that, I remember what Tsurouka-san said back on the snowfield— The world is all about competition.
And somehow, it makes me think— maybe people compete because they’re afraid.
Afraid of having their pride hurt… afraid because, deep down, they’re weak… because they’re cowardly… and can’t overcome fear.
Come to think of it—
the people who used to bully me…
when I ran into them years after graduation,
they were strangely polite… almost submissive.
It was probably because
they didn’t know how I’d changed as an adult…
and somewhere deep down,
they were afraid—
that I might get back at them.
Back then,
they only made themselves feel superior
by putting me down.
It wasn’t that they were truly strong.
When I think about it like that,
I feel like I can almost understand
why the windows at my old school kept getting smashed.
They all just wanted to feel safe—
pretending to be strong,
acting like they were winning
in some kind of competition.
Human beings…
maybe we don’t act arrogant because we’re strong—
maybe we act strong
because we’re weak.
Trying to win against others…
joining in when someone’s being attacked…
spreading whatever’s blowing up online…
pulling out a knife to protect yourself,
and then going too far because there’s no turning back…
going along with the crowd…
building up arms to stand against another country…
At the root of all of it—
it might be the same thing.
Not strength.
Not righteousness.
Just—
fear.
Fear of being alone.
Fear of being wrong.
Fear of being left behind.
And so,
we try to stand on the winning side.
We line up with the loudest voices.
We look away from the ones being attacked.
It’s not so much malice—
it’s closer to cowardice.
Maybe we’re just looking for a place
where we won’t get hurt,
hiding behind others.

I can’t stop thinking about what’s happening in the washroom.
I turn, forcing my legs to move— unsteady, like when you stand up too fast after they’ve gone numb— staggering as I make my way back toward the door and look through the small window.
On the glass, the figures of the people gathered in the corridor behind me are faintly reflected, overlapping with the rain streaming down outside, blurring the view beyond.
Even so, when I strain my eyes toward the washroom,
I can just make out Tanaka-san’s head lying on the slatted floor…
and beyond her, a black mass—lying still, unmoving.
Is that… it?
Something cold drops into my chest.
Then— a flicker of unease.
Tanaka-san…
her face—just moments ago, her teeth, her gums, even her jawbone had been exposed.
And yet… the face I see now, in profile—
it looks… intact.
as if nothing had ever happened.
Am I seeing things…?
Or—
A chill runs down my spine,
and before I can stop myself,
I look away from the window.

"Will someone just tell me what the hell’s going on?! My heart’s pounding over here, seriously!" the athletic guy’s voice cuts in from the back.

"Ah… it hurts… ah… this is… this is awful… ah…" a low groan drifts from the left.
When I turn, I see the guy in the black tracksuit propped on his hips and elbows, twisting in pain.
A severed piece of the tentacle is still stuck to his cheek— alive, writhing.
With a wet, slick sound, it slides across his skin.
As if praying it isn’t real, he keeps touching it— again and again— checking, confirming.
—If Mari-senpai hadn’t grabbed my arm… that could have been me.

From the right, I hear a woman crying.
When I turn, I see one of the two women Tsurouka-san spoke to at Hakubajiri— the tall, slender one who looks a bit like Sakai Wakako— collapsed on the floor, her shoulders trembling as she weeps.
Then I notice them— two packs lined up along the edge of the corridor in front of her.
Light green… and wine red.
A small beige teddy bear keychain hangs from one of them, swaying gently… even though no one is touching it.
The slender woman is on her knees in front of the packs, clutching the light green one, crying.
Between sobs, over and over— "Akane… Akane…" she calls her name.
My chest tightens as I watch.
—The two of them, back at Hakubajiri, suddenly come back to me.
The shoulder straps of their packs pressed against their chests.
"Staying in a tent?" Tsurouka-san had asked, and they both nodded with a smile.
The woman now crying—owner of the wine red pack— had smiled calmly,
while the smaller one… now gone… had answered brightly, "Yeah, that’s right."
A little teddy bear dangled from her pack, swaying with each step.
They said they’d come from Gifu,
planning to cross from Karasawa to Happo Ridge.
When the Kaerazu Kiretto came up,
the smaller one laughed a little— "I’m a bit nervous about it…" and the slender one had said, "But I’m really looking forward to seeing the stars."
When Yabe-san mentioned blueberries,
the smaller one smiled— "I love blueberries," she’d said.
They laughed together when Yabe-san teased Tsurouka-san, and listened with interest as he talked about the mountains.
At the end, the smaller one—now gone— had said,
"See you up on the ridge,"
and the two of them had walked off,
their footsteps light, crunching against the trail—
I feel like I can still hear that sound.
My eyes return to the small beige teddy bear swaying on the light green pack in front of me.
The thought that its owner is no longer here crushes my chest,
and I look away, clenching my fist.
I feel unbearably small— standing here, able to do nothing.

"Hey, wait a second! Don’t just leave us out of this!" the athletic guy shouts from the back.

"Excuse me—have you seen Kishida-san? A middle-aged woman… strong-willed…"
The voice cuts through the athletic guy’s shouting.
At once, the room falls silent.
I turn to the right.
A middle-aged man is standing there.
The moment I see his face, something clicks.
—That pair.
The middle-aged couple with something about them.
The woman… the strong-willed one— she’s the one who was attacked in the washroom.
—So her name is Kishida-san…
There’s no way I can tell him— she died in there.
I glance at Mari-senpai, Yabe-san, and Yohei.
They all seem to have realized the same thing— that the woman he’s asking about is the one from the washroom.
Our eyes meet.
No one can bring themselves to say it.
Mari-senpai, Yabe-san, Yohei… and me— we exchange glances, words failing us.
Everyone is struggling— what should we say?
How do we even tell him?
"Um… in the washroom… there was… an incident…"
Mari-senpai’s voice is small, trembling.
"Y-you’re Yagi-san, right…? Um… well…"
the man from reception—the other Shō—stammers.
—Yagi-san…
So that’s his name…
Different surname… so they weren’t married after all…
A meaningless thought drifts through my mind.
The receptionist lowers his gaze for a moment, unable to finish his sentence,
then leans in and whispers to the lodge owner.
The owner opens his mouth—closes it—opens it again.
No words come.
Finally, in a small, trembling voice—
"I’m… terribly sorry… but… Kishida-san has… passed away…"
"W-what… did you say…?"
The man’s eyes widen.
His whole body goes rigid.
"What do you mean ‘passed away’? Where is she?"
"In the washroom…"
the receptionist says, hesitating.
"There’s… something dangerous creature in there…"
Mari-senpai starts.
"What do you mean ‘something dangerous’?!"
he shouts—
and without hesitation,
he turns toward the exit leading to the washroom.
"…No… that’s not possible… That can’t be right!"
His voice sharpens with anger as he reaches for the lock.
"Wait—it’s dangerous! You can’t go out there!"
People rush to stop him.
Mari-senpai grabs his arm—
Yabe-san and Yohei move in as well.
"Let go! She might still be alive!"
The muscles in his arms strain as he tries to shake them off.
"Please—calm down!"
Mari-senpai raises her voice.
But he turns—
glaring at all of us—
and shouts,
"Why didn’t anyone help her?!"
Silence falls.

“A dangerous creature? You saw it, right? What the hell was it—some kind of animal? …or a person?” came a voice from the back—the guy in glasses and jeans,

“No… no… this can’t be real…!” the middle-aged man’s voice trembled as tears ran down his cheeks.

“Injured!? Wait—are they dead!?” the athletic guy shouted from the back, his voice rising in panic.

At that moment, the slender, beautiful woman who had lost the small, cheerful girl rose from in front of the light green backpack, turned, and stepped forward.
Her tear-soaked eyes met the middle-aged man’s, and in a hoarse voice, she said:
“…We couldn’t save her… Akane—she came here with me—she was taken in there too. We couldn’t… no matter what we did, we couldn’t save her…”
The man’s shoulders twitched.
Her voice—thin, trembling—seemed to drain the heat from his anger in an instant.
His eyes fixed on her. His mouth opened slightly, as if to speak—
but no words came out.
The hands that had been restraining him—Yabe-san’s and Yohei’s—slipped away as the strength left his body.
“…Yoko…!”
He forced the name out, and dropped to his knees.
Covering his face with both hands, he broke into uncontrollable sobs.
The sound of his crying echoed through the tense air.
—So her name was Kishida Yoko…
Kishida Yoko… and Yagi-san…
What was their relationship?
The thought flickered through my mind.
“…Yoko…”
Yagi-san whispered the name again, his voice shaking.
Mari-senpai gently placed a hand on his shoulder, and Yabe-san spoke softly, urging him to calm down.
But his grief didn’t subside—he remained collapsed there, unable to move.
No one around him could find the words to say.
They just stood there, frozen.
Only the faint sound of wind from beyond the rain-streaked window quietly marked the passage of time.
And yet—
I couldn’t bring myself to care for the woman sobbing beside the backpack, or for the man broken on the floor.
All I could think about was what had become of that thing.
I felt ashamed of myself for it.

“Was there blood? In the washroom…? How bad was it—how much blood was there?” the guy in glasses and jeans called out from the back, his voice loud, prying.

The middle-aged man remained collapsed in tears, crushed beneath a sense of helplessness, calling Yoko’s name over and over again.
I watched the scene, a deep ache spreading through my chest.
In the quiet rhythm of the falling rain, no one could speak.
One by one, tears welled up in the eyes of those who had witnessed it.
I hastily wiped at the tears rising in my own eyes with the back of my hand, but my throat tightened, and no words would come.
It felt like I was trapped in some kind of nightmare.
If it were a dream, something this dark—this terrible—should have forced me awake by now…
Instead, rather than waking up, I felt myself drifting, as if my consciousness were slowly slipping away.

What pulled me back to reality was a groan rising from the floor.
“It hurts… someone… do something… it hurts…” the young man in the black tracksuit writhed, letting out a low, broken moan.
Kobayashi Ren-san tried desperately to bring order to the chaos around us, but even his voice was shaking.
Yohei closed his eyes, as if trying to come to terms with what was happening.
“H-holy…” the guy in glasses and jeans let out a small, strained sound as he noticed the severed piece of tentacle still wriggling on the young man’s cheek.
The lodge owner frowned deeply, staring at the twitching fragment clinging to the black tracksuit’s face.
Yohei grabbed my arm and whispered into my ear, “Hey… this is seriously bad, isn’t it?”
Mari-senpai bit her lip, frozen in place, unable to move.

“S-so what the hell happened here!? What is this!?” the athletic guy kept pressing, grabbing Kobayashi Ren-san’s sleeve like a gawking bystander, refusing to let up.

“…This is your fault…” the young man in the black tracksuit muttered hoarsely, his lips trembling as he looked at me, as if he could no longer hold it in.
“Huh?” I asked reflexively.
“You guys… brought that thing here… because of you… I’m…!” His face twisted, soaked in tears and sweat, as he glared at us.
“Damn it… damn it…” he slammed his slime-slick hand against the floor.
—Wasn’t it because he tried to grab it… that it latched onto him?
The thought flickered through my mind for a moment.
The way he pounded the floor, cursing, made it seem like he understood that himself—like he regretted what he’d done.
But when I thought about it… maybe he was right.
If I hadn’t found that thing in the first place…
A crushing pain tightened deep in my stomach, and a cold chill spread through my chest.
I felt faint, as if I might black out at any moment.
As if this were all just a dream…
For a fleeting second, I wondered—
is this what it feels like for someone who’s taken a life in an accident?

“That thing, right? That dangerous creature—you’re the ones who brought it in, aren’t you? …Yeah, we saw it too! You guys brought that creepy black thing in here!” the athletic guy shouted, stepping forward aggressively.
The man in glasses and jeans jumped in right after him.
“Take responsibility! We could die because of this!”
Even if they said that, there was nothing I could do.
—They’re only this worked up because Tsuruoka-san kept going after them…
The one who should be getting blamed—Tsuruoka-san—had already run off, and now it was all falling on me.
…But in the end, it was my fault for finding that thing.
…Why did I have to find something like that?
The weight of guilt threatened to crush me.
Murmurs spread all at once, and the air in the hallway grew heavy.
“I-I don’t even know what to say…” I tried to respond, to offer some kind of excuse, but Yohei clicked his tongue softly and grabbed my shoulder, silencing me.
“Is now really the time to argue about who’s to blame?” Yabe-san said.

“Sho, where’s Morishita-san?” the lodge owner asked the receptionist, Sho-san.
Even though I knew he wasn’t talking about me, hearing my own name made my heart jump.
“She’s in the staff room,” Sho-san at reception replied.
“I see… good.” The owner nodded, then continued, “Sho, make an announcement throughout the lodge. Tell them to lock the nearest exits and secure all entrances. And while you’re at it, ask if there’s a doctor among the guests. Once you’re done, check with Morishita-san and make sure every exit is properly locked.”
“Understood.” Sho-san answered, then hurried off toward the front desk.
As the focus shifted away from the accusations against us, I felt a small sense of relief.
But there was no telling when they might turn on us again.
Just thinking that made the unease in my chest churn, refusing to settle.

At that moment, slow footsteps sounded from the stairway entrance.
“Dad, what happened?”
“Hold on. The lady will go ask.”
Voices drifted from the stairwell, and then she appeared—Uchida-san.
“There,” Yabe-san said.
For a split second, I didn’t understand what she meant—
but then I saw her: Ayako Uchida-san, the doctor who had been talking with Yabe-san earlier, her white long-sleeved shirt rolled up at the arms.
Yabe-san’s “there” must have been her answer to the owner’s request—whether there was a doctor among the guests.

“…Did something happen?” Uchida-san asked in a calm, steady voice.
“She’s a doctor,” Yabe-san said to the lodge owner.
“That’s a relief… There’s some kind of dangerous creature outside, and its tentacle latched onto him—we can’t get it off. He’s been like this the whole time, in pain… Is there anything you can do?” the owner said, the mole beside his nose shifting as he spoke.
The moment Uchida-san saw the writhing fragment of tentacle clinging to the young man’s face, her brow tightened slightly.
Her expression quickly settled again, but that brief flicker was enough to show how serious the situation was.
“Is there somewhere we can lay him down—like a table or something?”
Her voice remained composed, but there was an urgency beneath it.
“A table… maybe one in the dining area…?” the owner said.
“That will do.” No hesitation.
Even as she spoke, Uchida-san’s eyes were already scanning Naoki-kun’s body.
Beside them, the slender woman who resembled Wakako Sakai slowly lifted her face, traces of tears still clinging to her cheeks.
“…I’ll help. I used to be a nurse.” Her voice sounded as though she were trying to convince herself.
“Akane… she used to be a nursery teacher. And now… this…”
No one had asked, but she said it anyway.
As if trying to prove that the small, cheerful girl who was no longer here had lived—had mattered.
The lodge owner nodded, then called out to Kobayashi Ren-san.
“Ren-san, can you bring a stretcher?”
“Got it!” Ren-san ran toward the front area, where the reception and shop were, opened a tall white cabinet at the corner between the shop and the dining area, pulled out a stretcher, and hurried back.
“Alright. Ren-san and I will carry Naoki-kun,” the lodge owner said.
—This is my fault. I have to do something too… I thought.
My legs were still trembling, but somehow I felt like I could stand now.
“I’ll help too!” I called out.
“Me too,” Yohei said, stepping forward without hesitation.
“Appreciate it… Bon Curry,” the lodge owner said with a faint smile. The mole beside his nose shifted slightly as his cheek lifted.
For a brief moment, the tension in the air softened.
But as we tried to place Naoki-kun onto the stretcher, a chill ran through me.
That thing—still clinging to his cheek—was moving, as if it were alive.
Naoki-kun’s hands were slick with mucus. Not wanting to be touched by them, I instinctively moved to take his legs—but the owner and Kobayashi-san had already taken that position.
—So they didn’t want to be near it either… I thought.
The severed tentacle looked slightly rounded at the tip now—shorter than before.
—If only it would just die like this…
The stretcher lay on the corridor floor.
A sheet of white canvas stretched tightly between two poles.
We began carefully placing Naoki-kun’s body onto it.
Trying not to touch the writhing fragment on his cheek or his slippery hands, I reluctantly gripped the base of his right arm. Yohei supported the left.
At his legs, the lodge owner and Kobayashi-san each took one side.
Tension and resolve showed clearly on every face.
Kobayashi-san removed Naoki-kun’s sandals one by one and set them aside before taking hold of his legs.
“All right, here we go! One, two—!” the owner called out.
I held my breath and lifted, pulling Naoki-kun’s arm toward me, feeling his weight press against my waist.
Right beside my left arm, the severed piece on his cheek still writhed, twisting.
Like a creature struggling with the last of its strength.
And at the end of my right arm, Naoki-kun’s hand—slick with mucus—slid faintly, sending a sickening sensation up through my grip as we guided him onto the stretcher.
I swallowed hard, but forced myself not to let go, carefully settling his body in place.
“…You okay? Careful…” Yohei muttered, his voice shaking.
Each time the tentacle twitched, unease stirred deep in my chest.
If we let our guard down even for a moment, it could have latched onto us too.
But right now, all we could do was lay him down safely—
and move on to the next step.

“So, how do we do this? One person per end?” the lodge owner said.
“Yeah, let’s do that,” Kobayashi-san replied.
—Could I take the foot end…?
That thought crossed my mind, but the owner and Kobayashi-san had already taken the ends of the poles near Naoki-kun’s legs.
—Figures… they don’t want to be near the tentacle either… I thought.
Yohei took the end of the stretcher on the stairway side of the corridor, and I grabbed the end along the wall lined with climbing route maps.
Dr. Uchida, the slender beautiful woman, Mari-senpai, Yabe-san, Yagi-san—who had just lost Kishida-san—and the two onlookers, the athletic-looking guy and the glasses-and-jeans guy, all stood watching us.
“All right, lifting now… careful… ready—go!” the owner called out.

“Okay… let’s move. Slowly,” the owner said.
The four of us supported Naoki-kun’s body on the stretcher and began walking down the corridor.
I glanced at the writhing piece of tentacle near my left arm, shuddering as I tightened my grip.
The weight in my hands dragged at my waist, and my legs still trembled, barely steady enough to hold me.
Dr. Uchida, the slender woman, Mari-senpai, Yabe-san, and even the two onlookers followed alongside us, clustering around the stretcher like people crowding around a festival mikoshi.
Not seeing the middle-aged man, I wondered where he’d gone. I glanced back while still carrying the stretcher—he was still standing frozen by the exit.
Feeling the strain in my arms and hips, I staggered forward in an awkward posture.
To look ahead, I had to turn my head to the left.
As we moved, someone muttered under their breath, “It’s… still moving…”
For a moment, everyone’s steps grew heavier—but Dr. Uchida’s clear voice cut through the tension.
“It’s okay. There’s no bleeding or shock at the moment. We need to move quickly. I’ll go back to my room and get my first aid kit.”
With that, she turned and hurried up the stairs.
The slender woman who resembled Wakako Sakai looked startled at those words and hurried back toward the end of the corridor where her backpack was.
She was probably going to get her own first aid supplies.
As we passed the stairwell on the right, I noticed the father and child who had been cooking with us in the kitchen, watching us anxiously.
I turned and looked at Naoki-kun’s face on the stretcher.
The tentacle on his cheek was still writhing.
A chill ran through me—and at the same time, I almost felt like laughing.
—If I’d followed Tsuruoka-san’s voice and jumped in back then…
I had tried to grab it—and this guy in the black tracksuit had lunged first.
If he hadn’t… it might have been me.
…If Mari-senpai hadn’t pulled my arm, if I’d just kept standing there… it could’ve been me too…
For some reason, that thought brought back that strange feeling—
like when you see a deep wound and almost feel like laughing.

The four of us carrying the stretcher moved in unison, slowly advancing down the corridor.
Naoki-kun’s body still twitched slightly under the influence of the tentacle, and the faint tremor traveled through the poles into our hands, keeping all of us on edge.

“Got it—careful!” Kobayashi-san answered.
At one point, when the boy in the black tracksuit groaned and his body shifted slightly, I instinctively tensed too much and nearly lost my balance.
But Yohei beside me steadied me, and together we quickly regained control.
On Naoki’s cheek, the severed tentacle twitched faintly, and a sticky sensation seemed to creep toward my hand.
Even so, the four of us moved forward, step by step, without faltering.

“All right—almost there!” the owner shouted, and some of the tension finally eased among us.
When the dining hall doors came into sight, Yohei and I gathered our strength and took the final steps forward.
Step by step, we advanced, turning the corner.
The creak of the stretcher—and the pounding of my own heartbeat—were the only sounds echoing through the corridor.
Yohei kept his gaze lowered, watching the tentacle and the mucus, carefully placing each step.
