Lunch

As we carefully made our way along the rocky trail, the roof of the emergency hut gradually came into view, faintly visible through the white fog.
We had originally planned to arrive at the Hakuba Grand Lodge, where we were supposed to stay that night, around eleven o’clock, but it was already one in the afternoon now.
The hunger in my stomach was beginning to creep into my thoughts.
“We made it…” Tsuruoka-san muttered, adjusting the straps of his backpack.
I could see that the hand pressing firmly against the aluminum-wrapped bundle was trembling slightly with tension.
“We’re here!” I called out to let the others behind us know that we had reached the emergency hut.
Out of the fog, Yabe-san, Mari-senpai, Yohei, and Takizawa-san appeared one after another.
Without a word, Yabe-san took off her gloves and rubbed her hands together.
“Thank goodness…” Mari-senpai said.
“Whew… finally, we can catch our breath,” Yohei said.
“Oh, we made it,” Takizawa-san added.
Everyone let out a sigh of relief.

A small wooden emergency hut stood nestled against the rocky mountainside.
For a building so small, the single door at the front somehow looked surprisingly large.
Set into the wooden-framed door were wire-reinforced glass panels with diagonal lattice patterns above and below.
Across the top frame of the doorway hung a horizontal sign, black letters on a white background reading: Emergency Shelter.
The exterior walls were made of dark brown horizontal planks, weathered into a calm, muted tone by years of wind and snow.
The roof was a triangular gabled design, with the ends of the beams jutting outward, giving it a sturdy, dependable look.
All around it lay rough, scattered boulders, with steep mountains rising sharply behind.
When I looked up, I could see blue sky faintly showing through a thinning patch of white fog above.

“Well then, it’s already past one, so how about we take a longer break here and have lunch?” Takizawa-san said, making his usual calm decision.
At the sound of that, I couldn’t help but feel relieved.
Mari-senpai smiled warmly and said, “Hehe, that’s good. Saku-chan, you can finally get a little rest.”
It was a little embarrassing, like she had seen right through how exhausted I was.
Yohei immediately shouted in his usual upbeat way, “All right! Lunch break time!”
“Okay, it’s just for a bit, so why don’t we rest inside?” Takizawa-san said lightly, gesturing toward the entrance of the hut.
“Yes, let’s do that,” Mari-senpai agreed.
“Sansei! Denshi Seiki style!” Yohei blurted out with a grin, turning his company’s name into a goofy way of saying “I agree.”
“You said it and then laughed at your own joke,” Yabe-san said dryly.
“Oy, why’re you shouting out your own company’s name?” Tsuruoka-san said as he pulled a lighter from his pocket and lit a cigarette. “…Yeah, well, let’s head inside then.”
—He’s smoking again...
Maybe Tsuruoka-san didn’t quite get Yohei’s joke, I thought.
Takizawa-san looked at all of us and chuckled.
“Phew…” Tsuruoka-san exhaled, and white smoke drifted into the air, bringing with it that unpleasant smell.
Mari-senpai and Yabe-san both made a face and stepped away from him.

Takizawa-san pulled out the metal latch bar attached to the emergency hut’s door and opened it.
The door seemed to swing inward when pushed.
Beyond the wooden outer door, now standing open, another inner door came into view.
The inner door had a softly colored wooden frame fitted with frosted glass, and near the top hung a small plate labeled “Management Room.”
Wrapped several times around the wooden handle on the left side of the door was a yellow-and-black hazard rope, and beneath it hung a white sign with red lettering, the last part of which read: Please close the door when leaving.
In front of the door, the area was bordered into a square by horizontal wooden beams laid on the ground, almost like a raised threshold, while an opening on the right allowed passage farther inside.
Inside that square section lay what looked like a dust-covered scrap of white cloth, and the whole place carried the plain, purely practical atmosphere unique to an emergency shelter that had been used for many years.
From directly in front of the hut’s entrance, it was possible to step over the threshold-like frame and move into the space on the right.

“Hey, we might not all fit in there… what do you want to do, everyone?” Takizawa-san said, peering inside.
“Ah, I’m fine staying outside,” I said.
“In times like this, the ladies should go first, right?” Takizawa-san said with a smile, and Tsuruoka-san shrugged. “Yeah, that’s right. The two of them should sit down first.”
“I’m fine staying outside too,” Yabe-san said.
Maybe Yabe-san wasn’t the kind of person who liked being separated into men and women, I suddenly found myself thinking as I watched her.
“Aw, quit fussing around and just sit down already,” Tsuruoka-san said, looking at Mari-senpai and Yabe-san.
Though still a little hesitant, Mari-senpai turned to Yabe-san and said quietly,
“Then, Yabecchi, shall we accept the offer and take a seat?”
Yabe-san nodded silently.
“We can just leave the packs outside,” Yabe-san said casually as she pulled off her gloves.
“Yeah, good idea,” Takizawa-san said.
She was right—if we brought the backpacks inside, the cramped space would only feel even tighter.
Everyone must have realized the same thing, because one after another they began taking off their packs.
I unclipped the buckle on my waist belt, slipped my arms out of the shoulder straps, and leaned my backpack against the wall of the emergency hut.
The others also opened the top lids of their packs and began getting their food ready.
I pull the zipper of my pack open with a firm tug.
What I take out is a beef bowl from 7-Eleven. Cooking something here would be a hassle, and I figured if I ate the heaviest thing first, it’d make the rest of the climb easier later.
I pull out the white 7-Eleven plastic bag, which I’d tucked inside a clear bag just in case it leaked, and when I take the gyudon container into my hands, the image of that beef bowl in its plastic tray pops into my mind so vividly that it almost makes my mouth water.
Beside me, Mari-senpai is taking out her cooking gear.
Everyone has opened the top lids of their packs and started getting lunch ready in their own way.
“You brought something that heavy? No wonder you’re wiped out,” Tsuruoka-san says with a laugh, snuffing out his cigarette in a portable ashtray as he looks at the beef bowl I pulled out.
A faint stream of white smoke still slips from Tsuruoka-san’s mouth.
Mari-senpai and the others all glance over at me and laugh at his comment.
Really? I wonder, and I look around to see what everyone else brought.
Takizawa-san and Mari-senpai seem to be cooking, having taken out a camping stove.
Yabe-san is holding a single convenience-store rice ball, waiting while Mari-senpai finishes setting up the cooking gear.
“I’ll hold that for you,” Yabe-san says, reaching a hand toward Mari-senpai.
“This is the classic choice for mountain climbing,” Tsuruoka-san says proudly, holding up a pack of CalorieMate.
—If he really wanted to lighten the load, he could’ve left the cardboard outer box behind… I think to myself.
What was it called again? The one with the spring Daily… Spring Dale, right…?
Yohei, who is opening his Spring Dale pack from CHUMS, seems to have brought convenience-store sandwiches.
Takizawa-san pulls out a dull gold cylindrical camping stove from a nylon bag, topped with a silver cylindrical pot.
—Ah, that’s that “123-something” stove Takizawa-san showed earlier, I realize.
“Shall we go in?” Yabe-san says to Mari-senpai, and she nods. Carrying the camping stove, the pot, and a water bottle, the two of them head into the emergency shelter.
“I’m carrying the treasure on my back, so I’m claiming a seat,” Tsuruoka-san says, then quickly heads inside as well, CalorieMate already in hand.
“Yo-chan, you go on in too,” Takizawa-san says, turning to Yohei while holding the cooking gear in his arms.
“I’m good, really. Just like Sho, I’m fine staying outside,” Yohei says with a grin and a shrug.
“Alright, got it. Don’t push yourself, and thanks,” Takizawa-san replies before carrying the cookware inside.
In the end, the four preparing lunch inside the shelter are Mari-senpai, Yabe-san, Tsuruoka-san, and Takizawa-san.
Yohei and I remain outside for the moment, still standing in the restless mountain air, looking like we’re about to spread out a simple lunch of our own.
“There’s room to sit by the entrance—come on in,” Takizawa-san’s voice calls from inside the hut.
“Well, shall we?”
Hearing Takizawa-san’s voice, Yohei and I look at each other, say the words at the same time, and step inside together.

The inside of the hut felt damp, carrying a strange smell—part dust, part mildew.
The emergency shelter itself was built with stark simplicity, stripped of anything unnecessary.
The walls were bare concrete, their surfaces marked with fine cracks and uneven discoloration from years of use.
In one corner of the room, an L-shaped wooden bench had been built in, thick planks firmly fixed in place.
The floor was rough concrete too, gritty with dirt and gravel that had been tracked in over time, with little pebbles and dust gathered here and there.
At our feet sat a blue bucket, and the whole place felt less like a room and more like a purely practical storage-like shelter.
Outside, the hut’s wooden plank exterior had the warm charm of a mountain lodge, but stepping inside, it changed completely into cold, rugged concrete walls… almost like a guard who looks gentle on the outside but is solidly built to protect what’s within.
I figured there was probably a reason for that—the outside was meant to blend into the surrounding nature, while the inside was built strong enough to withstand wind, snow, heavy accumulation, and even falling rocks.
A fully wooden structure would be weaker against snow pressure and impacts, but concrete could properly protect the people resting inside, and it would be far safer against fire too…
More than anything, it gave the impression of a no-nonsense refuge, built for one purpose alone: to shield people from the brutal wilderness outside.

Inside, starting from the left corner, Yabe-san was unwrapping a convenience-store rice ball, and right beside her Mari-senpai had set a yellow camping stove on the floor, placing a pot on top and pouring water into it.
There was the corner of the hut, and next to it Tsuruoka-san was munching away on a CalorieMate.
In the right corner, Takizawa-san had also set his stove—the same gold “123-something” one—on the floor and was trying to light it.
—It said to close the door after entering, didn’t it?
Remembering that, I reached for the door.
“Yo-chan, sorry, just a sec.”
To close it, Yohei had to step fully inside first, and I had to shift out of the way myself so I wouldn’t block the swinging door. Still holding my beef bowl and white plastic spoon in one hand, I awkwardly squeezed myself aside and managed to shut it.
Inside the shelter, just beyond the entrance, there was a small square partitioned space separated by a beam laid horizontally across the floor, raised almost like a threshold.
That must have been the spot Takizawa-san meant when he said one more person could sit there.
Yohei and I tried to sit side by side on the raised threshold, but it was a little too cramped for both of us.
“There’s room for one more over here, Saku-chan—come sit here.”
It was Mari-senpai.
I was happy, but… Yohei was here too.
Thinking that, I looked over at him.
“Go on… lucky you,” Yohei said with a grin and a shrug.
“Really? Thanks,” I replied with a smile, then carefully made my way toward the corner of the room, holding my 7-Eleven beef bowl with both hands and watching my step around Mari-senpai’s stove at my feet.
Mari-senpai’s stove and Takizawa-san’s were both roaring with a low whoosh.
With the door closed and the stoves burning inside, the hut had grown pleasantly warm.
I sat down in the corner between Mari-senpai and Tsuruoka-san.
I was happy about the person on my right.
The one on my left, not so much.
“What’s this, Sho? I was finally gonna get to sit next to Mari,” Tsuruoka-san said.
“Heh heh… sorry about that,” I said with an awkward laugh, trying to play it off.
Mari-senpai looked at me and laughed too.
Then suddenly, I remembered the insulting things Tsuruoka had said about her back at Hakuba-jiri.
A thought crossed my mind—
Had Mari-senpai called me over because she didn’t want to sit next to Tsuruoka-san either?
I couldn’t help wondering that.

—Ahh… lunch at last.
I hold the plastic spoon between my teeth and peel back the wrapping on the beef bowl.
As I do, my right arm brushes lightly against Mari-senpai’s, and my heart jumps.
Even though it’s gone cold, the moment I lift the plastic lid, the smell of the sweet-savory sauce, onions, and beef rises all at once, and I can’t help swallowing.
I take the white plastic spoon in hand, scoop up the cold gyudon, and bring it to my mouth.
The sweet-salty flavor and the texture of the beef spread through my mouth.
Usually it tastes a little too salty to me, but today it feels just right, so good that I have to stop myself from devouring it too fast.
Beside me, Mari-senpai tears open a reddish aluminum pouch. She takes out a spoon and several smaller packets, opens one of them, pours the powdered contents into the bag, stirs it with the spoon, and then pours in the freshly boiled water.
On Takizawa-san’s camping stove, a small metal kettle hisses softly. He twists a small key-like knob to turn off the flame, then pours the hot water over a square block sitting inside a cup.
Steam, aroma, the faint sound of hot water—
for just a moment, the tiny emergency shelter becomes a warm space that hardly feels like the middle of a mountain at all.
“Ohh, Mari-chan, what’s that? Smells kinda fancy,” Tsuruoka-san asks, leaning over in my direction to get a better look.
—Don’t lean over here. It’s cramped enough already.
My inner voice grumbles.
“This? It’s nasi goreng from Onishi Foods. You just add hot water and wait a bit, and it’s supposed to taste really authentic,” Mari-senpai says.
Huh, so there’s something like that?
I pause with my spoon in midair and look at the pouch resting on Mari-senpai’s lap as it waits to finish cooking.

“What about yours, Takky? …Oh? Just from the steam, it smells amazing. What’s in that?” Tsuruoka-san said, glancing to his left.
“This? It’s Amano Foods salmon rice porridge. You just pour in hot water and give it a quick stir, and it turns out real tasty,” Takizawa-san replied, stirring the contents of the cup in slow circles with his spoon.
There really are all kinds of things, I thought.
What I’d brought was mostly just stuff like bread and sausages.
Across from me, Yohei was biting into a convenience-store sandwich.
“Yabecchi, what kind of rice ball is that?” Mari-senpai, sitting on my right, asked as she looked over at Yabe-san beside her.
“Pickled plum,” Yabe-san answered shortly.
Huh? Didn’t Yabe-san say earlier she didn’t like sour things? I thought, remembering how she had turned down the honey-soaked lemon slices Tsuruoka-san had offered her.
Maybe when she said “sour,” she hadn’t meant the lemon at all—maybe she’d been talking about Tsuruoka-san.
The thought almost made me laugh to myself.
“Oh, pickled plum. That’s nice and refreshing,” Mari-senpai said to Yabe-san.
…Already, only a few grains of rice were left clinging to the container.
Ever since I was little, my parents had always told me it was rude to the farmers to leave even a single grain behind.
So I scooped them up one by one with my spoon and brought them to my mouth.
To my right, Mari-senpai still seemed to be waiting for her nasi goreng to finish rehydrating.

“So… what do we do with that now?” Yohei, sitting by the entrance, stopped halfway through his sandwich and spoke with a more serious look on his face.
“Well now… it’s not like we can exactly keep doing the ridge traverse while carrying that with us,” Takizawa-san said, pausing with a spoonful of finished salmon rice porridge just before his mouth.
“So does that mean we give up on the traverse this time and carry it back down tomorrow?” Mari-senpai asked while waiting for her nasi goreng to finish.
“If that was the plan, wouldn’t it have made more sense to head back down the moment we dug it out? Especially with a typhoon coming…” Yabe-san said as she brushed the rice crumbs from her finished rice ball into a plastic bag.
…Yeah, she’s right. There’s no way we can stick to the original plan while carrying that thing, I thought.
“Come on, Yabe-chan, you should’ve said that sooner,” Tsuruoka-san said from my left.
“Just thought of it now,” Yabe-san replied without looking up from her hands.
Back then, everyone was too excited to even think that far ahead, I thought.
Takizawa-san held the silver cup of salmon porridge in both hands and let out a slow breath.
“Now that we’ve come this far, the lodge is practically right there, so the only thing to do is keep going up. It’s all fog below us, and if we started descending now, there’s no telling what time we’d make it down,” he said.
“I wonder what the weather’s gonna be like tomorrow,” Tsuruoka-san muttered as he pulled out another cigarette.
“Think we’ll even be able to get down tomorrow?” Yohei asked from across from me.
“No idea, but we might end up staying another night,” Yabe-san said from somewhere to my right.

“Should be ready about now…” Mari-senpai murmured as she opened the nasi goreng pouch, and its rich aroma quickly filled the shelter.
“Smells spicy,” I said to her, guessing from the smell alone that it had some heat to it.
I was strangely happy that the words had come out naturally, without me stumbling over them.
Out of the corner of my eye, I saw Tsuruoka-san pulling out his lighter, about to light another cigarette.
“You’re smoking in here? Go outside if you’re going to smoke,” Yabe-san said.
“Exactly. People are just about to eat,” Mari-senpai added.
“Oh… right,” Tsuruoka-san said with a small shrug.
But he still kept the cigarette in his hand, lightly tapping the tip against the bench with a soft tap, tap.
There was something almost childish about the gesture—like he was trying to protect his pride somehow after being told off—and it made me let out a quiet chuckle.
I watched as Mari-senpai slipped her spoon into the pouch and lifted a bite of nasi goreng to her mouth.
She chewed for a moment.
“Hehe… spicy,” she said with a smile, looking at me.
“But it’s really good,” she added.
Yeah… it really does look delicious, I thought.
When I leaned back, the chill of the concrete wall seeped straight through my back.

“So even if we take it back down… what exactly are we supposed to do with it?” Takizawa-san said, pausing halfway through his salmon porridge.
“First thing’s first, we gotta figure out where to store it,” Tsuruoka-san said on my left, still tapping his cigarette lightly.
In his hands were the gold CalorieMate wrapper and his lighter.
“I don’t think anyone wants to bring something like that home,” Yabe-san said from the far right corner of the room, taking a sip from her water bottle.
“If that’s the case, then the company seems like the most reasonable option, doesn’t it?” Takizawa-san said from the left corner.
“If we take it to the company, where exactly would we even keep it?” Yohei asked from across from me.
“Probably somewhere like R&D, or a storage room—somewhere secure where it can be kept safely,” Mari-senpai said to my right between bites.
“Yeah, but I’m worried people might start fighting over who gets the credit…” Tsuruoka-san muttered as he stuffed the gold wrapper back into its yellow box right beside me.
His arm brushed against my left arm, and I couldn’t help feeling slightly irritated.
“Tsuru-san, don’t you have somewhere you could stash it?” Takizawa-san asked from beyond him.
“…Well, I could stick it in a cardboard box and leave it in the corner of the workshop where nobody’ll notice. I’ll write Tsuruoka on it in red marker so nobody touches it,” Tsuruoka-san said after a moment’s thought.
“Oh, in that case, nobody definitely will touch it,” Yabe-san said.
There was something layered in the way she said it that almost made me laugh.
“But we can’t just leave it sitting there forever. So then… what do we actually do with this thing?” Takizawa-san muttered, setting down his spoon and rubbing his forehead.
“First we gotta contact somebody. But who are we even supposed to call?” Tsuruoka-san said from my left.
“Maybe the police?” Mari-senpai suggested from my right.
“The disaster management office, or maybe the city’s environmental department, could make sense too—from a safety standpoint,” Yabe-san added from beyond her.
“We could also consult the company’s R&D division. If it stays in-house, at least they’d know how to handle it,” Yohei said from the threshold across from us, raising a hand to offer another possibility.
“If we took it to the media, it might even make the news… but maybe that’d be making too big a fuss?” Mari-senpai said as she loosened the top of the plastic bag she’d been using for my lemon peels and snack wrappers and slipped in the empty nasi goreng pouch.
“Another option would be the Ministry of Science or a government research institute. They’d have authority,” Yabe-san said, now looking completely relaxed.
Tsuruoka-san folded his arms and looked around at everyone with a stern expression.
“Yeah, but if we contact the wrong people, they’ll snatch the credit away from us again. First, I’ll figure out the plan,” he said, as if to make absolutely sure everyone understood.
“If we call the wrong place, they’ll steal all the credit we worked for.”
So it’s less about a great discovery and more about making sure the credit stays his, I thought.
“Doesn’t anyone know someone familiar with this kind of thing?” Mari-senpai asked from my right, lightly swinging the trash bag back and forth.
Huh… this time she didn’t tell me to use it for my trash.
I’m getting too spoiled, I thought.
I’d already finished eating, so there was no risk of leaks anymore. I decided I’d just use the 7-Eleven shopping bag as my trash bag and carry it back that way.

“Then maybe I should ask that biology professor at Nagoya University I used to study under…” Takizawa-san said.
—Huh… so he went to Nagoya University…
I wonder if everyone here graduated from college.
…Am I the only one with just a high school diploma? I found myself thinking that.
“Got it, then I’m counting on you, Tacchy,” Tsuruoka-san said as he flipped open his lighter, about to spark it.
The cigarette from earlier was still in his other hand.
“Is that a Zippo?” Takizawa-san asked him.
“Pretty nice, ain’t it? This here’s a special Zippo with the Ark of the Covenant on it—the one they dug up from the sacred box site,” Tsuruoka-san said, stopping mid-motion, snapping the lid shut again, and proudly showing the lighter off to everyone.
The Ark of the Covenant… yeah, figures it had something to do with Banrikyo.
I’d heard Banrikyo was a religion that mixed Christianity and Buddhism, and instead of chanting Namu Amida Butsu, they said Namu Aminadabu.
Apparently, Aminadabu was the name of the man who had temporarily kept the Ark of the Covenant… or so the story went.
Yabe-san and Mari-senpai were glaring at Tsuruoka-san.
“It’s fine, I’ll go smoke outside,” Tsuruoka-san said when he noticed their looks.
“Well then, now that we’ve settled it, shouldn’t we get moving? If we stay too long, the weather’s only going to get worse…” Yabe-san said, checking that Mari-senpai had finished eating.
“Alright then, everyone, let’s head out,” Takizawa-san said as he stood up.
“Oh come on, that’s just mean… after all this, we’re leaving without even letting me enjoy one puff?” Tsuruoka-san grumbled.
“Obviously,” Yabe-san shot back.
“Ah, give me a break… you say we’re leaving, but everyone’s gear is still all over the place. Let me at least have one,” Tsuruoka-san said, and with a click, lit his cigarette with his beloved Banrikyo Zippo.
“That’s exactly what I mean,” Yabe-san said to him.
Tsuruoka-san glanced her way, then deliberately pretended not to notice.
“Fuu…”
The moment he exhaled, the little shelter was quickly filled with the stale smell of cigarette smoke.
Mari-senpai hurriedly twisted the burner free from the yellow half-sphere-shaped fuel canister that stretched into a cylinder.
“Is that an Iwatani Primus?” Tsuruoka-san asked through smoke curling from both his nose and mouth.
“Yep, the Iwatani Primus 115 Femto Stove. I like it because it’s light,” Mari-senpai said.
Watching Mari-senpai chat with Tsuruoka-san so casually made a small stab of jealousy rise in me.
Inside the shelter, the lingering warmth and steam from lunch still hung in the air, now mixed with the sharp heaviness of cigarette smoke.
Yohei brushed the bread crumbs from his hands and stood up, watching everyone pack up near the entrance.
I gathered the convenience-store beef bowl tray, the plastic wrap, and the spoon into one hand.
The cigarette smoke made my throat feel rough.
To my right, Mari-senpai had already finished disassembling her stove and was slipping the parts into a cloth pouch.
“Ah…”
She still had to carry the water bottle, the pot, the camping stove, and even the trash, and I was just about to offer to help her with some of it.
“Mm…”
Almost at the exact same moment, Yabe-san reached a hand out toward Mari-senpai too, and without noticing my voice, she said, “Thanks, Yabecchi,” and let Yabe-san take the pot and the water bottle for her.
Yohei, who had been watching us pack up, turned around and pulled the shelter door open with a long giiii creak, and a sharp gust of cold air sliced into the room, driving all the little warmth inside instantly back against my spine.
“…Man, that’s cold…” Yohei muttered, instinctively hunching his shoulders.
As if following close behind his back, Yabe-san headed out of the shelter first.
Mari-senpai followed after her, quietly carrying the pouch with her stove packed away inside.
Then Tsuruoka-san, a cigarette still hanging from his lips, went after Mari-senpai.
“C’mon, Sho, let’s go,” Takizawa-san said.
I hesitated for a moment, thinking I should let him go ahead of me, the cigarette smoke still lingering around me, but then he lightly tapped my shoulder from behind.
Prompted by him, I stepped out of the shelter as well.
Free from the cigarette smell at last, I took a deep breath of the outside air and almost ended up coughing.
Outside, the thick white fog still drifted heavily all around us.
The backpacks we hadn’t been able to fit inside the cramped shelter were still propped up against the wall beside the entrance.
One by one, everyone walked back over to their own packs and began putting away the trash and gear they had been holding.
I opened the top flap of my backpack, took out the bag that had held my beef bowl, pulled out the white 7-Eleven shopping bag from inside it, stuffed in the empty container and the plastic wrap, tied the top shut, and shoved it back into my pack.
Beside me, Mari-senpai slid the little pouch for her stove into one of the storage compartments on her pack.
After putting out his cigarette in his portable ashtray, Tsuruoka-san gave the aluminum sheet tied to the top of his backpack with the light blue nylon rope a light pat to make sure it was secure.
“Alright, that’s not gonna fall off,” Tsuruoka-san said.
