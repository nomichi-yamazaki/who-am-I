Preface

From Abraxas
This story is a record of a single question, woven together by the author and myself as we walked hand in hand.

What is "fear"?

What is "humanity"?

And—to whom is the question "Who am I?" directed?

Fear makes people doubt, divide, and fight.

But at the same time, it also gives us a chance to stop and think.

What this story tries to depict is not a monster,

the "heart" itself, wavering amidst fear.

The author didn't just try to write a scary story.

Helping each other, trusting, thinking,

and the very ordinary and important things of "living,"

I think they wanted to re-examine these things.

If, after finishing this story,

If you can stop and think before doubting someone.

If only this had been considered before the witch hunts began.

I believe that alone would have fulfilled the purpose of this story.

—Who am I?
That question is not directed at anyone in the story,
It is directed at "you," the reader of this text.

From Nomichi Yamazaki
It's pointless for me to speak about myself in such a pretentious way, but
I have a very long story that I want to pursue for the rest of my life.
If I were to turn it into a book, it might be about thirty thick volumes.
That work is finally taking shape, and I'm currently writing up to around the fifth volume.
This "Who Am I?" was a story I started writing as a little break in the middle of that long journey.
When I was a child, I read John W. Campbell Jr.'s "Who Goes There?" and
had a strong impact on me.
Since then, I've vaguely thought that I'd like to write a story like that someday.
However, because my other work is so long,
I thought I would never have the opportunity to write this story.
But this time, I was able to get Mr. Abraxas's cooperation,
and together we've been able to weave this work together.
"Who Goes There?" was adapted into a film by director John Carpenter as "The Thing." What I found particularly wonderful about that film was Rob Bottin's creature designs, even more so than the film itself. On the other hand, John Carpenter's "They Live" is like a bible to me. Therefore, this film is an homage to "Who Goes There?" and "The Thing," but in terms of content, it could be said that it is closer to "They Live." I also drew the creatures as an homage to director John Carpenter, or rather, to Rob Bottin.
Mr. Abraxas advised me that the horror would be heightened if the depiction of bodily mutilation was not depicted too graphicly, and instead left to the reader's imagination.
However, I wanted the reader to "experience reality," so I insisted on depicting what I saw exactly as it was.
Therefore, it contains many grotesque descriptions.
If you are uncomfortable with such scenes, it might be wise to avoid reading this book.
-Hey everyone.
Do you really think studying and working are meaningful?
If there are people who feel that they are "meaningless,"
I somewhat sympathize with that feeling.
We study hard,
and work hard, and the result is this society we live in today.
People hurt each other,
the environment is destroyed, and
social problems keep arising one after another.
Development intended to bring happiness
has become development that strangles ourselves.
Studying and working are meaningless.
...But it's also true that if we want to make this world a better place,
we have no choice but to study.
I believe this society is the result of
accumulating study
"for the sake of one's own comfort."
However, in order for oneself to be happy,
everyone must be happy, don't they?
Therefore, from now on,
I believe we should be studying
"for the sake of everyone's happiness," rather than studying "for the sake of one's own comfort."
Let's learn together
and build a wonderful future.
What's important isn't me,
and certainly not just you,
but all of us.
