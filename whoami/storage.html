Storage

Without a word, they shoved me into the storage room.
Beyond the wooden door stretched a cramped, dim space with nowhere to run.
A single sixty-watt bulb hanging from the ceiling cast an uneasy glow over the narrow room.
The weak light threw deep shadows across the shelves, giving the canned food, worn rags, and old cooking utensils strange, unsettling outlines.
Dust drifted lazily through the thin beams of light.
“We’ll at least leave the light on for ya…”
The owner left those words behind and lowered his eyes.
Everyone had been consumed by tension and fear, and there was no sign the situation would calm down anytime soon.
The weapons I had built with my own hands had ended up being used to restrain me.
The irony pierced deep into my chest.

As soon as I was shoved into the cramped storage room, the heavy wooden door slammed shut behind me.
A moment later, the door rattled slightly, followed by the sharp metallic click of the padlock being fastened outside.
Hearing it lock, I clenched my teeth.
—Nobody’s really at fault. They’re just scared.
That was what I tried to tell myself.
But deep inside my chest, something hot and bitter creaked quietly under the strain.
Voices and footsteps drifted away outside.
A sharp smell stung my nose.
The damp scent of old wood mixed with the sour odor of burnt oil.
From the cardboard boxes stacked on the shelves came the smell of wet paper and dry rice sacks.
Behind that lingered the metallic scent of canned food, the cloying sweetness of old seasonings…
…and somewhere underneath it all, the faint scorched smell of dust slowly burning.
The bare bulb overhead crackled softly with heat as dust gathered and singed against it.
Fwoooosh… clatter… creeeak… crack-crack… shhhhhh… tap… tap…
Beyond the wall opposite the entrance seemed to be the outside world, and through it came the sounds of rain and wind hammering against the lodge.
Whenever the storm noises briefly died away, the only thing left ringing in my ears was the sound of my own heartbeat.
A dim, narrow room.
Immediately to the left of the entrance was a flashlight resting in a holder mounted to the wall.
The storage room itself recessed deeper on the right-hand side from the doorway, with shelves standing against both the near and far walls.
The shelves were simple things—three wooden boards supported by four posts—and they were crammed with stacked cooking pots and cardboard boxes.
Written by hand across the labels were words like “Dry Noodles,” “Canned Food,” and “Seasonings.”
Boxes filled every shelf from top to bottom, and even more were piled in front of them.
At the far-left corner of the lowest shelf in the back sat a long metallic-blue metal case.
Judging from the pipe wrench beside it, it was probably a toolbox.
The aisle between the shelves was barely wide enough for a single person to squeeze through.
The naked sixty-watt bulb hanging from the ceiling cast a yellowish light that failed to reach every corner of the room, leaving long shadows pooled beneath the shelves.
I imagined the owner had left the light on because, even if he couldn’t be certain whether I was human or not, I still looked human enough for him to feel guilty about shutting me in here alone.
I pressed my ear against the door and listened carefully.
I could hear the distant murmur of voices from the dining hall.
Not clearly enough to make out what they were saying.
Standing there forever felt pointless, so I sat down on one of the cardboard boxes near the edge of the front shelf.
Laughter drifted faintly from outside.
Had throwing all the blame onto me made them feel safe now?
Even though I wasn’t one of those things…
Even though the real thing might still be among them somewhere…

I slowly drew in a breath and let my eyes drift across the words written on the cardboard boxes stacked along the upper shelves.
“Dry Noodles.” “Canned Food.” “Seasonings.”
Ordinary words.
Yet now they looked like relics from some distant world.
Just a few hours ago, everyone had been laughing together over dinner—
and now, those same people had nearly turned on me like a mob.
I pulled my knees close and pressed a hand against my forehead.
The smell of old wood filled the room.
Musty. Damp. Like something left half-dry for too long.
The storm battered the wall across from me while distant voices and the scrape of chairs drifted faintly through the building.
It felt hard to breathe.
—This wasn’t supposed to happen.
I never imagined the weapons I built myself would end up cornering me instead.
I had made them to protect everyone.
And now those same weapons were feeding their fear.
—And even though I wanted them to trust me… nobody would.
Suzuki Sho-san and Mari-senpai had tried desperately to defend me, but everyone else’s eyes burned with suspicion.
They had been swept away by the instinct to choose safety over truth.
Like some curse had been cast over the room, I had been isolated completely.
—Tsuruoka-san…
His words had spread through everyone’s minds like sparks sinking into dry tinder.
It’s safer to doubt someone.
That feeling swallowed the room whole.
Had my very existence become the symbol of everyone’s fear?
I clenched my fists and ground my teeth together.
I was scared.
But somewhere along the way, I felt like I had already lost the urge to run.
—This whole situation feels absurd.
In The Frog Prince, a frog turning back into a human is something people can smile about.
But this…
This wasn’t funny at all.
Unable to prove to anyone that I was truly myself, I sat locked away in a storage room.
Deep inside my chest, anger, fear, loneliness, and bitter irony twisted together—burning quietly, steadily, without ever going out.

As the waves of panic slowly receded, colder thoughts began to fill my head instead.
—Calm down. I’m still alive.
—This place might not be safe, but at least nobody’s attacking me right now.
When I thought about it, Mari-senpai had brought me snacks while I was watching that thing in the drying room. Suzuki Sho-san had even stayed alone with me while we built the weapons together.
If I really were one of those things, then shouldn’t the two of them have been turned into one as well?
The more I thought about it, the less sense it made that I was the only one locked in here.
—Maybe Tsuruoka-san really does hold a grudge against me for not joining the Banrikyo group… or for taking too long to arrange the solenoid valve.
Or maybe everyone was just too terrified to think clearly anymore.
Maybe they simply hadn’t even considered the fact that Mari-senpai and Suzuki Sho-san had both been alone with me too.
The thoughts swirled endlessly in my head.
Was I locked up because Tsuruoka-san personally resented me… or was I just unlucky?
Even trying to think calmly, the darkness made everything feel uncertain.
If I pointed that out to the others, then Mari-senpai and Suzuki Sho-san might end up locked in here too.
At least then I wouldn’t be alone… but I couldn’t bear the thought of putting Mari-senpai through something like this.
And if I said it out loud, like some kind of accusation, she might end up hating me for it.
What was I supposed to do?
The longer I sat there thinking by myself, the more the loneliness and fear inside my chest tangled together, blurring the sharp edges of my thoughts.
The stacks of cardboard boxes in front of me looked heavy and oppressive, as though they reflected the state of my own heart.

After the door closed, all I could think about was the way everyone had looked at me with those cold eyes… and the fact that they had locked me in here.
For a while, nothing else even entered my mind.
But as my eyes gradually adjusted to the dimness, my attention drifted toward the cardboard boxes stacked on the shelves.
—I wonder what’s inside them…
Almost without thinking, I pulled out a few boxes within reach.
One contained spare plastic wrap, paper towels, and aluminum foil.
Another was filled with extra seasonings—soy sauce, cooking oil, and other supplies.
The bottled water and cups of Donbei were probably meant for the mountain lodge’s shop.
At the bottom of a different box, old retort-pouch meals were packed tightly together.
Bright yellow packages.
Bon Curry.
I almost laughed.
The fact that, in a moment like this, I was relieved there seemed to be no shortage of food felt absurd.
A few canned goods were mixed in between the boxes as well.
From gaps in the oil-stained labels came the faint smell of curry spices drifting through the air.
I wasn’t even hungry, and yet the smell felt strangely vivid, enough to make me quietly catch my breath.
Near the bottom shelf, brown rice sacks were stacked one on top of another.
One of the sacks lying beside them had already been opened, its mouth folded over and tightly wrapped with a wide brown paper tie.
—Still… this is stupid.
If everyone ended up trapped in this mountain lodge for days, then locking up someone they thought might be one of those things inside the food storage room meant they’d lose access to all these supplies too.
I guess when people are pushed into that kind of fear, they stop being able to think calmly about things like that.
That was what I found myself thinking.

I suddenly realized just how thirsty I was.
There should’ve been some bottled water in here somewhere.
Well… they locked me up in this place. Taking one bottle probably wouldn’t hurt anyone.
Holding my breath, I searched through the cardboard boxes with trembling hands.
Rustle… shuffle…
The dry sound of paper scraping together mixed with the smell of dust in the air.
Then my fingers brushed against something cold.
A plastic bottle.
“…I Lohas…”
I glanced at the small label.
Clear water inside a 500-milliliter bottle wrapped in a pale blue label.
The sound of the cap twisting open echoed softly in the cramped storage room.
Gulp… gulp… gulp…
“...Phew...”
The water soaked into my parched throat.
Its coolness spread deep into my body, easing, if only a little, the burning tension that had been building inside my chest.
One swallow, then another.
Each time the water flowed down through my throat, some of the panic inside me seemed to loosen just a little as well.

After quenching my thirst and sitting alone in the storage room with nothing to do, I gradually began to calm down a little.
The feeling in my chest—that unbearable pressure like something was about to tear apart inside me—still hadn’t gone away.
But all I could do now was steady my breathing and try to sort through everything that had happened.
That crevasse.
That was where we had found the strange object that looked like a UFO.
And beside it… there had been that black, fetus-like thing.
We carried it all the way back to the mountain lodge and stored it in the drying room.
The athletic guy and the glasses-and-jeans man had started taking pictures without permission, so I’d been told to stand guard over it.
But while I dozed off for a moment, that black fetus-like thing disappeared.
I panicked and rushed off to tell everyone—
and then I heard the scream.
When I ran to the washroom, I found one of those things attacking people.
Akane-san.
The lone man whose lower lip jutted out.
Tanaka-san.
The woman from that suspicious middle-aged pair staying at the lodge…
The scene in front of me had blurred together with memory and panic until I could barely tell what was real anymore.
And then I remembered.
That middle-aged woman had come back into the dining hall afterward.
Right.
That was what I’d felt I was forgetting.
Akane-san, who should’ve been dead, had returned looking normal again too.
Which meant… that middle-aged woman—Kishida-san—might also have been one of those things in disguise.
Tanaka-san’s face had melted away, leaving the bone underneath exposed…
and yet later, when I saw her again, her face had somehow returned to normal too.
And after that, when I went back to check, both the bodies and everything else had vanished.
Didn’t that mean they had all become those things?
…Or had I only imagined Akane-san dying?
Had I only imagined Kishida-san being attacked?
Could they actually still have been ordinary human beings the whole time?
…No.
That couldn’t be it.
Naoki-kun—the only one who had survived—still had a tentacle attached to his cheek.
His body had continued mutating right in front of us until he became that horrible thing.
If we had left him alone… would he eventually have returned to looking normal too?
But even if the body looked like Naoki-kun again, the thing inside wouldn’t really be him anymore.
That fear tightened around my heart like a vice.
What had those things intended to do after taking human form?
Tsuruoka-san had insisted they planned to attack people one after another and turn everyone into more of their kind.
But was that really true?
If it was, then that middle-aged woman was still somewhere inside the lodge.
Which meant everyone else could already be in danger too.
…And then another thought struck me.
What if some of the campers we had reluctantly let inside out of the storm were already among them?
If even one of those things had been let into the dining hall, then everyone gathered there could be attacked.
The thought alone made my chest tighten, and I felt sweat slowly running down my back.

—I have to warn Mari-senpai and Yohei…
That woman should have been attacked by one of those things… and yet she’d been inside the dining hall earlier.
Kishida-san and Yagi-san—the man who had been with her—weren’t in the dining hall now, but they had to be somewhere inside this lodge.
If nobody did anything, Mari-senpai and Yohei might end up being attacked too.
“Hey! Somebody! Is anyone there?!”
I shot to my feet and rushed toward the door.
“Listen to me! I’m not one of them! That woman was inside the dining hall! Mari-senpai and Yohei are in danger!”
I pounded on the door again and again with my fists.
The heavy wood gave off dull thuds that echoed through the cramped storage room.
I shouted until my throat burned raw.
But the only thing that answered me… was silence.
Behind me, the wind whistled like a flute, and somewhere above, the metal roof creaked faintly under the storm.
I stood there with my forehead pressed against the door, breathing hard.
Even then… I still couldn’t stop.
“Please… somebody… listen to me…”
The strength slowly drained from my fists.
“Mari-senpai… Yohei…”
Murmuring their names weakly, I finally sank to my knees where I stood.
No answer came from the other side of the door.
All I could hear was the distant whistling of the wind and the faint groaning of the metal roof overhead.
“…Whatever. Nobody’s going to listen to me anyway.”
I muttered the words and slumped down where I was.
Even I knew how pathetic I looked, but I didn’t have the strength left to care anymore.
—Why did this have to happen to me?
…This is exactly why I never wanted to go mountain climbing in the first place…
Takizawa-san kept pushing me to come…
Though really, it was my own fault for not being able to say no…
But even so… despite how exhausting it had been, climbing the mountain had actually been so much fun…

Today’s climb came back to me in fragments.
“Sho! Is your pack heavy? It’s pretty rough for beginners, you know! …But hey, the weather’s great today—I’m looking forward to it!”Yohei’s cheerful voice had echoed along the trail, easing the tension I’d been carrying inside me.
Takizawa-san’s words — “You’ll be fine. Anyone can make it if they pace themselves.”— had reassured me, and I’d laughed at Tsuruoka-san’s ridiculous story about piton chips.
Hakuba-dake rising beyond the forest road. Korenge. Mikuni-zakai.
Takizawa-san pointing out which direction Toyama lay in while Yohei and I talked about music as we walked.
Mari-senpai asking me to take photos.
Yabe-san and Tsuruoka-san arguing about how chanting “panties” made your fatigue disappear, while I struggled not to burst out laughing.
The break at the mountain lodge. The coffee Takizawa-san brewed for us. The time spent surrounded by Baumkuchen and marshmallows.
Laughing together with Akane-san and Takamura-san.
The tension and excitement of crossing the great snow valley with crampons biting into the ice.
And then I remembered the thing we dug up.
That black fetus-like mass we’d uncovered together, shouting in excitement as we worked side by side.
None of us knew it was dangerous. We’d been completely absorbed in the thrill of it.
Lunch at the emergency hut. The convenience-store gyudon that tasted unbelievably good. The beauty of the mountains that made us forget our exhaustion. The strange climber’s-high feeling that kept our legs moving forward.
And the way we laughed at our shadows inside the rainbow of the Brocken spectre.
Even Tsuruoka-san’s wild imagination came back vividly.
Scientists from all over the world gathering here. News crews. Academic conferences. Exhibitions. Interviews swarming the mountain lodge —
the fantasy had swept us all up, filling us with childish excitement.
I could still remember us sitting on the lodge cushions, laughing together.
And then the silly exchange with the lodge owner about Bon Curry after dinner…
That mountain trip had been so much fun.
But now… look at what it had become.
Akane-san. Takamura-san. Yabe-san.
Akane-san, who’d smiled while talking about how much she loved blueberries —
that “Something” had gotten her.
And then the “Something” wearing Akane-san’s face had stood outside the glass, pounding on it and begging to be let inside…
That man with the lower lip jutting out, the one who’d stopped and stared at us while we dug up the creature —
he became one of the monster’s victims in the washroom.
“please be very careful.”
The man around sixty who’d warned us like that…
Later, he was the one pointing a knife at me,
one of the people who locked me inside this storage room.
“I’ll give you a hand!”
Naoki-kun had said that while helping us unload the “Something” onto the dirt floor near the reception desk.
Then the tentacles wrapped around him,
and his body twisted into something grotesque.
And Tsuruoka-san set him on fire.
“Me? …I teach geography.”
Tanaka-san, the high school teacher who’d said that with a shy smile —
she’d tried to save Naoki-kun after the tentacles latched onto him,
and her face and arms had melted away until bone showed through.
And then… there was me.
Suspected of being one of the “Somethings,”
locked away inside this dark storage room.
I was terrified and wanted to run away, but there was nowhere to go — nothing but mountains outside, and a raging storm beyond them.
And I was dressed in nothing more than a short-sleeved T-shirt and shorts.
