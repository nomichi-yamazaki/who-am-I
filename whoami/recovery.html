Recovery

“All right. I won’t oppose it. But be careful. If we don’t do this cautiously, it could get dangerous,” Takizawa-san said.
Tsuruoka-san nodded once to him, then stood there for a few moments as if deep in thought.
Then, as though an idea had suddenly struck him, he slowly lowered himself onto the snow and began loosening the thin nylon straps of the crampons attached to his mountaineering boots, taking them off.
The very same Grivel 10-point crampons he had been bragging about.
Tsuruoka-san’s expression was deadly serious, and I could tell all his focus had shifted to preparing to chip away at the ice.
“All right, let’s do this.”
The moment he finished removing the crampons, he gripped one in his hand and began slamming its points against the ice with a harsh clatter-clatter.
Each strike sent shards of ice carved loose by the crampon spikes bursting outward in every direction.
Some of the spray hit my arm, and for an instant I felt tiny cold beads against my skin.
Takizawa-san took off his pack, pulled out a mountaineering knife, and carefully began shaving away the thinner sections of ice.
Mari-senpai also set down her pack and, after thinking for a moment, pulled on a thick pair of rain gloves to protect her hands from the cold before looking around and picking up a stone she found nearby.
When she swung it down against the hard ice, a sharp crack rang out, and fine splinters of ice scattered everywhere.
I need to help too.
That was what I thought, but I hesitated, unsure how best to join in.
After setting down her own pack, Yabe-san crunched her way a little farther up across the snow and picked up a rough, fist-sized stone lying there.
“If we’re digging it out, it’ll be faster if we all do it together,” she said, moving beside Mari-senpai and bringing the stone down against the ice.
Yohei copied Tsuruoka-san, removing his crampons and hammering at the ice with the spikes.
I figured the stone method looked best too, so I glanced around, picked up a decent-sized rock, and joined the excavation.
I smashed the stone into the ice, breaking it apart.
Fragments of shattered ice flew toward me.
The surface of the stone grew wet, and the meltwater seeped onto my hands, making the cold bite deeper.
“Everyone, be careful not to damage it. We’ve gotta dig this out carefully,” Tsuruoka-san said, stopping the hand that had been swinging the crampon and rising to his feet.
I brushed the chunks of broken ice downward with my hand.
My fingertips were freezing.
I shouldn’t have said digging it up was a good idea.
Only now did the regret really hit me.
I rubbed my chilled fingers together to warm them, then went back to work.
The others were doing the same—breaking away the ice, then scraping it off by hand, over and over.
That’s right… I had a pot in my pack.
With that thought, I went back to my backpack, opened the top lid, loosened the drawstring cinching the opening shut, and pulled the pot out from inside.
Using the pot, I scooped away the ice everyone had broken loose.
“Saku-chan, that’s smart.”
Hearing Mari-senpai say that made me unexpectedly happy.

Yohei paused his hands and looked back toward the trail.
Wondering what it was, I turned my eyes that way too, and halfway along the route stood a lone man—probably in his forties, thin, with a balding head—staring straight at us.
He wore a pale gray long-sleeved shirt over his wiry frame, a navy fleece tied around his waist, and a large dark brown pack on his back. Through his glasses, his gaze stayed fixed on us.
Even from this distance, I could make out the distinctive weakness in his face—the way his lower lip jutted forward enough to be noticeable even from here.
“Hey… check that out,” Yohei muttered under his breath, already returning his hands to the work.
At his voice, everyone paused for just a moment.
Mari-senpai gave the man a quick glance, then immediately looked away and went back to digging.
Yabe-san straightened at the waist and looked over at the solitary hiker for a second longer.
I also looked away at once, deliberately pretending not to notice him, and kept my hands moving against the ice.
Takizawa-san interrupted his work only briefly, cast a short look toward the man, then returned to what he was doing.
Tsuruoka-san, on the other hand, paid it absolutely no mind, still swinging the crampon and scraping away at the ice as if nothing else in the world existed.
I flicked my eyes toward the man one more time.
He remained motionless, continuing to stare at us, but after a while he slowly resumed walking and passed on along the trail.
“Ah… there he goes,” Yohei murmured softly again.
Then all of us returned to our separate tasks and began moving our hands against the ice once more.

As we kept breaking away the ice, the outline of the black object gradually began to emerge with a little more clarity.
Because the chipped surface had become uneven and kept bending the light, what lay inside still wasn’t completely clear, but that tar-black, tightly curled shape could almost be seen as a fetus hugging its knees—while its limbs, bent into sharp Z-like angles, made it look almost insect-like, like some strange being unlike anything I had ever seen before.
Floating there in black against the cold transparency of the ice, its form felt disturbingly real, stirring a restless unease deep in my chest.

Almost without thinking, I lowered my gaze and saw several groups of climbers making their way up the slope of the snow valley below.
Their steps looked light and effortless, and they still didn’t seem to have noticed us yet.
But the memory of that lone man in his forties staring at us wouldn’t leave my mind.
Should we really be doing this?
The way that man had looked at us with such meaning left a quiet sense of guilt somewhere deep inside me.
In the stillness of the snow valley, the thought that our small movements here might be causing trouble for strangers I didn’t even know sent a faint chill along my spine.
I just want this to be over soon… I want to stop feeling like this.
Thinking that, I kept working.

“What are you all doing?”
A low, calm voice suddenly reached us.
When I turned around, a man in his sixties was standing there in the snow valley, looking our way.
His hair was short and streaked with gray, and behind his glasses his eyes were gentle, steady, and kind.
“Well, seems we stumbled onto a bit of a discovery,” Tsuruoka-san said, stopping his hands and rising to his feet, sounding almost proud. “We’re just taking a look.”
“I see. But if you push yourselves in a place like that, it could cost you your lives, so please be very careful,” the man said, his voice gentle yet firm as his eyes moved across each of us one by one.
“Yeah, yeah, we know, we know,” Tsuruoka-san said with a laugh, puffing his chest out just a little.
But for me, that guilty feeling sat heavy in my chest like a stone.
For a while now, it had felt as though the eyes of the climbers coming up the slope had been fixed on us the whole time, making it feel like we were doing something wrong.
Is it really okay for us to be doing this here?
I whispered the thought to myself in silence.

Clang… clang… clang… clang…
The sound of crampons striking ice still rang out without pause.
The black fetus-like thing before us now looked as if it were sealed inside a block of ice perched on a pedestal, and we had reached the point where, if we broke the section connecting it to that base, the whole thing might come free in one solid mass.
The surrounding snow had been carved away so drastically that, looking at the snow valley as a whole, only this spot had become a hollow crater-like pit—the kind of sight that made you want to say, Ah… we really went this far, didn’t we?

The scraping sounds against the snow stopped when a pair of quick, fluttering footsteps approached from a short distance away.
When I turned around, two young women in colorful hiking clothes were climbing up the snow valley with bright smiles on their faces.
“Excuse meee! Are you all okay?” called the one in the light blue jacket, raising her voice toward us.
The other woman, wearing a yellow knit cap, stopped a little farther back and lifted the camera hanging from her neck.
Tsuruoka-san paused his hand for only a second to glance back, then immediately returned to the work.
Instead, Takizawa-san looked up and answered with a casual wave of one hand.
“Oh, yeah, we’re fine!”
His voice echoed across the snow valley, sounding strangely cheerful against all that white silence.
“Ohhh, okay! That’s good!” the woman called back with a smile, then turned to say something to her friend.
The two of them didn’t come any closer and simply continued climbing, laughing together as they went.
Watching their backs disappear up the slope, I felt a faint itch of unease somewhere deep in my chest, the kind of feeling that wouldn’t let me settle.
Seriously… what are we even doing out here?

Tsuruoka-san dug the crampon spikes into the ice behind the buried object, scraping away at it with a harsh grr-grr-grr.
The metallic sound of ice being gouged out stabbed at my ears, and shards of flying ice brushed against my cheek.
Then came a dull, heavy grrk through the ice—
and the object suddenly broke free from the frozen wall with a heavy roll, beginning to slide slowly down the slope.
“Ooh…!” Takizawa-san shouted.
In that instant, everyone froze.
Then, after a beat of stunned silence, cheers burst out from all of us at once.
“We did it!!”
“Oh! It’s out!”
Yohei’s shout as he thrust the crampon he’d been holding into the air came almost at the exact same moment Tsuruoka-san said it, straightening his back with triumph.
Inside my chest, I let out a small Yes! of my own and found myself clenching my fist without even thinking.
The fatigue in my arms and shoulders from all that digging throbbed with a slow heaviness.
But laughter echoed around us, blowing that weariness away in an instant.
“We did it.”
Even Yabe-san, usually the quietest among us, said that with the faintest smile touching the corner of her lips.
For just a moment, that smile looked almost childlike, and somehow it made me happy too.
The realization swelled quietly inside my chest—
that something which had slept in the ice for decades had been dug out by our own hands.
“Man… it finally came out!” Takizawa-san said, wiping the sweat from his forehead with a grin.
“Everyone really worked hard for this,” Mari-senpai said too, straightening up and stretching her back.

The moment I looked at the thing still trapped in the ice, I couldn’t help drawing in a breath.
It was probably around fifty or sixty centimeters long.
Cradling the ice-covered mass like a baby, Tsuruoka-san lifted it experimentally in his arms.
“It’s fine. It’s not that heavy.”
At those words, everyone let out a quiet breath of relief.
“So then… what do we wrap it in?”
Takizawa-san rummaged through his pack, then said, “Hold on, I’ve got just the thing,” and pulled out a small folded silver thermal emergency blanket.
“Tsuru-san, you had rope, right?” Takizawa-san said.
“Yeah, yeah, I got it.”
As he answered, Tsuruoka-san pulled the rope from inside his backpack.
It was the very rope we’d been planning to use for anzailen.
Now that I’d started casually using a word like anzailen myself, I felt a little embarrassed somehow.
The rope looked strong, twisted together from strands of pale blue, orange, green, and white.
We all gathered around, spread the silver sheet out on the snow, then carefully rolled the object onto the center of it.
The underside was still covered in the uneven frozen texture of the ice, so it still wasn’t completely clear, but beneath what looked like a rounded head, the body seemed almost like a back—its shape rising in ridges like a spine and ribs layered one after another.
Everyone’s eyes focused on the hands folding in the edges of the sheet and tying the rope around it.
“See? Told you bringing the rope was the right call, didn’t I?” Tsuruoka-san said with a grin as he wound the rope around the object now wrapped in the crinkled, gleaming silver sheet.
His tone was playful, but underneath it I could hear a trace of frustration too—like part of him was still annoyed that after carrying it all this way, he hadn’t gotten to use it for anzailen after all.

Just then, a young man and woman appeared on the trail a short distance away.
The two of them stopped and seemed to be discussing something as they looked in our direction.
The next moment, the man pulled out his smartphone from his pack and pointed the lens toward us.
Click.
“They’re taking pictures of us,” Yabe-san said under her breath.
A ridiculous thought flashed through my mind—What if this gets posted on social media and everyone starts tearing into us, like “What the hell are these idiots doing?”
“So what do we do, senpai?” Yohei asked, careful not to look directly at them.
“…Leave them. Doesn’t look like they’re coming over here,” Tsuruoka-san said.
We kept watching for a moment, and before long the pair started climbing again.
The tight feeling wound up inside my chest loosened just a little, and I started moving my hands again.

When I looked down into the valley below, white clouds were beginning to boil up from beneath us.
The spot where we had put on our crampons earlier was already gone from sight.
The cotton-like mist drifted upward in a slow dance, constantly reshaping its edges as it climbed steadily toward us.
Yabe-san was right, I thought.
“Let’s hurry,” Takizawa-san said.
We tightened the rope securely around the bundle wrapped in the thermal blanket.
“I’ll carry it,” Tsuruoka-san said, rolling it tight and fastening it onto the top of his pack with nylon straps.
The white mist rose until it reached us, and in moments everything around us was swallowed into a world of pure white.
The view vanished, and even the sounds around us seemed to be absorbed into silence.
At this rate… we can’t see anything.
Are we going to get lost?
…Will we even make it safely to the mountain hut?
…If we end up stranded out here, this won’t be some great discovery anymore…
The thought made my stomach twist, my chest tightening with that same painful anxious pressure.
Right in front of me, Tsuruoka-san finished fastening that object wrapped in the thermal sheet firmly to the top of his pack, then hoisted the pack up onto his shoulders with a hard motion.
“Okay…”
The moment Tsuruoka-san let out that short breath, the sound of the tightening straps creaked and sank into the snow-muted silence.
