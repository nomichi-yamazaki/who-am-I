Dinner

Beeee… click. “Dinner is ready. Those who have ordered a meal, please proceed to the dining hall.” Beeee… click.
The announcement echoed from a cream-colored plastic speaker mounted in the corner of the ceiling.
“Well then, shall we?” Takizawa-san called out.
As we stepped out of the lounge, the two young women who had been on their phones came out from the opposite side.
They were laughing cheerfully, though there was a faint sense that they were still aware of their surroundings.
From the way they acted as if they didn’t know us, it seemed they hadn’t realized we were the ones digging something up in the snowfield.
…Or maybe they had noticed—and simply chose not to get involved.
We started walking toward the dining hall.
On our left was the lodge entrance, and a faint, peculiar smell drifted from the shoe lockers—something like leather… or perhaps sweat.
Ahead to the left were the reception desk and a small shop, where the coming and going of staff created a quiet murmur of activity.
Looking straight ahead, the corridor seemed to narrow as it stretched into the distance. On the left side, there were doors with glass windows in their upper halves, and beyond them, more glass-paneled walls lined up in an orderly fashion.
On the wide corridor to the right, pillars stood in neat rows down the center, leading toward the wash area.
Ahead to the right, between the pillars, I could see a staircase—and figures slowly descending from above.
As I walked, I felt a small sense of comfort in the spaciousness and order of the place… yet at the same time, I realized I had become just a little more sensitive to the movement of people and the smells around me.
“I’ll go get Yabe-san,” Mari-senpai said, starting to turn toward the stairs— —but just then, from the upper steps, I saw that same middle-aged couple with something unsettling about them, followed by Yabe-san… and the woman from the lounge—the one Tsuruoka-san had driven off—coming down together, laughing.
“Perfect timing,” Yohei said with a grin.

“He’s a bit off, you know—just barges right into people’s personal space without a second thought.” Yabe-san’s voice drifted down from the upper part of the stairs.
—Huh? Are they talking about Tsuruoka-san?
Come to think of it, he had been pretty pushy earlier… and that woman ended up leaving because of it… I thought.
“We’ve got someone like that where I work too,” the woman who had left the lounge said to Yabe-san.
The two of them came down the stairs, laughing lightly together.
“Oh yeah? What do you do?” Yabe-san asked.
“At a hospital. I work there too.”
“Ohh—so you’re a doctor?” Yabe-san said.
“Well… yes.”
—Oh… so she’s a doctor…
No wonder she seemed so composed, I thought.
The two of them exchanged glances and laughed again as they reached the bottom of the stairs.
Watching them, I couldn’t help widening my eyes.
I had never seen Yabe-san open up and laugh with someone like that before.

Mari-senpai also stopped walking, narrowing her eyes slightly as she watched the two of them.
“…Hey, has Yabe-san always been this talkative?” she whispered quietly to me.
Yabe-san and the woman who had left the lounge approached us.
Yabe-san glanced briefly in our direction, then turned to the woman and whispered something softly into her ear.
She responded with a small laugh.
—Could they be talking about Tsuruoka-san?
I found myself wondering.
“Thank you for earlier,” the woman said as she came up to us, giving a slight bow.
“Sure.” “Yeah, thanks.” “Thanks.” “Thanks.” “Thanks.”
We each responded in turn.
Yabe-san shot the woman a meaningful glance, and she nodded in return.
“Well then, I’ll be going… See you later.”
With that, she gave a small wave to both us and Yabe-san, then headed toward the dining hall ahead of us.
—So she’s not planning to eat with us…
Maybe Yabe-san suggested it, since Tsuruoka-san’s here…
I thought.
Tsuruoka-san watched the exchange from the corner of his eye, but in the end, he couldn’t bring himself to say a word.
That silence felt strangely satisfying.
I made a small, private fist pump in my mind.

Walking down the corridor, we arrived in front of the dining hall.
The sliding door the woman—driven off earlier by Tsuruoka-san—had just closed rattled as someone pushed it open with a clatter-clatter.
—Ah… curry.
That smell… could it be Otsuka’s original Bon Curry?
I thought.
As we stepped inside, one hiker was already seated.
He was the man in khaki trekking pants and a beige fleece—the one dressed almost entirely in beige—who had come down the stairs earlier while we were checking in and headed toward the wash area… the same man who had warned us to be careful while we were digging in the snowfield.
He was tall and lean. With thinning hair streaked with gray, he looked to be in his sixties.
He sat in silence, as if quietly drifting within the atmosphere of the dining hall.
Inside, long rectangular tables were arranged in neat rows.
The space felt open, with pillars standing at regular intervals, yet there was a calmness to it all.

At the counter in the very back, plates of curry served on white plastic dishes were neatly stacked, each separated by a translucent white plastic spacer.
The smell was both sweet and spicy, with a faint hint of something slightly charred, enough to make my stomach almost growl.
I stopped for a moment and took a quiet breath.
As we approached the counter, the two young women who had been on their phones in the lounge were already there.
They quickly picked up trays and began taking their curry.
Carefully lifting the stacked plates with the translucent separators in between, they also took a set with a spoon placed inside a glass of water.
There was something about their smooth, natural movements that caught my attention.
Without saying a word, yet somehow mindful of each other, they exchanged small smiles as they made their way to open seats.
Watching them briefly, I started thinking about the order in which we should grab our own trays.
Behind the counter, the staff—perhaps relieved after finishing a rush—stood gathered around a stainless steel prep table, chatting quietly.
There was the girl from the shop who looked a bit like Nagano Mei, wearing a red bandana and a matching apron… Naoki, the guy in a black Adidas tracksuit… and Kobayashi Ren, the mountain photographer who had helped carry that thing to the drying room.
Keeping their voices low but gesturing as they spoke, I could faintly hear bits of their conversation—
“Do you think it’s really a mammal fossil?”
“That was seriously creepy…”
“Show me the photos you took later.”

At the counter, the one standing in front of us turned out to be the same woman who had been talking with Yabe-san.
I wasn’t sure why she had said, “I’ll be going… See you later.”and gone ahead earlier, but now she looked slightly awkward.
Takizawa-san and Mari-senpai, who also seemed a bit uncomfortable, followed after her as we took our curry.
I picked up a rectangular tray, removed the plastic spacer from the plate, and stacked it with the others that weren’t being used.
Then I took a plate of curry and placed a glass of water—with a spoon inside—onto the tray.
Next to the curry rice were two pieces each of fukujinzuke and rakkyo.
—I could’ve done without the rakkyo…
I thought.
When I turned around, the dining hall had already begun to fill with hikers taking their seats here and there.
At a table along the wall, the two young women I had seen at the counter were now seated, chatting as they lined up their phones beside their plates of curry.
Further back, near the window, the beige-clad older man sat alone, quietly bringing his spoon to his mouth.
There was something about him that seemed to belong in a mountain lodge dining hall… and for some reason, the image lingered in my mind.

“Well then… where should we sit?” Takizawa-san said, lowering his voice as he glanced around.
“The window side looks cold,” Mari-senpai remarked.
She was right—the glass by the windows was still faintly fogged with condensation, and sitting near it would probably chill your back.
I found myself shrugging slightly.
Outside, it was already dark, and the interior of the dining hall reflected faintly on the glass.
When I casually looked over, the woman who had been talking with Yabe-san had taken a seat at the edge and had begun quietly eating alone.
Seeing that, Mari-senpai murmured, “Is she not eating with us?”
“…Just leave her be. That’s the kind of person she is,” Yabe-san said shortly.
“Then how about near that pillar in the middle? Easier to move around,” Yohei suggested.
“Yeah, good view of the whole place, and it’s open,” Takizawa-san nodded.
We chose a table near one of the central pillars.
It felt naturally comfortable, with a clear view of everything around us.
Each of us took our seats and set our trays down on the table.
As the white plates and glass cups lined up on the wooden tabletop, a strange sense of comfort settled in.
It finally felt real—that we were staying in a mountain lodge.
Across from Takizawa-san sat Tsuruoka-san.
Next to him was Mari-senpai, then Yabe-san beside her.
To Takizawa-san’s side sat me, and Yohei next to me.
In front of me was curry rice served on a white plastic plate resting on an aluminum tray.
Beside it, a spoon sat in a glass of water.
The aroma of curry stirred my appetite.
“Alright then—let’s eat,” Takizawa-san said.
The others echoed their own “let’s eat,” and we began.
I took the spoon from the glass, gave it a quick lick, then scooped into the mound of curry rice and brought it to my mouth.
A rich, spicy flavor spread across my tongue.
—Yeah… it’s Bon Curry. The original one, too.
“This is Bon Curry, isn’t it? The original kind…” I said.
“Oh? The one in the yellow package? You can tell? Impressive,” Takizawa-san said, sounding genuinely impressed.
“I thought it might be retort curry too, but I can’t believe you can tell that much,” Mari-senpai added.
“Well, I kind of get it too, but not down to that level…” Yohei said, tilting his head.
“Even if you say ‘original,’ can you really tell how it’s different from what they make now?” Yabe-san asked with interest.
“Yeah. The original has a distinct character—you can tell it apart from the others. These days, though, they’ve become more similar… But I’ve heard that the old version, the kind that came in aluminum pouches you had to heat in hot water, was even better. My dad talks about it a lot…” I explained.
“Knowing what kind of curry it is doesn’t fill your stomach any more, you know. Would be nice if you paid that much attention at work too,” Tsuruoka-san said.
“Uh… yeah…” I replied, unsure how to respond.
“Do you really need to know that much about food?” Yohei chimed in with a laugh.
“But if you can taste the difference, isn’t that fine?” Yabe-san said calmly.
“Curry all tastes pretty much the same anyway, doesn’t it?” Tsuruoka-san added.
“Come on, Tsuru-san—that’s the fun part. That’s why things like sake tasting and wine sommeliers exist,” Takizawa-san countered.
“There’s nothing wrong with having knowledge,” Mari-senpai said.
Listening to the exchange, I found myself almost laughing.
The rhythm of Tsuruoka-san’s teasing remarks and the way Yabe-san and Takizawa-san fired back felt oddly pleasant.
It was the kind of back-and-forth you’d never see at work, and somehow, it made the space feel just a little warmer.

Come to think of it, why does food taste so much better in the mountains?
It’s just rice with Bon Curry poured over it, yet it tastes unbelievably good.
As I lifted another spoonful to my mouth, I suddenly thought of my father.
He used to say it all the time:
“Retort curry? It’s gotta be the original Bon Curry.
The old aluminum pouch ones were even better than the original they sell now.
If you’re making curry with roux, it’s House Vermont Curry.
And if you’re going from powder and flour, then S&B curry powder—no contest.”
His preferences didn’t stop at curry.
Instant ramen had to be Charumera, instant yakisoba was Arabian Yakisoba, cup ramen was Nissin Cup Noodles, and cup yakisoba was Peyang—
One after another, his standards for taste came drifting back to me.
Grandpa taught me a lot about how the world works, but from my father… I guess all I really picked up were things about food, Western rock music, and old celebrities.
…Now that I think about it, I haven’t had Kiyoken’s shumai bento since moving to Nagoya…
When I called a friend back in Yokohama the other day, he said the shumai bento that used to be 600 yen is now 1,070…
…I liked it because it was cheap and good, but once it goes over a thousand yen, I probably wouldn’t go all the way to Yokohama just to buy one…
Maybe if I had some other reason to be there… but still.

Before I knew it, the warmth and aroma of the curry pulled me back into the reality of the dining hall.
When I lifted my gaze, everyone was quietly eating, focused on their plates.
My father’s voice was gone.
And yet, the memory of those flavors remained, firmly etched into both my tongue and my heart.

Clack… rattle…
The windows trembled.
It seemed the wind had picked up.
“Hey, Yabe-chi, how did you get so friendly with that woman all of a sudden?” Mari-senpai asked, sinking her spoon into her curry.
“Oh, Uchida-san?” Yabe-san said.
—Wait, you even got her name?
I thought.
“Her name’s Ayako Uchida. Apparently she’s a doctor at a hospital. I heard a noise outside my room and thought maybe everyone was coming back, so I opened the door—and there she was. We started chatting, and she said, ‘Looks like a typhoon’s coming.’ So I told her, ‘Well, worst case, I’ll just stay another night.’”
Yabe-san said this with a slightly bashful smile, then took a sip of water.
“Then she asked about my plans. I told her I was thinking of heading over toward Yarionsen, and she said she was planning to go down to Tsugaike. After that, we got into talking about the hut at Hakuba Oike and different routes.”
“Makes sense. Talking about the mountains is an easy way to hit it off,” Takizawa-san said, nodding as he scooped up a piece of meat with his spoon.
In that instant, a flash of lightning lit up outside the window.
For a brief moment, the reflection of the dining hall on the glass vanished, replaced by a brightness like daylight.
In that afterimage, I caught a glimpse of orange and yellow tents outside, warped and bent under the force of the wind.

Tsuruoka-san lowered his voice and spoke to Takizawa-san.
“Hey, Takky, about that thing—you said you were gonna contact a professor at Nagoya University, right? That professor… can we really trust him? He’s not one of those types who just spout theories from behind a desk, is he?”
Takizawa-san set his spoon down and narrowed his eyes slightly, as if thinking.
“No, he’s solid. He’s very field-oriented, and students like him too. He’s well-known nationwide for his research on insect ecology. He actually goes into the mountains himself to collect specimens—he’s not the kind of academic who just sits at a desk.”
“Huh… I see.”
Tsuruoka-san replied, still looking only half convinced as he took another bite of curry.
Rumble… rumble…
Thunder rolled outside.
Clatter… rattle…
The windows shook in the wind.
—Yeah… the typhoon really is getting closer.

Mari-senpai lowered her voice and leaned slightly toward Yabe-san as she spoke.
“Hey, Yabe-chi… when you were coming down the stairs, you said ‘that guy’s a bit off’… were you talking about him?”
She glanced sideways, subtly indicating Tsuruoka-san.
Yabe-san’s lips curled just a little, and she nodded without a word.
“Hehe… figures,” Mari-senpai chuckled under her breath.
—They’re really gossiping about Tsuruoka-san right in front of him…
I could never do that…
I thought.

“I don’t trust people who’ve got fancy titles but nothing to back ’em up. If it’s someone who actually gets out there and does the work, then maybe they’re worth listening to,” Tsuruoka-san said to Takizawa-san.
Takizawa-san smiled and nodded.
“That sounds like you. But don’t worry—he may be a bit eccentric, but he’s got real results to show for it. I’ll vouch for him.”

Yabe-san continued casually, moving her spoon as she spoke.
“She said she’d run out of the lounge, so I told her, ‘That’s probably just how he is.’”
Tsuruoka-san’s eyebrow twitched.
“Hey… you’re not talking about me right now, are you?”
He frowned, sounding genuinely puzzled.
“Who knows,” Yabe-san replied flatly.
Mari-senpai then smirked and added,
“Tsuruoka-san, you go barging straight into people’s personal space—that’s why you throw them off.”
“What was that!? I’m just trying to liven things up! I am being considerate, you know!” Tsuruoka-san shot back, raising his voice—though the tips of his ears had turned faintly red.
“Haha… well, in the end, didn’t things work out pretty well between them? People from the city are really calm, huh. If it were me, I’d honestly be a bit freaked out,” Yohei said with a grin, shoveling rice into his mouth.
Maybe he was trying to smooth things over, but I couldn’t quite follow what he meant.
If he was talking about that doctor, they hadn’t exactly hit it off… and I had no idea who he meant by “people from the city,” or what was supposed to be scary.
I couldn’t really tell what Yohei was getting at.

I finished my curry in no time, set my spoon down, and felt a slight sense of something missing.
Curry is a strange thing.
You get so absorbed in eating it that before you know it, your plate is already empty.
When I glanced to the side, Mari-senpai and Yabe-san were still eating at their own pace.
Trying to fill the gap, I casually looked around the dining hall.
At the tables here and there sat people staying at the lodge.
Along the wall, the two “mountain girls” who had come down the stairs during check-in and were in the lounge earlier were chatting brightly.
The one with her hair tied back, wearing a pink cardigan, leaned forward in her colorful mountain-patterned leggings, eyes sparkling as she said, “Isn’t this view, like, insanely amazing?”
Beside her, the other girl smiled lazily and replied, “Yeah… but we should probably save our energy,” glancing down at her phone as she spoke at an easy pace.
At the table next to them sat a middle-aged man and woman—the pair with that slightly questionable air I’d noticed when we went to drop off our luggage.
The woman, probably in her early fifties, was lively and cheerful, her red softshell jacket adding to her energetic presence.
The man, around the same age, had a calm demeanor and gently supported her.
Their easy banter added a quiet warmth to the space around them.
At a table on the far side sat the woman in her thirties—the doctor who had grown friendly with Yabe-san.
She wore a calm, deep green Chinese-collar-style top with a beige jacket and dark gray slacks.
She didn’t speak much, but the gentle way she interacted with those around her gave off a quiet sense of reassurance.
Near the window sat the older man in his sixties, the one who had walked toward the wash area earlier during check-in.
With short, graying hair and sharp eyes, he carried a calmness that suggested long experience in the mountains.
Now and then, he looked out the window, a faint smile crossing his face.
Finally, near the two young women, sat the pair of office-worker-looking men in their thirties who had interrupted us back when we were spreading that thing out in the dirt-floored area.
One wore glasses, a navy windbreaker, and jeans.
The other, more athletic-looking, wore a red fleece vest, his build solid—his tone bold, though his voice carried the slightest tremor.
Their light, back-and-forth exchange added a touch of ease to the dining hall.
Were they sitting near the young women on purpose, hoping for something?
I wondered as I set my spoon down and absentmindedly watched them.
Everyone was spending their time here at their own pace, enjoying the mountain in their own way.
The warmth of the dining hall and the lingering taste of curry in my mouth made me feel just a little at ease.
Watching the people gathered in this lodge, even the slight dissatisfaction from my too-quickly-finished meal began to fade.
“Shall we go?”
“Yeah, let’s.”
Hearing the voices, I looked over to see the two young women from the lounge standing up from their seats.
They gathered their dishes and headed toward the counter.
At the neighboring table, the two office-worker men stood up at the same time.
—Yeah… maybe they were paying attention to those two after all.
I thought.
The one with glasses hurriedly gathered his dishes, muttering under his breath, “H-hey, don’t… this might be bad…”
The athletic one laughed it off, “Idiot, don’t worry about it,” as he carried his dishes toward the counter.
Though his voice sounded confident, there was just a trace of fear in the glance he shot at his friend.
Watching them move about, I found myself observing how everyone in the dining hall acted at their own pace.
The two young women moved lightly, smiling as they went.
The two office workers, trading small jabs, still looked out for each other as they returned their dishes.
Seeing it all, I felt a quiet sense of relief.
…I see. There really are all kinds of people in this lodge.

Bang! The window rattled violently in the wind, and the sudden noise made me flinch.
The wind seemed to be getting stronger.
When I listened closely, I thought I could hear a faint rushing sound.
Maybe it had started to rain.

“Ahh, that was good,” Mari-senpai said, glancing sideways at Yabe-san as she set her spoon down. Yabe-san gave a small smile in return.
“…The original Bon Curry… maybe I’ll try buying some next time,” Mari-senpai said to me.
“If you search ‘original Bon Curry’ on Amazon or something, it’ll come right up. My dad even buys the 30-pack boxes,” I said, letting out a small laugh, hoping to get a reaction.
“Hahaha, well, no wonder you can tell it’s the original in one bite,” Takizawa-san said with a laugh, and the others joined in.
That made me feel quietly relieved.
“I could probably recognize Arabian Yakisoba too,” I added, getting a bit carried away.
“That another 30-pack?” Mari-senpai asked with a grin.
“Yeah, he buys those six packs of five servings each. And I might be able to tell Peyang too…” I said, riding the momentum.
“Hey, with Peyang you can tell just by looking at the cup,” Yohei shot back.
“Oh—right, I guess so. Didn’t think of that,” I said, and everyone laughed again, just like I’d hoped.
“Bon Curry, you know, used to come in silver packaging back in the day,” Tsuruoka-san suddenly said, still chewing.
“You just said that earlier, Sakuma-kun,” Yabe-san replied dryly, setting her spoon down and narrowing her eyes slightly.
For a moment, laughter spread through the dining hall, and Mari-senpai let out a soft chuckle, her shoulders shaking.
Tsuruoka-san waved a hand, smiling a little sheepishly.
“No, no, I just said it to get a laugh, you know—to liven things up.”
“Oh, really?” Yohei said, half exasperated but still grinning.
“You little—” Tsuruoka-san pretended to raise his fist.
At that, I couldn’t help but burst out laughing, and the room naturally filled with warm laughter.
Tsuruoka-san’s slightly offbeat remarks somehow eased everyone’s mood.
A flash of lightning—then a few seconds later, a low, distant rumble.

“Alright then, shall we get going?” Seeing that Mari-senpai and Yabe-san had finished eating, Takizawa-san stood up and glanced around the room.
Tsuruoka-san and Yohei also rose reluctantly, preparing to return their dishes to the counter.
As we headed toward the counter, the faces of the other guests came into view.
The middle-aged man and woman were tidying up their meal with light laughter.
Matching the woman’s lively energy, the man quietly supported her, helping here and there.
The female doctor sat alone, calmly setting down her spoon, casually listening to the surrounding chatter.
By the window, the elderly man watched the room from a slight distance, a faint smile on his face.
As I returned my tray, I thought to myself, There really are all kinds of people staying in this lodge…
Watching everyone spend their time in their own way, I felt a quiet warmth—something that filled me even more than the curry had.

I was still savoring the aftertaste of the original Bon Curry, but I picked up my tray with the empty plate and headed toward the counter.
When I got there, the same three from earlier—the girl who looked like Nagano Mei, the guy in the black Adidas tracksuit, and Kobayashi Ren, the mountain photographer—were working briskly behind it.
They took the dishes from the return slot, sorted and discarded the leftovers, quickly rinsed everything, and placed them into metal racks.
The whole process was so smooth and efficient it was almost satisfying to watch, their coordinated movements making me think, They really know what they’re doing.
Then, as I lowered my gaze, I noticed something that felt slightly different from the warm atmosphere before.
At the back of the kitchen area, among the group handling the dishes, stood the lodge owner.
He had short white hair, unshaven stubble, and a mole beside his nose.
With his solid build and stern expression, he seemed intimidating—even though he was just silently observing, there was a certain pressure in the air around him.
As I gently slid my used plate onto the return shelf, I found myself thinking,
Whoa… he looks kind of scary. Is he angry? Or is that just his natural expression…?
But at the same time, another thought began to grow.
Still… that Bon Curry was really good… If I tell him “It was delicious,” would that scary face soften into a smile? Or would he just ignore me…?
Driven by that small curiosity, I gathered my courage and spoke up.
“The original Bon Curry was delicious!”
Despite his intimidating look, the owner’s eyes lit up, and he leaned forward with a proud grin.
“You can tell? Bon Curry’s a must in a mountain lodge!”
I had braced myself, wondering if it was okay to talk to him—but that worry vanished instantly.
“The old aluminum pouch ones had a much richer flavor than what they make now!” he said enthusiastically.
“My father says the same thing,” I replied, and the owner leaned in even more, continuing eagerly.
“The aluminum pouch ones, you just dropped them in hot water and the aroma would burst out. The ones now are more convenient, sure, but the smell’s just not the same…”
“But the current original Bon Curry still smells really good,” I said.
The owner nodded with a broad smile.
“Exactly, exactly! That’s the heart of the original. And the thickness of the roux, too. Back then it was richer—perfect when mixed with rice.”
Someone who had seemed so stern and unapproachable at first was now talking so cheerfully…
I couldn’t help but smile.
“Wow, you remember all those details. That’s impressive,” I said.
He waved his hand lightly.
“Details matter. Curry in the mountains—every moment counts.”
I nodded.
“Yeah, I think it tastes even better because we’re up here. The same curry at home wouldn’t feel this special.”
“That’s right! That’s the charm of a mountain lodge!” he said, smiling as he met my eyes.
Well… maybe not that much, I thought, but still, sharing that moment over curry felt like something uniquely special to this place.
With a grin, the owner added proudly,
“We make hamburg steak and omelet rice too, but Bon Curry’s the one you can’t skip!”
Despite his first impression, the more we talked, the more enjoyable he became.
I felt quietly happy that, through something as simple as Bon Curry, I had connected with him.
As I was enjoying our little Bon Curry exchange, Tsuruoka-san’s voice suddenly cut in from the side.
“What’s this, Sho? Buttering up the lodge owner so you can move in here?”
“Maybe I will,” I said with a laugh.
Not that I really meant it…
“Oh, anyone who loves original Bon Curry is welcome here. …I’ll feed it to you every night,” the owner said, laughing.
“Every night might be a bit much,” I replied, and he burst out laughing.
The stern expression he had earlier was completely gone.
Maybe he’d just been tense before, making sure nothing went wrong—I found myself thinking that as I looked at him.
