Exploring the Lodge

A brief silence settled over the room, until Yohei suddenly looked up and said, “Hey… why don’t we take a look around the lodge while we’re at it?”
With his shirt halfway over his head as he was changing, Takizawa-san replied, “Oh, yeah—that sounds good.”
“Hmm… might as well have a look around,” Tsuruoka-san added, giving a somewhat uninterested nod.
And just like that, we started getting ready to head out and explore the lodge.
Takizawa-san was still in the middle of changing, Yohei grabbed only a small pouch and stayed light, and Tsuruoka-san and I straightened our clothes before stepping out of the room.

We walked over to the Seppō room across the hall, and Takizawa-san knocked on the sliding door.
Knock, knock…
“Yes…?” came Mari-senpai’s voice from inside.
“We’re thinking of taking a quick look around the lodge—what do you want to do?” Takizawa-san asked.
From inside, Mari-senpai’s voice came back, a little hurried.
“Yabecchi, what do you think?”
“…I think I’ll just stay here…” Yabe-san replied.
“Oh, yeah? Then I’ll go take a look,” Mari-senpai said.
“Okay,” came Yabe-san’s quiet answer.
“Hang on a second. I’ll call you when I’m ready, so wait in your room…”
We heard footsteps approaching the door as Mari-senpai spoke.
We headed back to our room for the moment.

Back in the room, the four of us fell into idle, aimless conversation.
At one point, Takizawa-san seemed to remember what we had left behind earlier, and with a grin, he said to Tsuruoka-san,
“Hey, Tsuru-san… you think that thing’s gonna get lonely, being left there like that?”
“There ain’t no way we could’ve just kept it here,” Tsuruoka-san replied with a wry smile, and Yohei immediately jumped in,
“Then why not just sleep with it? Hold it close all night.”
Laughter broke out in the room.
He can joke around with Tsuruoka-san like that, huh…
There was no way I could pull that off.
“Hey, you’re pushing it,”
Tsuruoka-san shot him a glare, and Yohei waved both hands dramatically.
“Alright, alright, my bad, my bad—I’ll shut up!”
More laughter followed, and the tension in the room eased a little.
Just then, a light knock echoed from the hallway.
Knock, knock.
“I’m ready,” came Mari-senpai’s voice as the door slid open.
She was wearing a green-and-blue checkered wool shirt.
She still had on her beige trekking pants.
So she changed… I thought.

We stepped out into the hallway again and, together with Mari-senpai, started back the way we had come.
Straight ahead, we could see the communal sleeping area on the opposite side, with rows of pillars standing in place.
There was no ceiling—just exposed beams and the underside of the roof visible above.
We reached the corridor with the stairs, turned right, walked to the end, then doubled back to the left and headed down the stairs.
Our footsteps echoed against the wooden floor.

When we reached the first floor, we arrived at the crossroads where hallways stretched out in all directions.
We headed right first.
On the left, nearest the corner, was a small shop booth—through the gap between the pillar and the back wall, we caught a glimpse of drinks, packaged trail snacks, hand towels, and souvenir badges lined up on display.
Further in, a cream-colored door marked with a white rectangular sign reading Staff Only stood on the wall, serving as the shop’s entrance.
Just beyond that was a wooden sliding door with its upper half made of latticework set with clear glass, and further along the wall, a row of matching windows allowed a view inside.
We moved in that direction.
Through the windows on the left, we could see several long rectangular tables arranged neatly in rows—it looked like a dining hall.
Inside, guests were already seated, some holding steaming cups, others chatting casually.
The space was large, with pillars standing throughout.
The wooden tables and chairs were well-worn, their edges smoothed and polished with age, giving off a warmth that felt fitting for a mountain lodge.
Looking further in, there was another sliding door of the same design on the far side of the dining hall, and just before the very end, a plain wooden sliding door without windows—likely leading to the kitchen.
It was still too early for dinner preparations, but the faint clatter of dishes could already be heard.
On the right were staff rooms—the doors firmly shut, not a sound coming from within.
At the very end stood a gray metal door, stark and cold in its industrial sheen, with a sign that read: Power Room – Authorized Personnel Only.
“Huh…”
We all took a brief look around, then turned back.

From the opposite direction, the girl who worked at the lodge—the one called Aya-chan, who looked a bit like Nagano Mei—and the young man in the black Adidas tracksuit, the one who had come with the owner when we opened the black bundle earlier, walked toward us as they talked.

“Hey… but seriously,”
the guy in the black tracksuit was saying,
“is this really because of global warming?”

“I saw this on YouTube,” he went on,
“some people were saying that stronger typhoons are just part of a natural cycle that’s always existed.”

Tilting her head slightly, Aya-san replied,
“Yeah… there are a lot of videos like that.
But a lot of them mix up ‘what it feels like’ with ‘what the data actually shows.’”

Right… she did say she studies environmental science at university… I thought.

“Data?”

“Yeah. When you line up records collected by different, unrelated observation groups,” she said,
“things like typhoon intensity, rainfall, and duration—
they’re all shifting in the same direction compared to the past.”

“But still, some people say the increase in CO₂ is just from natural causes too, right?”

“You can’t say nature has zero influence,” she answered calmly.
“But when data gathered from different places, using different methods, all show the same trend…”

As we walked down the hallway,
I found myself recalling things I’d seen on the news.

“Temperatures are rising. The oceans are warming too.
That heat becomes energy for storms,” Aya-san said.

“…Or maybe it’s just one of those periods, you know?” the tracksuit guy muttered.

“To dismiss it as ‘just a coincidence’ or ‘your imagination’…”
she said,
“the numbers line up a little too perfectly for that.”

Her voice was quiet, but firm.

We didn’t stop walking,
and tried not to make too much noise as we passed by them.

We make things we don’t need,
throw them away almost immediately,
and then make more again.
The heat and gases released by that cycle
are slowly, but surely, throwing this planet out of balance.

Thinking that, I unconsciously slowed my pace.

“Deserts spread,
sea levels rise,
forests burn,
and typhoons grow stronger,” Aya-san’s voice continued from behind us.

“…Isn’t that more like… an ideology or something?” the tracksuit guy asked.

“No,” she said.
“It’s not about anyone’s beliefs or position.
It’s just what the numbers are showing.”

At that moment, thunder rumbled in the distance.

Rumble… rumble…

We glanced at each other.

“…Honestly,
this typhoon right now is more convincing than any video.”

We heard the tracksuit guy mutter quietly.

Aya-san seemed to smile just a little.

“Right?”

We said nothing as we passed them by.

And yet,
for some reason,
their conversation lingered in the back of my mind.

We went straight through the crossroads this time.
On the left was a Japanese-style lounge, the scent of tatami mingling with the smell of hot water from a kettle.
Inside, a woman sat alone, relaxing quietly.
As we moved down the corridor, there were rooms on both sides.
Neither had doors at the entrance; instead, wooden lattice frames stood on either side, loosely separating the rooms from the hallway.
The room on the left was Japanese-style, while the one on the right appeared to be Western-style.
Above each entrance, a white rectangular panel displayed the same label: Lounge.
The room on the left had a raised tatami floor, while the one on the right was neatly arranged with tables and chairs.
Further along, on the left, there was a windowless wooden sliding door secured with a padlock, a sign above it reading Storage.
The crude metal lock only stirred the imagination of what might be inside.
On the right, we slid open a lattice-framed door fitted with frosted glass.
It was the same place where the three young men had been doing that “I Can Do It” skit earlier.
The sizzling sound of oil—juu~—crackled in the air, along with the faint smell of garlic.
Peeking inside, I saw a boy, probably in elementary school, and a man who looked like his father, gathered around a frying pan set on a camping stove.
Just beyond the sliding door was a step down to a wooden platform, and further ahead, a concrete floor with a tall wooden table placed on it.
Along the left and right walls facing outside were simple wooden benches.
On the wall hung a faded poster of Mount Shirouma, its edges torn with age.
The father and son stood around the pan on the center table.
“Hey, don’t let it burn—keep stirring.”
The father steadied the pan while guiding the boy’s hands with the chopsticks.
“Aye aye! Chef coming through!”
The boy raised his voice theatrically, pretending to toss the pan with exaggerated flair.
“Hey, knock it off. If the oil splatters, it’s dangerous.”
The father frowned slightly and gently restrained the boy’s hand.
“Ehh? But that’s how they do it on TV!”
“TV is TV. We’re using fire in the mountains—be careful.”
The boy pouted, but began stirring the vegetables properly as told.
I couldn’t help but let out a small chuckle.
…This is kind of nice, I thought to myself, glancing at Mari-senpai.
She met my eyes and whispered with a faint smile,
“They seem close.”
We quietly slid the door shut.

“Oh—hey, it says Old Wing over here!”
Yohei pointed it out.
A weathered wooden plaque bore the simple black characters: Old Wing.
What he pointed to was above the sliding door at the very end of the corridor.
The door was wooden, with a lattice-framed glass panel at the top—just like the entrance to the lodge.
Takizawa-san frowned slightly. “Old wing, huh… wanna check it out?”
Tsuruoka shrugged. “Well, I’m curious. Might as well.”
“Alright… let’s go.”
I said it, aware of a faint uneasiness—but my curiosity was winning.
Takizawa-san stepped forward and pulled the door open.
It slid aside with a faint creak.
Beyond it, a short connecting passage appeared.
It was extremely simple—a dim space covered by a corrugated metal roof slanting downward to the right, nailed onto exposed rafters.
The walls on both sides were wooden planks, with gaps here and there where cold outside air slipped through.
Since the floor of the old wing sat lower, the passage sloped downward in a narrow wooden ramp.
At the far end stood a pair of double doors—darkened wood, lattice-framed, with glass panels set into them.
No lights seemed to be on inside.
The glass reflected the five of us, faint and distorted.
The iron fittings on the doors caught the dim light, glinting dully—
as if waiting for someone to step inside.
“…No lights on. You think it’s okay?” Takizawa-san whispered.
“…Won’t we get in trouble if we just go in?”
I said, remembering how that receptionist—who happened to share my name—had scolded me earlier just for leaving the entrance door open.
“Seriously? You’re worrying about that now?”
Yohei gave my back a light smack.
“More scared of the caretaker than ghosts?”
“If you’re scared, you can wait here, you know.” Tsuruoka-san added.
“I’m not scared or anything… I just don’t want to get yelled at again.” I muttered.
—No one’s turning back… guess I’ve got no choice but to go.
Besides… I do want to see what’s inside.
And I’d hate being the only one who didn’t.
We stepped onto the narrow wooden ramp with its slight drop.
“Oh… it’s not much of a height, but it’s steeper than it looks,” Takizawa-san said.
Tsuruoka-san carefully checked his footing and moved ahead.
I followed behind.
The wooden boards sank ever so slightly underfoot, heavy with moisture.
Cold air slipped through the gaps in the walls, rattling the metal with a faint clatter… clatter…
“Man… stuff like this kinda gives me the chills,” Yohei muttered with a half-laugh.
Without thinking, I reached for a thin wooden rail and hunched my shoulders slightly as I moved forward.
It was barely fifty centimeters long, but every time I glimpsed the space below through the gaps in the boards, a cold shiver ran through me.
“Whoa—my face looks messed up in this reflection! Scarier than a ghost!”
Yohei joked, staring at his warped reflection in the glass.
—Yeah… mine looks distorted too. Kind of eerie.
“Geez, Yohei… honestly, it is kind of creepy,” Mari-senpai said, letting out a small laugh.
“Huh? Creepy? You mean me?” Yohei shot back.
“Yep,” she said, smiling.
“Come on…” Yohei laughed.
“Just kidding. I meant… the room beyond.”
Mari-senpai said quietly.
Takizawa-san reached for the door to the old wing.
The iron fittings creaked faintly as the double doors slowly—
Giii…—swung open.
In that instant, our reflections on the glass vanished,
replaced by the light from our side spilling into the darkness within.
The interior began to emerge faintly under the light,
but beyond a few steps ahead, everything sank into shadow.
There was no telling what lay deeper inside.
The air of the old wing was thick—
a blend of damp wood soaked over many years,
the slightly musty scent of tatami and fabric,
dust-laced earth,
and somewhere beneath it all, a faint lingering trace of old kerosene heaters.
It was a smell that felt oddly nostalgic… yet heavy.
When I glanced back, I noticed along the middle height of the door,
several narrow metal plates bent into U-shapes, mounted with their openings facing upward.
Leaning nearby were square wooden bars—
it seemed they could be slotted into those metal fittings to lock the doors shut from the inside.
—Places like this usually have a switch right by the entrance…
Is the power even connected out here?
Thinking that, I reached toward the wall beside the door,
tracing it with my fingertips.
Amid the rough texture of the wood, I felt four small square protrusions.
“…Found them.”
Holding my breath slightly, I pressed them one by one.
Click. Click. Click. Click.
The dry sounds echoed—
and the next moment, a bare bulb overhead flickered to life,
casting a dim glow over the aged corridor and its pillars.
Looking straight ahead from the entrance,
there were communal sleeping areas on both the left and right.
Turning left,
a central passage stretched along the length of the building,
with three-tiered sleeping platforms lining both sides.
At the edges of each tier, square pillars stood in rows,
firmly supporting the tatami-covered levels.
At the far end of the central passage,
a window let in a faint trace of light into the dim interior.
At both the front and back ends of the sleeping sections,
wooden ladders led up to the upper tiers.
Their worn surfaces spoke quietly of the building’s long history.
The sleeping area on the right side mirrored the same structure.
Light filtering through the windows created mottled patterns across the tatami,
and ladders were fixed at both the near and far ends.
The floorboards of the corridor sagged slightly under the weight of moisture,
and together with the thick pillars and beams,
they carried the heavy, familiar atmosphere unique to old wooden buildings.
Beyond the sleeping areas, the view was obscured by their shadows,
but the narrowing perspective hinted that the path split further ahead to the left and right.
Then I looked straight ahead.
There, several tall sheets of plywood—nearly two meters high—
stood arranged like a wall, completely blocking the way.
Some leaned at odd angles,
others overlapped,
gaps opened between them here and there—
all of it hastily, unevenly set up.
Yellow-and-black hazard rope was wrapped around them again and again,
as if to firmly reject anyone trying to go further.
And at the center—right where your eyes would land—
a sheet of paper was pasted on,
large red letters scrawled across it.
—KEEP OUT.
The words stood out in the stillness of the old wing,
raw, almost alive—
pressing a quiet, relentless warning against us.
When we reached the dead end blocked by the plywood,
I noticed, on the far right—partially hidden behind the sleeping area—
a sliding door set into the wall.
The upper portion had a lattice with glass panels,
but the entire exterior side had been covered over with plywood nailed from the outside,
shutting out any light.
—Ah… this place…
So this is the entrance to the old wing.
The door we passed by earlier.
I murmured to myself.
“So it’s sealed off beyond here. Guess they don’t use anything past this point anymore,” Takizawa-san said with a slight shrug.
“Well, that’s obvious. The moment you see plywood stacked up and tied off like this, it’s clear they’ve got no intention of letting anyone through,” Tsuruoka-san snorted, arms crossed.
“I wonder what it’s like beyond here…” I said.
“Wanna take a peek?”
Takizawa-san stepped closer to the plywood barrier and gently slid one panel aside.
A narrow gap opened—just enough to look through.
Beyond it stretched a dim corridor.
Along the left wall, windows stood at regular intervals,
faintly reflecting the presence of the outside world.
Through those windows, I could see an empty, open space—
nothing inside, completely bare.
From its size, it looked like it had once been a dining hall.
On the right side, a row of wooden doors lined the corridor,
all shut tight.
There was no way to tell what kind of rooms lay behind them.
“Man… this really makes you want to explore,” Yohei said, clearly tempted to go further.
“Hey, come on. It literally says Keep Out.
If we go any further, we’re definitely getting yelled at by the manager.”
I said quickly, half-worried he might actually suggest going in.
“Hehe… you’re right.”
Mari-senpai smiled softly.
“Let’s head back. …Still, it had a strange atmosphere, didn’t it?”
