Drying Room

Just as the commotion in the drying room began to settle, Tsuruoka-san suddenly frowned.
“If stuff like this can happen, maybe someone ought to keep an eye on it.”
A brief silence fell over the room.
Unspoken, everyone’s gaze shifted—who would take the watch?
I darted my eyes around, trying not to be singled out.
—I’ve got a bad feeling about this… He’s gonna tell me to do it… I just know it.
“Sho. You keep watch.” Tsuruoka-san said.
—Of course…!
I flinched instinctively.
Keep watch? Me…? There are plenty of other people here.
“M-me…?” I said, unable to hide the protest in my voice.
I looked at Yohei, silently pleading—but he quickly averted his eyes.
“You found it, didn’t you? …Then you’re the one who watches it.” Tsuruoka-san said.
—I found it? Says the guy who went and named it “Shirouma Tomoakinosu” after himself…
“Yeah. You found it, so you watch it,” Yabe-san added, arms crossed, her tone cool.
—I thought she’d be on my side… she’s always pushing back against him…
It felt like something clenched tight deep in my chest.
And because what she said made perfect sense, I couldn’t argue back.
“That’s not fair—leaving it all to Saku-chan,” Mari-senpai spoke up immediately.
Mari-senpai is always kind to me…
“Then Mari, you wanna take watch?” Tsuruoka-san said.
“W-why do I have to do it?!” Mari-senpai snapped, frowning.
—If even Mari-senpai’s getting this mad… I guess it really does come down to me… I thought.
As sparks began to fly between Tsuruoka-san and Mari-senpai, Takizawa-san let out a heavy sigh.
“Alright, how about this. We do shifts. Sho takes the first hour, then someone relieves him. Fair enough?” Takizawa-san proposed.
There was a quiet authority in his voice—the kind that naturally pulled people into line.
“I’ll have someone take over later. For now, you keep watch,” Tsuruoka-san said with a short nod, turning his gaze back to me.
The urge to protest rose up to my throat.
But the atmosphere in the room wouldn’t allow it.
—It’s just one hour. If I tough it out for an hour, I can relax after that.
It’s not like I have to do anything—just sit there, maybe listen to some music on my phone. Better to get the annoying part over with first… I told myself.
“…Fine. I’ll do it. I don’t really have a choice. But make sure someone takes over after an hour,” I said.
I’d actually thought someone should be watching it from the start—but I kept quiet because I didn’t want to be the one told to do it… and now here I was anyway.
…Whenever I try to avoid something I don’t like, it always ends up chasing me down…
The world just isn’t made to go your way.

As a small act of resistance, I tried to set a condition.
“It looks pretty hot in there… mind if I go change first?”
In my head, I’d already decided—if they said no, I was definitely refusing.
“Yeah, sure. Go change, then come back and keep watch,” Tsuruoka-san said without hesitation.
“We’ll keep an eye on it here. Just hurry up and get changed,” he added.
—…Seriously, if that’s the case, why don’t you all just keep watching it together?
Honestly, he just doesn’t want anyone else seeing it because he wants to make a name for himself…
If it ever does become famous, I’m making sure it’s called Shirouma Shonosu, not Shirouma Tomoakinosu…
Grumbling to myself, I gave a small shrug and started heading back to the room.
“Why don’t you take your time a bit?” Mari-senpai whispered to me.
Even when Tsuruoka says things that get on my nerves, I can put up with it because Mari-senpai’s here.
I felt a faint warmth spread in my chest.
Even so, while her words eased me a little, that stubborn resentment was still smoldering inside me.
—Hmph… if everyone’s watching it anyway, they might as well stay there. It’s not like I need to be the one stuck there alone, sweating over it…

I walked along the passage between the washroom and the lodge, my slippers clattering softly against the wooden slats.
Left in front of the drying room were Tsuruoka-san, Mari-senpai, Takizawa-san, Yohei, and Yabe-san.
For a split second, everything lit up as if it were daytime.
A moment later, thunder rumbled across the sky.
Rattle… I slid open the lodge door and stepped inside.
Rattle… As I shut it behind me, the sound of the rain faded to almost nothing.
I turned back toward the reception, walked a few steps, turned right, climbed the stairs, turned right again at the top, then left, sliding open the door to the Hinode room and stepping inside before closing it behind me.
Inside, I took off my sweat-soaked fleece, opened my pack, and pulled out a pair of shorts from a stuff sack, changing out of my black trekking pants into beige ones.
As I checked my reflection in the mirror, I slowly tried to steel myself for what lay ahead.
But deep in my chest, a lump of unease remained.
—It’s fine. This is just keeping watch. If someone comes, I just stop them… But is it really that simple?
Mari-senpai had told me to take my time, but I couldn’t relax at all. The moment I finished changing, I rushed out of the room, turned right into the corridor, looped left, went down the stairs, turned left again, and headed down the hallway. Sliding the door open with a rattle, I stepped outside.
There they were—Takizawa-san, Tsuruoka-san, Mari-senpai, Yabe-san, and Yohei—leaning against the pillars that supported the plastic corrugated roof over the passage, waiting without really talking.
The rain drummed steadily against the plastic roofing.
“Oh, there he is,” Tsuruoka-san said, pushing himself off the pillar as he noticed me.
His words were brief, but there was a faint pressure in his gaze—we’re counting on you.
Mari-senpai gave a small smile, folding her arms. “Well… I’m counting on you,” she said softly.
Her tone was gentle, as if she were trying to ease my tension.
Takizawa-san let out a small sigh, shrugging his shoulders. “Alright then. I’ll leave it to you,” he said.
There was a hint of apology in his expression.
Yabe-san stood with her arms crossed, giving a slight shrug as she looked at me—but there was something in her expression that suggested she simply didn’t want to do it herself.
Yohei avoided my gaze, leaning silently against a pillar.
Like he wished he wasn’t even here.
I could feel that subtle tension in the air, and something tightened deep in my chest.
—None of them want to do this…
…If they don’t switch with me after an hour, I’m definitely going to complain.

My eyes drifted into the drying room, where the thing wrapped in the aluminum sheet lay silently on the table.
I took a small breath and straightened my shoulders, as if bracing myself.
—It’s fine. This is just a watch.
All I have to do is stop anyone who comes near… but is that really all there is to it?
The thought crossed my mind again.
Inside me, unease, tension, and a faint sense of responsibility tangled together.
Still, feeling everyone’s eyes on me, I gave a quiet nod.
“Honestly… this is such a pain. Just make sure you come back in an hour, alright?” I said.
“Well then, we’ll head back to the room. I’m leaving the rest to you,” Tsuruoka-san said shortly, still leaning against the pillar. His voice was calm, but there was a trace of pressure behind it.
Mari-senpai gave a small smile and, glancing over her shoulder, said gently, “Hang in there, Saku-chan. I’ll bring you some snacks later.” Her voice softened the tightness in my chest, even if only a little.
Takizawa-san let out a small sigh, shrugged, and said, “Sorry about this. I’m counting on you.”
His words were brief, but his sense of apology came through naturally.
Leaning against the pillar, Yohei glanced over at me and said, “I’ll come get you later, Sho.”
It sounded casual, but there was a hint that he hadn’t completely abandoned me.
Yabe-san, arms crossed, muttered a little awkwardly, “Well… see you later.”
That faint softness in her expression lingered in my chest.
I could only nod, taking in each of their words one by one.
One by one, they quietly headed back to their room.

The wind blowing in felt strangely warm—unpleasantly so.
Even with it this warm outside… I still had to stay inside that even hotter drying room.
With a rattling sound, I slid the door open and stepped inside.
It was warmer than outside—and it smelled worse.
Above me, the rain hammered loudly against the corrugated plastic roofing.
Drip… drip… the sound of water falling.
A faint trickling, like a small stream running somewhere nearby.
On the table, the thing wrapped in aluminum sheeting lay motionless.
—Alright… no choice. I just have to stop anyone who comes near… but is that really all there is to it?
Deep inside, unease, tension, and a faint sense of responsibility swirled together.
Still, all I could do now was trust myself and square my shoulders.
I turned back and slid the door shut with a clatter.
The sound of rain dulled, becoming muffled—quieter somehow.
The moment I realized I was completely alone in the heat and humidity of the drying room, a heavy weight slowly spread in my chest.
Earlier, the air had been filled with the dry scent of wood, the stale smell of half-dried clothes, and the damp odor of wet leather and rubber—mixed faintly with something like sewage.
But now, layered over it, there was something else… something faintly fishy, almost like the smell of the sea.
Could it be… coming from whatever was wrapped in that aluminum sheet, now that the ice had melted?
(…You’ve got to be kidding me. I’m supposed to just stand here and watch this?)
I muttered inwardly, though there was no one to hear it.
Letting my gaze wander around the room, I noticed a folding pipe chair with a brown vinyl cushion leaning against the wall.
Relieved, I walked over, carried it to the side opposite the table entrance, and unfolded it—the metal legs clicking dryly into place.
As I lowered myself onto the seat, the thin cushion sank under my weight.

Wooden walls. A wooden ceiling. Two sets of fluorescent lights—each holding two long tubes, about 40 watts—were mounted lengthwise across the ceiling.
The floor was concrete, covered with wooden slats typical of a drying room.
Beyond the table, on the far wall, there was a sliding wooden door with a lattice frame, its upper half fitted with clear glass.
To the right of the entrance, a wooden-framed window with a lattice pattern held another pane of glass.
The glass was fogged, glowing faintly with the yellow light from the corridor outside.
Beyond it was pitch darkness, and the sound of rain tapping against the glass rose and fell at irregular intervals.
To the right, most of the wall was taken up by a shoe rack, neatly lined with hiking boots and sneakers.
On the left side, ropes stretched between hooks fixed on both the near and far walls, the ropes themselves covered in clear vinyl and tinted a sturdy green.
Numerous hangers—some holding clothes, others empty—hung along them.
The hangers were a mismatched mix: wire hangers coated in white or blue vinyl, black and white plastic ones, and even a few wooden ones, all arranged in no particular order.
Some of the wire hangers had clusters of plastic clothespins dangling from them—pink, yellow, green, blue, white.
Clothes hung from the line: rainwear in bright pinks, blues, and yellows, wool shirts in reds and blacks, greens and blues, and sturdy beige parkas made of thick fabric.
In the wooden wall near the entrance, where the ropes were fixed, countless small holes dotted the surface.
They told a quiet story—hooks driven in, torn out under the weight of wet clothes, and hammered back in again, over and over.
In front of me stood a large table, and on it lay several pairs of mountaineering gloves, scattered without much order.
A deep green waterproof shell glove, a thin gray fleece inner glove, black hard-shell gloves, blue lightweight trekking gloves, red insulated overgloves, and a pair of worn brown leather gloves.
Each lay stacked one over the other, not yet opened or spread, making it hard to tell their true shape or feel.
Only the differences in color and material stood out.
Velcro straps at the wrists, rubberized grips, the slick surface of nylon, the soft lining of fleece.
And to my left, slightly ahead of me, the thing wrapped in aluminum sheeting lay motionless.
A faint, fishy, almost seawater-like odor drifted from it, cutting through the smell of wood and damp clothing.
The dim light seen through water-streaked glass, the orderly rows of shoes, the scars left by hooks in the wall, and the colors and shapes of the hanging clothes—
all of it seemed to quietly remind me that I was alone here, sealed inside this place.
Inside the drying room, I was left alone in a space thick with heat and humidity.
The sound of my own heartbeat echoed deep in my ears.
The thing wrapped in aluminum lay still on the table.
Plip…
A droplet fell.
Looking closer, I saw water seeping from the aluminum sheet, swelling into rounded beads by surface tension, slowly spreading toward the edge of the table.
As the amount increased, it began to spill—one drop at a time—over the edge.
For a brief instant, the outside flashed as bright as daytime.
The reflection of the room in the window vanished, replaced by a clear view beyond.
Through the glass, to the right side of the lodge wall, I could see a gray metal door.
To its left, a lattice window glowed faintly from within.
A moment later, a low rumble rolled across the sky—like a heavy roller grinding over the ground above.
And then again—
Plip…
Another drop fell from the edge of the table.

“Of course… it ends up being me…”
I muttered inwardly, but my gaze was already being drawn toward it.
The aluminum sheet resting over the frozen mass—its silver surface gleamed with a cold, unsettling sheen.
—Has the ice melted…? What does it look like inside?
Until now, I hadn’t been able to observe it closely through the ice, and in the chaos earlier, I barely got a proper look at whatever had been revealed beneath as it melted.
But now it was different.
No one else was here.
—Maybe… just a little peek?
A small resolve took root deep in my chest.
Remembering the cold touch of the aluminum, my hand moved almost on its own.
Before I could even put my thoughts into words, I was already imagining the moment I would lift the silver sheet in front of me.
The form of that thing that had been encased in ice.
Details I hadn’t noticed before.
Now was the time to see them.
Something stirred deep inside my chest.
Tension—and anticipation.
But both were steadily swallowed by a stronger urge: the need to know what was in front of me.
I slowly steadied my breath and reached out toward the aluminum sheet on the table.
As I pinched the edge and lifted it, it made a faint metallic whisper—shara…—and damp air clung to my face.
The sheet peeled back, catching the light for an instant—
and what lay beneath began to reveal itself.
From the gap I’d opened, the first thing that came into view was a pitch-black mass, curled in on itself as if hugging its knees.
For a split second, my brain tried to insist it was human—then immediately rejected the idea.
No… it looked similar, but something about it was fundamentally wrong.
Where a face should have been, there were no eyes, no nose.
Only a round opening where the mouth might be—gaping hollow.
Peering into it, I saw thin, triangular membrane-like folds layered from the rim toward the center, overlapping like petals, as if sealing the opening from within.
A smell—like salt mixed with iron—slid into the back of my throat.
Its arms were drawn tight along its sides.
At an angle that reminded me of a mantis’s folded scythes.
From the wrists extended only two long fingers, each tipped with needle-like claws.
Its legs were built the same way, pulled in close, the soles turned inward toward its body.
From the heel, droplets fell—
each one striking the thin film of water on the aluminum sheet, sending up tiny ripples.
The surface of it was wet, a black membrane repelling the melting ice.
Drip… drip.
Soft water sounds on aluminum.
Plip…
And again, a drop slipped from the edge of the table, falling onto the wooden slats below.
Its head bulged outward like a light bulb, its surface densely covered with countless tiny bumps—finer than grains of rice.
For some reason, looking at it reminded me of The Scream I’d once seen in an art book.
At the center of its torso, where the abdomen would be, there was a circular groove tapered at both ends.
A thin vertical indentation ran between the two pointed tips, connecting them.
Between its legs, a ring-like swelling protruded, from whose circumference multiple radial grooves extended inward.
Like wedges cut from a cake—
and somehow, those wedge shapes seemed to echo the structure of the round mouth I had just seen, sending a faint unease through my chest.
Not wanting to touch it directly, I pulled at the edge of the aluminum sheet and carefully rolled it slightly to change the angle, peering at its back.
Its rear surface had a rounded, home-plate-like outline, with a ridge running down the center like a spine, and rib-like protrusions lined up symmetrically along both sides.
At the very bottom, the form ended in a rounded W-shape, where melted water pooled—
occasionally breaking surface tension and dripping down in quiet drops.
Nowhere did I see the rise and fall of breathing.
No trace of body heat. No tremor of skin.
And yet, each time the fluorescent light slid across its black curved surface, I had the uneasy illusion that the reflection lagged—trailing after my own movement.
The sound of my heartbeat grew louder inside my ears.
(…This really isn’t from this world.)
An unformed sound rolled in the back of my throat as I slowly tightened my grip on the edge of the aluminum sheet.
Should I cover it back up… or keep looking?
A damp chill clung to my fingertips.
The darkness outside was scattered by condensation, and in the window, only the faint shadow of the room wavered.
The plink… of a droplet falling from the edge of the table onto the wooden slats below echoed softly.
Above, the sky rumbled again, low and distant.

Clatter!

The sudden sound of the drying room door sliding open made my shoulders jolt.
My heart leapt, and in a panic—like I’d been caught doing something I shouldn’t—I hurriedly pulled the aluminum sheet back over it.
Mari-senpai stood in the doorway.
“Haah… you scared me…” I said, trying to play it off.
“You were looking at it?” she asked.
“Yeah… I was wondering what it looks like now that the ice has melted…” I replied.
“Has it melted already?”
Letting out a small sigh, Mari-senpai stepped closer, unable to hide her curiosity. She reached out and peeled back the aluminum sheet, studying it intently.
“…That’s gross.”
She muttered it after a moment, yet her eyes stayed fixed on it, her head tilting slightly.
“Ugh… I’m really not good with stuff like this… but… I can’t stop looking…”
After a few seconds, she finally looked up and held out a convenience store bag she’d brought with her.
“Here. Pocky. Just eat this and take it easy.”
She pulled out a red box and placed it on the table.
There was something gently caring in the gesture—like an older sister looking out for me.
Then she showed me a sheet of paper she’d been holding.
“Put this up. There were people trying to open it earlier, right? Stuff like this actually helps.”
The paper read:
Property of Nagoya University. Unauthorized opening or contact is prohibited. Violators will be subject to penalties.
Each corner had a strip of duct tape already attached.
Mari-senpai gave a small smile as she handed it to me, and as I took it, I couldn’t help thinking, There’s no way this actually belongs to Nagoya University…
Just because Takizawa-san went there, Tsuruoka-san had thrown the name around like it meant something.
I let out a quiet laugh.
“Totally something Tsuruoka-san would do,” she said.
The way she said it made it clear she hadn’t written it herself—she wanted me to know that.
“…Thanks, Mari-senpai.” I said, grateful for her thoughtfulness.
“I should get back before people start wondering… see you later.”
She gave a small wave, then slid the door shut behind her.

In the quiet that had settled back over the drying room, I took a slow, deep breath, trying to steady the rapid pounding of my heart.
A low whooom rolled through the air—then, with a sharp creak, the lodge shuddered for an instant.
I took the paper Mari-senpai had brought and, using the strips of duct tape already fixed to its corners, carefully pressed it onto the aluminum sheet covering it.
Through the thin layer of metal, I could feel the uneven contours beneath my hand.
The surface was firm—yet it had a faint, unsettling give to it.
If someone still tries to touch it after this… next time, I’m siding with Tsuruoka-san and giving them a piece of my mind.
Plink…
A droplet fell from the edge of the table.
Hoooo…
The wind howled somewhere high above.
The steady hiss of rain continued without pause.
Once again, the world outside the window flashed as bright as day.
A moment later, thunder rolled across the sky.

…What should I do now… maybe I’ll just eat the Pocky Mari-senpai brought and listen to some music…
I drop into a folding chair and stretch my legs out onto the table.
The green slippers remind me faintly of the cold floor beneath them.
I pull out my phone, open YouTube, and tap on a playlist I made myself.
A blast of guitar and pounding drums fills the cramped drying room.
Cum On Feel the Noize by Quiet Riot—yeah, this is perfect for blowing away this murky, suffocating mood.
People these days are all about hip-hop and rap and whatever else, but me—I grew up on old-school hard rock, probably because of my dad.
I tear open the package, pull out a single Pocky stick, and toss it into my mouth.
The sweet, slightly firm chocolate gives me a strange sense of comfort.
Sitting with that thing off to my front-left, I let the music carry me as my mind drifts back over everything that’s happened.
When I first stood at the trailhead, my chest was tight with anxiety.
Can I really make it all the way…? What if I burn out halfway…? Those doubts slipped out before I could stop them.
But Takizawa-san just laughed. “You’ll be fine. Anyone can make it if they pace themselves.”
Tsuruoka-san snapped back,“Don’t start whining already,”
And Mari-senpai gently cut in, “It’s your first time, right? That’s normal,”
Each of their words, in their own way, lifted me… warmed something inside me.
And now, here I am—actually made it to the mountain lodge in one piece.
Guess even someone like me can climb a mountain… realizing that, I felt a small spark of confidence.
—BAM!
A mass of wind slams into the lodge, and the whole structure jolts, letting out a sharp creak.
The wind’s getting stronger.
…So we’re really heading straight into the typhoon after all…

“Sign in, please…”
As Cum On Feel the Noize fades out, the thick, layered synth riff of Turn Up the Radio by Autograph kicks in.
The moment I hear that opening—one of my all-time favorites—I can’t help but grin.
A flash of lightning… then the rush of rain swells louder against the roof.

Once the climb began, the scenery kept shifting—forest roads, stairways, beech groves, lingering snowfields… one after another.
I remember the break at Hakuba-jiri, too… and the brief exchanges with other climbers.
There were two women.

Plink.

Something above it… slowly melting, dripping down.

Next up after Turn Up the Radio—Crazy Crazy Nights by KISS.
