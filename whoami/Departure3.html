Departure Again

"Well then, shall we go?" Takizawa-san called out.
"Tsuru-san, yours is the heaviest, so you take the lead. Set the pace." Takizawa-san said to Tsuruoka-san.
In the white world wrapped in fog, Takizawa-san’s voice sounded muffled.
"Ohh, then I’ll take point." Tsuruoka-san said, and with a zaku, zaku crunch of boots, he began walking over the snow.
"Everyone, don’t lose sight of the person in front of you. And make sure to keep an eye on the person behind you too." Saying that, Takizawa-san lightly tapped my shoulder.
"Saku-chan, stay right behind Tsuru-san. Don’t fall behind." Takizawa-san told me.
"Okay." I answered and started walking after Tsuruoka-san.
Tsuruoka-san glanced back at me for a moment.
Maybe he was being considerate, or maybe he just didn’t like having me right behind him... but honestly, I didn’t exactly like having to walk behind him either.
"Alright, I’ll go next." I heard Yabe-san’s voice from behind.
It seemed Yabe-san would be right behind me.
I still couldn’t quite figure her out, but after everything that had happened so far, having her behind me felt strangely reassuring.
Still, I worried about being separated from Mari-senpai.
What if something happens to her? I found myself thinking.
In the fog where nothing could be seen, I followed the faint silhouette of Tsuruoka-san ahead of me.
At my feet, the trail of footprints we had made earlier stretched on like a thin line.
That was our only guide.
When I glanced back, Yabe-san’s figure flickered in and out like a shadow behind the curtain of white fog.
She seemed to be keeping just enough distance so she wouldn’t disappear from sight.
As we kept walking, a faint, faded red line drawn across the snow appeared beneath our feet.
"Here it is." Tsuruoka-san stopped and waited for everyone to gather.
It hadn’t been a great distance, but just getting back to the trail was enough to make me feel relieved for the moment.
Out of the fog, one by one, Yabe-san, Mari-senpai, Yohei, and finally Takizawa-san appeared.
"Phew..." Mari-senpai breathed.
"Finally." Yohei muttered.
Short breaths and short words passed between us.
We lined up in the same order again and began walking in single file along the red line.
The sound of snow being crushed beneath our boots and the sound of my own breathing echoed in the fog-sealed world.
After following Tsuruoka-san along the red line in the snow for a while, the crunching sound of his footsteps suddenly vanished.
The red line at my feet also came to an end, and we stepped out onto an area where rough rocks jutted from the ground.
"This is where the snowfield ends." Tsuruoka-san said.
As he stopped, he carefully lowered his subdued green backpack—with that glittering silver-wrapped bundle still strapped on top—then sat on a rock and reached for his boots, beginning to remove his gaiters and crampons.
I stopped as well, still gripping my backpack straps, and looked around at the white fog-covered surroundings.
One after another, Yabe-san and Mari-senpai emerged from behind.
The moment I saw Mari-senpai’s face, I felt myself relax, the tension slipping from my shoulders without thinking.
She looked at me and smiled warmly.
"The snowfield’s over." Tsuruoka-san called back to Takizawa-san.
"Yeah. Then let’s take off the crampons. And let’s rest a bit too—just five minutes." Takizawa-san said.
Only five minutes? I want to rest longer than that... I thought to myself as I took off my pack and sat down on a rock.
I lifted one leg and loosened the nylon straps fixing my crampons to my boots, then removed them.
After putting them into their case, I slipped them into my backpack pocket.
My throat was dry, so I pulled a plastic bottle from the side of my pack, loosened the cap, and brought it to my lips.
The water flowed into my mouth from the bottle’s opening.
Drinking water in the cold mountain air tasted unbelievably good, soaking into my exhausted body.
Feeling a chill, I took the fleece that had been tucked into my rain cover and slipped it on.
Mari-senpai was removing her pink gaiters.
"How much farther do we still have to walk?" I asked.
"Hmm, probably about another three hours." Takizawa-san answered.
Three more hours... I thought inwardly.
We still had a long way to go.
But for now, even this short break and a single mouthful of cold, clear water gave me strength for what was ahead.
"Don’t let your body cool down. Put something on if you’re cold." Takizawa-san added.
"Yeah." That’s exactly why I put it on... I thought as I nodded, stretching lightly on the rock to loosen my stiff body.
My eyes drifted toward Tsuruoka-san’s backpack.
On top of it, that object wrapped in the aluminum thermal sheet was still firmly secured.
"Senpai, your backpack’s looking pretty serious now." Yohei said with a laugh, pointing at it.
"Well, yeah, but it’s wrapped tight with rope and the thermal sheet, so it’ll be fine." Tsuruoka-san puffed out his chest proudly.
"Tsuru-san, good thing you brought that rope." Takizawa-san said with a grin.
"Of course! Gotta brag about that, don’t I?" Tsuruoka-san replied proudly in his Owari dialect.
Yabe-san gave a small laugh. "Still, it does look kind of heavy."
"Hmm, it’s a lot lighter than carrying a baby. Still, gotta move carefully." Tsuruoka-san said, resting a hand on his pack and lightly steadying the silver bundle.
"Everyone remember this—I’m the one who found the UFO first." Tsuruoka-san added.
"Yeah, yeah." Yabe-san replied dryly.
Maybe the ice inside had started melting a little.
The lower part of the aluminum wrapping had bulged slightly into a rounded shape.
"How far are we going before the next break?" Mari-senpai quietly asked Takizawa-san beside her.
"Let’s make it to the emergency shelter. The snow’s over now, so from here it’ll be a rocky climb." he answered.
"Sounds easier than the snowfield... but rocky paths seem tiring too." I said.
"Well, we’ll take it easy. No pushing too hard." Takizawa-san’s voice carried a note of reassurance.
But can I really trust that?
He said we’d rest every hour before too, and we barely stopped at all...
Leaders really have it good. They get to walk at their own pace and do things however it suits them...
When I looked at my watch, five minutes had already passed.
I found myself oddly nervous, wondering when Takizawa-san would give the signal to move out again.
Just as I was thinking that, Takizawa-san stood up and gave the word.
"Alright then, Tsuru-san, let’s get moving again."
"Okay, let’s go... from here it’s all rocks, so watch your step." Tsuruoka-san stood up, hoisted the backpack with the silver-wrapped object on top, and called out from the front.
Maybe more ice had melted inside, collecting into water—the rounded bulge at the bottom of the silver wrapping swayed as he moved, and I caught it out of the corner of my eye.

"Tsuru-san, that thing looks pretty heavy... be careful, okay?" Yohei said, sounding a little worried.
"It’s fine. Looks aside, it’s tied on really securely." Tsuruoka-san replied in his Owari dialect as he started walking again, carefully placing each step.
I gripped the shoulder straps of my backpack tightly and followed after him, checking the rocks beneath my feet as I moved.
The world around us was pure white with fog, but I could still tell where he was from his footprints ahead and the sway of his backpack.
"Take it slow. Don’t push yourselves." Takizawa-san called from behind.
"Okay." I nodded.
"Tsuruoka-san, make sure you carry it safely." Mari-senpai’s voice came from somewhere behind us.
"Yeah, yeah. It’d be a disaster if I dropped it." Tsuruoka-san laughed a little shyly, lifting his right hand to lightly steady the load strapped on top of his pack.
As I regulated my breathing and checked my footing on the rocks, I found a strange sense of reassurance in the faint silhouette of Tsuruoka-san’s back, barely visible through the fog.
"How much farther is it to the emergency shelter?" I asked.
"Not too much farther. I’d say thirty minutes... maybe an hour." Tsuruoka-san answered, half-turning his head toward me.
Wrapped in white fog, it was a silent world filled only with the crunch of boots on rock and the sound of our breathing.
The object protected inside the aluminum thermal sheet was moving carefully along this path together with us.
Watching it, I found myself once again hoping we would all make it safely to the shelter together as soon as possible.
As we walked along the mountain trail strewn with loose rocks, we moved through mottled patches of white fog, thick and thin areas slowly shifting and changing shape as they drifted around us.
Whenever the fog thinned, I could still see remnants of the snowfield lingering off to the left.
As we crossed a slightly steep slope covered with large, rough white rocks, water came trickling down through the stones from the upper right.
Bridging over that little stream were two narrow wooden bridges made of squared beams, one nearer and one farther ahead, spanning from rock to rock.
Across the tops of the bridges, several short wooden slats had been fastened sideways in rows, serving as footholds to keep them from being slippery.
The back of Tsuruoka-san, with the silver gleaming bundle tied by a light blue rope on top of his dark green backpack, carefully kept its balance as he crossed the wooden bridge one step at a time, then gradually disappeared into a thicker patch of the drifting white fog ahead.
"Are you okay?... There’s a bridge here." I called out to the person behind me—probably Yabe-san, though I couldn’t clearly see her through the white mist. From the sound of footsteps, I knew she was still following.
The fact that I actually felt like calling out to Yabe-san like this was probably because I was still a little excited about that strange object we had just found.
"I’m fine." Yabe-san’s voice came back from behind.
What I had really wanted to ask was whether Mari-senpai, who should be behind her, was okay too... but with Yabe-san there, I held myself back.
Then I stepped onto the bridge myself.
From the steep slope up to the right, water came splashing and scattering down through the rocks, slipping beneath the wooden bridge laid between the stones before flowing away toward the lower left.
If I lost my balance even a little, I felt like my body might slip left and get swept away by the rushing water too.
The thought made my heart beat a little faster.
It felt kind of like an adventure.

I kept following behind Tsuruoka through the drifting white fog.
The silver object strapped to the top of his backpack swayed faintly through the mist, and somehow it felt as if it were guiding me—this way, this way.
After a while, I noticed a green rope stretched along the side of the trail between patches of grass and jagged rocks, and beyond it, small flowers were blooming vividly even through the white haze.
“This area is called Nebukappira,” Takizawa-san’s voice came from behind. “A long time ago, it was known for Shirouma-asatsuki, a wild onion species unique to Mt. Shirouma, with pale purple flowers that look like little onion blossoms.”
Behind me, I could hear Mari-senpai and Yabe talking.
“Shirouma-ogi is blooming.”
“Oh, and there’s Takane-nadeshiko too.”
—Ah… everyone’s still right behind me.
Hearing their voices made me feel a little relieved.
I didn’t know the names of the flowers, but even just the brighter ones stood out vividly enough through the fog.
“If it weren’t so foggy, this whole place would probably be a flower field,” I heard Mari-senpai say from behind.
“Because of someone, huh…” Yabe replied with a light laugh.
Yabe-san’s laser-sharp comments were as sharp as ever...
When I looked ahead, I could see Tsuruoka-san’s back swaying, the silver bundle still strapped on top.
He didn’t seem to realize they were talking about him.
Before long, the trail gradually turned into a stretch of loose, rocky ground, and we continued climbing upward in zigzags.
