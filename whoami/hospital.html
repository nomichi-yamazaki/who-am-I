病院

White.
That was the first thing I saw.
Somewhere within that endless whiteness, a faint sound drifted through.
—Birdsong.
A few short chirps. Then silence. Then another.
I knew that sound.
It was like the birds I'd heard at the trailhead of Mount Shirouma, in the cold morning air, while tightening the laces of my boots.
...Huh?
I had no idea where I was. My eyes drifted from one thing to another.
My body felt unbearably heavy. It refused to move the way I expected.
Turning my head, I found a pale pink curtain beside me.
It wasn't completely drawn shut. A thin ribbon of light slipped through the narrow gap.
Was it morning? Or nearly noon? The gentle light offered no answer.
I was lying in the middle of a hospital bed with safety rails on both sides.
Slowly, my gaze drifted upward again.
Mounted on the wall above my head was a cold, utilitarian console.
Small English labels were printed across it.
OXYGEN
VACUUM
I understood the words, yet they somehow failed to feel real.
A hospital.
Probably.
A strange sensation in my arm drew my attention downward.
A tube disappeared into the skin of my forearm.
Beside the bed stood an IV pole, a clear bag hanging from its hook.
Inside, the fluid fell one drop at a time... steady... unhurried.
There was almost no sound.
Only the quiet certainty that the drops kept falling.
Expecting the familiar sting of disinfectant, I drew a slow breath through my nose.
—Nothing.
The air was so utterly odorless that it caught me off guard.
The sharp antiseptic smell I remembered from hospitals long ago was nowhere to be found.
Instead, it was the complete absence of any smell that unsettled me.
I felt as though I'd been dreaming.
What the dream had been about, I couldn't remember.
All that remained was the feeling of overwhelming exhaustion... of being unable to do anything except simply exist.
I couldn't tell whether time was moving forward or standing perfectly still.
It felt as though an eternity slipped between each blink.

From beyond the curtain, I heard the faint murmur of voices.

"...I heard they're finally shutting down Kitaazumi General Hospital next door."
"Really? They had an emergency department, didn't they?"
"Yeah. Apparently they've been running in the red for years. If patients stay too long, the hospital gets paid less."
"So... the longer they treat someone, the more money they lose?"
"Exactly. Makes you want to discharge people as soon as you can. But some patients have nowhere to go. In the end, the hospital has to make up the losses somehow."

Their voices were low and matter-of-fact.
It sounded less like gossip than a routine report.

—Hospitals can go out of business?

If even hospitals—the places meant to save lives—can go out of business, then you can't really call that a society anymore.
That's not society.
That's insanity.

That's what I thought.

"It's supposed to be a place that saves lives, and yet the numbers come first."
"...That's just how it is. A hospital's a business, too."

After a brief silence, another voice spoke up.

"Ours could be next... There's talk that things haven't been looking too good lately."

Silence settled over them for a moment.

"So... does that mean whether you live or die matters less than whether you make money?"
"That's certainly how it sounds."
"...That's messed up."

White... no, the conversation lingered.
Those words refused to leave me.

So this was how society worked.
Even in a place where lives were meant to be saved,
what mattered wasn't whether someone lived,
but whether the place itself could keep going.

—Was this really
the kind of society most people had chosen?

Numbers stood above human lives.
And within that system,
I was just another patient lying in a bed.

This is messed up.
The thought came without warning.

Maybe it was all the same.

War.
Environmental destruction.
The endless stream of crises that filled the news.

They all looked different,
but their roots were the same.

People competed.
Only the winners survived.
Only what generated money was considered valuable.

And somewhere in that competition,
life stopped being something to protect
and became a cost.
A number.

Even hospitals going under...

...were, in the end,
just another consequence of chasing profit.

Hadn't we been calling
a world like this "normal"
all along?

It wasn't because of monsters.
It wasn't because of one evil person, either.

This...
was the shape of the society
most people had chosen.

It was no different in the mountains.

Even when the weather turned bad.
Even when everyone knew it was dangerous.
"This is our only chance."
"We've already paid for the trip."
"We have to make it."

Little by little,
there were always more reasons to keep going
than to turn back.

When it should have been simple.
Making it home alive
should have been what mattered most.
Yet somehow,
plans and results became heavier than life itself.

Mountain huts.
Trails.
There were times when they should simply be closed.
Yet they stayed open,
because closing them meant losing customers.

Even danger
became a headline.
A story.
Something to be sold
as a challenge.

The mountains had done nothing.
The force driving people forward
wasn't nature.

It was us.

Just then,
the sliding door at the entrance whispered open with a soft shhh.
A figure dressed in white stepped inside.
A nurse.
Her footsteps were quiet, carrying nothing with them but reality.

The figure in white stopped at the edge of my vision.
"...You're awake?"
Her voice was calm, and closer than I'd expected.
A woman's voice.
I tried to answer, but my throat refused to cooperate.
Instead, my eyelids moved ever so slightly.
"Are you all right? Do you know where you are?"
She spoke again.
This time, her voice was just a little gentler.
I gave the faintest nod to show that I did.
Even that tiny movement sent a dull ache deep into my neck.
"Can you tell me your name?"
My name.
For a moment, I had to think.
It should have been the easiest thing in the world, yet the words caught somewhere inside my mind.
"...Sakuma... Sho..."
My own voice sounded strangely far away.
"All right, Sakuma-san."
As she spoke, the nurse placed two fingers against my right wrist.
Her fingertips were cool.
A gentle pressure... then they lifted away.
At the same time, her other hand wrapped a blood pressure cuff around my arm.
The fabric tightened as it filled with air.
"Do you know where you are?"
The ceiling.
The curtain.
The IV stand.
"...A hospital?"
"That's right. You're in a hospital."
Something about the way she said it stayed with me.
Especially the words, You're in a hospital.
The nurse checked the IV bag, then gently pressed the connection where the tube met the line.
The clear fluid continued to fall, one drop at a time, as steadily as before.
"Is there anywhere that hurts badly?"
Pain.
The moment she asked, dull aches began surfacing from every part of my body.
Each breath scraped painfully somewhere deep inside my chest.
When I tried to take a deeper breath, the pain around my right ribs flared a heartbeat later.
—Don't move.
The thought came the instant I understood.
Then a strange sensation shot through my left shoulder.
It felt as though something had slipped out of place between my neck and shoulder.
When I tried to move my arm, the strength gave out halfway.
Something was wrong with my right hand, too.
I could still feel my fingertips, but they wouldn't obey me.
A dull ache slowly spread from the base of my little finger to my wrist.
"...It... hurts... when I move..."
"I'm sure it does."
There was no surprise in her voice.
No denial, either.
She accepted it as nothing more than a simple fact.
I lowered my eyes from the ceiling and cautiously looked down at my own body.
Beneath the white sheet, it was all still there.
...And yet it refused to move the way I wanted it to.
"...I..."
I meant to speak, but only a rasp escaped my throat.
"Where... am I... injured?"
Without taking her eyes off the IV tubing, the nurse answered in the same calm voice.
"You've fractured two ribs on your right side, your left collarbone, and one of the bones in your right hand.
But your life is not in danger."
My life was not in danger.
The words slowly began to take shape inside my mind.
...I survived.
The moment I thought that,
the darkness inside the mountain hut... and that restless murmur... began to seep back into my mind.
"Another climber found you unconscious during your climb.
They contacted the mountain hut, and you were brought here from there."
The climb.
The mountain hut.
The words reached me first.
Their meaning followed a moment later.
"Right now, we're keeping you under observation with oxygen and IV fluids. Your treatment is finished, so you can relax."

Relax.
Even after hearing those words, the weight in my chest remained.
After finishing her examination, the nurse quietly rose to her feet.
"I'll come back to check on you later. If you need anything, just press the call button."
With that, she turned her gaze beyond the curtain.
A moment later, the sliding door whispered shut once again.
All that remained
was the slow drip of the IV
and the faint birdsong drifting in from outside.

My eyes drifted after the slow drip of the IV.
A steady rhythm.
Pulse.
Blood pressure.
Oxygen.
—Something clicked.
The pressure of the blood pressure cuff around my arm
blurred into the rise and fall of Naoki-kun's chest in the mountain hut dining hall.
The white hospital ceiling
flickered into the yellow glow of a bare light bulb.
A clear face shield.
A white kappogi apron.
Uchida-san.
Takamura-san.
"Hold him steady while we observe it. And whatever you do, don't touch the tentacle..."
A calm voice.
Yet beneath it lay a tension wound painfully tight.
The beam of a black flashlight
fell across the red, glistening thing clinging to Naoki-kun's cheek.
Its tentacles writhed endlessly.
They glistened in the light, slick like exposed intestines.
"Pulse is seventy-eight... stable."
"SpO₂ is ninety-eight percent. Stable as well."
Takamura-san's voice
merged perfectly with the nurse's voice I'd just heard.
The electronic beeping...
for a fleeting instant,
became the sound of the mountain hut dining hall.
"Naoki-kun, stay still for just a moment, okay?"
The gloved hand
resting gently on his shoulder.
I could even remember its warmth.
"Do you think observation alone will really be enough...?"
"Yes. For now... it's all we can do."
Their voices remained low.
It couldn't be removed.
All they could do was wait.
And then...
The face melting away.
The tentacles growing shorter.
The eyelids slowly closing.
My throat tightened.
This was a hospital.
I was lying in a white room,
an IV running into my arm.
And yet...
part of me was still trapped
inside that mountain hut dining hall.
I hadn't taken
a single step out of it.
"..."
Without thinking,
my eyes dropped to the IV needle in my arm.
The clear tubing.
The needle buried beneath my skin.
...What if that...
...What if one of those things...
The thought barely began
before I squeezed my eyes shut.

The moment I was alone, it all came back.
Not because I wanted to remember.
It simply came, whether I wanted it to or not.
"Saku-chan... my... my eye... came out..."

Yohei changing before my eyes after we fled into the old lodge.
My best friend...
becoming something else
before I could even cry out.
I could only stand there and watch.
A one-eyed monster.
The generator room.
A mass of flesh with faces and tentacles pushing through its surface.

Yet even she...

and then...
The restroom.

Crawling toward the urinal...
urine streaming from his eye.
No matter how hard I tried not to think about it,
that was the image that remained the clearest.
A swarm of those things.
Uchida-sensei...
and Morishita Aya-san...
The warmth of Aya-san's hand
wrapped around mine
was still so vividly real.
Then she was swallowed whole...
by something like a gigantic caterpillar.
The washroom.
Something like a pitch-black fetus
crawling across the floor like a spider.
It changed...
and attacked Akane-san,
the woman from that suspicious middle-aged pair,
and the balding man in his forties with his lower lip jutting out, wearing glasses.

Tanaka-san's cheek...

The dining hall.

The man in his sixties.

Like an elephant's trunk.
Then—
The dining hall wall.
Bursting apart.
That enormous thing.
The lodge owner's face.

Raw flesh.
Arms.
Legs.

It should have been over.

It all began again.

企業も、政府も、マスコミも、宗教も、
みんな金や立場でつながり合い、
同じ輪の中で動いている。
その輪の中を、
自分たちの損得を守るために、
人は無意識に走らされる。
「気づいた時点で、もう流されっぱなしじゃない」
おじいちゃんのその言葉だけが、
雪の音の中に残っていた。
――人は、
仕組みの中で生きるうちに、
同じ方向を向くように誘導されていく。
それが、
あの時、僕がたどり着いた答えだった。
人は、
生きるのに必死になると、
周りを見る余裕をなくす。
低い給料で、
長い時間働いて、
毎日の生活だけで精一杯になれば、
遠くの出来事を、
自分の頭で考える力も、
だんだん削られていく。
そうなると、
怖いかどうかよりも、
「みんながどうしているか」が、
一番の判断基準になる。
みんなが向いている方向。
みんなが信じている言葉。
みんなが怒っている相手。
そこに並んでいれば、
少なくとも、
一人きりにはならずにすむからだ。
自分だけが助かろうとして、
流れに乗る。
自分だけが安全でいようとして、
同じ側に立つ。
けれど、
みんなが同じことをすれば、
結局、
その社会全体が、
自分の首を絞める形になる。
誰かを蹴落として得た安心は、
ほんの一時のもので、
気づけば、
蹴落としたはずの場所に、
自分も立たされている。
――自分だけ幸せになろうとしても、
みんなが幸せになることを考えられなければ、
結局、
同じ穴を掘り続けるだけなんじゃないだろうか。
恐怖が、
ナニカを生んだように。
恐怖は、
人間にも、
同じことをさせる。

……その時だった。
ベッド脇の小さなテレビが、
リモコンに触れた覚えもないのに、
低い電子音を立てて点いた。
〈――次のニュースです〉
落ち着いた声の女性アナウンサーが、
感情を含まない口調で読み上げる。
〈SNS上での誹謗中傷を苦に、
　また一人、若い男性が命を絶ちました〉
画面には、
モザイクのかかったスマートフォンの画面。
切り取られた言葉、拡散された悪意、
「正義」を名乗る無数の匿名の声。
〈投稿は短時間で拡散し、
　本人の釈明が届く前に炎上状態となっていました〉
炎上。
その言葉が、
山小屋で見た“連鎖”と、
ぴたりと重なった。
〈専門家は
　「集団心理による過剰反応」と指摘しています〉
画面が切り替わる。
〈――続いて政治の話題です〉
別のアナウンサーが現れ、
少しだけ声の調子が変わる。
〈裏金問題などの影響で支持率が低迷していた政権ですが、
　新たに就任した首相は、
　隣国への強い警戒姿勢を示しました〉
地図が表示され、
赤い円が、じわりと日本列島を囲む。
〈首相は
　「このままでは我が国の安全が脅かされる」
　と発言し、
　防衛力の強化を訴えています〉
その言葉の直後、
街頭インタビューの映像が流れた。
「怖いですよね。
　何をしてくるか分からないですし」
「向こうが敵意を持ってるなら、
　こっちも備えないと」
「戦争は嫌だけど、
　やられる前に、っていうのは……」
誰も、
実際に攻撃されたとは言っていない。
誰も、
直接、何かを見たわけでもない。
ただ、
「かもしれない」という言葉だけが、
画面の中で増殖していく。
〈SNS上では
　隣国に対する強い言葉が相次いでおり、
　一部では不買運動や排外的な発言も見られます〉
恐怖が、
言葉に形を変えて、
広がっていく。
〈政府は冷静な対応を呼びかけていますが――〉
その続きを、
僕はもう聞いていなかった。
恐怖を感じた瞬間に、
姿を変え、
数を増やし、
周囲を巻き込んでいく。
攻撃される前に、
逃げ場を失う前に、
自分たちで恐怖を膨らませて、
先に牙を剥く。
危険なものだと勝手に思い込み勝手に決めつけて無意味に恐れて攻撃してしまい墓穴を掘ってしまう。
無意味に恐れて攻撃してしまい結果的に自分の首を絞めてしまう。
――それは。
あの山小屋で見た、ナニカの振る舞いと、そして人々のナニカに対する振る舞いと、 あまりにも、よく似ていた。
