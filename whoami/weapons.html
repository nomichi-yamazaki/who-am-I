Weapons

“I’m gonna go look for anything we can use as weapons.”
I heard Suzuki Sho-san from reception mutter quietly.
When I looked over, he noticed me watching and our eyes met.
“I’ll help too.”
—Even if I stayed here, it wasn’t like I had the power to heal Naoki-kun or do anything useful.
I felt like I had to do something.
The moment I thought that, the words came out on their own.
Maybe somewhere deep down, I also just couldn’t bear watching Naoki-kun get worse and worse right in front of me.
Suzuki Sho-san gave a small nod, and the two of us began walking through the dining hall, weaving around pillars and tables.
Rattle…
We opened the dining hall doors and stepped out into the hallway.
“Hang on a second. I’m gonna grab the keys,” Suzuki Sho-san said in a low voice as he quickly turned around.
He headed down the hallway to the right toward the office.
On the wall opposite us was a wood-grain door with signs reading “Office” and “STAFF ONLY.”
Suzuki Sho-san pushed the door open and disappeared inside.
As I watched him go, the crushing heaviness I had felt while standing beside Naoki-kun and witnessing his transformation drifted back into my mind.
And yet, with Naoki-kun no longer directly in front of me, I also felt the slightest release from the tension gripping the depths of my chest.
Even the tightness still lingering in my shoulders eased a little as I focused my mind on searching for weapons.
A short while later, Suzuki Sho-san returned carrying a key with a yellow oval-shaped plastic tag dangling from it.
We started walking briskly down the corridor together.
As we walked, Suzuki Sho-san suddenly spoke.
“You’ve got the same name as me, right? Sho?”
“Yes. Sho.” I answered.
Maybe he thought I’d made a pun out of “Yes, that’s right” and “Yes, I’m Sho.”
“Heh.”
Suzuki Sho-san gave the faintest hint of a smile.
Suzuki Sho-san and I reached the end of the hallway, stopping in front of a gray-painted metal door.
A sign hanging on it read: “Power Room — Authorized Personnel Only.”
In front of the heavy door, its cold metallic surface giving off a dull sheen, we paused for a moment and exchanged a glance before nodding to each other.
Suzuki Sho-san inserted the key and slowly turned it.
Click—
The lock disengaged, and the heavy iron door creaked open with a long metallic groan.
DADADADADA…
The instant the door opened, the sound of machinery from inside came rumbling out toward us.
The deep engine noise and the smell of oil pushed out through the gap together.
Suzuki Sho-san handed the closing door off to me as the door closer tried to pull it shut again, then reached along the wall on the right and switched on the fluorescent lights.
The fluorescent tubes flickered on and immediately began giving off a faint electrical hum.
Concrete walls.
Concrete ceiling.
A concrete floor layered with dust.
—It felt like there was a concrete box hidden inside the wooden lodge…
The emergency shelters I’d stopped at during mountain climbs had been built kind of like this too…
I felt a slight resistance stepping down onto the dusty floor in slippers instead of work boots, but there wasn’t much choice.
Just inside the entrance was a small landing, with a short set of two steps leading down farther ahead.
To the front right stood a long rectangular construction generator painted yellow, stretching toward the right side of the room.
The engine noise had to be coming from that generator.
Between the generator and the wall were several red kerosene containers lined up along the concrete wall, each fitted with white round caps of different sizes.
Beyond the generator, farther ahead on the right side from where we stood, I could see a workbench set up against the wall.
To the left of the workbench—directly ahead from where we stood—was a steel shelving unit painted an emerald green color, positioned against the far wall, with piles of cardboard boxes stacked carelessly across it.
Along the back wall between the workbench and the steel shelves, long construction materials had been propped upright: rafters, metal pipes, cable molding used for running electrical wires, and other long pieces.
In front of the steel shelves, directly ahead of us, sat a red fire pump.
Mounted to the wall immediately left of the steps descending from the landing was a pale beige control panel that was probably used to operate it.
From beside the fire pump to the area in front of the shelving unit, flat construction materials—plywood boards, corrugated metal sheets, plastic roofing panels, acrylic boards—had been leaned haphazardly against the left wall.
On the concrete floor between the fire pump in front of us and the generator to the right were two circular manhole covers, one nearer to us and one farther back.
Following behind Suzuki Sho-san, I stepped down the two stairs from the entrance landing and placed my feet onto the cold concrete floor.
“I’m gonna avenge Tanaka-san.”
Suzuki Sho-san said it as he walked deeper into the room.
—Just like I thought… Suzuki Sho-san was fixated on “taking it down.”
That was what I found myself thinking.

After passing the generator on the right and taking another step forward, I looked further to the right and spotted a tall gray cylindrical stove pipe rising upward—one of those Sunpot heater chimneys you often see at schools.
To the left of the stove sat a sturdy-looking workbench.
The bench was made from thick dark-brown wood, and several tabletop tools had been firmly mounted onto it, making it clear the space was fully set up for maintenance work.
If you stood facing the workbench, there was a vise on the front left corner, and farther back behind it sat a small bench grinder fitted with two grinding wheels.
On the front right corner stood a compact tabletop drill press.
Toward the back of the workbench were rows of small parts drawers lined up side by side.
When I turned my eyes farther right, I saw another steel door painted the same gray color as the entrance door, set into the wall beyond the workbench and generator.
Leaning beside it were an aluminum extension ladder and a stepladder.
Below them sat a fire extinguisher, and above it hung a red sign with white lettering reading: “Fire Extinguisher.”
Farther along the front side of the long yellow generator deeper inside the room were a small air compressor, a compact portable generator, about three stacked red metal gasoline cans, and a handcart.
They sat there quietly, almost as if waiting for something.

“But what could we even use as weapons…?”
I turned toward the steel shelves as I spoke, my eyes drifting over the shelves themselves and the metal pipes and rafters leaning between them and the workbench.
“To protect everyone, first we’ve gotta keep that thing from getting inside,” Suzuki Sho-san said, standing slightly ahead to my right as his gaze traced along the long materials stacked between the shelves and the workbench.
“Then, if we can rig up some kind of trap… maybe we can either kill it or lock it away somewhere.”
—Keep it from getting inside…
I stared at the steel shelves while thinking it over.
The shelving unit had three levels, each one packed tightly with supplies sorted by purpose.
On the top shelf were long cardboard boxes for fluorescent lights stacked side by side, along with other boxes printed with pictures of light bulbs.
Just looking at them made me imagine the smell of electricity and warm light somehow lingering in the air.
On the middle shelf sat three large cardboard boxes, stained with grime and partly crushed, some of their upper corners split open vertically where the cardboard had torn.
Written across them in thick red marker—far from neat handwriting—were the labels: “Electrical,” “Plumbing,” and “Maintenance.”
At a glance, it was obvious the supplies were organized by type of work.
The bottom shelf held neatly bundled coils of cables and rope.
Stiff gray F-cables.
Soft white parallel cords.
Black rubber cabtire cables.
There were also hoses, yellow-and-black hazard rope, wire bundles, and steel wire rope stacked together, while turnbuckles and shackles used for tensioning wire had been piled near the end.
The whole thing looked almost like the supply rack of a small fortress, prepared to handle every kind of repair or emergency work imaginable.

—There’s electrical wire here…
That was what crossed my mind.
“If we stripped the insulation off some of these wires and stretched them around the places it might come in… do you think that could stop it from entering…?” I muttered.
“Hm… maybe. But we don’t even know where it’ll come from,” Suzuki Sho-san replied.
“And if we start rigging up every window and door in the lodge, we might all be dead—or rescue might arrive—before we even finish.”
“True…”
I looked around the room again.
Boards… electrical supplies… piping… woodworking materials…
—Set a trap for it…
My slippered foot lifted awkwardly for a moment, and that was when I noticed the manhole cover beneath me.
“What if we opened the manhole… lured it over somehow… then trapped it inside?” I said.
“If we could pull that off, sure…” Suzuki Sho-san crossed his arms and frowned slightly.
“But we still don’t know anything about that thing. We don’t even know what would lure it in. And trying to drive it somewhere might just make things more dangerous instead.”
“In the end… it looks like close combat might be our only option.”
Suzuki Sho-san slowly swept his eyes across the room.
“…So then. What in here could actually work as a weapon?”
I looked toward the workbench on the right.
On the shelf beneath it were power tools—electric drills, grinders, circular saws, sanders—as well as toolboxes and protective gear like goggles, masks, and helmets.
Underneath that shelf, directly on the concrete floor, sat bags of cement and cardboard boxes.
A thick metal pipe lay off to one side.
“What about… hitting it with a steel pipe or something?” I said before I’d even fully thought it through.
“Wouldn’t getting that close be dangerous?” Suzuki Sho-san replied.
“…What exactly is that thing anyway? Do you know anything that might help us understand it? …You guys were the ones who brought it in, right?”
He didn’t say it in a blaming tone.
But because it was true, my chest still tightened painfully.
“Yeah…”
I struggled for words, but somehow managed to continue.
“I think… it might actually be an alien.”
“…There was something like a UFO down inside a crevasse in the snow valley. And that thing was buried nearby.”
I never even considered whether he might not believe me.
Because Suzuki Sho-san had seen the creature himself.
He’d witnessed what it could do with his own eyes.
“I see…” Suzuki Sho-san murmured thoughtfully.
“Normally I’d just say, ‘What kind of nonsense are you talking about?’ and leave it at that…”
“But if that’s true… and if something capable of building a UFO exists… then it probably has considerable intelligence.”
“It might even be smarter than humans.”
“…So then the question becomes: how the hell are we supposed to beat something like that in close combat?”
As he spoke, the scene in the washroom flashed back into my mind.
The thing extending tentacles from every direction.
Wrapping itself around people.
Spraying green liquid that melted them apart.
When I thought about that, getting close to it seemed impossible.
What the hell was I even saying… trying to attack it with a steel pipe…?

“Right… that green liquid the thing sprays—it melts people. That’s seriously dangerous,” I said.
“Yeah…” Suzuki Sho-san nodded slowly.
“…When you think about it that way, maybe we ought to focus on defense too.”
—Defense means shields…
And shields means something flat…
“Wait. There are acrylic boards over there, aren’t there?” I said.
“What if we used those as shields? If we stand them upright, drill just two holes, and run rope through them so they can be carried by hand, they could work like makeshift shields, right?”
“I see… so they’d work as protective barriers,” Suzuki Sho-san said, his expression sharpening slightly.
“We could attack while using them to block the thing’s tentacles and that liquid.”
“…And all we’d need to do is drill two holes and attach rope. That’d be simple enough to make.”
“…Alright. Let’s go with that.”
He glanced around the room again.
“…Then the next problem is offense.”
“…When people think ‘weapons,’ it’s usually blades or firearms.”
“…What did that thing’s skin feel like? Did it seem like something you could stab or cut?”

“I only touched it through the aluminum sheet, but it didn’t feel completely rigid. More like thick, tough skin with a little elasticity to it. But when that toilet-lid-looking thing opened up, the inside looked soft… almost like raw meat.” I said.
“But if getting close is dangerous, we need some kind of ranged weapon.” Suzuki-san said.
“Fire arrows or something?” I suggested.
“Yeah, fire arrows would be nice… but this lodge is wooden. If we start a fire, we’re finished. And with this storm outside…” Suzuki-san muttered.
“Then what about the opposite? Maybe spraying it with a fire extinguisher could kill it?” I said, remembering the extinguisher standing behind the door beside the workbench.
“Hm… I wonder. Even humans don’t die from extinguisher foam. Still, we won’t know unless we try. At the very least, it might help slow it down if it comes at us. We should prepare one just in case… Acrylic shields and fire extinguishers, huh… We still need something that can do real damage, though.” Suzuki-san said.
“What if we throw fluorescent tubes at it? Those aren’t LED tubes, right? The old fluorescent ones shatter when they take an impact.” I said.
“Maybe, but the gas inside them is argon. It doesn’t even hurt humans, so it probably wouldn’t affect that thing either.” Suzuki-san replied.
“True… but what about the broken glass? Couldn’t the shards injure it?” I asked.
“If we hold them ourselves, we’ll get cut. And if they’re scattered around, we’ll end up stepping on them too. Maybe as a trap on the floor…” Suzuki-san said.
“Like caltrops… except we’d step on them while trying to escape.” I muttered.
“Exactly. We could end up hurting ourselves instead… There’s an air compressor here, though. Maybe we could fire nails with it?” Suzuki-san said.
“That might work.” I answered.
“But even if a few nails stick into it… would that really be enough to kill it?” Suzuki-san murmured as he looked toward the gap between the steel shelves and the workbench.
“Electrical conduit pipes, huh… Maybe we could make spears and throw them at it?” Suzuki-san said as he looked toward the metal conduits leaning against the wall between the steel shelves and the workbench.
“That might work… If we cut the ends at an angle, they might pierce it. …And there’s a portable generator over there too, right? What if we connected wires to it and electrocuted the thing?” I said.
“That’s actually a pretty good idea… But can we really set something like that up easily?” Suzuki-san asked.
“There’s a bundle of parallel cord over there. We’d just need to attach a plug on one end so it can connect to an outlet, then wire the other end to the conduit spears. It’s not that complicated.
…I work in electrical systems, so if we’ve got the materials, I can rig it up.” I explained.
“Yeah… there should be spare plugs in that box over there.” Suzuki-san said, pointing toward the cardboard box labeled “Electrical” on the steel shelf.
“Alright then, let’s go with that plan. I’ll cut the conduit pipes and make the spears.” Suzuki-san said as he started walking toward the space between the shelves and the workbench.
“Okay. Could you make two of them? We’ll connect wires to both, then run current between the spears after they’re stuck into the thing…” I said.
“Now that sounds interesting. Alright, let’s do it. Time to get to work… Here.” Suzuki-san held out his hand toward me for a high five.
When we first met, he’d been complaining at me, and I’d carried resentment over it the whole time. Yet somehow, now we were standing here like this. As I slapped his outstretched hand with a sharp smack, I couldn’t help feeling the strange irony of fate.

—Alright… first, I should attach a plug to the parallel cord…
I walked over to the steel shelving unit and pulled out a bundled coil of parallel cord from the bottom shelf.
Then I dragged one of the cardboard boxes on the middle shelf closer to me.
It looked like people had done this countless times before—the front side of the box had collapsed into a triangular opening, exposing the contents inside.
Blue and white boxes of matching sizes were neatly gathered together in stacks.
I took a plug out of one of the boxes with a picture of a plug printed on it, then carried it together with the parallel cord over to the workbench where the tools were kept.
Suzuki-san had already dragged a high-speed cut-off saw out from in front of the generator and was untangling the power cable wrapped around the machine.
“Are the tools inside that toolbox?” I asked him.
“Yeah.” Suzuki-san answered while plugging the cut-off saw’s power cable into an outlet mounted near the base of the workbench legs.
“Got it.”
I stepped over the saw’s cable, walked to the front of the workbench, and placed the plug and the parallel cord on top of it.
“The red toolbox is for electrical work, and the blue one’s for plumbing. …Though stuff gets mixed together sometimes, so if you can’t find something, check the other box too…” Suzuki-san said while rattling through the conduit pipes leaning against the wall between the shelves and the workbench.
I pulled out the red toolbox.
It was a metal toolbox with two folding upper trays. When I lifted the handle and opened the lid, the top tier appeared, and when I spread the trays apart to both sides, the lower compartment underneath was revealed.
—Oh, there’s a crimping tool… Which means there should be terminals somewhere too.
I began pulling open the small drawers of the parts cabinet lined up along the back of the workbench, checking them one by one.

Screws… 3 mm, 4 mm, 5 mm, 6 mm…
Lengths: 10 mm, 15 mm… 20 mm… washers, nuts… nails, coarse-thread screws, screw eyes, cotter pins… Ah, here are the electrical terminals.
Ring sleeves… 1.25 with 4 mm openings… There they are. These should work.

I took a pair of wire cutters out of the toolbox, loosened the outer end of the parallel cord that had been bundled into a loop, and pulled the cable free.
Using the cutters, I split the tip into two separate leads, stripped the insulation from each end, inserted the terminals, and crimped them down with the crimping tool.
Then I repeated the same process on the other side.

Next, I used a Phillips screwdriver to remove the screws holding the plug together and split the plug casing open.
I clamped the parallel cord with the attached terminals inside, loosened the screws on one side of the plug prongs, slipped the terminals underneath, and tightened the screws back down, securing the wires to the plug.
Then I did the same thing on the opposite side.

The generator-side connector was finished.
For the spear-side wiring, I pulled the cable from the inside of the coil instead.
If the wire came from the inside, then even if we threw the spear with the cable attached, the cord would unwind smoothly instead of tangling itself up.
At the opposite end, I inserted the blades of the cutters lengthwise and split the parallel cord apart.
The single joined cable separated cleanly into two independent wires.

Nngiiiiiiiiiin—!
Sparks flew through the air.
Suzuki-san was using the high-speed cut-off saw to slice the tip of a 19-millimeter conduit pipe at an angle, shaping it into a spear.
“There. One down…” Suzuki-san muttered as he rolled the finished spear across the concrete floor.
Clang… clang-clang-clang…
The metal spear rattled noisily as it rolled.
I picked it up.
—Sandpaper… where’s the sandpaper…?
Looking beneath the workbench, I spotted several different kinds stacked near the edge.
I pulled out a coarse sheet and sanded the section of the spear where the wire would be attached.
—Electrical tape… electrical tape… Ah, there it is.
On the left side of the workbench was a vise, and between it and the sideways-mounted bench grinder farther back sat what looked like a battery charger for a cordless drill, one battery still charging on it.
Beside that was an empty Shiroi Koibito cookie tin filled with random odds and ends—assorted screws, cotter pins, rubber gaskets, even a translucent orange disposable lighter.
Inside the tin were a roll of blue electrical tape and another roll of red tape that was almost completely used up.
Using the cutters, I stripped about twenty centimeters of insulation from the wire, wrapped the exposed section around the sanded portion of the spear, then wound the end tightly around the base of the stripped section to secure it in place.
After that, I began wrapping electrical tape around the connection point.
The first loop went straight around once, then from the second wrap onward I overlapped each turn halfway across the previous layer, building it up evenly.
“You’re good at that…” Suzuki-san said as he watched me wrap the handle area of the conduit spear with electrical tape.
“I’m used to it. …And when you hold the spear, make sure you only grab the part covered in tape. Otherwise you’ll electrocute yourself.” I said.
“I know that much. …Couldn’t we just stab it first and plug the generator in afterward?” Suzuki-san replied.
“Oh… yeah, true. Then it wouldn’t matter where we touched it. …Honestly, I’m not even sure a hundred volts would be enough to kill it. I’d rather step the voltage up with a transformer or something… but we probably don’t have time for all that.” I said.
“Yeah… probably not.” Suzuki-san answered.

Nngiiiiiiiin—!
Suzuki-san cut another conduit spear while I connected the wiring.
That was how the electric spears were completed.
“Next up, acrylic shields.” Suzuki-san said as he headed toward the fire pump area.
I followed after him.
Walking in slippers made it awkward.
We stopped in front of the stack of flat materials leaning between the fire pump and the steel shelves.
“How many should we make?” I asked.
“Let’s start with two for now.
…It’s fine, I’ll carry them. Could you cut some rope from over there?” Suzuki-san said.
“Got it.”
—There was a heavy-duty pair of scissors in the toolbox… the spring-loaded kind.
I headed back to the workbench and pulled the scissors out.
The handles were coated in orange rubber.
Carrying them over to the shelves, I cut the striped safety rope into suitable lengths.
Meanwhile, Suzuki-san carried the acrylic sheets over to the workbench.
“Alright, I’m gonna drill the holes, so could you hold the acrylic steady for me?” Suzuki-san said.
“Okay.” I answered as I grabbed one edge of the sheet.
Suzuki-san fitted a drill bit—about ten millimeters wide—into the cordless driver and began drilling into the acrylic sheet resting on the workbench.
“If you force it too hard, these things crack.” he muttered while drilling.
Even as he said it, tiny fractures spread from the edge of the hole.
Pale wood shavings scattered across the acrylic sheet.
—Ah… he’s drilling straight into the workbench top too.
He drilled a second hole.
After finishing the first sheet, we moved it aside and repeated the process on the second one.
I held the opposite side flat while he worked.
Finally, Suzuki-san finished drilling both shields.
I didn’t want the rope ends to fray, so I took the disposable lighter out of the Shiroi Koibito tin sitting beside the vise and lit it.
Holding the flame to the rope ends, I melted the countless nylon fibers together into hardened tips.
A strange burnt nylon smell drifted through the room.
The kind of smell that felt unhealthy just breathing it in.
After sealing both ends, I handed one piece of rope to Suzuki-san.
He threaded it through the holes in the acrylic sheet, making a handle.
I heated the ends of the second rope as well, then threaded it through the holes of the other acrylic sheet leaning nearby.
After passing both ends through, I flipped the shield over and tied the rope ends together behind it.
I folded one end back, threaded the other end upward beneath the loop, brought it downward again, passed it under the folded section once more, then fed the tip from top to bottom through the opposite side of the loop.
Finally, I pulled both ends tight, locking the knot firmly in place.
“Alright… let’s go.” Suzuki-san said as he watched me finish the knot.

Together, we loaded the equipment onto the handcart: the two acrylic shields, the portable generator, the pair of conduit spears, and the parallel cords attached to them.
Suzuki-san pushed the cart while I steadied the load so nothing would fall off.
When we reached the two-step landing near the exit, Suzuki-san pushed down on the handcart’s handlebar, lifting the front wheels up onto the edge of the second step.
Keeping the momentum, he shoved the cart forward and brought the rear wheels up onto the first step.
Then he pushed the handle down once more, raising the front again and rolling the front wheels onto the top landing before forcing the rear wheels up after them.
The whole time, I kept both hands on the cargo to stop it from sliding loose.
I twisted the lever on the heavy steel door and pushed it open.
While I held it there, Suzuki-san maneuvered the cart out into the hallway.
“Oh, I’ll get it.” I said when Suzuki-san started to turn back toward the generator room.
I realized he was probably going to switch off the fluorescent lights.
I reached over and flicked the switch.
The generator room instantly fell dark.
Then I stepped out into the hallway through the steel door Suzuki-san had left open for me.

The dining hall windows are double-paned, so if that thing gets in, it’ll probably be through either the entrance or the washroom exit.” Suzuki-san spoke briefly. The way he talked was efficient, almost reassuring. “So if we leave the cart and shields at the crossroads, we can respond no matter which way it comes from.” “Oh… maybe we didn’t need to bring the generator after all.” I said, glancing at the electrical outlet mounted low on the hallway wall. “Yeah, maybe. But if we short something out, the breaker’ll probably trip, right? If the generator takes the hit instead, at least we won’t lose the lights.” Suzuki Sho-san replied. “That’s true.” I answered. We pushed the cart out into the middle of the crossroads. The acrylic shields on top reflected a dull sheen beneath the hallway lights, the spears lying beside them. The cart’s wheels gave a soft skrrrkk sound against the wooden floorboards, and I lightly braced one foot against it to keep it from rolling. Faint fingerprints from my sweaty hands remained on the surface of the shield. “From here, we can throw this stuff toward either the entrance or the washroom fast. One person watches this corner, another watches the other side — that should cover us for now.” Suzuki-san continued, calmly giving instructions. “Then let’s head back to the dining hall and tell everyone. After that, maybe the lodge owner should decide what we do next.” Suzuki-san said. “Yeah. Let’s do that.” I replied. With the preparations complete, Suzuki-san and I slowly headed back toward the dining hall— leaving the cart and shields behind at the crossroads.
