Entrance

It happened then.
“…Hey… doesn’t it sound noisy near the entrance?”
Kobayashi Ren-san frowned slightly, and the moment I heard his voice, my heart lurched.
The dining hall fell silent at once as everyone strained their ears.
Mixed into the roar of the storm outside came the sound of rattling glass from somewhere near the entrance.
Gacha-gacha…
It wasn’t the sound of rain or wind.
It sounded deliberate.
Somehow, I had already felt that myself—but the moment Kobayashi Ren-san spoke, I realized it hadn’t been my imagination after all, and something twisted uneasily deep inside my chest.
“…Is someone near the entrance?”
Morishita Aya-san whispered under her breath.
Gata… gata… gasha—
From the direction of the reception area—the entrance—I could hear the violent sound of glass doors being shaken.
And mixed in between those noises…
I thought I could hear several human voices.
At times they sounded like cries for help.
At others, like faint whispering.
“…Don’t tell me there are other climbers outside…?”
Morishita Aya-chan murmured in a trembling voice.
But no one answered her.
The lodge owner slowly stood up, and the ceiling lights cast his shadow long across the wall.
“I’ll go check.”
His voice was low and trembling, but even so, I could feel the sense of responsibility driving him forward.
The words tightened painfully around my chest.
I knew he had to be terrified.
And yet, just seeing someone willing to move made me feel relieved.
Watching his back as he forced himself to stand because of that responsibility warmed something inside me, if only a little.
But then, the instant the ceiling light illuminated only half of his face, for some reason he suddenly looked like a corpse.
My throat went dry.
“I’m coming too.”
Suzuki Sho-san immediately stood up.
His eyes were filled with determination, yet somewhere inside them lingered the darkness of someone who had already accepted what might happen.
Kobayashi Ren-san stood as well.
Then Morishita Aya-san rose too.
“Morishita-san, maybe you should stay here…” Kobayashi Ren-san said.
“I’m going too. I work at this lodge too.”
Morishita Aya-san answered firmly.
—I’m glad I wasn’t someone who worked at this lodge.
Somewhere deep down, part of me felt relieved.
Just as the four of them started toward the entrance, Suzuki Sho-san turned back and looked at me.
“Sho… will you come with us? We might need those weapons.”
At those words, it felt as though my heart stopped for a moment.
—So… after all, I have to go too…
But honestly, that was only natural.
After all, I was the one who had discovered that thing in the first place…
I have to do what I can too…
“The weapon” Suzuki-san and I had made together—
the one we had fashioned from metal pipes taken from the power room, shaped into crude spears.
We might need them now.
Back then, I never imagined that thing could take human form.
Would a spear carrying barely a hundred volts even work against something that looked human?
And even if it did…
Could I really drive that spear into something wearing Akane-san’s face?
“…Okay.”
Even my own voice trembled as I answered.
I didn’t want to go.
But I knew I had no choice.
The feeling stiffened my stomach and lower back with dread.
The sound of chairs scraping against the floor mixed with approaching footsteps.
The air inside the dining hall remained painfully tense, everyone holding back even the sound of their breathing.
From beyond the wall—from the direction of the reception area—the sound of straining wood and rattling glass came again.
Gashan… gata-gata-gata…
There was no mistaking it now.
Someone—or something—was trying to force the door open.

“Let’s go.”
The lodge owner turned back toward us, his low voice echoing through the silence.
We moved through the dining hall, weaving between the tables and wooden pillars as we headed for the still-open doorway.
Behind us, the uneasy murmuring of the people left behind spread softly through the room before gradually fading into the distance.
Wearing only our slippers, we walked down the hallway toward the intersection.
Ahead to the left, I could see the fire hoses lying in a chaotic heap on the floor in front of the still-open fire hose cabinet.
…Maybe Kobayashi Ren-san simply hadn’t had the strength left to fold those hoses back into the box.
That thought crossed my mind.
“…We don’t know what’s out there. Let’s get ready, just in case.”
At Suzuki Sho-san’s voice, I nodded and picked up pair of the spears lying on the floor.
The sticky feel of adhesive seeping from beneath the layers of vinyl tape clung to my palm, while the coldness of the metal bled through it.
A split white electrical cord extended from each spear.
The cords ran from the inside of a neatly coiled bundle resting atop a cart.
At the opposite ends, protruding from the outer side of the bundle, were plugs connected directly to the generator.
If I accidentally touched any exposed section without tape, I could electrocute myself.
And if the metal tips of the two spears touched each other, they would short-circuit and blow the generator’s fuse.
Just imagining that made the uncertainty of what might happen next feel even heavier.
Suzuki-san moved to start the generator.
The sound of him yanking the black plastic T-shaped handle rang unnaturally sharp through the hallway.
The moment the white pull-cord snapped outward, the generator roared to life, and vibrations traveled up through the floor into my legs.
That low mechanical growl mixed with the storm outside, making it feel as though something had awakened.
The smell of hot oil and scorched metal filled the air, stinging the back of my throat.

While Suzuki Sho-san and I prepared the weapons, the lodge owner, Kobayashi Ren-san, and Morishita Aya-san began walking toward the entrance.
Suzuki Sho-san pushed the generator loaded onto the cart toward the front entrance, and I followed beside him carrying the pair of spears.
I could feel the heavy weight of the metal pipes in both hands.
Still wearing their sandals and sneakers, the lodge owner and the others stepped down onto the concrete dirt-floor area of the entranceway and stopped in front of the sliding doors.
Suzuki Sho-san left the cart at the edge of the hallway, one step higher than the concrete floor, while I climbed down still wearing my slippers, pulling electrical cord after cord from the bundled coils atop the cart as I approached the others.
Suzuki Sho-san came down onto the concrete floor carrying the two shields we had made from acrylic boards.
Rain and wind slammed violently against the entrance doors while whoever was outside rattled them relentlessly.
Reflected in the dark glass were the figures of the lodge owner, Kobayashi Ren-san, Morishita Aya-san, and Suzuki Sho-san and me preparing our weapons.
Nothing beyond the glass was visible at all.
Mixed into the roar of the storm came the clattering of the doors being shaken and the sound of several voices together, making unease churn deep inside my chest.
“Sho, lend me the flashlight.”
The lodge owner spoke, and Suzuki Sho-san lowered one of the shields, steadying it with one hand while pulling a black flashlight from his pocket with the other.
He tossed it toward the owner, who caught it skillfully.
Suzuki Sho-san handed one of the acrylic shields to me, then held his hand out toward me without a word.
I passed one of the spears to him.
With the two spears finally separated, the danger of accidentally short-circuiting them eased a little.
And somehow, something that trivial made me feel just slightly relieved.
I held the acrylic shield by the striped rope handle with my left hand, while gripping the vinyl-taped section of the spear made from electrical conduit in my right.
The lodge owner clicked on the flashlight.
The instant the LED beam struck the glass, an intense white reflection burst into my eyes.
The scenery outside almost vanished inside the glare.
But when the owner pressed the face of the flashlight tightly against the glass door, the beam projected through the other side, and the reflection disappeared enough for us to finally see outside.
Rain hammered against the glass, the light scattering wildly through countless droplets.
Within the shifting white circle of light, I could make out a man and woman—the female-led couple.
They were the pair who had photographed us while we were digging up that thing, the same couple whose boyfriend had been getting scolded by the woman while trying to set up their tent when we arrived at the lodge.
There too were the three college guys who had been doing their ridiculous “I can do it!” comedy routine earlier.
And beside them stood an older man, maybe in his seventies.
I didn’t think I had seen him before.
All of them stood there squinting into the light as though trying to shield their eyes from it.
—Ah… these are the people who were staying in tents outside…
And Akane-san…?
Akane-san’s figure was nowhere among them.
A faint wave of relief loosened the strength from my body.
Beyond them, distant mountain silhouettes and the rocky ground nearby emerged dimly through the storm like blurred shadows.
The collapsed tents nearby and the soaked backpacks scattered around them appeared sharply illuminated by the flashlight beam, while the distant ridgelines dissolved into vague gray shapes.
The harder I stared into the darkness, the more sharply I became aware of the difference between near and far.
And with it came the unsettling illusion that something might be hiding just beyond the edge of the light.

Bang!
The sliding door rattled violently, and wind burst through the gap, striking against my body.
“Please, help us…! Our tent got blown away in the storm!”
One of the three college guys—the playful one—shouted from outside the sliding door.
“Ah… so it’s the campers.”
The lodge owner let out a relieved breath and lowered the flashlight, reaching for the lock.
“But that Akane person from before wasn’t human, right? …How do we know everyone out there is really human?”
Morishita Aya-san placed a hand on the owner’s arm as he tried to unlock the door.
…Now that she mentioned it, she was right.
That thing had returned wearing Akane-san’s face.
…That’s right.
And after that, I thought I heard a man scream beneath the sound of the storm.
What if that hadn’t been my imagination after all…?
What if the thing pretending to be Akane-san had attacked someone out there…
…and was now wearing their face too…?
The thought sent a chill racing down my spine.
My body tensed again, and I instinctively tightened my grip around the spear in my right hand.
“…She’s right. If that monster is mixed in among them…”
Kobayashi Ren-san added grimly, and Suzuki Sho-san immediately nodded in agreement.
“It’s too dangerous to let them inside!”

“Please, open the door! We want to take shelter in here!”
The voice came from the woman of the female-led couple—the same pair who had taken pictures of us while we were digging up that thing.
“The rain’s getting worse! We’re freezing out here!” her boyfriend shouted beside her.
…But it should be strangely warm outside.
That thought crossed my mind.
Was I only thinking that because somewhere inside, I didn’t want to let them in?

The lodge owner folded his arms and stood there thinking with a grim expression.
“But can we really just leave them out there? There’s a storm raging outside… If we abandon them like this, there’s no telling what’ll happen to them.”
“But… but…”
Morishita Aya-san’s voice trembled.
“What if that monster is mixed in among them…?”

“Why won’t you let us in!?”
The woman from the couple screamed from the other side of the sliding door.
“We’re getting drenched out here, freezing in the wind! Please—we can’t just stand here waiting! Hurry! We just want somewhere safe to shelter too!”

The lodge owner frowned deeply and fell silent for a moment, thinking.
“But… we can’t just abandon them. Keeping people safe on this mountain is my responsibility.”
Kobayashi Ren-san narrowed his eyes.
“If we let them inside and that creature is mixed in among them, everyone here could be in danger. Safety has to come first.”
Suzuki Sho-san nodded in agreement.
“To be honest… the thought of letting them in scares me too.”
One of those people outside might already be that thing in disguise.
And yet there was no way to tell who was human and who was not.
For some reason, I suddenly remembered the believers of the Banrikyo cult back at my company.
You could never tell who believed and who didn’t just by looking at them.
And the thought crossed my mind that perhaps, hidden among these people too, there might already be some monster quietly concealing itself—just like a hidden Banrikyo believer trying to lure and recruit others—waiting for a chance to attack humans from within.

“Hey, just look at the weather out here! It’s dangerous to leave us like this!”
The sturdily built, short-haired guy from the “I can do it!” trio shouted through the door.
“Come on, man, just open it already!”
the playful guy from the group pleaded.
“What are you hesitating for? Aren’t you supposed to protect climbers?”
said the chubby, round-glasses guy from the trio.

“Man… if we can’t even make a decision here, leaving people outside after they came begging for help feels pretty damn cold, doesn’t it?”
The moment I heard that voice, I flinched slightly.
It didn’t sound like one of the people already gathered here.
When I turned toward the voice, I realized it was the athletic guy.
When had he come over?
Like a curious onlooker drawn in by the commotion…
“But… if we let them inside, and something happens…”
Kobayashi Ren-san spoke hesitantly.
“Then that becomes our responsibility too.”
…He was right.
If one of them—or all of them—was that thing, there was no telling what might happen if we opened the door.
They might attack us one after another until everyone here became one of those things.
The horrific scene in the washroom—the people engulfed by writhing black tentacles—flashed vividly through my mind again.
I could feel my whole body trembling.
My stomach and lower back quivered violently, and my legs felt ready to give out beneath me.
…I can’t fall apart now.
…Get a hold of yourself…
…You have to keep it together.
I repeated the words desperately to myself.
“…What are we supposed to do? Let them in… or keep them out…”
Suzuki Sho muttered heavily, his fist clenched tight.

“This is a mountain lodge, isn’t it? Isn’t protecting people your responsibility? Leaving us outside in weather like this could get us killed!”
The man who looked to be in his seventies shouted through the storm.
“Seriously, man, hurry up and help us already!”
the playful guy from the “I can do it!” trio yelled desperately.
…They were right.
If these people weren’t those things at all…
If they were just ordinary climbers caught in this storm after their tents had collapsed…
And we refused to let them inside…
If we ended up leaving them here to die…
The thought crushed painfully against my chest.
It hurt so badly it felt hard to breathe.

For a brief moment, silence fell.
Only the sound of rain could be heard outside.
The lodge owner let out a long breath, then spoke as though he had finally made up his mind.
“That’s enough. It’s my responsibility to protect climbers.”
Clack-clack-clack-clack…
The sound of the lock turning echoed through the entranceway, and the sliding door slowly began to open.
The instant it did, rain and wind burst violently into the lodge.
My hair whipped across my face, and I could actually feel my cheeks ripple in the gust.
The acrylic shield in my hand was nearly torn away by the wind, and I hurriedly braced it with the same hand holding the spear.
I had thought the air outside would be strangely warm.
But the wind pouring in was cold.
Wearing nothing but a short-sleeved T-shirt and shorts, I shuddered involuntarily.
The reflection of the lodge interior slid apart across the glass, and darkness opened between it.
Within the entrance light, the faces of the campers outside slowly emerged—the female-led couple, the three college guys, and the lone climber in his seventies.
Fear and relief were mixed together in their eyes.
…That’s right.
That black mass in the washroom was gone now.
Which meant it was still somewhere outside.
Even if these people really were ordinary humans…
there was no telling when or from where that thing might attack.
“Be careful!” I shouted instinctively.
“That thing is still out there…”
“Get inside quickly! It’s dangerous out there!”
Suzuki Sho-san glanced at me for only a second and nodded.
Maybe once the lodge owner had already decided to let them in, he figured there was no point hesitating anymore.
He shouted toward the people outside, and the drenched climbers rushed into the lodge with their backpacks still strapped to their shoulders.
But Suzuki Sho-san, Kobayashi Ren-san, Morishita Aya-san—even the lodge owner himself—all instinctively stepped backward, putting distance between themselves and the newcomers as though something dangerous had just entered the building.
And of course… so did I.
It wasn’t even a conscious decision.
Before I realized it, I had already backed away from the people who had come inside.
Along with the cold wind came a collapse of warm, damp air.
The smell of soaked clothing.
The smell of wet earth.
And beneath it all… something metallic.
Like rust.
My heart pounded painfully hard.
Human body heat was finally close enough to touch.
And yet, somehow, I couldn’t feel relieved.
Inside me, an alarm kept quietly ringing.
Something is wrong.

“Damn, it’s freezing…!”
Shivering violently in his soaked clothes, the playful guy from the trio let out a shaky yell.
“My fingers are seriously going numb!”
The sturdily built, short-haired guy hunched his shoulders tightly against the cold.
“It’s way too cold… I’m seriously at my limit…”
Even the chubby guy with the round glasses was trembling as he spoke.
Once everyone was finally inside, Kobayashi Ren-san hurriedly locked the entrance door again.
“Why the hell did you make us wait so long!?”
The playful guy snapped irritably, and the short-haired guy and the round-glasses guy immediately joined in complaining.
“Seriously, you should’ve let us in way sooner!”
“Give us a break already… our bodies can’t take this anymore!”
“Why’d you take so damn long!? It’s freezing outside!”
The playful guy shouted again, his drenched body still shaking.
“W-Well… I’m very sorry… there were… circumstances…”
The lodge owner stumbled awkwardly over his words.
“Honestly…”
Muttering that, the playful guy glanced back and forth between me and Suzuki Sho-san with a puzzled expression.
“So what’s with these two? …And what do you mean it’s dangerous outside?”
He was obviously staring at the acrylic shields and the spears made from metal conduit pipes that Suzuki Sho-san and I were holding.
If he were one of those things, he probably wouldn’t be looking at our weapons with that kind of confused suspicion.
…Thank God.
For just a moment, relief spread through my chest.
These people are human.
“There’s a dangerous creature outside… We’re carrying these just in case.”
Suzuki Sho-san explained calmly.
“What the hell kind of dangerous creature are you talking about…?”
the short-haired guy asked uneasily.
“I don’t care what it is. Just let us change already.”
said the man in his seventies impatiently.
“Well, anyway… please take off your shoes and come inside properly. Aya-chan, could you bring these people some blankets for now?”
the lodge owner said.
He glanced toward Morishita Aya-san and gave a small motion with his hand.
Morishita-san nodded back at him.
“I’ll help too,”
Kobayashi Ren-san added.
Morishita-san and Kobayashi-san stepped up from the concrete entryway onto the wooden corridor and headed toward the dining hall at the left side of the intersection.

The people who had just come inside sat down along the edge of the corridor and began pulling off their soaking wet shoes.
The playful guy rubbed his feet with both hands and let out a small sigh.
“Whew… my feet are finally starting to warm up…”
The sturdily built short-haired guy and the chubby guy with the round glasses took off their shoes as well.
While they were distracted, Suzuki Sho-san quietly switched off the generator and gave me a small glance.
I immediately understood it as a signal.
Put the weapons away.
I took the spear from him, handed him one of the acrylic shields in exchange, and stepped up onto the corridor in my slippers.

Morishita Aya-san and Kobayashi Ren-san returned from the direction of the office carrying folded blankets colored in brown and ocher stripes.
Under normal circumstances, they probably would have draped them over the climbers’ shoulders themselves, maybe even offered them gently with a quiet, “Here, please use these.”
But instead, the two of them simply placed the blankets behind the newcomers and let go quickly, carefully keeping their distance.
Watching them quietly set the blankets down and step back again stirred a faint sense of unease deep inside my chest.
If they had wanted to, they could have stepped closer and helped.
And yet no one did.
Because somewhere in everyone’s mind lingered the same fear:
What if one of them is that thing?
That caution was creating distance between us.
“If you’re cold, please use the blankets,”
Morishita-san said softly.
The man who looked to be around seventy frowned with visible dissatisfaction—whether because they had kept him waiting outside so long, or because no one would even place the blanket around his shoulders themselves, I couldn’t tell.
Still, he grabbed one of the blankets, draped it over himself, and wrapped it tightly around his body.

The lodge owner began explaining the situation to the newcomers.
“For changing clothes… yes, use the office over there. There’s a dangerous creature outside, so absolutely do not go back out. And… because of that, we’ve gathered all the guests together in the dining hall. Once you’ve changed, please come there as well.”
The campers who had just entered grumbled among themselves as they stepped up from the concrete entryway onto the corridor, slipped into the prepared slippers, and headed toward the office the owner pointed to—just to the right after turning at the intersection toward the dining hall.
Steam rose faintly from their backs, and water dripped steadily onto the wooden floorboards beneath them.
Wet slipper prints stretched in long trails down the corridor.
“Was there a fire or something?”
asked the sturdily built, short-haired guy from the trio.
There was more simple surprise in his voice than actual concern.
He had probably noticed the fire hose still pulled out from the hydrant near the intersection, lying there in a rough tangled heap.
“Well… yes, there was a small flare-up in the dining hall while refueling the generator,”
the lodge owner replied with a smile.
But at the edge of that smile, I caught a faint stiffness.
“But everything’s under control now. The fire’s out, and no one was injured. Please, go on inside.”
The short-haired guy nodded in relief, but listening to the exchange left a rough unease scratching quietly at the back of my mind.
…Naoki-kun’s body should’ve still been outside near the entrance.
Didn’t they see it?
If they had, surely somebody would’ve said something…
The thought suddenly crossed my mind.
Suzuki Sho-san stacked the two acrylic shields together onto the cart and pushed it back toward the intersection.
Carrying the two spears, I followed after him and set them down on the floor where he stopped the cart.
The lodge owner, Morishita Aya-san, and Kobayashi Ren-san headed back toward the dining hall.
The female-led couple, the three college guys, and the lone older climber entered the office near the intersection after opening the door.
The men and women all went in together.
Were they really going to change clothes in the same room?
…Well, that was their problem.
I didn’t care.
My body felt completely exhausted.
Together with Suzuki Sho-san, I headed back toward the dining hall too.

Yellow light.
Wooden walls. Wooden ceiling. Wooden floorboards stretching across the wide dining hall.
In the windows at the far end of the dining hall, the reflections of tables and pillars shimmered faintly against the darkness outside. When we returned to the dining hall, the air somehow felt even heavier than before.
Wet clothes clung to the skin.
Damp slippers stuck faintly against the floorboards.
Outside, the storm battered the walls hard enough to make the whole lodge tremble.
The dining hall in front of me no longer looked like a place where people relaxed after climbing.
It looked like an evacuation shelter.
The long rectangular tables were supposed to be lined up neatly, yet several had been dragged out of place, chairs shifted crookedly around them.
Seen from the entrance on the reception side, the eight-person tables were arranged three deep toward the back, split four seats on each side, with five rows stretching toward the right—fifteen tables in total.
Eight seats each.
If every chair were occupied, the room could hold one hundred and twenty people.
The table closest to the entrance.
The athletic guy and the jeans-and-glasses guy sat facing one another there.
Both sat with their arms folded, staring ahead in silence.
The athletic guy looked like he was about to crush the plastic bottle in his hand.
At the next table farther in,
Dr. Uchida sat at the far end on the right side.
In front of her sat Takamura-san, the former nurse.
And beside Takamura-san sat Mari-senpai.
The three of them huddled together almost like a family.
Having lost Akane-san, Takamura-san sat with her head lowered, gripping the back of one hand tightly with the other.
Dr. Uchida and Mari-senpai kept their hands gently on her shoulders from either side, supporting her.
Closer to the front sat Takizawa-san.
His face looked calm, but his clenched hands never moved.
Across from him sat Tsuruoka-san.
He repeatedly opened his mouth as though about to say something, only to shut it again.
Farther back sat Yohei.
His leg bounced restlessly beneath the table.
Beyond him sat Yabe-san, arms folded, staring silently at a single point somewhere in space.
At the table by the windows farther in, a man in his sixties sat completely alone, absentmindedly rubbing a paper cup with his wrinkled fingers.
One table closer toward the kitchen from where the older man sat,
the two mountain-girl women in their twenties sat shoulder to shoulder together.
Until recently they had been cheerful and lively.
Now neither of them smiled anymore.
They leaned close together, occasionally glancing uneasily toward the kitchen.
The table one row closer to the front sat empty.
It was the table beside the one where Naoki-kun had been lying.
At the table in front of that, the father with the child sat holding the kid close, whispering softly over and over,
“It’s okay… it’s okay…”
One table closer to the reception side from there was the table where the athletic guy and the jeans-and-glasses guy sat.
On the center table lay the stretcher we had used to carry Naoki-kun outside.
No one sat near it now.
But the white stretcher sheet still glistened faintly with the slime that had once coated the surface of Naoki-kun’s body.
And beside the table one row closer to the corridor than the stretcher—
the burned remains of where Naoki-kun had caught fire still stained the floor.
Part of the wood remained blackened and charred, still damp in places, while even the nearby chair legs were streaked dark with soot.
Just looking at it made the back of my throat sting.
It felt like his screams were echoing again deep inside my ears.
As though nobody wanted to remember what had happened there, everyone had unconsciously gathered around the reception-side tables instead, avoiding the area where Naoki-kun had lain.
No one spoke.
Every gaze in the room had turned toward us—the group that had gone to check the entrance.
“How was it…?”
someone whispered softly.
But nobody answered.

I stood there for a moment, silently taking in the entire dining hall.
The air felt unbearably heavy.
Everyone looked like they wanted to say something, only to swallow the words back down.
Even the creaking of chairs sounded unnaturally loud.
As I started toward the table where Takizawa-san and the others sat, my eyes drifted toward Tsuruoka-san.
He sat with both elbows planted on the table, wearing a grim expression.
Was he trying to think up excuses for setting Naoki-kun on fire?
I stopped for just a second and swallowed hard.
Part of it was because I had never been comfortable around him to begin with…
but somehow I simply couldn’t bring myself to sit beside him now.
Something inside my chest felt rough and unsettled.
After a brief hesitation, my gaze shifted toward the entrance-side table where the athletic guy and the jeans-and-glasses guy were sitting.
One seat near the edge—closer to Tsuruoka-san’s side—was empty.
Without saying anything, I walked over there instead.
When I lightly pulled back the chair, its wooden legs scraped against the floor with a low sound.
Someone swallowed audibly.
Someone else coughed quietly.
I sat down in silence and placed both hands on the table.
There was no warmth left there anymore.

The lodge owner, Kobayashi Ren-san, Morishita Aya-san, and Suzuki Sho-san didn’t sit at any of the tables.
Instead, they walked toward the kitchen, weaving their way between the rows of tables and wooden pillars.
“What should we do about this…?” Suzuki Sho-san murmured quietly.
The thing he was looking at lay beyond the table where Naoki-kun had been resting.
That area should still have what Naoki-kun had vomited there.
“Be careful. It still might… attack someone,” Kobayashi Ren-san warned in a low voice as he stepped forward.
His words carried heavily through the room, not even drowned out by the sound of the storm.
Morishita Aya-san instinctively drew back and bit her lip.
For a brief moment, silence fell between the three of them.
—There’s no way vomit could attack people…
That was what I thought.
But the truth was, none of us really understood what that thing was.
And because of that, I couldn’t say for certain that nothing would happen.
Eventually, the lodge owner lowered his shoulders and muttered while staring at the floor,
“I don’t feel like cleaning it up right now… Let’s rest a little first.”
Morishita Aya-san nodded, and Suzuki Sho-san gave a weary shrug.
The lodge staff moved from the kitchen side of the table where Naoki-kun had been lying back toward the reception side, then sat around the empty table between Naoki-kun’s table and the one where Uchida-san, Takamura-san, Takizawa-san, and the rest of our group were seated.
The table between them—between the lodge owner and Morishita Aya-san on Naoki-kun’s side, and Kobayashi Ren-san and Suzuki Sho-san on Takizawa-san’s side—felt almost like a boundary line separating tension from exhaustion.
The lodge owner lightly clapped his hands once, and the people around him naturally settled their breathing.
Within the silence, each person sank into their own thoughts while remaining constantly aware of everyone else around them.
“…Even just sitting like this helps calm me down a little,” Morishita Aya-san murmured softly.
“Yeah,” Suzuki Sho-san replied, spreading both hands across the table before letting out a deep breath.
They remained seated there for a while.
The sound of the wind outside almost seemed to grant their exhausted minds a brief, quiet pause.
The breathing of the others blended into the silence of the room, drifting through the heavy, damp air.
