大雪渓

Stepping onto the snow—just thinking about it made my chest beat a little faster.
It reminded me of the first time I ever stepped on fresh snow as a kid in winter.
But when I stood at the edge of the glacier, the bright white sparkle I had seen from afar vanished, replaced by a surprisingly stark reality.
The snow surface was pitted, as if countless balls had been pressed into it, and the raised portions were soiled and dulled to a muddy black.
A faint red line stretched across the snow, seeming to mark our path.
Takizawa-san led the way, followed by Mari-senpai, Yabe-san, me, Yohei, and finally Tsuruoka-san at the rear.
Until the very moment I would take my first step onto the snow, I pictured in my mind the sound my crampons would make.
Takizawa-san, Mari-senpai, and Yabe-san stepped onto the glacier one after another.
I was right behind them in third position, with Yohei trailing me and Tsuruoka-san even further back.
I planted my first step onto the snow.
Crunch… crunch…
The crampon spikes bit into the snow, sending a firm, cold sensation up through my soles.
Small chunks of snow broke off and tumbled sideways from my feet.
A cold wind rushed down the valley, brushing my cheeks and chilling the back of my neck.
I shivered slightly, yet somehow a light, buoyant feeling filled my chest.
—Walking on the glacier.
The scenes I had only ever seen on TV or in books were now right beneath my feet.
A strange, thrilling sense of disbelief, mixed with a faint surge of excitement.
When I lifted my head, I saw multiple ridges jutting out from the flanking mountain lines, and between them the white glacier snaked zigzag toward the summit.

Suddenly, far up ahead… deep into the snowy slope, something caught the sunlight for just a moment and flashed.
—What was that? I wondered as my eyes caught the glint.
It was such a fleeting moment that I almost stopped in my tracks.
But Yabe-san’s back, walking just ahead of me, was right in front of my eyes, and I hurriedly lowered my gaze back to the snow beneath my feet.

Crunch… crunch…
The sound melted into the quiet valley air as our line slowly continued upward.
Mari-senpai, rounding the switchback, glanced back down at me and smiled warmly.
—She’s actually pretty glamorous… I thought, noticing Mari-senpai’s chest from the side.
Wait, what am I thinking? I chastised myself.
I turned again with the curve, focusing on Yabe-san’s back and catching glimpses of Mari-senpai’s back ahead.
Crunch, crunch… The crampon spikes bit into the snow, and the cold air brushed my cheeks.
Snowflakes kicked up with each step slipped into my boots and soaked my socks, chilling my feet.
Ah, that’s why Mari-senpai had mentioned wearing gaiters, I realized.
Mari-senpai’s back, carrying a purple backpack, appeared and disappeared behind Yabe-san’s black backpack, standing out sharply against the white snow and clear blue sky.
With practiced steps, she moved lightly over the unstable snow.
Watching her back, my mind was suddenly drawn to a warm memory.

That day, under the full-bloom cherry trees of Yoshino Mountain, Mari-senpai stood beside me in the bustling crowd.
“Let’s come again together next year,” she had promised, and those words still linger in the back of my ears.
In the midst of the murmuring crowd, it was her voice, speaking only to me.
I still believe it was a special moment.
Now, the white snow before me and the memory of cherry blossoms seemed to intersect, quietly igniting a warm glow in my chest.

Looking down at my feet, I saw the pale reddish-brown line trailing behind on the snow-stained surface, and my smoky green Caravan boots alternately stepping forward.
The crampons I was wearing had spikes only on the arches of my feet, leaving the other parts without any spikes.
To avoid slipping, I had to place my feet flat on the snow so the arch could land properly, but I kept unconsciously kicking backward, leaving the spikes unengaged and my force slipping away. The sensation underfoot felt unreliable.
My stride naturally shortened, and my movements grew awkward.
—I have to dig the spikes into the snow more firmly… I thought, frustrated at myself for not walking as steadily as I wanted.

The surface of the snow was not just pure white; it felt like a cushion packed with fine particles, forming depressions as if countless volleyballs or soccer balls had been pressed into it.
The effect was like a frozen, undulating sea painted in white.
Along the ridges connecting the depressions, dirt, fine wood chips, and small stones stained the snow black, adding a touch of realistic life to the otherwise pure white surface.
I looked up, and the full expanse of the glacier unfolded before me.
The ridges on either side were dotted with green trees, full of vitality.
But what drew my eye even more were the gray stones and rocks, exposed and forming rugged ridgelines.
The ridges overlapped in multiple layers, and the glacier zigzagged its way through them up toward the summit.
The contrast of the blue sky, green nature, and gray rocky ridges created a scene of overwhelming beauty and harshness at the same time.

“Hey, what’s that over there?” Yohei suddenly called out from behind me, and Takizawa replied in his slightly Nagoya-accented voice, “Ah, it’s shining, isn’t it!”
―Ah, that must be the same light I saw up there a moment ago, I thought.
Mari-senpai tilted her head and said, “Really, that’s a strange way for it to shine.”
“I thought so too,” I said.
“Isn’t it a car wheel cap?” Tsuruoka said, looking up from the lower left just before the turn.
“Tsuru-san, there’s no way a car wheel cap would be up there,” Takizawa-san, at the front, replied with a light chuckle.
“…How does anyone even think of a car wheel cap?” Yabe-san, in front of me, muttered, and everyone held back a laugh for a moment, easing the tension.
Compared to a moment ago, the shining object was gradually getting closer.
A shining object… well, maybe it’s just a piece of broken mirror or aluminum foil or something like that, I thought.

Takizawa-san turned forward and started walking again, and following him, Mari-senpai and Yabe-san moved ahead. I started walking once more as well.
The snow beneath my feet received the crampon spikes with a crisp, crunchy rhythm, the tiny sounds of breaking snow keeping time like a steady beat.
The cold, tightened air filled my lungs, and my breath escaped in white. The clear chill felt refreshing, and the sense that I was truly walking deep in nature kept swelling inside me.
My smoky green Caravan boots stepped forward one after the other, and the trail of my own footprints stretched straight across the snow behind me.
Around us, the mountain remained wrapped in the same unbroken silence. The green trees along the ridge to my right and the gray rock face to the left looked almost like part of a slow, motionless film scene.
Now and then, the wind sweeping overhead brushed coldly across my cheeks and parted my hair.
My body was beginning to grow damp with sweat, and the backpack against my back felt a little heavier, but I told myself that too was proof that I was truly climbing the mountain.
When I glanced back, the footprints carved by my crampons had already grown small, trailing away behind me as a line across the white snow.
The place where we had first put on our crampons looked tiny far below.
Without realizing it, we had climbed quite a lot, and a faint sense of pride rose in my chest.
When I looked forward again, the glacier continued in endless zigzags, with the ridgelines on both sides seeming to press closer.
Above us, the sky spread out in a deep, powerful blue.
Alright… just one more push.
But I could feel fatigue beginning to settle into every part of my body.
My thighs and calves started to ache, and my breathing grew rougher.
My shoulders were tight under the weight of the backpack, and even the muscles in my arms throbbed faintly.
Even in the cold air, sweat was slowly beading on my skin.
When I looked at my watch, I realized it had long been over an hour since we stepped onto the glacier.
A small complaint slipped through my mind.
—He said we’d take a break every hour, but we’re not stopping at all…
I wish someone would say we should rest…
Even without putting it into words, my body was honestly begging for it.
A small wave of frustration began to spread through my chest, but at the same time, another voice inside me urged me onward.
—I can still go a little farther, I told myself.
The snow beneath my feet blurred, and the foot I tried to step forward with lagged just slightly behind.

With each breath, the memory of that day quietly resurfaced.
The day I accidentally forgot to place the order for the solenoid valve from Hashimoto Industries.
I had been exhausted that day too.
Back then, I’d been buried in preparing materials for our QC activities.
Activities for quality control—that was the official idea, at least.
Finding small inefficiencies and minor defects on the shop floor, then improving them little by little.
That was what it was supposed to be.
But in reality, it had turned into little more than a hunt for “presentation material.”
Everyone hated becoming the QC leader.
Because everyone knew it was a lot of trouble for very little reward.
And somehow, in the flow of all that, the role ended up being handed to me.
I got flattered a little—“Sho, you can do it.”
And in the end, I couldn’t quite bring myself to refuse.
Once I actually started, it turned out to be a real headache.
There was never any clear finish line with QC activities.
Whether there was a real issue or not, we were expected to come up with some kind of improvement.
So we ended up forcing meaningless little things into “solutions.”
What I presented that time was just a slight change in how the tools were arranged.
Sure, it did make them a tiny bit easier to grab.
But when it came down to what had really changed… even I couldn’t honestly say.
Before I knew it, it had become the same thing over and over.
Things arranged vertically got turned sideways,
and the sideways things got turned vertical again.
Even so, it all had to be labeled as “improvement.”
Make the materials, give the presentation, get evaluated—
and that was the end of it.
Meanwhile, the time for the work that actually mattered kept getting eaten away.
And yet, if you made even one mistake, you got blamed properly for it.
…What was that even supposed to be?
We were supposed to be improving things, and yet somehow, something important kept getting worn away.
That feeling was what stayed with me most vividly.
That’s right.
It was because work was already busy, and on top of that I had to deal with QC too…

The solenoid valve, a critical component of the production line, had arrived late from the manufacturer, stopping the line and bringing a flood of complaints from customers.
I could still vividly remember Tsuruoka-san shouting at me and being publicly grilled in the company meeting afterward.
—I was sure I’d checked everything… so why did I forget? What was wrong with me…?
That panic and self-reproach rose from deep inside me as my strength began to fade.
My frustration at myself for missing the timing to place the order because my mind had stopped working under fatigue chilled my heart even more than the snow beneath my feet.
—Thinking about this while climbing only makes the exhaustion worse.
…No, wait.
Just a little while ago, everyone had been giving bitter smiles because Tsuruoka-san brought up work and ruined the mood.
We came here to enjoy ourselves, so why talk about work?
…And yet, what about me right now?
As I climbed the snow valley, my mind was filled with nothing but work.
—So I’m no different.
A small breath slipped from my lips before I realized it.

When I lifted my face again, another sharp flash ran across the upper part of the glacier ahead.
The light I had already noticed several times now appeared clearer than before, and a little larger too—proof that it was definitely getting closer.
It gleamed for only the briefest instant, then vanished again the next moment as we kept walking.
Again and again, the same pattern repeated.
Apparently, it was reflecting the sunlight depending on the angle from which it was seen.
Whenever the wind blew, fine grains of ice danced up from the surrounding snow, mingling with the light and confusing my eyes.
Even so, that single point of brilliance was different.
—What on earth is it?
Suppressing the urge to stop in my tracks, I narrowed my eyes and stared at it.

Another conversation with my grandfather came back to me.
It was about that night when I forgot to place the solenoid valve order with Hashimoto Industries and ended up stopping the production line.

“…Hey, Grandpa. Don’t things like that just mean the people on the floor are the ones forcing everything to keep running?”
“What do you mean, Sho?”
“Because companies have to make profits, and the government depends on companies… so it feels like all the strain eventually gets pushed down onto people like me.”
Grandpa thought for a moment before answering slowly.
“In a way, you could look at it like that. The ruling party often receives votes and money from companies, so policies can naturally drift in directions that are convenient for business.”
“Then… does that mean the media, the entertainment world, even organized crime and religion are all part of the same circle in the end?”
“Yes, some people see it that way. The media has to be mindful of sponsors, the entertainment industry moves with popularity and money, organized crime can get involved where money flows, and even religious groups sometimes have points of contact with governments and companies through land or financial management.”
“…So ordinary office workers like us are just being made to run around inside that circle without even knowing it?”
“It can certainly feel that way. But, Sho—once you’ve noticed it, you could also say you’re no longer being swept along by it completely.”
“When I think about how everything’s connected… it kind of frustrates me, but strangely enough, it’s also interesting.”
“Exactly. Rather than getting angry, it’s enough to first just observe how the system works. If you put too much force into it, you’ll only wear yourself out.”
With that, my grandfather smiled quietly.
…There I go again, thinking about things like this.

Haa… haa… haa… haa…
Crunch… crunch… crunch… crunch…
All I could hear was my own breathing and the sound of my boots sinking into the snow.
—Ah… my legs hurt. I’m exhausted.
My thighs burned with a dull heat, and my calves felt as if they had been packed with lead.
Every time I forced one foot forward, the muscles deep in my thighs let out a small silent scream.
I wanted to keep up with Yabe-san’s back, but the distance between us was slowly, steadily widening.
—How can everyone keep walking at the exact same pace like that?
Each time my breath grew ragged, a small sense of helplessness swelled in my chest.
Why am I so lacking in stamina…?
That thought clung to me, mixing with the exhaustion.
—…I wish someone would say, “Let’s take a break.”
I muttered it over and over in my mind.
That promise—“We’ll rest every hour”—had long since passed, and yet no one stopped.
My frustration and unease spread through me like sand being poured down my dry throat.
Being the leader must be nice, I thought bitterly.
When it got hot, he could stop whenever he wanted and take off a layer. If it got cold, he could put one back on. Even the timing of breaks was entirely his choice.
All I could do was keep following.
Footsteps approached from behind.
“Hey, Sho. We’re gonna leave you behind~”
With that easy voice, Yohei patted my shoulder lightly.
The warmth of his palm lingered for just a second on my sweat-chilled shoulder.
“It levels out up ahead. Hang in there!”
Leaving those words behind, Yohei passed me and moved lightly ahead.
The black pack on his back, with its rainbow-colored tape, swayed as he climbed.
What was it called again? Springdale, the one with the spring-daily?
I dragged my aching legs and watched his back for a while.
There was no hesitation in his stride.
It felt as though he existed on an entirely different plane from me.
A moment later, a cold wind brushed my cheek.
All I could hear was my own rough breathing and the faint sound of tree branches rubbing somewhere far away.
The silence around me suddenly felt unnaturally sharp, and the pounding of my own heart rang louder in my ears.
—Why am I this weak?
As the exhaustion tightened around my body, the shame pressed heavily against my chest.
Little by little, it began to feel as if I alone was being left behind, and the anxiety slowly spread.
I tried to close my eyes and take a deep breath, but for some reason I couldn’t breathe properly, and a restless unease began stirring deep in my chest.
Loneliness and impatience rushed over me, and the pain in my body only drove them deeper.
—I absolutely don’t want to be left behind… I can’t stop here.
I told myself that, tightened my shoulders hard, and forced my gaze back to the slope ahead.

I found myself recalling another conversation with my grandfather.

“Hey, Grandpa… the government, corporations, the media, the entertainment world, religious groups—they all depend on each other, right?
So… doesn’t that mean the way they think naturally starts leaning in the same direction too?”

“Ah, that’s one way of looking at it. And normally, the opposition parties should be the ones pushing back against that… but that side isn’t so simple either.”

“Not simple?”

“The support base for opposition parties is often made up of labor unions. But when it comes to unions in public institutions that truly prioritize everyone’s happiness, there actually aren’t that many.
There are far more unions tied to private companies, so the thinking naturally tends to prioritize their own gains and losses.”

“…Then in the end, does that mean the government and the opposition start saying basically the same things?”

“There are certainly moments when it can look that way.”

As I pressed my boots into the snow, I let the words that had been building inside my chest spill out.
—Why is it… in a world like this, no one seems to notice?
It’s like ordinary people are being held down from above.
They’re worked to the bone for cheap pay, and yet they still act like everything’s fine…
Having been bullied as a child, thoughts like that pierced straight into my heart.

“You’d naturally feel angry if that’s how it seems,” my grandfather said.

“To be honest, they start to look like villains to me. The government… and the people gathered around it.
When elections come close, they suddenly dangle a carrot and promise ‘good things are coming.’
And everyone gets made to run after that carrot… without even realizing they’ll be whipped afterward.”

My grandfather didn’t answer right away. After a short pause, he finally spoke.

“Yes… there are times when it can certainly look that way. But, Sho…
I think it’s not always that people don’t notice.
Sometimes, even when they do notice, they simply don’t know what they’re supposed to do about it.”

“…Sometimes it almost feels like people hate peace so much that they deliberately hurt each other.”

“It may be that, deep down, they don’t truly want to hurt anyone.
They may simply have something they’re trying to protect.
And that’s exactly what makes everything even more complicated.”

“…So a peaceful world where everyone can be happy really isn’t something simple after all.”

“That’s right. But knowing that is never meaningless.
Once you understand how the system works, even just a little, it lets you make different choices.”

“The world really is… so complicated.”

“Yes. But that’s precisely why it’s worth thinking about.”
With those words, my grandfather had smiled gently.

Crunch… crunch… crunch… crunch…
Footsteps began drawing closer from behind, and it felt as though I were being driven forward by them.
The sound came up on my right, and Tsuruoka-san pulled alongside me, then passed with a grin.
“Ohh, getting tired?”
I was suddenly wrapped in the sensation that I alone was being left farther and farther behind.
The inside of my chest turned cold, and a tightness slowly coiled deep in my lower stomach.
—What if… I get separated like this?
What if I’m the only one who can’t make it to the mountain hut?
Those impossible fears and the tension they brought only made my legs feel even heavier.
Anxiety and humiliation swirled inside my chest, and more than once I almost said, “Can we take a break?”
But the words never left my mouth.

A quick glance upward told me that Mari-senpai, walking just behind Takizawa-san, had looked back toward me.
She said something to Yabe-san, who was following behind her.
I couldn’t hear what they were saying, but from the seriousness in her expression and the gentle tone of her voice, I could tell she was worried about me.
Just seeing that made my heart feel a little lighter.
On a harsh mountain like this, knowing that someone was looking out for me—
that alone made me feel like I could keep going.
A moment later, Mari-senpai stopped.
Yabe-san hesitated for just a second, then kept moving forward behind Takizawa-san.
—Was Mari-senpai waiting for me?
The thought made me happy, and at the same time painfully aware of how pathetic I must have looked.
Zack... zack... zack... zack...
Dragging my heavy legs, I kept walking across the snow.
I suddenly felt thirsty.
While still moving, I reached back toward my pack and pulled out my water bottle.
I unscrewed the cap and took a sip.
It was cold, and that single mouthful felt like water from heaven.
When I swallowed, the exhaustion in my body seemed to ease just a little, and it felt as if a small light of hope flickered back to life inside my chest.
I tightened the cap, slipped the bottle gently back into my pack, and kept climbing.
Yohei and Tsuruoka-san, who had passed me earlier, caught up to where Mari-senpai was standing and watching me.
After only the briefest of greetings, they passed her too and continued upward.
When I looked farther ahead, I could see Takizawa-san, Yabe-san, Yohei, and Tsuruoka-san moving in their usual rhythm, one after another.
Little by little, I drew closer to where Mari-senpai was waiting for me.
Not wanting to make her worry, I tried to look a little more energetic as I approached.
At last, with great effort, I reached the spot where she had been waiting.
“Are you okay?” Mari-senpai asked.
“Thank you. I’m okay. I’m totally fine,” I said, trying not to let her see how pathetic I really felt.
At the sound of my voice, Mari-senpai smiled softly.
“You don’t have to push yourself. As long as you keep moving forward, even little by little, you’ll reach the lodge eventually... Let’s go slowly together.”
Her words made my chest grow warm.
At the same time, guilt and frustration over how slow my legs were still twisted inside me.
But I couldn’t put into words how much it meant to me that she had waited.
I nodded timidly and stepped up beside her.
Together, the two of us began climbing the snowy slope, one careful step at a time.
Farther above us, Takizawa-san led the group like a true leader, with Yabe-san, Yohei, and Tsuruoka-san following behind.
They were still moving powerfully ahead, but Mari-senpai and I kept our pace, leaving a little distance and moving without rushing, steadily and surely.
Just having her beside me made the dark feelings deep in my chest slowly begin to settle.
The exhaustion and pain both seemed to drift a little farther away.
Her calm voice and even her footsteps felt as though they were filling the cracks in my tired heart.
If you keep moving forward, you’ll get there someday. Let’s go slowly together.
Mari-senpai’s words replayed in my mind, wrapping around me like a warm campfire in the middle of the cold wind.
As we continued forward at our slow pace, my gaze naturally lifted upward.
Takizawa-san, Yabe-san, Yohei, and Tsuruoka-san were walking in a neat line, keeping a steady rhythm.
Their backs looked dependable, yet somehow distant, and a faint ache pricked inside my chest.
Among them, I noticed Yabe-san looking down toward us.
Her expression seemed thoughtful, as if she were quietly watching over our situation.
Soon, Yabe-san spoke up and called something to the others, then came to a stop.
Takizawa-san, Yohei, and Tsuruoka-san nodded at her, and Yohei and Tsuruoka-san passed her and continued ahead.
Zack... zack... zack... zack...
My own footsteps felt a little steadier now than before.
Could it really make this much difference just having Mari-senpai beside me?
Maybe fatigue is partly a matter of the mind after all, I found myself thinking.

Mari-senpai and I slowly drew closer to where Yabe-san was waiting.
The moment she saw us approaching, she came jogging lightly back toward us with quick zack-zack steps in the snow.
—She already climbed all the way up there, and now she’s coming back down here for us... she still has that much energy left? Amazing, I thought.
Yabe-san looked at me with that unreadable expression of hers, then after a short pause, she began to speak.
“Try climbing backward while being careful not to slip,” she said casually, and I couldn’t help widening my eyes.
—What? Climb backward? That’s ridiculous... I muttered inwardly, but before I could question it, Yabe-san immediately demonstrated.
Slowly and carefully, she began climbing the slope backward.
One step at a time, checking her footing with perfect certainty.
“See? Give it a try.”
Prompted by her voice, I copied her movements, still half in doubt.
Then, strangely enough, the pain that had been throbbing in my thighs and calves began to ease, and my steps suddenly felt much lighter.
The aching heaviness in my legs, which had been tormenting me until moments ago, faded as if it had all been a lie, and I found myself climbing with surprising ease.
“What? It makes that much difference...? I can’t believe it.” I stared in disbelief.
Yabe-san gave a small laugh before continuing.
“But you can’t climb backward forever. You don’t need to lift your feet high. Just slide them forward a little—that’s enough. And for the back foot, instead of fully stretching it out and bringing it forward, bend your knee with a little click first.”
“Bend my knee?” I asked, tilting my head.
“Exactly. Your thighs hurt because you’re lifting your legs too high. Your calves hurt because you’re kicking backward. If you give your knee a little click, it creates enough space to bring your foot forward without kicking back.”
“And one more thing,” she continued. “If you keep staring at your feet, your body leans forward and you’ll tire yourself out even faster. Keep your body straight and walk while looking at eye level. That naturally fixes your posture and reduces the backward kicking motion, which means less strain on your legs. Once you get that far, stop thinking of it as climbing at all—just think of it as moving your feet forward one after the other.”
Hearing that, I gave a small nod.
It was a perspective I never would have noticed while staring only at the ground beneath me.
I tried doing exactly as Yabe-san had said.
Keep my body straight. First bend the knee with a little click, then slide the foot forward.
A little click, then forward.
Click, then forward.
I’m just moving one foot in front of the other...
Just one foot at a time.
...She was right.
This way, there was hardly any strain on my calves or thighs at all.
“That’s it.”
After checking my movement, Yabe-san turned forward again and began climbing in long strides, the zack-zack of her steps quickly carrying her back up to Takizawa-san and the others in the blink of an eye.
“Takizawa-san told us to take small steps, didn’t he?” I said to Mari-senpai as I watched Yabe-san stride upward in huge steps.
“He did. Yabe-san’s just... special,” Mari-senpai said with a laugh.
—Yabe-san, if you’d taught me this from the beginning, I wouldn’t have had to suffer this much...
Thinking that, I began climbing again, slowly but with much surer steps, replaying Yabe-san’s words in my mind.
Zack... zack... zack...
As Mari-senpai and I kept moving upward, slowly but steadily, we gradually began catching up to the line ahead—Takizawa-san, Yohei, Tsuruoka-san, and Yabe-san.
From time to time, Takizawa-san looked back toward me, clearly checking on how I was doing, and I could feel that he was easing the pace to match my slower steps.
—He’s watching out for me too...
That feeling spread warmly through my chest.
