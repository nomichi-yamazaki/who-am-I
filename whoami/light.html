光の正体

The object up to the upper left, the one that had been catching the sunlight every now and then, was finally getting closer.
Voices from the group ahead—Takizawa-san, Yohei, Tsuruoka-san, and Yabe-san—drifted back to us on the wind.
“Why don’t we go take a look?” came Tsuruoka-san’s voice.
“No, it’s dangerous to leave the climbing route,” Takizawa-san said.
“There could be a crevasse. Better not,” Yabe-san added.
Tsuruoka-san came to a stop, and Takizawa-san, Yohei, and Yabe-san all stopped as well, turning back toward him.
“Tsuru-san, better not...” Takizawa-san said.
Tsuruoka-san stood there, staring longingly toward where the shining object lay.
“Even if you go, you’ll just make everyone wait... and you’ll probably come back without figuring out what it is anyway, right?” Yabe-san said.
“It’s not just about making everyone wait... If we figure out what it is, won’t that clear things up? Well then, you all wait here. I’ll go check it out.”
With that, Tsuruoka-san unclipped the buckles of the pack on his back, lowered it onto the snow, then carefully turned and began crossing the slope sideways toward where the shining object was.
“Tsuru-san! Better not—there could be a crevasse!” Takizawa-san shouted loudly.
“I’ve got ten-point crampons. No problem at all.”
Tsuruoka-san was already walking across from the upper right of Mari-senpai and me toward the left.
“That’s not the point,” I heard Yabe-san say, but Tsuruoka-san kept going without even looking back.
“Just gonna take a quick look,” he said to Mari-senpai and me as he crossed in front of us.
“Uh—oh, okay,” I answered.
After watching Tsuruoka-san head toward the shining object, Mari-senpai and I finally reached where Takizawa-san, Yohei, and Yabe-san were standing.
“Honestly, once Tsuru-san gets an idea in his head, nobody can stop him,” Takizawa-san said with a shrug and a half-laugh.
We all stared at Tsuruoka-san’s back as he moved farther and farther away.
His silhouette gradually grew smaller as he headed toward the shining thing.
“What if it really is a wheel cap after all? Seriously...”
After a brief silence, Yohei cracked a joke and laughed.
“As if!” Yabe-san shot back with a laugh, though something about her expression suggested she still wasn’t fully convinced.
The laughter between Yohei and Yabe-san softened the silence of the snowy mountain.
For a while, we simply stood there, laughing together as we wondered what that shining thing could possibly be.

Along the ridge to our right, the skyline stretched in a sharp, unbroken line, and each time the wind swept through, shafts of summer sunlight spilled down through gaps in the clouds.
Far below to the left lay the direction of the trailhead, and in the distance, the spot where we had put on our crampons looked no bigger than a tiny mark.
The lower the slope descended, the deeper the green became, and the wind blowing up from there carried a mingling of icy chill and the faint scent of grass across the snow’s surface.
Straight ahead, the peak of Mount Shakushi rose with heavy, imposing presence, the snow valley spreading wide as if cradled in its embrace.
Across the middle of that snow valley, Tsuruoka-san was making his way across.
His black T-shirt and gray pants stood out starkly against the pure white snowfield.
He stepped forward one careful stride at a time, testing the ground beneath his feet, his body tilting ever so slightly so he wouldn’t slip.
Now and then, I could see him extend one arm out to the side, a small gesture to keep his balance.
I stopped with the others and watched his back as he moved farther away.
The distance between us shouldn’t have been that great, and yet it felt as if only Tsuruoka-san alone was walking through a different flow of time.
Keeping my eyes fixed on his retreating figure, I watched with half-disbelieving amazement, thinking, He’s really going all the way out there.
Tsuruoka-san spread his right arm once more to steady himself, then moved forward again.
His black T-shirt fluttered in the wind, and around the knees of his gray pants, a fine spray of snow clung in a pale dusting.

Then, all at once, Tsuruoka-san’s neck jutted slightly forward.
Something ahead of his gaze must have caught the light from within the snow.
In the very next instant, his feet stopped.
One foot remained half a step forward, his whole body frozen as though every muscle had locked in place.
Without thinking, I held my breath.
He looked like someone who had suddenly discovered a color that had no business existing in a landscape he knew by heart.
For a fleeting second, even the wind died, and all I could hear was the pounding of my own heartbeat in my ears.
Tsuruoka-san leaned once, then twice, then a third time, as if checking the glint again and again, and before long he stood utterly still, so motionless he seemed to dissolve into the white of the snow valley itself.
He was standing right at the edge of a crevasse, leaning out to peer into the depths below.
Like a frog pinned beneath the gaze of a snake, there was not the slightest sign that he meant to move.
What happened? What on earth did he find? I wondered.
I glanced at Takizawa-san, Mari-senpai, Yohei, and Yabe-san.
All of them wore the same puzzled expression.
“What is it, Tsuru-san? What did you see?” Takizawa-san called out, raising his voice.
A beat of silence followed.
When Tsuruoka-san turned back toward us, his face was strangely serious.
“…Could this be a UFO?” he shouted.
“Huh?” Takizawa-san narrowed his eyes.
A UFO? What in the world did he find that made him say that?
The thought was so absurd it almost made me laugh.
No way it’s actually something ridiculous like a car wheel cover… right?
That ridiculous idea slipped into my mind.
“Oh, come on, stop fooling around and let’s get going already,” Mari-senpai called to him, sounding half exasperated.
“Quit joking around and come on. That spot’s dangerous,” Takizawa-san shouted loud enough for him to hear clearly.
Yohei waved a hand in agreement.
“Yeah, seriously—we’re leaving you behind!”
“It’s probably just some shiny trash… or maybe a discarded pot lid,” Yabe-san said with a cold little smile, one eyebrow raised.
“No, I’m serious!” Tsuruoka-san raised his voice, using his hands to show some kind of round shape.
“Tsuru-san, this ain’t the time for jokes. Let’s get moving,” Takizawa-san said again.
“There’s something like a silver dish buried in it! And underneath, there’s even copper-colored coils wrapped around it…” Tsuruoka-san shouted desperately toward us.
What is he even talking about?
Had he found something disappointingly ordinary, and now he was acting all this out just to trick us?
He should stop messing around and come back already… I thought.
“Hey! Come take a look! I’m telling you, it’s seriously a UFO!” Tsuruoka-san turned toward us and yelled at the top of his lungs.
“There he goes again,” Mari-senpai said with a wry smile, fluttering her hand dismissively.
“Is it really, though? Senpai, last time you said you saw a kappa too,” Yohei said.
“Tsuru-san, that’s enough. This really isn’t the time,” Takizawa-san shouted again.
“Exactly—the crevasse is dangerous, so come back,” Mari-senpai called after him too.
“No, I’m telling you, it’s real! Just come over for a second—you’ll freak out when you see it!” Tsuruoka-san insisted.
“We have to clear the Great Snow Valley before noon, or the fog rolls in and it gets dangerous!” Yabe-san shouted.
“Just come over here first! Stop saying that and come look—you’ll understand when you see it!” Tsuruoka-san kept pressing.
“I’m serious! It’s not like I just want to keep you waiting… once we know what it is, it’ll put our minds at ease, right? Come on, just for a second!” he yelled.
Listening to all of them go back and forth, I tilted my head, still only half convinced.
A UFO… what could he possibly be looking at? Still, if he’s going this far, it’s probably not just a mirror or a scrap of foil… it must be something strange. Though honestly… what if it really is a car wheel cover?

“What do you think Tsuruoka-san actually found?” I said.
“…Well, maybe it wouldn’t hurt to go take a look… and besides… it gives us a chance to rest a little…” I muttered.
“I think going over there will just end up wasting time,” Yabe-san said coldly, folding his arms.
“Tsuru-san, what did you really find over there?” Takizawa-san shouted toward him.
“You’ll know when you come see,” Tsuruoka-san shouted back without taking his eyes off the crevasse.
“But if he’s insisting that much… I *am* curious,” Mari-senpai said, one hand lightly covering her mouth, though her gaze was already being drawn past him toward whatever lay beyond.
“…Well, if it really *is* the real thing, I guess that’d be interesting,” Yabe-san murmured, still standing there with his arms crossed.
“Senpai, why don’t we go check it out? Looking’s free, right?” Yohei said to Takizawa-san.
“Senpai, come on, let’s go take a look,” Yohei pressed again.
“If it really is something, that’d make this way more interesting, wouldn’t it?” he added eagerly.
“….” Takizawa-san frowned deeply, staring at the snow at his feet.
“If it’s just a quick look and then we head right back…” Mari-senpai said, her eyes fixed on the crevasse now.
“Ahh, fine then. Just a quick look, all right?” Takizawa-san finally nodded as if giving in, put a hand to his waist, and stepped forward.
*Going to look is one thing, but what about the backpack Tsuruoka-san left behind?*
*Is it really okay to just leave it here like this?*
*…Please don’t let me be the one who ends up carrying it…* I thought.

“Honestly… just a little farther, all right? The second it feels dangerous, we turn right back.”
As Takizawa-san said it, his voice suddenly rose on the words just a little farther, clearly making sure Tsuruoka-san could hear him.
Tsuruoka-san, meanwhile, paid no attention at all to what was happening on our side, completely absorbed in whatever lay inside the crevasse.
“Fine… but if it turns out to be nothing more than a car wheel cover, I’m not forgiving him,” Yabe-san said coldly, arms folded and chin tucked, though I could sense the faintest trace of curiosity mixed into her voice.
“Well, honestly, that’d almost make it funnier,” Yohei replied under his breath with a quiet laugh, his shoulders shaking.
“What are we supposed to do with this backpack? …Honestly, Tsuru-san…”
Muttering that, Takizawa-san hoisted the pack Tsuruoka-san had left behind on top of the one he was already carrying.
Carrying two people’s packs by himself… Takizawa-san’s stamina is incredible, I thought.
“All right then, everyone, let’s go. Step exactly in Tsuru-san’s footprints. There could be crevasses, so don’t go wandering off on your own.”
Saying that, Takizawa-san slowly took the first careful step.
Behind him came Yohei, then me, then Mari-senpai, and finally Yabe-san.
I focused every nerve on the feel of the snow beneath my boots as I carefully placed each step into the prints Tsuruoka-san had left behind.
If there had been a crevasse where Tsuruoka-san walked, he could have fallen straight in and this would’ve turned into something terrible…
The only reason we were able to walk this way now was because he had made it there safely by sheer luck.
That thought wouldn’t leave my mind.
To our upper right, a ridgeline linked one mountain peak to the next, and from beyond it the cold wind came sweeping through.
Below us, the snow valley spread out almost like the slope of a ski resort.
When I thought about how far we had climbed from down there to reach this point, I felt a quiet sense of awe settle over me.
Carefully following the footprints Tsuruoka-san had left in the snow, all of us made our way toward where he stood.
The snow underfoot was slightly packed, making it easier to walk, but I could still feel the need to bite the crampon points firmly into the surface so I wouldn’t slip.
“What do you think Tsuruoka-san found that made him call it a UFO?” I asked, glancing back slightly toward Mari-senpai.
“I don’t know, but it is intriguing. If he’s making this much fuss, it can’t be anything ordinary,” she answered, her brows knitting just a little, though her voice carried clear curiosity.
I kept my eyes fixed desperately on my footing, but in the back of my mind, I couldn’t help feeling a flicker of excitement over what on earth Tsuruoka-san had seen to call it a UFO.
To be honest, part of me stayed cool and skeptical, thinking, It’s probably just some piece of aluminum foil or something catching the light.
And yet, that tiny spark of anticipation refused to go away.
Do UFOs really even exist…? Well, we’ll probably just get there and be disappointed, I told myself.
Even so, my body kept moving naturally toward where Tsuruoka-san was waiting.
“Ahh, I can’t wait to see what it is!” Yohei said brightly, his voice bouncing with excitement, though even in his joking tone I could tell his eyes were fixed straight ahead.
As I felt the cold from the snow slowly seep deeper into my feet, I looked up at the sky for a moment.
The blue above us was wide open, but the wind was so strong it felt like it stabbed at my skin.
Tsuruoka-san… what did you see? If it really is a UFO, that’d be insane, I thought as I kept walking over the snow.
Ahead of us, Tsuruoka-san—black T-shirt, gray pants—was growing closer.
Maybe he had noticed the sound of our footsteps, because he glanced briefly back our way.
There was something almost triumphant in his expression.
Takizawa-san and Yohei, walking in front, stopped dead just before reaching him.
They froze as if they had turned into statues of ice.
Even Yohei, who had been cracking jokes only moments before, stood there with his mouth hanging open, robbed of words.
What…? What did they see?
My chest tightened uneasily.
Each step I took made the white snow give a sharp little crunch beneath me.
Only a few more steps to the edge of the crevasse.
Peering quietly over Tsuruoka-san’s shoulder as he stood at the rim, I looked down too.
And then… I saw it as well.
In that instant, my heart gave one violent leap.
Forgetting even how the cold air stabbed into my lungs, I narrowed my eyes and stared.
This… no way… is this really a UFO…?
A voice of denial surged up inside my head.
No, wait—part of an airplane? Some kind of weather observation device?
But… it was far too large.
The shape was wrong too.
More than anything, I couldn’t think of a single reason something like this should exist in a place like this.
Before I realized it, I was blinking over and over again.
What lay before me was something strange and uncanny, the kind of object I felt like I had only ever seen in dreams.
Its metallic surface, glossy as a mirror, caught the sunlight slanting in from above and threw it back in a blinding shimmer.
The shape was like a flat dish turned upside down, with only the rim rising ever so slightly.
It looked as though part of a gigantic disc, almost entirely buried, had pushed just enough of itself through the snow to reveal its face.
Looking closer, I realized the metallic dish was not just metal at all—it was faintly translucent, allowing a ghostly glimpse of the inner structure beneath the surface.
The visible rim of the dish tilted slightly upward, letting us see just a little of its underside.
That section was different from the rest of the mirror-bright surface, gleaming instead with a brown metallic sheen.
If the whole thing had been exposed, it might have looked like a short cylinder—or perhaps a ringed disc lined with countless fine vertical grooves, something like a donut-shaped magnet wrapped in coils.
That coil-like section shone with the intricate, beautiful complexity of a precision machine.
And yet the full shape remained hidden.
Buried deep in the snow, the object seemed to be keeping half its secret to itself, as if it were silently speaking to us.
Was it truly a UFO?
Or some unknown machine from somewhere else on Earth?
Takizawa-san’s and Yohei’s faces were right in front of me.
Both of them had gone completely speechless, able to do nothing but stare in stunned disbelief at the object.
That quiet shock slowly spread through my own chest as well.
I edged forward cautiously, drawing even closer, my heartbeat pounding harder and harder.
My own face reflected back at me in the glossy dish, warped and twisted out of shape.
I had never seen anything like this in my life.
I looked again, and then again.
The more I stared, the more it felt impossible to call it anything but a UFO.
No matter what else I tried to explain it as, nothing matched this size, this form.
No unknown scientific instrument I could imagine had ever looked this large or this impossibly complex.
This wasn’t a mistake.
It really was… wasn’t it?
A UFO.
For one impossible moment, it felt as if we might truly be standing at the edge of the mystery of an unidentified flying object.
With that thought filling my mind, I stood frozen atop the hardened snow.
Crunch, crunch—the sound of boots biting into snow came closer from just behind me.
When I turned, Mari-senpai and Yabe-san were walking toward us at a careful pace.
From the look on their faces, they had already read something in the frozen stillness of the two standing in front of me.
Mari-senpai stopped.
The moment carried the strange feeling that the very air had frozen solid around the sight of something too unbelievable to accept.
Yabe-san approached in silence, and when she finally stood at the edge of the crevasse, a long moment passed before she murmured in a small voice,
“A UFO… seriously?”
At those words, a chill ran sharply up my spine.
The whisper carried a strange weight, so unlike Yabe-san’s usual cool, controlled tone.
Both of them were staring at the same gleaming metallic dish that had held the rest of us spellbound.
But now the atmosphere around us held something more—an unspoken tension that refused to take shape as words.
I steadied my breathing and looked at the strange object once more.
For the briefest instant, it felt as though I were peering into something from the future.
“I’m the one who found it, you know,” Tsuruoka-san said proudly.
Before he could even finish speaking, my heart gave a violent thump!
“Ah!” The cry slipped from my mouth before I even understood what had happened.
My foot had slipped on the snow.
“Ah!” Almost at the same instant, Mari-senpai let out a startled cry too.
A dull pain spread through my tailbone where it slammed into the hard surface.
For a split second, it felt like my breath had stopped completely.
My body was sliding—my weight carried on my backside over the snow.
This is bad—this is bad! Panic surged through my mind in waves.
If I didn’t stop, I would slide all the way down.
Desperately, I bent my knees and drove the four crampon points of my mountaineering boots deep into the snow.
I could feel the cold, hardened surface pushing back against me beneath my feet.
Time seemed to slow, and only then did my mind begin to catch up with what had happened.
When I looked back, everyone else was still a little higher up the slope.
I realized I was the only one who had slipped, sliding just slightly downward on my own.
“Saku-chan, you okay?” Takizawa-san called out.
“Yeah… that surprised me,” I said, forcing a laugh to play it off.
“The one who got surprised was us. What was that, ski practice?” Yohei teased. “You didn’t have to make such a dramatic scene out of it.”
“That startled me too. Are you okay? Did you hit your tailbone?” Mari-senpai asked.
“Ah, I’m totally fine. …Yohei, just remember this for later,” I said with a grin, even though my backside was honestly throbbing.
Yohei just laughed down at me.
“Well, good thing it ended with just that. If it had been someone, he might’ve slid all the way to the bottom and started bragging about it,” Yabe-san said without so much as a smile.
She means Tsuruoka-san, doesn’t she?
The thought made me laugh before I could stop myself, and when I looked at Tsuruoka-san on instinct, he seemed to notice my gaze and looked back at me too, making me hurriedly avert my eyes.
Ahh… but my butt really hurts. Even though I fell on snow…
When I looked at the track where I had slipped, I could see the inside was frozen solid—a large mass of ice with granular snow piled over its surface.
The path I had carved through it ran in straight vertical streaks where the powdery grains had been scraped away.
And inside that ice, I could see something black embedded within it.
What is that?
I narrowed my eyes, but the warped refraction through the icy surface kept it from coming fully into focus.
Even so… it looked almost like a curled-up, pitch-black fetus, as if it were somehow still alive.
My gaze locked onto the thing with a freezing intensity, and the words caught in my throat.
“There’s… something there…”
My voice trembled, and forcing the words out was all I could manage.
Crunch, crunch—Tsuruoka-san came down toward me, stamping through the snow.
“Sho, you okay?” he said.
For a moment, I stared at the hand he held out toward me.
To be honest, because it was Tsuruoka-san, part of me really didn’t want to touch it.
But I couldn’t exactly refuse, so with reluctant resignation I grabbed his hand.
In the next instant, my body was pulled sharply upward as Tsuruoka-san hauled me back up.
“Thank you,” I said, getting to my feet and brushing the snow from my pants.
For a moment, I found myself thinking that maybe he had a gentler side after all… though with everything up until now, another thought immediately followed: He’s not going to try and recruit me into the Banri-kyo later or something, is he?
But what on earth is this…?
I stared once more at the black object trapped inside the ice.
It was covered in pale, semi-translucent ice, and within it a dark shape floated faintly, curled up like a fetus.
Thoughts spun round and round in my head, trying to name it, but no words would come yet.
If the thing buried there was somehow connected to the UFO… then could this possibly be an alien body?
The thought flashed suddenly across my mind.
No way… there’s no way that could be real, I told myself.
Sensing everyone’s eyes fixed on me, I looked up.
Takizawa-san was staring over with furrowed brows, unease written plainly on his face.
Mari-senpai wore a slight grimace, her eyes silently asking me what I had seen.
Yohei was looking at me with naked curiosity.
Yabe-san stood with her arms folded, calm as ever, as if waiting coolly for my reaction.
And beside me, Tsuruoka-san.
His expression was as unreadable as always, and I couldn’t tell whether he was staring at whatever lay in the ice—or quietly studying me.
“What is it, Saku?” Takizawa-san called out.
I swallowed hard and finally forced myself to speak.
“There’s… something buried in here…”
My voice trembled faintly, but I tried desperately to steady myself as I said it.
At my words, everyone wore blank, puzzled expressions for a moment.
“What is there…?” Takizawa-san asked from a little higher up the slope, his brow tightening further.
I swallowed again and pieced the words together little by little.
“It’s like… some black thing curled up like a fetus… inside the ice.”
The moment those words left my mouth, Mari-senpai’s eyes widened.
For an instant, it felt like everyone’s expressions changed at once.
The air itself seemed to stir with the sense that something was about to happen.
“Which part?” Yohei said, the corner of his mouth twisting as he stepped closer, curiosity sharpening in his eyes.
Yabe-san remained above us with her arms crossed, staring down at me in silence, watching the situation unfold without a word.
To my left, Tsuruoka-san’s face did not change at all as he kept staring fixedly at the black shadow inside the ice.
“Could it be… an alien?” Tsuruoka-san murmured.
Someone let out a small, uncertain laugh.
“An alien?” From above us, Yabe-san shook her head in exasperation.
“Well, if something that looks like a UFO is right here, then I can’t say the chances are zero, can I?” Tsuruoka-san said.
He seemed determined to convince himself that what he had found was truly a UFO, and that what lay here had to be an alien corpse or something like it.
Everyone gathered around me now, all eyes drawn toward the object trapped inside the ice.
At the sight of that black, curled shape, words failed them, and even Yohei was left standing there with his mouth hanging open.
“What the hell is this…” Takizawa-san muttered.
For a while, we could do nothing but stare in silence at the unnatural sight before us.
The mix of unease and curiosity in the air felt colder against my skin than the ice itself.
“This is a huge discovery! We could become famous overnight!” Tsuruoka-san’s voice shattered the silence.
“So, Tsuru-san… what exactly are you planning to do?” Takizawa-san asked, lowering his voice just a little as he looked between me and him.
“Isn’t it obvious? We dig it out right now,” Tsuruoka-san answered without a trace of hesitation.
“You should leave it alone. We can’t rule out the possibility that it carries some kind of pathogen,” Yabe-san said.
“She’s right. Even if we know it’s not going to suddenly come back to life, there’s no telling what might happen…” Takizawa-san added carefully.
“Senpai, if we start digging this thing out here and it turns into trouble, we’re gonna be everybody’s laughingstock,” Yohei said from a cautious distance, trying to keep it light.
“You say that, but if we don’t do it here and now, then who will? If we let this moment slip away, we’ll never get another chance. Yeah, there’s risk—but the courage to act is what gives something real value!”
Tsuruoka-san’s voice rose with conviction.
“If we start digging this out now, what time do you think it’ll be by the time we’re done? Once it gets into the afternoon, the fog rolls in, and after two o’clock they don’t allow climbers in here,” Yabe-san pointed out coolly.
Yabe-san’s talking a lot more than usual…
Maybe even she was getting worked up by something so far outside everyday life.
That was what I found myself thinking.
“Ah, it’ll be over in no time,” Tsuruoka-san said with an easy laugh, brushing the concern aside.
“Even if we don’t dig it out now, we can contact someone and have specialists excavate it properly,” Yabe-san said.
For a moment, everyone seemed to consider her suggestion.
“That way we still stay the discoverers, and there’s less chance of an accident. I think it’s a good idea,” Mari-senpai said, sounding supportive.
But Tsuruoka-san folded his arms and slowly swept his gaze across everyone’s faces.
“It’s not that simple. We’re deep in the mountains out here. By the time specialists arrive, snow and ice might bury it all over again and erase the evidence.”
He pressed on, refusing to let it go.
“Besides, we’re the ones who found it. That means we have to be the ones to dig it out here. If we just hand it off to experts, the value of being the discoverers gets watered down.”
Then he said it as if he were stating the most obvious truth in the world.
“The world runs on competition.”
“A chance to win like this doesn’t come around twice. There’s no way I’m letting it slip through my fingers.”

—I see.
So that’s what it is…
The world is competition… a competition for profit.
I traced those words through my mind once more.
This world runs on competition.
Competition to survive.
Competition to make money.
And when I looked at it that way,
all the strange feelings and quiet discomforts I had been remembering as we climbed the mountain
began to feel as though they were connecting into a single line.
A competition for profit.
That’s why employers try to get by with as few people as possible.
Low wages, too much work.
Pushing each individual right up to their limit.
And as a result, people lose the room to build a family, the room to raise children,
and it turns into a declining birthrate.
Like the QC activities at my company, where even when everything is already running at peak efficiency, we still have to sacrifice the work that actually matters just to force out some “improvement” to report.
Because things have to be sold to become money,
people force themselves to make things no one truly needs,
then force themselves to sell them.
And for that, mountains are cut away, rivers are polluted,
and the environment is slowly destroyed.
Governments receive money and votes from corporations.
So they try to protect systems that keep corporations profitable.
They collect consumption taxes even from people with little money,
then ease the tax burden on companies.
In that way, the shape of society is arranged
so the competition can continue.
To win that competition,
people even join hands with dirty kinds of power.
Use organized crime, and rivals can be kept down.
Politicians, corporations, religious groups—
all of them can push aside whoever becomes inconvenient.
And organized crime receives money in return.
—A competition for profit
creates the need for those kinds of forces in society.
Religious groups join hands with political power,
and governments shape society in ways convenient to those groups.
Emphasize the existence of an enemy,
and people become anxious.
Anxiety turns into money and votes.
Inflame a sense of crisis,
and weapons sell,
approval ratings rise.
Sometimes it is more convenient for neighboring countries to remain on bad terms,
because it benefits both governments.
To keep people from looking at the suffering in their own lives,
they sometimes put on a fight on purpose.
The winners are both governments.
The losers are soldiers, and ordinary people.
To make people offer up their lives,
flags and national names are treated
as if they are sacred things beyond question.
The media survives on corporate advertising.
So it reports in ways convenient for corporations.
Even public broadcasting,
if the people at the top are tied to government,
ends up speaking in ways convenient for government too.
Inflame a crisis,
goods sell,
politics moves,
money circulates.
And so,
crises are created again and again.
Government, corporations, religion, entertainment, organized crime,
the press, even private-sector unions—
all of them, inside this competition for profit,
use one another,
and end up facing the same direction.
All to keep this structure going
for the overwhelmingly larger number of ordinary people who remain outside that circle.
And yet,
those ordinary people
still end up choosing the very people who keep it going.
Money gathers at the top,
and the farther down you are, the harder life becomes.
—And still,

For someone to win,
someone else has to be pushed too far.
And the person who has already been pushed
can only cling even harder to the competition.
—Because the moment they stop competing, they lose.
Tsuruoka-san’s words
were not especially malicious.
He had only spoken aloud the idea this society treats as ordinary common sense.
And yet,
something caught painfully in the back of my chest.
I found myself thinking that.
I see, I thought.
For people who work in private companies,
they themselves—and the company they belong to—
have to survive the competition first.
So naturally they drift toward anything that gives them even a slight advantage:
drawing closer to the state, hoping for favorable policy,
leaning toward ideas that accelerate the competition for profit.
Meanwhile, people like my grandfather,
who spent their lives in public work,
tend to value not one person winning,
but everyone being able to live without hardship.
Everyone creates together,
everyone shares together,
and it circulates back through society—
it is only natural
that such a way of thinking would emerge.
This is not something that can be dismissed
as simply “right” or “left.”
The real difference is whether your thinking centers on yourself,
the organization you belong to,
or perhaps your nation.
Or whether you try to include even someone far away in another part of the world
when you think about happiness.
Maybe that difference
is not a difference in what is right,
but in the range of what one is able to see—
the breadth of one’s perspective.
Listening to Tsuruoka-san’s words, that was what I found myself thinking.
