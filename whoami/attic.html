Old Building Attic

When I reached the highest level, I found myself in a wide sleeping area.
Tatami mats covered the floor, and there was no sign of anyone else.
To my side lay the dim attic.
The roof sloped down on both sides, forming an inverted V overhead. Beneath it, beams, support posts, and braces intersected in a tangled wooden lattice that stretched deep into the shadows.
Looking up, the dark silhouettes of the supports twisted across the ceiling. Looking down, the yellow glow of bare light bulbs spread across the floor below, their shadows slipping through the gaps between the beams and making the footing seem to sway beneath me.
If I reached up from here...
I could climb onto the beam.
And once on it, I could walk farther into the attic.
But one misstep would be the end.
This was the third floor. If I slipped from the beam, I would plunge all the way down.
Even so, going back was not an option.
...Who would be waiting for me down there?
Who could I trust?
Even my best friend Yohei... was gone.
My chest tightened, and for a moment I forgot how to breathe.
My mind still refused to accept the fact that Yohei was dead.
Just a few hours ago, we had been joking around, taking pictures, talking about getting back to the studio after the trip and finally starting a band together.
And now Yohei no longer existed anywhere in this world.
I clenched my teeth against the tears threatening to blur my vision, planted a hand on the edge of the tatami platform, lifted myself up, and placed a foot onto the beam.
A wave of dizziness washed over me, and my body swayed.
I wrapped one hand around a nearby support post and carefully shifted my weight onto the beam.
The soles of my bare feet felt the faint grit of dust clinging to the wood.
In the New Wing, the presence of heat had sometimes made it possible to forget the cold.
But the Old Wing was different.
There was no fire, no warmth, no sign of life. Cold air crept through the gaps between the beams, brushing against my bare arms and legs.
It wasn't enough to make me shiver, but the chill still crawled along my spine.
Even so, my body felt strangely hot. Maybe it was because my heart was pounding so hard with fear that it felt as though my blood were boiling.
The sensation made me acutely aware that I was still alive—and that Yohei was still down below.
Spreading my arms for balance, I began making my way across the beam.
Each step crushed the dust beneath my feet, producing a faint, dry crunch.
Crunch... crunch...
The sound seemed unnaturally loud.
The beam's edges had been worn smooth with age, and its rounded surface looked treacherously easy to slip from. I could feel every grain of dust and every rough splinter of wood beneath my feet.
The beam was barely wider than two hand spans, and every time my body tilted to one side, the muscles throughout my body screamed in protest.
If I lost my balance, I would fall.
But if I didn't keep moving deeper inside—I'd be caught.
Outside, the storm was raging.
Rain hammered against the roof with heavy thud... THUD... sounds, like giant hammers striking metal, while the wind shook the entire building.
Each gust sent a faint tremor through the beam beneath my feet.
A bad kind of tremor.
The kind that made the whole structure feel as though it might collapse at any moment.
Yohei was dead. Yohei was no longer in this world.
My best friend, Yohei...
The same Yohei who had talked with me about starting a band together...
I no longer knew who was friend and who was enemy.
I couldn't trust anyone.
The people I thought I could rely on—I couldn't even be sure they were still themselves anymore.
Was Mari-senpai safe?
The thought surfaced again and again, only for me to force it away each time.
I was worried about her.
Desperately worried.
I wanted to see her.
But I was afraid to.
Even if I found her, how could I know it was really Mari-senpai?
She could smile and approach me just like Yohei had... and then, in the very next moment...
I didn't know.
I didn't know anything anymore.
Who was friend and who was enemy.
Whether this place was safe or not.
The radio was useless.
The signal icon on my phone remained stubbornly fixed on No Service.
Outside was a storm and a darkness so deep that even headlights were powerless against it.
Three thousand meters up a mountain... there was nowhere left to run.
With every step along the beam, it felt as though I were walking the edge of a cliff.
The pounding of my heart echoed inside my ears, and every time my sweaty hand left a support post, I feared I might slip.
But I had no choice except to keep moving forward.
If I turned back, I would die.
If I stopped, I would die.
If I kept going... I might survive.
Beyond the beam lay only darkness.
I had no idea what might be waiting there.
But what lay behind me was even more terrifying.
So I swallowed hard and took another step.
And then another.
With each step, the groan of the roof in the wind and the relentless pounding of rain shook through my head, making it feel as though both the beam and my body were swaying together.
Every time I stretched out a hand to keep my balance, tension surged into my fingertips, while my elbows and shoulders warned that they were nearing their limits.
My breathing grew heavier.
If I hesitated for even a moment over where to place my foot, I could slip and plunge into the sleeping quarters below.
The fear crawled across my skin and raced through every part of my body.

Then, from somewhere below and behind me, a voice called out.
“...Sho...”
For a moment, my chest tightened.
It was Yohei's voice.
Without a doubt, it was Yohei's voice.
There was no way I could mistake it.
The inside of my ears felt hot.
But if I answered, it would be over.
The moment I responded, I would have no way of knowing whether it was really Yohei—or the Thing wearing Yohei's skin.
Don't answer.
No matter what.
His name almost escaped my throat, but I clenched my teeth and forced it back down.
Keeping my eyes fixed ahead, I took another step along the beam.
And then another.
My breath trembled, and my body felt ready to shake apart.
I wanted to answer him...
But I was terrified...
My knees threatened to buckle beneath me.
“...Shooo...”
The voice was closer this time.
Not from the floor below anymore.
From somewhere near the beams just beneath this level.
It was moving.
Don't answer.
Don't answer.
Don't answer.
And then—
Rustle... rustle... rustle-rustle...
Behind me came the dry sound of wood and dust scraping together, like the skittering of some insect.
It was getting closer.
Fast.
Quick, jerky movements that never stopped.
My back froze on its own, and before I realized it, I had turned around.
What I saw was not “Yohei.”
In the dim attic darkness, it wore faded khaki pants and a T-shirt bearing the cover art of a BOSTON album—the one with the Ovation guitar transformed into a spaceship.
It was wearing Yohei's clothes, but its body was not human.
Its entire form was pitch black, curled like a fetus.
Its surface looked less like skin and more like wet clay.
Where its face should have been, there were no eyes, no nose, nothing at all—
Only a single large circular hole gaping where its mouth ought to be.
Just like the black fetus-like thing we had discovered on the Great Snow Valley.
Clinging belly-down to the beam, it spread its limbs like a spider and crept toward me.
Rustle... rustle...
“Where are you going, Sho...?”
The voice was still Yohei's.
Yet it echoed from that hollow opening despite the absence of any mouth or throat.
It sounded as though it were being poured directly into my mind.
“If you become one of us, you can look like this too...”
The black fetus-like body trembled slightly, as though savoring its own amusement.
“Remember what we talked about?
How famous we'd become if we made this thing part of the band...?”
The words traced a memory with cruel precision.
They hurled our shared dream back at me exactly as it had been spoken that night after dinner.
“If you join us—maybe you could even travel through space in a flying saucer...”
The circular opening widened ever so slightly.
It looked as though it were smiling.
“Come on, Sho. There's nothing wrong about it.
This is where you belonged from the very beginning.”
Rustle... rustle... rustle... rustle...
The sound of it crawling along the beam grew faster.
I had only one path left.
Either I fell... or it caught me.
My throat trembled on its own, and a scream threatened to escape.
Even so, I had to keep moving.
If I didn't take the next step—
it would all end here.

I hurried along the beam as fast as I dared, desperately trying to escape.
With every step, my footing shifted slightly from side to side, causing my body to sway.
I had to duck beneath the overhead braces as I moved forward, keeping myself in a constant crouch. Every time I felt my balance falter, tension shot through my shoulders and hips, and my breath caught sharply in my throat.
Between the beams yawned open spaces.
Through them, the yellow glow of the incandescent bulbs below seeped upward, casting wavering shadows.
The shadows were only moving with the light, yet they looked as though something beneath me was reaching up, trying to grab hold of my legs.
The edge of my vision trembled on its own.
Behind me, the rustle... rustle... of pursuit traveled through the beam itself, reaching the soles of my feet as vibrations.
It was getting closer.
I knew it.
It wasn't just the sound.
Through the beam, I could feel the mass of it advancing toward me.
Even as I fled, the thing behind me became more real than the path ahead.
A chill swept across my back as though I had stepped into a cold wind, and every hair on the back of my neck stood on end.
If my breathing faltered even once, I could fall.
If I looked back, it would be over.
And yet—its presence was so overwhelming that some part of my body felt ready to react on its own.
The gritty dust beneath my feet scraped against my soles, and every effort to maintain my balance tightened the muscles throughout my body.
My heart hammered in my ears.
My breathing grew ragged.
Whatever was pursuing me wasn't merely drawing closer.
It was bearing down on me.
It felt like a mass of warm, damp air pressing against my back, chilling the sweat across my skin in uneven patches.
Mixed with the scent of dust and rusted metal was something else—something faintly sweet and organic, like the smell of a placenta.
A beam creaked softly beneath the weight behind me.
I could feel it.
The pursuer's weight was truly there.
Heavy.
Even without looking, my brain understood that there was a living thing back there, breathing.
Then suddenly, from behind and to my right, I sensed something rushing past me.
My entire body froze for an instant.
In that instant, I glanced to the side—
And there it was.
Yohei's head.
Looking directly at me.
But attached to it was a sight so impossible that my mind refused to accept it.
Its neck had stretched to an unnatural length, and sprouting from it like countless warts were tiny human arms and legs, writhing ceaselessly.
The sheer grotesqueness of it made me misstep with my left foot.
The moment my footing slipped, it felt as though my body had been torn upward before being pulled into empty space.
An instant later, I had the overwhelming sensation of falling into a direction where no ground existed.
My stomach lurched violently upward.
My throat clenched shut on its own.
For a split second, the weight of my body vanished.
I'm falling.
The certainty pierced straight through my brain, and the edges of my vision exploded into white.
At the last possible moment, I threw out both hands and caught the beam.
Cold sweat burst from my skin as I fought to hold on.
Hanging beneath the beam, I strained every muscle in my body to keep myself from falling.
The rough wood bit into my palms, and my fingertips turned white from the effort.
Then I happened to look up—
And there was Yohei's face.
The elongated neck connected to a beam off to my right.
Khaki pants.
A BOSTON T-shirt.
Everything was familiar.
But the expression staring down at me was wrong.
Wrong in a way that made my skin crawl.
It was smiling.
“Sho... that was close.
Good thing you didn't fall.”
The voice was Yohei's.
The smile was Yohei's.
And yet something about both felt twisted, as though a perfect imitation had been bent ever so slightly out of shape.
A sheet of ice ran down my spine.
At that very moment, a sharp, piercing beeeep rang out inside my head.
It wasn't a sound entering through my ears.
It resonated through my entire skull.
My vision warped violently.
The boundary between light and shadow melted away, losing all definition.
Depth vanished.
The attic seemed to collapse into a flat surface, as though the world had suddenly become two-dimensional.
My sense of balance went haywire.
It felt as though my body were tipping forward, backward, left, and right all at once.
The beam beneath me suddenly seemed no wider than a single thumb.
At the same time as that unbearable tone pierced through me, Yohei's head began to slide away toward the black fetus-like body behind him.
Slowly, it retreated backward.
Farther and farther away.
Then, at the edge of my vision, it was swallowed by the darkness of the black figure.
The only thing left on the beam was me.
My body still trembling from terror and the lingering vibrations.
My heart still racing wildly in my chest.

Pouring every ounce of strength into my arms, I hauled myself back onto the beam.
I forced my right leg upward, searching for purchase against the rough wood until I finally managed to pull myself into a prone position.
My lungs heaved uncontrollably.
Bright specks flickered across my vision.
I should have felt relieved.
I should have been thinking, I survived.
But that wasn't what weighed most heavily on my heart.
Yohei... wasn't Yohei anymore.
My best friend.
The friend who had always been by my side.
The friend who had talked about the future with me.
He had become... that.
The thought lodged in my throat so tightly that even breathing felt difficult.
Yet at the same time, another feeling lingered deep inside my chest like a thin veil of mist.
Why didn't he attack me?
He had been right beside me.
He had even grabbed me.
Had he mistaken me for one of his own...?
Or was I still being... evaluated?
No answer came.
But one thing was certain.
If I stayed here, it would be over.
Down below.
The dining hall.
There was only one choice left.
I had to go down.
I crawled along the beam until I reached a support pillar, then slowly extended my legs over the edge.
Sweat on my fingertips made the wood slippery.
If I fell, it was over.
I had no idea what might be waiting below.
Even so, I stretched out an arm and grabbed the corner of the pillar.
Now there was only one way down.
Pressing my arms and the insides of my legs tightly against the wood, I wrapped myself around the pillar and began lowering my weight little by little.
The friction burned.
My exposed arms scraped against the rough surface, sending sharp, scorching pain through my skin.
Gritting my teeth against the sensation that my skin might peel away, I endured each passing second, though every moment felt like an eternity.
Scrape...
Scrape...
By the time my muscles were screaming and my arms felt ready to cramp,
my feet finally touched something solid.
The floor.
I'd made it.
My knees trembled on their own, threatening to buckle beneath me, but I forced myself to stay upright.
Suppressing the ragged breaths rising in my chest, I scanned the dim dining hall beyond.
There was no sound.
No sign of anyone.
Only a heavy, stagnant atmosphere hanging motionless in the air.
Feeling the lingering sting in my arms, I told myself that I had to decide what to do next.
I had to take the next step.
