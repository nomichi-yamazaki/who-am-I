Departure from Hakuba-Jiri

“Sorry to keep you waiting. Alright then, let’s get moving little by little,” Takizawa-san said as he returned to his pack.
At the sound of his voice, everyone silently began shouldering their own packs.
I gripped the shoulder straps with both hands, set the pack on my knee, twisted my body, slipped my right arm through the right strap and heaved the pack up onto my right shoulder, then threaded my left arm through the other strap.
Zaku, zaku, zaku, zaku…
With Takizawa-san in front, followed by Mari-senpai, Yabe-san, then me, with Yohei behind and Tsuruoka-san bringing up the rear, we started walking with the souvenir-shop-like mountain hut on our left.
To our right, a stream ran alongside us, its water whispering shaa… choro-choro-choro… as it flowed over the stones.
Keeping the stone steps we had used for the toilet on our left, we began climbing the stair-like rise of stones set between the grass on the right side of the stone terrace where the school-like building stood above.
A glance at my watch told me it was a little past six-thirty.
Soon the path became a rocky mountain trail, loose stones scattered between grass on both sides.
Slender trees thickened around us, and we drifted a little farther away from the stream on the right.
The narrow trail climbed the slope at a gentle angle, and maybe because the break we’d just had had lightened my body, the first steps felt surprisingly easy.
Haa, haa, haa, haa… The sound of my own breathing, mingling with the breaths of the people ahead and behind.
Each time the soles of my boots struck stone, a faint kachi clicked beneath my feet.
The ground wasn’t flat, so my footing never felt fully stable.
My feet kept wanting to twist sideways almost immediately.
My gaze naturally dropped to my own steps, and all I found myself watching was the movement of my feet in their smoky-green hiking boots—right, left, right again—as they alternated forward.
The path of stone and grass flowed backward beneath my feet, sliding away behind me.
At the very top edge of my vision, I could just catch glimpses of the heels of Yabe-san’s black hiking boots, quietly lifting and landing again.
As if matching that rhythm, my own feet kept moving forward as well.
My breath was starting to quicken a little, but the mountain air was cold and felt wonderful, like pouring clear water deep into the very back of my lungs.

After a little while, green-covered ridges rose up on both sides, and the valley between them opened out in a broad, gentle sweep.
Farther ahead, along the stream, a patch of lingering white snow came briefly into view, giving off a chilly presence despite the height of summer.
Meltwater from that snow raced into the stream, reaching my ears as a quiet murmur.
Just before the snow by the stream, something like a small stack of stones poked up from the grass.
“There’s… something like a marker up ahead,” I said, pointing toward it.
“Ah, that’s what we call a cairn,” Takizawa-san answered, glancing back from the front of the line.
“A cairn? …I didn’t know that.”
—Huh. So it’s called a cairn. I had no idea.
A small wave of embarrassment washed over me, and I instantly regretted saying something unnecessary.
“Well, just because it’s called a cairn doesn’t mean you should go careening into it,” Tsuruoka-san cut in from behind.
Mari-senpai turned back toward Yabe-san, and almost at the same moment the two of them burst out laughing.
“…Who would even do that?” Mari-senpai murmured under her breath with a laugh, and Yabe-san’s shoulders shook as she laughed too.
“Ahh, senpai, you’re really on a roll from the very first steps today,” Yohei said to Tsuruoka-san.
—More like roll over from the very first steps, I thought.

The cairn itself was built in a triangular stack of stones, and across that triangular face a prominent long rectangular plaque had been fixed.
In black letters it read: Hakuba Mountain National Forest.
Beneath that: Daisekkei Cairn.
And below that: Chushin Forest Management Office.
Following behind Takizawa-san, Mari-senpai, and Yabe-san, I passed by the side of the cairn.
There were signboards attached beside it as well.
They were long horizontal rectangles, two of them mounted one above the other.
The upper sign was dark brown with white lettering that read Caution: Slippery, and below that, Nagano Prefecture / Nagano Road Association.
The lower sign was a weathered brown board marked, from left to right: Hakuba Summit, 4 km, Snowfield Terminus, 3 km, and Sarukura, with left- and right-pointing arrows drawn above the 4 km and 3 km respectively.
At the very bottom, almost quietly tucked away, were the words Hyogo Prefectural Kobe High School.
It struck me as a little strange that the name of just one school was displayed so openly like this.
Maybe it was simply a donation from their mountaineering club, or perhaps the place held some special connection for them—questions like that drifted through my mind.

The trees around us suddenly fell away, and before my eyes spread a vast slope strewn with rocks.
Misshapen stones of every size lay scattered everywhere, telling their own story of how unstable the footing would be.
Ahead, the snowfield loomed close, its pure white mass lying silently in the broad valley where the ridges on both sides opened wide.
The vivid contrast of living green, gray rock faces, and the flawless white of snow created an atmosphere that felt somehow sacred.
The snowfield wound its way toward the summit in a zigzag, threading through the intricate valley formed by the ridges reaching down from the far side and the ridgeline on our side alike.

“We’re really at the snowfield now,” Mari-senpai said, looking up at the white slope ahead.
“…Alright, from here on it’s the snowfield. Everyone, let’s get our crampons on!” Takizawa-san said, and all at once everyone began taking off their packs.
I slipped mine from my shoulders too and pulled out the borrowed crampons from inside the pack I’d set on the ground.
Mine were the simple four-point crampons I’d rented at Sarukura Lodge.
The rental fee had been 1,500 yen for the day, and apparently 500 yen would be refunded when I returned them at the lodge.
Tsuruoka-san was pulling out his crampons too, from a huge, thick nylon case.
—So those are the crampons he was bragging about earlier… I thought.
—Uh… wait. How am I supposed to put these on?
Just as I was staring down at my boots, trying to figure out how to attach them, Mari-senpai seemed to notice my hesitation.
She paused what she was doing, gave me a warm smile, and crouched down in front of me.
“Saku-chan, with crampons, you secure the toe firmly first, then wrap this strap around behind your ankle and pull it tight. Make sure it’s snug, okay? If it’s loose, it might come off while you’re walking.”
As she spoke, she fitted one of the crampons onto my boot to show me.
“Ah, thank you.”
Even Yohei was putting his on by himself, and it made me feel a little pathetic that I couldn’t even manage this much alone.
“Um… like this?” I said, copying what Mari-senpai had shown me as I threaded the strap behind my ankle.
“Yep, just like that! It’s a little tricky at first, but once you get used to it, it’s easy.”
Mari-senpai nodded kindly, then turned back to fastening her own crampons.
Her gentle voice, along with the kachakacha clinking of metal fittings all around us, rang softly through the still mountain air.
Watching Mari-senpai’s hands as she tightened the straps on her own boots, I carefully pulled my own belt snug.
I could feel my heart beating just a little faster at her kindness, and for a fleeting moment, there was a faint warmth in the air between us.
—This shouldn’t come off now.
It still felt a little awkward, but once it was attached, I definitely felt safer.

I noticed that Mari-senpai was wrapping something like a pink cover around her shins.
Looking around, I saw that Tsuruoka-san and Yohei were putting on similar things too, in blue and yellow.
Takizawa-san and Yabe-san, though, weren’t wearing any.
“…Um, what’s that?” I asked Mari-senpai.
She paused, smiled a little, and pointed at her ankles.
“This? They’re gaiters. They keep the bottoms of your pants from getting wet from snow or rain. They also stop snow from getting inside your boots.”
“Huh… there’s something like that?” I replied, genuinely impressed.
Mari-senpai gave a small shrug and smiled, almost bashfully.
“Well, I’m a girl. Even getting a little wet makes you cold.”
Looking around, Takizawa-san and Tsuruoka-san had already finished putting theirs on smoothly, and Yohei cheerfully called out, “Alright, now we can really power through this!”
I steeled myself too and looked down at my boots fitted with crampons, staring at the edge of the snowfield ahead.
This was where the real challenge began.
“There might be crevasses here… maybe we oughta anseilen, don’t you think?” Tsuruoka-san said, pulling a light blue rope from his pack.
—Anseilen? What’s anseilen? I thought.
“…Huh? Anseilen on the Hakuba Great Snowfield? I’ve never heard of anyone doing that. That’s what the red route line is there for, isn’t it?” Yabe-san said, shaking her head with a half-amused smile.
“No, no, you never know what might happen. Safety first.” Tsuruoka-san looked completely serious.
—What are they even talking about?
What was he planning to do with that rope anyway? I wondered.
“This is the kind of place where tourists come up in sneakers. Wouldn’t it be embarrassing to rope ourselves together here?” Yabe-san said.
“What’s anseilen?” I asked.
“It means tying everyone together with a rope,” Mari-senpai explained with a laugh.
“Tsuru-san, were you seriously planning to anseilen here?” Takizawa-san asked with a wry smile.
“Well, you said there might be crevasses, so…”
After hesitating for a moment, Tsuruoka-san reluctantly stuffed the rope back into his pack.
Probably because no one was willing to humor him any further, and everyone simply went back to focusing on their own preparations, he had no choice but to give up.

A sudden gust of wind swept through.
The coldness brushed across the sweat on the back of my neck, and a small “Oh…” slipped out before I knew it.
The heat that had built up in my body under the sunlit trail cooled all at once.
It was almost a little too chilly now, enough to raise goosebumps along my bare arms.
“From here on, it gets slippery, so stay sharp,” Takizawa-san said as he gave the straps of his crampons one last tug to check them.
“Roger that, captain,” Mari-senpai replied with a laugh, and Yohei grinned.
“Oh, then should we march in salute from here?” he joked, snapping a salute and pretending to march in place.
“Hakkōda Mountains?” I said, watching him.
“Don’t say creepy stuff like that, Sho. I’m making it back alive,” Yohei said, dropping the salute and stopping his feet as he laughed at me.
“Well then, now that we’ve got our crampons on, let’s try not to cramp on the mood too early,” Tsuruoka-san said with a grin.
“There’s someone slipping already, and we haven’t even stepped onto the snow yet,” Yabe-san shot back, making Mari-senpai giggle under her breath.
That exchange made the corners of my own mouth loosen too.
