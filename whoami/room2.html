宿泊部屋

I can't take this anymore.
I don't understand any of this anymore.
Mari-senpai is gone.
Those words echoed over and over inside my head.
Now that Mari-senpai was no longer Mari-senpai,
reason, judgment, and priorities
all seemed meaningless.
Whether there was a storm outside,
or nothing but darkness,
I just wanted—
to get away from this building.
The moment that thought crossed my mind,
my body began to move on its own.
There was nothing left to protect me anymore.
I wanted something—anything—to keep me safe.
A short-sleeved T-shirt.
Shorts.
Bare feet.
And—
between the rain, the sweat,
and whatever I had touched,
my whole body was soaking wet.
I was completely defenseless.
At the very least,
I wanted something warm and dry to wear.
The moment I became aware of how cold I really was,
my teeth began to chatter.
Goosebumps rose along my arms,
and the feeling in my fingertips slowly faded.
At this rate,
I wouldn't even be able to move
before I had a chance to escape.
That was what I thought.
My room.
My spare clothes were there.
Dry clothes.
Something to warm my body,
if only a little.
It wasn't a decision born of reason.
It was almost pure instinct.
I want to live.
That was all.
And so,
before heading back out into the storm,
I decided to go to my room first.

Mari-senpai is gone.
Mari-senpai is gone.
Mari-senpai is gone.
Like words that had lost all meaning,
the same sounds kept circling endlessly in my mind.
I couldn't save her.
I told her I would protect her.
I wanted us to escape together.
That voice.
That warmth.
That hand.
All of it—
just moments ago—
had truly been there.
And yet,
in a single instant,
it was taken away,
beyond anything I could do.
It felt as though
a vast hole had opened inside my chest.
No matter how deeply I breathed,
nothing could fill it.
I didn't even have the strength to cry.
I didn't have the strength to scream.
All that remained,
like a broken machine,
was that same phrase,
repeating over and over.
—Mari-senpai is gone.
I don't care anymore.
I don't care what happens now.
I just want to get out of here.
I just want something
that can protect me.

The room where I was supposed to stay.
If I could get there,
I could get outside.
I could escape this horrible world.
I could forget everything and become nothing.
Clinging to that thought alone,
I ran.
The passage between the raised sleeping areas.
The edges of the tatami mats,
the shadows cast by the pillars—
places I would have paid attention to just moments ago
were now nothing more than scenery rushing past.
My footsteps sounded unnaturally loud.
My breathing was ragged,
and my throat burned.
It hurt,
as if my lungs themselves refused to take in air.
The staircase at the center of the lodge
loomed closer and closer ahead.
Close.
Almost there.
As I reached the corner of the sleeping area,
I instinctively turned left
to pass in front of the stairs.
—And then.
An unbearable stench struck my nose.
Like rotting flesh
left out in the rain,
it was sweet, heavy,
and twisted the pit of my stomach.
The instant I looked down,
I knew what it was.
Something one of those things had disgorged.
Eyeballs... strands of hair...
clumps of what must once have been human flesh,
human cells.
Before I could even think,
my body reacted.
I leapt away from them,
pathetically, desperately,
more wildly than I would have thought possible.
Hop, hop—
I bounded over the mess.
My mind was completely blank.
I couldn't even scream.
Carried by that momentum,
I ran past the landing of the stairs.
Away from the large sleeping hall.
The air in the corridor
changed ever so slightly.
I turned right.
At the edge of my vision,
the staircase leading down.
Don't look.
Don't look at it.
Keep going.
On my left,
the entrance to the six-person room.
Dark.
I couldn't bring myself to think
about what might be inside.
Farther ahead,
the section where the four-person rooms stood in a row.
Here.
I turned left.
My heart pounded
like a machine on the verge of breaking apart.
My back felt terribly cold.
Even so,
my legs refused to stop.
And so,
stumbling forward,
I kept moving
toward where my room should have been.

The room closest to the front on the left was "Unkai"—Sea of Clouds.
Across from it was "Gekkō"—Moonlight.
Farther in, on the left, was "Hinode"—Sunrise,
the room where the men in our group had been assigned to stay.
And on the right...
"Seppō"—Snow Peak,
where the women were supposed to sleep.
Repeating those names in my mind,
I reached for the sliding door on the left,
the second room from the entrance.
Creeeak...
The old wood gave a quiet cry.
Inside the room,
four futons had been laid out neatly across the tatami floor.
The room was about six tatami mats in size.
There was only a narrow gap between the bedding—
just enough for a person to squeeze through—
and the only place to put luggage was along the walls.
There were no windows.
The damp air and the scent of tatami
felt strangely heavy,
as though they were settling deep inside my chest.
In each corner
stood small shelves and platforms for personal belongings.
Against the wall directly opposite the entrance,
a small heater sat by itself.
The ceiling was low,
with thick wooden beams partially exposed.
The chill mountain air
mingled with the scent of old timber,
and for just a fleeting moment,
it almost made me believe
that this place was comforting.
—No.
I couldn't let myself surrender to that feeling.
I pulled my pack closer
and rummaged through it, yanking out a change of clothes.
I stripped off everything.
Each time the soaked fabric peeled away from my skin,
a shiver of cold stabbed deep into my bones.
—If something attacked me right now, it'd be all over.
Well... if that's how it ends, maybe that's fine too...
Thinking that, I dried myself with a towel,
but my waterlogged skin
grew cold again almost immediately.
Outside, the rain was still pouring down.
Normally, I should have been putting on my rain gear,
but I didn't have it anymore.
I'd given it to Morishita Aya-san.
—That was the right thing to do.
At least, I wanted to believe it was.
But now...
she might already be...
part of that monster.
Don't think about it.
Not now.
Don't think.
Black trekking pants.
A wool shirt.
A navy fleece.
And over that,
my olive-green Sierra Designs 60/40 mountain parka.
Finally,
a pair of wool socks.
The surface of my body
was warm again.
But—
deep inside,
I was still frozen.
The cold within me
refused to move,
refused to leave.
There was still food left in my pack.
I might need it someday.
I hoped that day would never come.
But somehow,
I couldn't shake the feeling that it would.
I slung the pack onto my back.
The problem was—
my boots.
My climbing boots
were downstairs.
And downstairs...
those things were roaming around.
How was I supposed to get them back?
How was I supposed to
make it through there?
Lost in thought,
I stepped out of the room.

Just as I was about to slide the door shut—
Clack.
A small, dry sound.
Yet in the silence of the corridor,
it echoed far louder than it should have.
My heart tightened in my chest.
My body stiffened instinctively.
My breathing stopped,
and my ears strained to catch every sound.
—Again...?
Without thinking,
my eyes turned to the right.
The room called "Seppō,"
where the women had been supposed to stay.
From beyond the sliding door,
just now,
I had definitely heard it—
something moving.
The air
grew a shade colder.
Holding my breath,
I stood perfectly still,
careful not to make a sound.
—What is it this time...?

An awful thought surfaced
in the back of my mind.
Those things.
The thing I'd seen just moments ago.
It was hiding somewhere,
and it had made that sound.
That was all.
The moment that thought crossed my mind,
a cold bead of sweat ran down my back.
Then...
I should leave.
Right now.
There was no reason to stay here.
Getting my boots back
wasn't worth risking my life for.
...And yet.
My feet refused to move.
Something felt wrong.
If it was one of those things...
if it was one of them...
then the moment it noticed me,
it should have burst out
and attacked without hesitation.
And yet,
nothing came.
It remained hidden,
making only the faintest sounds from beyond the door,
as if it were holding its breath.
—Why?
I didn't want to think about it,
but I couldn't stop myself.
Could someone...
still be alive in there?
No.
That couldn't be true.
That's what I wanted to believe.
Everyone was already...
Mari-senpai...
Morishita-san...
everyone...
A sharp pain tightened in my chest.
But what if...
what if whoever was inside
was still human?
What if they were injured,
terrified,
holding their breath,
waiting for someone to help them?
...Could I really walk away?
Pretend I hadn't known?
Pretend I hadn't heard anything,
and simply run?
Inside my head,
the voice telling me to run
and the voice telling me not to leave anyone behind
twisted together into a tangled mess.
I couldn't take
a single step.
Then—
from inside the room called Seppō,
I heard a voice.
Clear this time.
“...Who's there?”
A woman's voice.
Trembling.
Quiet.
As if she were desperately trying to keep herself from being heard.
My heart gave a violent thump.
I knew that voice.
I didn't even have to think about it.
The name surfaced on its own.
...Yabe-san?
That's right.
I'd forgotten about Yabe-san.
She wasn't among the people I'd seen get taken.
Maybe—just maybe—there was still a chance that she was human.
And Yabe-san had always been someone who said exactly what needed to be said at the right moment.
If someone as dependable as her was still on my side,
that alone would be a tremendous relief.
Though...
the truth was,
I had no way of knowing whether she was still human or not.

To be honest,
at that moment,
I no longer cared.
Whether Yabe-san was human,
or one of those things,
it didn't matter anymore.
I'd already come this far.
If the one on the other side was one of those things—
then this would simply be the end.
I had no strength left to run.
No determination left to resist.
After losing Mari-senpai,
after breaking the promise I'd made to protect her,
something important inside me had already been hollowed out.
"…Yabe-san?"
My voice came out far more hoarse than I'd expected.
My throat burned,
and calling her name alone took all the strength I had left.
After a brief pause,
a timid voice answered from beyond the sliding door.
"…Sakuma-kun?"
The way she said my name
made something deep inside my chest tremble.
"Yes. It's Sho."
The calmness in my own voice surprised even me.
I forced myself to keep the trembling under control.
"…Sakuma-kun, Tsuruoka-san was giving you a hard time, wasn't he?"
Yabe-san's voice still sounded distant.
"He said you stopped the production line
because you forgot to buy that Omron limit switch..."
—No.
My mind cleared in an instant.
(It wasn't a limit switch that I forgot.)
(It was a solenoid valve.)
(And the manufacturer was... SMC.)
Cold sweat crept down my back.
(She's testing me...)
(She's deliberately getting it wrong to see whether I'm human or one of those things.)
If Yabe-san were one of those things,
she wouldn't bother with something so roundabout.
There'd be no need to confirm anything.
She could simply attack,
and it would all be over.
Deliberately saying something wrong
and waiting to see how I reacted—
wasn't that something only a human being would do?
And yet,
even as I thought that,
I wanted to believe.
Perhaps that contradiction itself—
the desire to trust despite the doubt—
was proof that Yabe-san was still human.
Those things could use a person's memories and knowledge
to deceive others.
So the truth was,
I couldn't know for certain.
But it didn't matter anymore.
I took a slow breath
in front of the sliding door.
"…Yabe-san."
As normally as possible.
Trying not to put too much emotion into my voice.
"The thing I forgot to buy
was an SMC solenoid valve."
For a moment,
everything on the other side fell silent.
"But..."
I continued.
"Those things seem perfectly capable of using
a person's memories and thoughts
to fool people."
My own voice felt strangely distant.
"So even if I gave the correct answer,
I don't think that alone proves
that I'm human."
Silence.
Only the rain
and the creaking of the building
echoed loudly in my ears.
Then—
I heard a small sound,
almost like a sigh.
"…If you were one of those things,"
Yabe-san murmured,
"you wouldn't say something like that, would you?"
Click.
A metallic sound rang out.
It was the sound of the lock
being undone from inside.
"…Come in."
The door opened,
just a crack.
Through the narrow gap,
I could see the darkness of the room
and Yabe-san's slender silhouette.
I hesitated for only a moment,
then quietly took a single step
into the room.

The moment I stepped into Yabe-san's room,
all the strength drained out of me at once.
I sank down onto the tatami floor,
unable to say anything for a while.

Yabe-san watched me from a short distance away.
Her expression seemed to hold her emotions in check,
yet her eyes missed nothing.
"...Sakuma-kun."
Suddenly, she called my name.
"What have you been doing all this time?"
There was no blame in her voice.
No attempt at comfort, either.
She simply wanted to know the facts.
I searched for the right words for a moment,
then answered honestly.
"You know I was locked in the storage room, right?"
Yabe-san's eyebrows moved ever so slightly.
"But there was an opening in the ceiling... and I managed to climb up into the crawl space above.
From there, above the dining hall... I saw everything that was happening down below."
Without saying a word,
Yabe-san looked at me as if urging me to continue.
"After that, I went to the staff room and met up with the lodge workers, Uchida-san, and Tsuruoka-san.
And then... I reunited with the people who were on the second floor."
The words grew heavier on their own.
"But Kobayashi Ren-san... started to change.
So I had to escape into the crawl space again."
A brief silence followed.
"...I see."
Yabe-san gave a small nod.
"So you witnessed everything that happened in the dining hall."
"...Yes."
For the first time,
Yabe-san lowered her eyes.
