新館２階

When I finally stepped onto the top stair, I let out a quiet sigh.
Even that brief moment of relief was swallowed by the roar of wind and rain.
The landing was narrow, and a wooden sliding door stood in front of me.
Above it was a small latticed window.
A faint yellow light seeped through it.
The room beyond was lit by incandescent bulbs.
The light should have felt warm.
But in these circumstances, it only made the place feel more unsettling.
I carefully stretched upward and peered through the latticed window.
Inside was a narrow hallway running left and right.
A wooden wall blocked the far end, and I could see open entrances on both sides.
They probably led to the large rooms beyond.
Then I thought I caught a faint sound that wasn't part of the storm.
Clack...
Maybe it was just the building settling.
But in a place like this, even the smallest sound could squeeze the heart with fear.
Holding my breath, I stood before the sliding door and slowly reached out, placing my fingertips against it.
When everyone had been gathered in the dining hall, Okubo-san had ordered all the entrances locked.
The memory crossed my mind, and a bad feeling spread through my chest.
(Is it locked...?)
If it was, I'd have to lift both sides of the sliding door at the same time and pull them inward to remove them from their tracks.
There would be no way to do that quietly.
Tense, I pulled the door ever so slightly.
—Sshh.
It opened.
The lock... wasn't engaged.
I narrowed my eyes for a moment.
After the announcement over the PA system, even the part-time staff had gone around checking the building.
It was hard to believe anyone could have simply forgotten to lock it.
Which meant someone—or rather, something—had passed through here.
The thought sent a chill down my spine.
Had it been left open on purpose?
So they could move freely in and out?
My breath caught in my throat.
I couldn't afford to let my guard down.
Keeping my heartbeat under control, I slowly slid the door open without making a sound.
Cool, slightly damp air drifted through the gap.
I stepped inside and gently closed the door behind me.
Rainwater streamed from my clothes and body, splashing onto the dry floorboards.
Within moments, puddles began spreading around my feet.
Just before the door closed completely, the storm outside seemed to surge louder for an instant—
then it was cut off.
For the first time in a while, my bare feet left the metal anti-slip treads behind and touched wooden flooring.
The relief was immediate.
Water dripped steadily from my soaked clothes, pattering onto the floor and gathering into shallow pools around me.
Silence.
...Or so I thought.
—Sniff...
A faint sound of crying drifted from somewhere inside.
For a moment, I stopped breathing.
The sobbing was quiet and fragile.
So faint it sounded as though it might disappear at any second,
yet somehow it clung to my ears.
Mari-senpai...?
...Was that Mari-senpai's voice?
Heat rose in my chest before I could stop it.
Why was she crying...?
Because everyone else had been taken?
Or because she didn't know what had happened to me?
But if she was crying, then there was still a chance she hadn't been taken.
Maybe... maybe I could still save her.
Hope stabbed at my chest.
And at the same time,
that hope felt almost like a trap.
The sound was coming from beyond the entrance on the left.
I slowly made my way toward it.
Water dripped from my soaked clothes onto the dry floor,
spreading into puddles around me.
The sensation of the floor clinging to the soles of my feet felt unnaturally loud.
As though my own body had become a source of noise.
When I reached the entrance,
another small sob drifted from the darkness beyond.
I steadied my breathing,
gathered my courage,
and stepped inside—

I slipped through the entrance on the left and carefully stepped inside.
The first thing that caught my eye was the wooden closet with sliding doors near the left wall.
The faint smell of old timber and dampness lingered in the air.
A dim passage stretched ahead through the center of the room, narrowing in perspective.
To the right was a raised tatami area that extended toward the back.
It was where climbers used to sleep side by side.
Beyond the closet on the left was a similar raised section.
Beneath the sleeping area was a crawl space.
The very place where I had been hiding not long ago.
That's where Kobayashi Ren-san had begun to change.
And where that "man"—the one who had pointed a knife at me from above and locked me in the storage room—had fallen down from the attic.
The memory alone sent an icy jolt through my spine.
Everything had fallen apart in that single moment.
At this point, it wouldn't be surprising if something emerged from anywhere.
Carefully, I scanned the ceiling.
The second floor had no ceiling panels installed, leaving the attic framework completely exposed.
Darkness and the shadows of the beams intertwined overhead.
It looked like the kind of place where something could be hiding if you stared long enough.
But—
No one was there.
Nothing moved.
Only a heavy, damp silence hung in the room.
There was, however, one place where a definite presence could be felt.
The closet on the left.
From inside it...
That faint, trembling sound of sobbing
was still drifting out.
—Could it really be Mari-senpai after all?
...At last... could I finally see her again?
...And to think she had been this close the whole time... right here on the second floor, so near the place where I had been hiding.
The answer had been right under my nose.
Fate could be unbelievably cruel sometimes.
My throat tightened.
The presence beyond the door felt far too fragile, far too frightening to place my hopes in.
And yet I couldn't stop myself from checking.
I stood before the closet.
As I reached out, I noticed my fingertips trembling slightly.
I drew a shallow breath.
Swallowed my fear.
Then gently placed a hand on the wooden sliding door.
—Gii...
The moment I pulled it even slightly,
the old wood answered with a low, damp creak.
Holding my breath, I hooked my trembling fingers around the right-hand panel and slowly slid it open.
The faint sound of sobbing seemed to spill through the widening gap.
As the door slid aside, the scent of old fabric drifted out, along with air that still seemed to hold the lingering warmth of a human presence.
Unused futons were piled carelessly on the upper shelf.
And beneath their shadow—
a green-and-blue plaid wool shirt.
Beige trekking pants.
Black hair tied back in a ponytail.
The woman sat facing left, her posture collapsed from seiza, her shoulders trembling with quiet sobs.
Something hot surged through my chest all at once.
Mari-senpai...
Mari-senpai—the one who always stood up for me whenever Tsuruoka-san picked a fight with me.
Mari-senpai, who had been kind to me ever since we met at company events—the barbecue, the ski trip, even the cherry blossom viewing at Yoshinoyama that spring.
Mari-senpai standing beside me beneath the cherry trees in full bloom at Yoshinoyama.
“Let’s come again together next year.”
Those words still lingered somewhere deep in my ears.
Mari-senpai's voice speaking to me alone amid the noise of the crowd.
Even now, I wanted to believe it had been a special moment.
“Wow, look, look! It’s so beautiful!
…Doesn’t it look like a postcard?
…And that shape—like twin peaks!”
I could almost hear her voice again as we walked along the mountain trail.
“Saku-chan, could you take a picture of me crossing?
Make sure you get the mountains in the background too.”
Mari-senpai asking me to take her picture as she crossed the timber bridge over the stream.
Mari-senpai flashing a cheerful peace sign from the middle of the bridge.
“Hehe, but those stream photos are totally Instagram-worthy, right?
Saku-chan, you got a good shot, didn’t you?
It was a great composition.
Will you send it to my phone later?”
Mari-senpai's voice echoed in my memory.
“Oh, wow—this pack opens all the way from the front?”
“Nice, right?
It’s really convenient when you need to grab something quickly.”
Our conversation back at Hakubajiri.
“Ta-da—Baumkuchen.”
Ta-da—Baumkuchen.
Ta-da—Baumkuchen.
Ta-da—Baumkuchen.
Mari-senpai's voice at Hakubajiri.
“Saku-chan, with crampons, you secure the toe firmly first, then wrap this strap around behind your ankle and pull it tight.
Make sure it’s snug, okay?
If it’s loose, it might come off while you’re walking.”
Mari-senpai teaching me how to put on crampons.
“You don’t have to push yourself.
As long as you keep moving forward, even little by little, you’ll reach the lodge eventually...
Let’s go slowly together.”
The gentle words Mari-senpai had spoken when I was exhausted.
“Hehe, that’s good. Saku-chan, you can finally get a little rest.”
I remembered her saying that at the shelter hut.
“Hehe… spicy,” she said with a smile.
“But it’s really good.”
Her voice came back to me from that meal in the shelter hut.
“Saku-chan, you’re lucky.
It’s not something you get to see very often.”
I remembered her words when we saw the Brocken spectre.

“...Mari-senpai! Thank goodness... you're safe!”
The moment I spoke, my throat tightened so badly I could barely get the words out.
Still facing sideways, Mari-senpai slowly lifted her head just a little.
She sobbed like a child who had cried so hard she could no longer breathe properly.
“Saku-chan... I... I...”
There was something deeply wrong in her voice.
What happened? What happened to her? Mari-senpai... what happened to you?
The sound of her voice sent a sharp pain through my chest.
“Mari-senpai, I'll protect you. Let's get out of here together. Everything's going to be okay...”
For a brief moment, her trembling stopped.
Then, in a hoarse whisper, she said,
“Saku-chan... my...
my eye... came out...”
Her eye came out...?
What does that even mean?
So what if you lost an eye?
You could wear an eye patch...
I wouldn't stop caring about you because of something like that.
Or... is that not what she means?
Unable to understand, I felt a sudden, crushing pain spread through my chest.
Slowly, Mari-senpai raised her right hand.
Resting in her palm—
was an eyeball, dark and wet like a piece of polished obsidian.
I caught my breath.
Something inside my chest froze solid.
Mari-senpai slowly lifted her face again and turned toward me.
The green and blue of her shirt.
Her cheeks wet with tears.
And then—
the eye on the left side of her face was gone.
Deep within the gaping hollow,
something moved.
Like a tongue—
and yet, not a tongue.
A slender tendril emerged, dripping with slime,
and began to slowly lick—
slurp... slurp...
—as though savoring the taste of Mari-senpai's own cheek.
A crushing sensation seized my heart all at once.
It felt as though a stake had been driven straight through my chest.
What must Mari-senpai have felt... after losing her eye and seeing herself reduced to this?
What must she be feeling right now?
Mari-senpai, who had always treated me with such kindness...
Mari-senpai, whom I had sworn to protect no matter what...
The instant my mind tried to imagine it,
my thoughts stopped completely.
The sounds of the world vanished.
And everything inside my head turned pure white.
