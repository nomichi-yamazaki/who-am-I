Lounge

“Alright, what should we do next?” Takizawa-san asked.
“Why don’t we take a short break in the lounge?” Mari-senpai suggested.
“Yeah, that sounds like a good idea,” Tsuruoka-san nodded.
“We did some exploring, but I kinda want to relax somewhere for a bit,” Yohei said with a grin.
“Yeah… let’s take it easy for a while, then decide what to do next,” I added quietly.
And so, we slowly began making our way back the way we had come.
On either side were the shared sleeping areas, and straight ahead stood the double doors of the old wing, silent and still.
Takizawa-san placed his hand on the doors and slowly pushed them open.
They creaked as they swung apart.
Takizawa-san and Tsuruoka-san stepped out of the old wing.
“Oh right, I’ll turn off the lights in here,” I said.
“Hold up, I’m going out first,” Yohei said, quickly stepping ahead of me.
“What’s with you? You think a ghost’s gonna pop out or something?” Tsuruoka-san laughed as he turned back.
“Well… it does feel kind of eerie when it gets dark…” Mari-senpai said with a small laugh, glancing over her shoulder.
“Sho… behind you…” Yohei said, looking toward me as I stood by the switch.
“Yeah, yeah,” I laughed it off, knowing he was joking.
After making sure everyone was out, I flipped the switches one by one—click, click, click, click—and the lights in the old wing went out.
The hallway fell into silence, filled only with the sound of our footsteps and breathing.
In the darkness, it somehow felt like the lingering tension of the exploration grew even stronger.
I took the door from the person in front of me, turned back, and closed it behind us.
Then I turned forward again and climbed the wooden slope, walking toward the sliding door of the main building as light gradually returned.
When we stepped inside, there was a storage room on the right, a shared kitchen for guests on the left, and further ahead on the right, a recreation room.
And just like that, we arrived at a place where we could finally take a breath.

“…After this, the wind and rain are expected to intensify rapidly in northern Japan as well… Please stay updated with the latest information.” The television was reporting on the approaching typhoon.
A pair of green slippers had been neatly placed in front of the entrance.
There was no door; instead, rectangular wooden posts stood on either side at slight intervals, subtly marking the boundary between the hallway and the room.
Inside, a woman sat alone, sipping her coffee.
She looked to be around thirty-four, wearing simple, thin-framed glasses, her black hair neatly cut just below her shoulders. She carried herself with a calm demeanor, dressed in gray trousers and a beige jacket.
Her features were gentle, with an air of quiet intelligence.
“Hey there!” Takizawa-san called out cheerfully to the woman.
“Well, well—pardon us for intruding,” Tsuruoka-san added, raising a hand with a slightly playful tone.
“Hey…” Yohei gave a small, slightly bashful nod.
“Excuse us…” Mari-senpai said in a calm voice.
“Hello…” I greeted softly, but clearly.
The woman responded with a slight bow.
“…Ah, yes, hello.”
Even to Tsuruoka-san’s somewhat joking greeting, she simply smiled gently and returned a quiet bow.
As we stepped inside, the floor rose slightly, transitioning into tatami, its softness and warmth spreading under our feet.
On the wall ahead was a window set in a wooden lattice frame, its glass fogged with condensation, diffusing the pale light from outside into a gentle glow across the room.
In the far right corner stood a shelf, and on top of it sat a small television—an old CRT, of all things—still calmly broadcasting the typhoon report.
Along the right wall, bookshelves were packed tightly with guidebooks and materials related to the mountains.
On the left wall hung framed photographs of the mountains and flowers around Hakuba, adding a quiet touch of nature to the room.
In the near corner, zabuton cushions were neatly stacked, while in the far corner sat a low stand holding a tray, several teacups placed upside down, and beside them a teapot and a tea canister.
Two low tables were arranged in a line on the tatami, stretching toward the back of the room.
The lounge was quiet and composed, yet filled with the lived-in warmth of mountain climbers passing through.
We took off our slippers, stepped into the room, and brought cushions from the edge before settling down.

From the television set placed in the far right corner of the lounge, the steady voice of an announcer flowed calmly.
“As of 4:30 p.m., here is the latest information on Typhoon No. 12.”
On the screen, a map of Japan was displayed, with the typhoon’s path traced in red.
Cloud movements and rain distribution were animated across the map, with intense purple and red areas spreading mainly along the Pacific side, indicating heavy rainfall.
The announcer continued in a composed tone.
“Typhoon No. 12 is currently moving north-northeast over the sea south of western Japan. Its central pressure is 955 hectopascals, with maximum sustained winds near the center reaching 38 meters per second, and gusts up to 55 meters per second.
Strong winds and heavy rainfall are affecting a wide area. Please remain on high alert for rising river levels and potential landslides.”
A red news ticker scrolled along the bottom of the screen, constantly updating wind speeds and the movement of the storm.
The announcer added another warning.
“The typhoon is expected to continue moving northeast, bringing prolonged effects across a wide region from western to eastern Japan through tomorrow. Please stay tuned to the latest weather updates.”
As we absentmindedly watched and listened to the broadcast, the five of us settled onto the tatami floor of the lounge.
The sound of the wind striking the roof outside blended with the television’s report, echoing quietly through the room.

Sitting on a cushion, I stretched out my legs and leaned back on my hands, turning my gaze to the right.
Across the hallway, I could see another lounge—this one in a Western style.
Like this room, it had no door at the entrance, leaving the interior fully visible. Rows of rectangular wooden posts lined both sides, subtly dividing the hallway from the room.
Inside, the floor continued at the same level as the hallway, with a long-legged table placed at the center and chairs arranged neatly around it.
Two young women were inside.
They were the same girls who had called out to us, asking if we were okay while we were digging up that thing… the same two who had come down the stairs chatting about a movie while we were gathered around something near the lodge entrance.
They had been talking about some actor from a mountain film—about how cool he was, or something like that…
Each of them now rested their elbows on the table, quietly scrolling through their phones.
A window lined the wall in front of them, its glass fogged with condensation, obscuring any view outside.

“…Man, this is something else. Spending a typhoon night in a mountain lodge like this… you don’t get that very often, right?”
Even though he really should’ve known better, Tsuruoka-san leaned on the edge of the low table with one elbow, grinning as he struck up a conversation with the woman who had been there before us.
“That’s true. Well… when you’re traveling, all sorts of things can happen.”The woman lifted what looked like her own stainless steel cup with both hands, brought it to her lips, and replied with a small, composed smile.
“Heh… city folks really are calm, huh. If it were me, I’d honestly be a little freaked out.”
Sitting cross-legged on a cushion, Yohei leaned forward as he spoke.
“Yohei, you panic just from a little thunder. Don’t make such a big deal out of a typhoon.”
Mari-senpai said, setting her teacup down.
The atmosphere softened.
As if to say see?, Tsuruoka-san shrugged his shoulders, while Yohei puffed his cheeks in mild annoyance.
The woman let out a quiet laugh and gave a small bow.
“Well, still… looks like it’s gonna get pretty rough out there tonight. Best not do anything reckless.”
Takizawa-san said, arms crossed, watching the television with a slightly more serious tone.
…The thing we left in the drying room—no one’s watching it. Is that really okay?
If something happens…
No… I can’t say anything now.
If I do, they’ll just say, “Then go check it yourself.”
No. Better to keep quiet.
I forced a smile, pretending to follow the conversation while those thoughts circled quietly in my mind.

“Still, typhoons really are a pain, huh. So, miss—are you staying here for climbing? Or just sightseeing?”
Tsuruoka-san continued casually, eyes still on the TV, a friendly grin on his face.
—Man… just like back at Hakubajiri, he goes for it the moment he sees a woman…
What a dirty old man… seriously…
Doesn’t he ever try to be a little more modest?
…Yeah, people from that Banrikyo really are a bit… different.
That’s what I thought.
“I was planning to do a bit of walking in the mountains… but with the weather like this, it’s going to be difficult.”
Holding the cup gently in both hands as if to warm them, the woman answered with a restrained smile.
Was she actually uncomfortable inside?
And still, she could keep smiling like that…
Watching her, I found myself thinking—this must be what it means to have that kind of adult composure.

“I see, I see! Well, that’s a shame. So what kind of mountains do you usually climb? Or is this more like a work trip?”
Leaning forward as if gaining momentum, Tsuruoka-san kept piling on the questions.
“Yes… well, something like that.”
The woman gave a faint, slightly troubled smile and lowered her gaze.
Out of the corner of my eye, I noticed Yohei grinning.
He was clearly enjoying watching Tsuruoka-san make a fool of himself.
Mari-senpai folded her arms, letting out an exasperated sigh.
Even so, Tsuruoka-san didn’t stop.
“But really, what a coincidence, meeting out here in the middle of nowhere! You know, we’re kind of like an expedition team ourselves—we even went as far as the old building earlier—”
At that moment, the woman’s gaze flickered.
Then, softly—“Ah.”
She picked up her cup and rose to her feet, a gentle smile returning to her face.
“I’m sorry… I just remembered something. I think I’ll go rest for a bit.”With a slight bow, she left the room.
As I watched her back, I couldn’t shake the feeling that her “remembering something” had been… deliberate.
—He tends to drift off a lot, you know,
Remembering what Yabe-san had said back at Hakubajiri, I thought maybe saying something now would get a laugh… but I couldn’t bring myself to say it.
“See? You were being so pushy, she ran off.”
Mari-senpai said with a teasing grin, as if voicing exactly what I had been thinking.
Yohei burst out laughing, and I couldn’t help but laugh along with him.
Tsuruoka-san crossed his arms, looking slightly annoyed—yet still managing a crooked grin.
“Hey, we couldn’t exactly talk about that with someone else around, right? So I figured I’d, you know… push a little on purpose.”
—Yeah, right. Total excuse.
I thought, barely holding back a laugh.

“Tsuru-san, by that, you mean the thing we picked up from the snowfield, right?” Takizawa-san said.
“Yeah, that’s the one,” Tsuruoka-san replied.
Yohei muttered, “Knew it… you’re seriously thinking about getting famous off that, aren’t you, senpai?”
Seizing the moment, Tsuruoka-san’s eyes lit up as he launched into his explanation.
“So listen—what do you think would happen if we told someone about it? Sure, it might start as just a small rumor… but rumors spread fast. Before you know it, it’ll be all over research labs and academic circles… might even get picked up by national magazines.”
Mari-senpai widened her eyes. “Wait… that big?” she said, half in disbelief.
Leaning back deeply into his cushion, Tsuruoka-san continued, his eyes sparkling.
“Think about it—first it spreads as a small rumor around town. Then maybe the newspapers pick it up, and the next week it’s on TV. Can you picture it? Nationwide coverage!”
Startled, Yohei let out, “Seriously… that much?”
Tsuruoka-san shook his head, gaining even more momentum.
“That’s just the beginning! Magazines, academic conferences, even international journals! Researchers’ll come pouring in. Our names’ll be right there too! Photos, interviews—man, can you imagine?”
Mari-senpai waved her hand, looking fed up. “Okay, that’s enough…”
But Tsuruoka-san didn’t slow down.
“Think bigger! Research teams from all over the world will gather in Hakuba. Airports, ports—packed with scientists. Hotels fully booked. The whole town in an uproar! They might even come interview us right here on these cushions!”
With a wry smile, Takizawa-san asked, “Come on, Tsuru-san… would it really get that big?”
Raising one hand and speaking louder, Tsuruoka-san declared with confidence,
“It will, it will! Don’t underestimate my imagination. This is what you call a major discovery! We’re standing right on the edge of a moment that’ll go down in history!”
I couldn’t help but let out a small laugh on the tatami.
—No way it’d actually get that crazy…
Even so, I could feel myself getting pulled in by his energy… a quiet excitement starting to build inside me.
Tsuruoka-san leaned even further forward, lowering his voice to a conspiratorial whisper.
“Just imagine it—scientists from all over the world gathering here in this lodge in Hakuba. The press will come, tourists will flood in. We won’t even have a place left to sit on our own cushions! Ahh, our names will live on forever!”
With a sigh, Mari-senpai murmured, “Your imagination really is something else…”
Still seated on the tatami, Tsuruoka-san pushed himself forward even more as he continued.
“Think about it—requests for exhibitions will start pouring in! Museums, science centers, universities—even overseas! Our thing will be put on special display! Ahh, the day our names are engraved on a plaque isn’t far off!”
Yohei gave a wry smile. “Come on… you really think the whole world’s gonna move like that?”
Tsuruoka-san shook his head lightly, his excitement only growing.
“Too naive, too naive! You think it’ll be some small exhibit? On opening day, researchers, reporters, students, tourists—they’ll all come rushing in! We’ll be sitting right here on the tatami, giving interviews!”
Mari-senpai shrugged, letting out another sigh. “Someone please stop him…”
But Tsuruoka-san only laughed, leaning in even closer.
“And then—our thing gets published in academic journals! Recognized by the scientific community, cited by researchers all over the world! Ahh, it’ll be studied at universities everywhere!”
Takizawa-san gave a strained smile. “Tsuru-san… calm down…”
But there was no stopping him.
“And after that! Once the exhibition ends, we might even get auction inquiries! Memorabilia, merchandise, replica requests for researchers—there’ll be no end to it!”
Tsuruoka-san shifted on the tatami, almost making it creak, and whispered again.
“In the end, requests for investigations will come from all over the world! Our thing will become a star of the scientific world! Ahh, my name will be recorded forever alongside it!”
With a satisfied grin, Tsuruoka-san clapped his hands sharply.
“Alright, that settles it! We’re all set! The moment the world changes—it’s going to happen right before our eyes! …And hey, I was the first one to spot that glowing thing, so this whole discovery started with me, got it…?”
At that moment, a flash of light burst outside the window.
—Oh… thunder.
I thought.
With that flash behind him, Tsuruoka-san somehow looked like Dracula, standing there with his cloak spread wide.
Rumble… rumble…
Thunder began to roll outside.
There was a long delay between the flash and the sound, so it still seemed far away.
