Paranoia

“What do you think Akane-san was trying to do if she got in here…?”
Beyond Tsuruoka-san, Yohei spoke while giving a small shrug.
The windows of the dining hall reflected the room inside.
Even now, it still felt as though Akane-san were standing somewhere outside those windows.
As if that thing wearing Akane-san’s form were peering in from somewhere beyond the darkness…
I lowered my gaze to the edge of the table and lightly tapped it with my fingertips.
—What was she really trying to do…?
“If that thing can take human form… do you think it attacks other people?” Mari-senpai asked while gently rubbing Takamura-san’s shoulder beside her.
Takamura-san kept her head lowered.
Right before her eyes, she had lost Akane-san.
And then something wearing Akane-san’s face had returned, begging to be let inside—yet she hadn’t been able to open the window for her.
What must Takamura-san be feeling right now?
Just imagining it made my own chest feel heavy.
I lightly clenched my fist against my palm.
—If something wearing Akane-san’s shape really attacked us… what would we even do…?

“It’s obvious, ain’t it? It was gonna attack everyone in here and turn us all into its own kind.”
Tsuruoka-san’s low voice echoed through the dining hall, the fist resting on the table trembling slightly.
I felt a tightness deep in my chest and slowly steadied my breathing.
—But is that really true…?
—Maybe it is… but can anyone really say for certain?
Whenever people decided something that absolutely, I always found myself thinking that.
Pressing my palm against the cold surface of the table, I tried to calm myself down.
“A different creature taking human form? Does something like that even exist?… What if she really was just a normal woman?… You people basically left her out there to die…”
The glasses-and-jeans guy sitting at the edge of my table suddenly cut into the conversation.
“No. We actually saw her head get crushed with our own eyes,” Kobayashi Ren-san said as he turned around from where he sat back-to-back with Takamura-san to face the glasses-and-jeans guy.
“What kind of human survives having their head crushed? And even if someone somehow did survive, there’s no way they could come back looking completely normal like that.”
Suzuki Sho-san, seated one chair away from Kobayashi Ren-san, also turned toward the glasses-and-jeans guy and nodded firmly in agreement.
“If it ain’t human, then what’re we supposed to do about it?… Maybe we gotta kill the thing?” the athletic-looking guy muttered while clutching his head and hunching his shoulders from his seat two spaces away from me at the end of the table.
“Kill it? How’re you supposed to kill something that looks human?” Kobayashi Ren-san said with a deep sigh.
Resting my chin on both hands, I narrowed my eyes and stared downward.
“Once that thing turns into a person, does it stay that way? If it can’t change shape anymore… maybe a knife’d work on it?” Yohei leaned forward and lightly tapped the table.
—Weapons? A knife?
Would the electric spears Suzuki Sho-san and I made even be enough to kill it?
Back when it was just that black mass, I thought they might work.
But could I really drive something like that into something wearing a human face?
Would a hundred volts even do anything at all…?

For a while, the conversation died out, and silence settled over the room once again.
“So… if we had left Naoki-kun alone, would he eventually have returned looking like himself again?”
The one who finally broke the silence was Uchida-san, seated at the edge of the neighboring table where the rest of our group was sitting.
“I think… probably,” Takizawa-san replied from the near end of the table, with Takamura-san and Mari-senpai seated between them.
“It would’ve looked like Naoki-kun… but it wouldn’t actually be Naoki-kun anymore. Just that thing.”
Yabe-san spoke quietly from across from Uchida-san.
“So Tanaka-san… and the others too… they all got turned into those things?” Suzuki Sho-san said grimly.
That’s right.
Akane-san hadn’t been the only one attacked by that thing.
Others had been taken too.
If that was true… then maybe the others would also return wearing their original faces.
Even if they came back… what was inside wouldn’t be them anymore.
…None of them are really in this world anymore, are they…?

Another stretch of silence drifted through the dining hall.
“Do you think that thing has intelligence?” Takizawa-san finally asked.
“I think so,” Yabe-san replied. “I mean… it was advanced enough to build a UFO.”
“So then… does that mean it’s disguising itself as humans and trying to turn us into its own kind one by one?” the athletic-looking man beside me asked.
“We can’t rule that possibility out,” Yabe-san said quietly.
“If that thing can take human form…” Glasses-and-Jeans began.
“Then… does that mean one of those creatures might already be hiding among us?” one of the women in her twenties at the table by the windows blurted out in a trembling voice. She was the one who’d been talking earlier about some movie actor she liked.
“We don’t know,” Suzuki Sho-san answered, turning toward her.
“How’re we supposed to tell the difference?” the athletic-looking man asked anxiously. “Between a human… and one of those things…”
He grabbed his head with both hands, his shoulders shaking.
I covered my face and slowly took a deep breath.
—Is there even any way to tell?
“I don’t know,” Kobayashi Ren-san said, shaking his head.
As the silence in the dining hall grew heavier and heavier, my thoughts churned noisily inside my skull.
—What are we supposed to do…?


“Sho… are you really Sho?”
Out of nowhere, Tsuruoka-san lifted his head and spoke from the edge of the neighboring table beside me. Then he stood up and slowly backed away from me.
—What…? What does he mean by that?
My heart lurched violently at the sound of his voice.
A restless murmur rippled through the room.
Everyone looked like they wanted to say something, yet none of them had the courage to speak.
But Tsuruoka-san’s words had already become a spark, slowly setting the fire of fear smoldering deep inside everyone’s chest.
“W-What…? I’m… Sho… I think…” I answered, my voice trembling.
The words caught in my throat, and my eyes drifted uncertainly toward the floor.
Even I couldn’t say with complete confidence anymore that I was really myself.
A dark unease spread quietly through my chest.
—Me… not being me…? That’s ridiculous… isn’t it…?
“Tsuru-san, what the hell are you talking about?” Takizawa-san demanded from across the table.
“You’re not joking… are you?” Morishita Aya-san asked uneasily.
“You were in that drying room with it the whole time, weren’t ya?” Tsuruoka-san said coldly, staring straight at me.
Takizawa-san inhaled sharply and rose to his feet.
Yohei stood up too. Then the athletic-looking man. Then Glasses-and-Jeans.
Without a word, they all moved away from me.
The air tightened painfully around me, and the weight in my chest sank even deeper.
Once one person moved, the others followed as though reacting to a signal.
No one seemed to be acting on logic anymore—they were watching each other’s faces, letting the mood decide for them.
The atmosphere itself was controlling the room.
“What happened in the drying room?” one of the women in her twenties sitting near the windows—the one who liked some movie actor—asked nervously.
“You people were taking pictures of that thing without permission, remember? So we had him keep watch over it. And he stayed there with that thing the whole damn time. And now we know that thing can turn itself into a human.” Tsuruoka-san said.
“Then maybe he’s infected!” the athletic-looking man shouted.
“What do you think, Yabe-san?” Takizawa-san asked.
“Well… he was with it the whole time. So I can’t say the possibility is zero,” Yabe-san replied.
Even Yabe-san—who I’d believed was on my side—was now looking at me with suspicion, as though I might be one of those things.
It felt like a stake had been driven straight through my chest.
“Yabe-san… come on…” Mari-senpai murmured. Usually she called her “Yabecchi,” but not now.
“Well then, Mari… can you say with absolute certainty that Sho’s human?” Tsuruoka-san shot back.
“That’s…”
Mari-senpai couldn’t answer.
“No… I don’t think that makes sense. He helped me build the weapons, remember? If he were one of those creatures, why would he help make something meant to hurt himself?” Suzuki Sho-san said.
Those words struck deep in my chest.
“That’s right… If Saku-chan were one of those things, there’s no way he’d help make weapons to use against himself,” Mari-senpai said quietly but firmly as she turned toward me.
At her words, I felt tears beginning to sting at the corners of my eyes.
But the voices of Suzuki Sho-san and Mari-senpai gradually faded beneath the growing unease spreading through the room.
“I heard he was with that monster the whole time…”
“They said the creature can copy people… maybe it’s disguised as him…”
“Wouldn’t it be safer to deal with him now before something happens?”
Those whispers spread through the dining hall in an instant.
People no longer wanted the truth.
They wanted reassurance.
It felt as though everyone in the room desperately wanted someone to blame—someone they could turn into the villain so they themselves could feel safe.

—It felt just like something I’d been seeing more and more lately, I thought.
The moment someone says,
“If you’re Japanese, you should love your country’s flag,”
the words spread instantly, wearing the face of righteousness.
Shared everywhere. Outrage everywhere.
Hatred toward neighboring countries.
Voices demanding that immigrants be shut out.
Before you realize it, the atmosphere tilts sharply to the right,
and a witch hunt begins—people suspecting each other, picking targets.
Politicians. Corporations. Religious organizations. The media.
Even the yakuza.
All of them facing the same direction,
shouting:
“Compete.”
“Win.”
“Be suspicious.”
And without even noticing it, people fall into step with those voices.
They think they’re thinking for themselves,
when really they’re just marching in rhythm with everyone else.
And in the end,
the very hands that push someone else out to feel safe
slowly begin tightening around their own throats.
What was happening in this dining hall right now
felt like a miniature version of all of that.

Tsuruoka-san’s eyes were ruled by anger and fear, and they never wavered.
“How can you possibly know that for sure!? That thing’s intelligent enough to build a UFO. Maybe it’s just pretending to be human like this…!”
Every word he spoke became part of the rhythm of fear tightening around me.
—Was Tsuruoka-san really doing this because he hated me personally? Because I never joined Banrikyo… because I was late arranging the solenoid valve…?
That thought even crossed my mind.
As if responding to Tsuruoka-san’s voice, more and more eyes slowly turned toward me.
Someone swallowed nervously.
Someone dragged a chair across the floor.
Every tiny sound blended into the rhythm of terror closing in around me.
Once suspicion had taken root, logic could no longer stop it.
In everyone’s eyes there was no longer understanding—only conformity.
Standing with the accusers felt safer.
That unconscious choice was pushing everyone in the same direction.
“Come to think of it, this guy’s barely said a word this whole time,” the man in his sixties muttered.
“Yeah. If he were really human, he’d be defending himself more,” said the father with the child.
The athletic-looking man and Glasses-and-Jeans—who had been arguing with Tsuruoka-san earlier—now spoke toward me as though they’d become his allies in an instant.
“Don’t get close to him. It’s dangerous.”
“Yeah. Better be careful.”
“Somebody do something! Are we really just going to let him stay here!?”
“That’s right…! If we keep him here, who knows what he might do!” the two women in their twenties cried out nervously.
My eyes wandered helplessly from face to face, unable to tell who I could still trust anymore.
The small sense of safety that had existed around the table collapsed in an instant.
“Yohei… I’m Sho. Come on… say something,” I managed to whisper.
But Yohei—who was supposed to be my best friend—said nothing.
He only stared at me silently, fear written all over his face.
Looking closer, I realized his usually cheerful body was trembling uncontrollably.
Something inside my chest cracked.
It felt as though everything I’d believed in—friendship, trust, camaraderie—was crumbling apart inside me.
“Owner, lock him up!” the father with the child shouted, and the other climbers immediately began raising their voices too.
“Yeah! What if he does something while everyone’s asleep!?” the man in his sixties yelled.
Without a word, Tsuruoka-san grabbed a chair and slowly raised it over his head.
Anger and fear flickered together across his face.
The older man who had been sitting alone at the edge of the room suddenly pulled out a climbing knife.
The instant the blade caught the light and flashed, I instinctively took a step backward.
“Do we have a net!? Rope would work too!” the father shouted.
“Owner, ain’t there somewhere we can lock him up?” Tsuruoka-san asked.
“Y-Yeah… there’s a storage room in the kitchen. That should do it… It locks from the outside…” the owner replied.
The same man who had jokingly called me “Bon Curry” before now spoke about locking me away.
My chest tightened painfully.
“Alright then. Let’s corner him in there. Sho… if you resist, you’re gonna get hurt,” Tsuruoka-san warned.
—If I were really one of those things, wouldn’t I have transformed already?
That was all I could think.
“Hurry up! Don’t take your eyes off him!” the athletic-looking man shouted.
“Don’t get close—he moved!” Glasses-and-Jeans yelled.
Both of them were supposed to hate Tsuruoka-san.
But now all of that anger had been redirected toward me instead.
—What are these people going to do to me…?
I could feel my back trembling.
I didn’t want to exist in this place anymore.
And yet I was still here.
I wanted this to be a nightmare so badly, but it was happening right in front of me.
My chest hurt.
I could barely breathe.

“Everyone, calm down!” Suzuki Sho-san shouted, stepping protectively in front of me.
“Please… everyone, think about this calmly!” Mari-senpai pleaded as well, spreading her arms as if trying to hold the panic back.
But the hysteria only continued to swell.
The athletic-built man and the guy in glasses and jeans near the entrance suddenly rushed out of the dining hall.
There were only two of them, but for one brief moment, the fact that fewer people remained in the room made me feel just a little safer.
“Get the chairs ready!”
At Tsuruoka-san’s shout, chairs were raised overhead one after another.
Even the father with the child.
Even Kobayashi Ren-san…
The man in his sixties advanced toward me with a mountaineering knife held out in front of him.
Somewhere outside, something clattered noisily.
I turned toward the dining hall entrance—and saw the guy in glasses and jeans come back in, holding one of the spears Suzuki Sho-san and I had made out of electrical conduit pipe.
A moment later, the generator roared to life with its rattling tat-tat-tat-tat-tat.
Then the athletic-built man returned as well, gripping the other spear as he came in behind him.
The small sense of relief I had felt vanished instantly.
I froze.
The electric spears we had built with our own hands were now being aimed at me.
The irony stabbed deep into my chest.
Suzuki Sho-san desperately stood in front of me, trying to shield me, but no matter how hard he argued, nobody would listen. Even his voice was shaking now.
“Don’t move! Stay back!” the athletic-built man yelled, leveling the electric spear at me.
“Owner, show us where the storage room is,” Tsuruoka-san said.
“Y-Yeah… this way.”
The owner walked past the athletic-built man and the guy in glasses and jeans, both holding the electric spears at the ready, and stepped out into the corridor through the dining hall entrance.
“Move. Follow the owner quietly!”
Tsuruoka-san raised the chair threateningly and drove me toward the hallway.
“Whether you’re one of those things or not… it might be safer for everyone if you stay alone,” Yabe-san said to me quietly.
—What was that supposed to mean?
Was she trying to comfort me… just in case I really was human?
“Leave this to me.”
The man in his sixties approached with the knife still raised.
The same man who had warned us to be careful back when we were digging up the thing.
—Are these people idiots?
If I really were one of those things, I would’ve already revealed myself by now.
That was what I thought, but even so, I walked along the side of the tables toward the corridor.
I could feel my legs trembling violently beneath me.
As I approached, the athletic-built man and the guy in glasses and jeans backed away with the electric spears leveled at me, retreating out into the hallway through the dining hall entrance.
—If that spear touches me, I’ll get electrocuted.
Because of my job, I’d been shocked by two hundred volts more than once before.
I knew a brief shock wouldn’t necessarily kill you.
But that didn’t mean it was safe.
Even a hundred volts can stop a heart if the current keeps flowing longer than a heartbeat or two…
At least, that’s what I’d heard.

I stepped out into the hallway.
The man in his sixties with the knife followed me out and moved to the owner’s side near the kitchen end of the corridor.
The owner, who had been watching me, finally turned his gaze forward and started walking.
At the very end of the hallway stood the gray-painted metal door of the power room—the same room Suzuki Sho-san and I had entered earlier to build the electric spears now being aimed at me by the athletic-built man and the guy in glasses and jeans behind me.
Driven forward by the two men with the spears, I walked after the owner toward the power room.
On my left, the windows set into the dining hall wall slid past one after another behind me.
On my right, a woodgrain-patterned door with a small sign reading “STAFF ROOM” and “STAFF ONLY.” in both English and Japanese passed by.
The dining hall entrance on the kitchen side disappeared behind us on the left, and when we reached a windowless wooden sliding door, the owner opened it and stepped inside.
The man in his sixties holding the knife followed after him.
Then me as well… driven onward by the electric spears held by the athletic-built man and the guy in glasses and jeans.
The moment I stepped inside, the kitchen air hit me—a mixture of damp oil smells and the cold gleam of metal.
Rows of stainless-steel worktables filled the room, cluttered with pots, bowls, and kitchen knives.
Overhead hung suspended shelves packed tightly with pans and cookware, close enough that raising a hand carelessly might knock into them.
The floor was slick with traces of water and oil, making my footing feel uncertain.

“Stay quiet… and get in there…”
The low voice of the man with the knife echoed through the kitchen.
Behind me stood the athletic-built man and the guy in glasses and jeans with the electric spears still raised.
To the right stood a stainless-steel dish cabinet, and beyond it, recessed slightly into the wall, was a wooden door.
At the edge of the door hung a padlock attached to a metal latch painted an emerald-green color.
That seemed to be the storage room.
The owner approached the door and inserted a key into the padlock.
The thought of being locked in there made my chest feel like it was about to split apart.
The athletic-built man and the guy in glasses and jeans carefully held position behind me so I couldn’t escape.
“Get in there!” the man in his sixties shouted.
“Sho-kun… I’m sorry. Everyone’s scared… so just for a little while… stay in here, alright?”
The owner’s voice trembled.
When our eyes met, his brows sank apologetically.
After looking at me for a moment, he reached a hand inside the doorway and pressed a switch with his fingertips.
A dry click echoed softly.
The filament of a bare lightbulb glowed dull red for an instant, then a dim orange light slowly spread through the storage room.
