Old Building

My heart pounded wildly with terror. My whole body trembled, and I could feel tears welling in my eyes.
The moment I finished barricading the doors, all the strength nearly drained from my body—but I couldn't stop moving.
The impacts echoing through the doors reminded me that I was still being hunted.
Right beside me was the entrance to the communal sleeping room.
The tiered sleeping platforms faded into the darkness, their shadowy depths resembling the gaping throat of some enormous creature.
If I was going to hide anywhere, it had to be there.
But the Old Wing was dark.
The lights hadn't been turned on.
From my side, I could make out the outline of the doors by the faint glow spilling in from the New Wing. But from the other side, the interior of the Old Wing should be almost impossible to see.
—If I stayed in the darkness, maybe I could avoid being noticed.
Clinging to that tiny hope, I slipped quietly into the passageway of the sleeping room and crouched down where I was.
My back pressed against the wooden wall, and the cold seeped through my shirt straight into my bones.
I forced myself to suppress the frantic pounding that felt ready to burst through my lungs.
Beyond the doors came dull sounds of something moving around, followed by violent blows striking near the metal brackets.
Each groan of the wooden beam and each screech of metal left my palms slick with sweat.
Please... don't come in here...
But if I moved before they broke through the doors, the noise might give me away.
Jiriririri... The fire alarm continued to ring through the storm, mingling with the sound of rain and wind.
Then, in the middle of that agonizing silence, the impacts beyond the doors suddenly stopped.
—I held my breath.
Beyond a single wall, it was still there.
A low, wet sound drifted through the darkness—a sound like breathing, like flesh rubbing against flesh, rumbling softly beyond the barrier.
Long moments passed.
In reality, perhaps not even ten seconds had gone by.
But after a silence that felt eternal—
"...Grrr..."
The impacts beyond the doors gradually faded, replaced by the dull scraping sound of something dragging flesh across the floor. Little by little, the heavy presence moved farther and farther away from the entrance.
Holding my breath, I remained perfectly still in the darkness, my shoulders trembling as I listened.
My heartbeat sounded so loud that I was afraid it would drown out any sign of movement. My legs were shaking.
I clenched my teeth and fought against the urge to collapse onto the floor.
...Had it finally gone?
Waiting for my eyes to adjust to the darkness, I hesitated again and again before finally summoning what little courage I had left and looking toward the entrance of the Old Wing.
Light still shone from the New Wing beyond the lattice windows of the double doors, making it surprisingly easy to see from where I was.
The creature from before was nowhere in sight.
Jiriririri... The fire alarm continued to ring relentlessly.
Careful not to make a sound, I crept forward, focusing every bit of attention on the soles of my feet.
Through the lattice window of the double doors, I peered up the sloping floor toward the corridor beyond.
Past the lounge rooms lay the intersection of hallways—
Beyond that stretched the passage leading toward the dining hall and staff room, where shattered glass and fragments of walls lay scattered from the Thing's rampage—
And at the very end stood the cold, expressionless gray metal wall.
The corridor seemed to narrow gradually in the distance.
My gaze was drawn deeper and deeper into it, yet there was no sign of the Thing anywhere.
"...Is it... gone?"
My throat was so dry that only a breath escaped instead of words.
For the first time in what felt like forever, a small weight lifted from my chest.
But then—another fear settled heavily over me.
Jiriri... The fire alarm stopped.
The only way to silence it would have been to reset the alarm button that had been triggered at the fire hydrant station and then restore the system from the fire control panel—or something along those lines.
And both of those were located on the first floor of the New Wing, exactly where the Things had been roaming moments ago.
So they really do have intelligence...
They know what to do. They understand how things work.
The realization sent a fresh wave of terror surging through me.
There was no telling what kind of traps they might set, how they might corner me, or how they ultimately intended to turn me into one of them.
What exactly are they?
How much do they understand?
How far does their knowledge go?
What are these aliens planning?
Do they even have a weakness?
...There was no way I could know.
And that uncertainty was terrifying.
These were beings capable of building spacecraft that could cross the vast distances between stars—technology beyond anything humanity had ever achieved.
No scientist on Earth could hope to match them.
And someone like me certainly couldn't outthink them.
I was trapped on a mountain nearly three thousand meters above sea level.
A typhoon raged outside, making escape impossible.
Neither the radio nor my cell phone could get a signal.
And to make matters worse, I was wearing nothing but a T-shirt and shorts—and I wasn't even wearing shoes.
What is going to happen to me?
What am I supposed to do now?
Terrified though I was, I forced myself to think of a way to survive.
The Old Wing was dark.
And if I couldn't see, then I wouldn't know if a Thing was hiding nearby.
Even if I had successfully barricaded the doors, if one of them had already gotten inside the Old Wing... I might never realize it until it was too late.
The darkness felt like both a shield and a knife pressed against my back.
If I turned on the lights, I might reveal my position.
But if I turned on the lights, then at least I would know whether there was a Thing lurking nearby.
And if I could see my surroundings, my chances of escaping would be much better.
Safety lay not in darkness, but in visibility.
There was no time to hesitate.
It was dark—but if it stayed dark, I wouldn't even know where to run.
I traced my hand along the wall until my fingertips found a row of four switches, and the moment I felt them, I pressed them without hesitation.
Click.
The dim glow of bare lightbulbs flickered to life all at once, illuminating the aged hallway and its wooden support beams.
The damp air carried the mixed smells of dust and old kerosene, stinging my nose.
Sleeping quarters lined both sides of the corridor.
Tatami-covered sleeping platforms stretched into the rooms, alongside old bunk-bed-like structures stacked three levels high and reached by ladders.
At the far end was a crossroads.
But directly ahead, the passage had been blocked off by several large sheets of plywood leaning against one another.
The plywood barrier was wrapped over and over with black-and-yellow warning rope.
At its center hung a sheet of paper marked with red letters.
KEEP OUT
