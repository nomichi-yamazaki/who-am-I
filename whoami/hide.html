Ren-san's body began to change.
Then another figure dropped from the beams above.
The beige-all-over man in his sixties.
The one who had threatened me with a knife.
They attacked everyone.
I ran.
I crawled back into the crawl space.
And hid.
Holding my breath.
And now...
The man in his sixties was slowly descending the stairs.
With the glasses-and-jeans guy and one of the women in her twenties fused to him.
Moving without making a sound, I simply prayed.
I tried to recall the names of those who had been taken.
Was Suzuki Shou-san okay?
What about Uchida-san?
And Morishita Aya-san?
Did they manage to get away?
The guest register... Shou-san must still have had it.
If it was still there, it could be the only clue left for telling who was still human.
I listened carefully.
Outside the walls, the rain and wind battered the building.
And beneath it all—nothing else.
No other sound.
Alright... maybe I should go back and check where it was dropped.
That was what I thought.
I moved forward, lying flat against the beam.
The space above and below me was barely enough to crawl through, with the ceiling boards pressing close above my head.
In the darkness, light spilled in from the opening Suzuki Shou-san had made to the upper left.
The further I went, the larger that light became, revealing floating dust particles drifting through it like white ash.
Farther ahead, above the dining hall and other open gaps, faint light seeped in as well.
It cast long shadows from pillars and thin metal rods holding up the ceiling panels.
Beyond the far-right wall was the outside.
Wind and rain struck from that direction, howling like some living beast.
Metal groaned.
Beams creaked under strain.
The smell in the air was damp wood, smoke, oil, and blood mixed together into something foul.
And yet, up here in the crawl space, it was slightly warmer than below.
Dust scraped against my palms and knees as I moved.
I crawled forward little by little over beams buried in years of accumulated dirt.
Every breath made my throat feel rough.
In the lit areas, dust swirled thickly, clinging to the air.
The shadows of the beams crossed and overlapped in strange patterns.
And every time some metal fixture shifted and let out a faint clink, my entire body tensed up.
I held my breath.
And made my way back to where it had all happened.
I reached the place where Suzuki Shou-san had removed the vertical panel separating the sleeping platform from the lower walkway—the long rectangular opening.
I stopped there.
Holding my breath.
Listening.
The wind rattled the walls.
The boards trembled.
Rainwater dripped somewhere from the gaps in the roof.
But beyond that—nothing.
No movement.
No groaning.
No wet, writhing sounds.
Only the distant storm beating against the mountain.
