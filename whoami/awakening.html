覚醒

I was wrapped up in the futon in my own room, in my own home.
Outside my window, the night lay silent. The lights of the town shimmered softly in the darkness.
After I lost consciousness in the crevasse, I was found by another group of climbers. The staff at the Hakuba-jiri mountain lodge contacted the rescue team, and they brought me to safety.
The next time I opened my eyes, I was lying in a hospital bed.
My father and mother were there to visit me...
When I was discharged, they took the long train ride home with me.
The moment I saw my house, a wave of relief washed over me.
I was alive. I had escaped that mountain lodge with no way out and made it back home.
But that relief was soon swept away by the ache stirring deep inside my chest.
Mari-senpai... Yabe-san... Yohei... Takizawa-san... Suzuki Sho-san... They were all gone now.
The lodge owner... Uchida-san... Takamura-san... and everyone else whose lives had become entwined with that nightmare drifted through my mind.
Their smiling faces... their faces twisted in terror... the desperate way they fought to protect one another...
Every memory tightened its grip around my heart until even breathing became painful.
Beneath the covers, my hands were clenched so tightly that my fingers began to ache.
The moment I closed my eyes, the lodge's corridors, the shadows stretched across the floor, the feel of those tentacles, and the echoes of terrified screams all came rushing back.
The line between dreams and reality still wavered, pulling my thoughts into a whirlpool of confusion.
Even so, the softness of my futon, the quiet of my room, and the simple fact that I was finally safe brought me a small measure of comfort.
I took a slow, deep breath... and let it out.
Closing my eyes, I curled up beneath the covers and sank into their warmth.
Gradually, the images and voices that had been swirling through my mind drifted farther and farther away, until only the quiet rhythm of my breathing remained.
The pain in my chest never completely faded, but drowsiness slowly weighed down my eyelids.
Feeling the warmth of my own body beneath the covers... I had no choice but to surrender to sleep.
Mari-senpai was smiling.
So were Yabe-san, Yohei, Takizawa-san, and Tsuruoka-san.
Their smiles were gentle... warm... as though time itself had come to a standstill.
And yet, something about the scene felt wrong.
I couldn't see it.
But I could feel something slowly drawing closer.
One of those things...
It had no clear shape, no shadow I could make out. Yet somehow, I could sense it creeping toward me—from beneath my feet, from beyond the walls—slowly... relentlessly.
I wanted to run.
I wanted to get away.
But my body felt as heavy as lead, refusing to move.
I have to protect everyone...!
Only my desperate cry echoed inside my mind. My body remained frozen, while fear tightened its grip around my heart.
Then, from somewhere deep within the silence, I heard a faint whisper...
"You too... You too..."
The voice belonged to no one.
And yet... somehow, I knew it was calling my name.
My chest tightened. My breathing grew uneven. Sweat trickled down my forehead, my palms turned cold and damp, and my whole body began to tremble.
Then... I woke up.
Birds were singing.
Soft morning light streamed into the room.
I was in my own bedroom.
But the sense of relief vanished in an instant.
In my dream... everyone had been alive.
In reality... they were all gone.
My chest tightened.
I couldn't catch my breath.
I made my way toward the bathroom, my legs threatening to give out beneath me.
Something tickled deep inside my nose.
When I looked into the mirror, it almost seemed as if my nose hair was twitching... twitch... twitch...
...What?
A chill ran through me as I leaned closer for a better look.
Something black, flat, and slender slowly emerged from one of my nostrils, swaying back and forth as though searching for something.
My heart lurched inside my chest.
A flood of memories crashed through my mind.
The lodge corridor... Yohei collapsing... Mari-senpai's cries...
Countless tentacles engulfing Takizawa-san.
The pale yellow fluid streaming from Tsuruoka-san's eyes.
Mari-senpai's eyes... They looked as though she believed in me until the very end.
But the shadow spreading at my feet told a different story.
No one could be saved.
"No... It wasn't a dream...!"
I squeezed my eyes shut and braced myself against the sink, barely daring to breathe.
But my mind only spiraled deeper into chaos.
Dreams and reality bled into one another until I could no longer tell where one ended and the other began.
I looked at my reflection.
Out of the corner of my eye, something black writhed.
Everything around me felt as though it might crumble at the slightest touch.
Every breath stabbed at my chest.
Even with my eyes closed, the pounding of my heart echoed in my ears like relentless tinnitus.
My sense of self began to dissolve.
The world around me twisted out of shape.
"You too... You too..."
The whisper came again.
It belonged to no one... and to everyone.
Fear.
Loneliness.
Despair.
They surged through every part of my body.
The instant I looked into the mirror again, something moved—without question—at the edge of my vision.
I had no idea what it was.
The only thing I knew... was that I was no longer the person I used to be.
My breathing grew ragged.
My throat tightened.
My whole body trembled.
In the mirror before me, countless shadows stared back at me.
I wanted to scream... but no sound would come.
All that remained was the pounding of my heart... and the whisper echoing deep inside my ears.
Then I felt something deep in my throat.
As though something were forcing its way up from within me.
A violent wave of nausea struck, and I lurched over the sink.
An instant later, countless black, glistening tendrils spilled from my mouth, spreading across the basin until they covered it completely.
I jerked my head up and looked into the mirror.
The black mass spread across its surface in an instant.
Then... nothing remained.
"Somebody... please tell me..."
"What am I supposed to do...?"

Slowly, I sat up from the futon in my room.
At first glance, the world seemed unchanged.
Morning light filtered through the gap in the curtains, and the distant songs of birds drifted through the air.
Yet somehow, amid that quiet, peaceful scene, I felt as though I alone had been left behind.
I stared at my reflection in the mirror.
The black waves... the slick, writhing shadow... the Nanika that had once filled me with terror had now become a part of me.
I had thought about it countless times.
If this was the way I had to live... perhaps it would be better to end it all.
But I couldn't.
Even when I held my breath... even when I gripped a blade... my body refused.
Death itself seemed to turn away from me.
And then...
a quiet laugh escaped my lips.
"...I see."
"I don't have a choice."
"I have to keep living."

No matter what I had become,
life would go on.
I would eat.
I would sleep.
And morning would come again.
At last, I began to understand...
that as long as I was still human,
that was my fate.

One day, I was walking through the city.
The warning bell at a railroad crossing sounded strangely far away.
A sharp electronic chime.
The crossing gates began to descend.
Beyond them, a small child stumbled while chasing after a ball.
"Watch out!"
My body moved before my voice ever could.
I wasn't thinking.
Not about what was right.
Not about fear.
Not about consequences.
There was only a life that had to be saved.
The next instant,
the world exploded into white.
Impact.
Noise.
The sky flipped upside down.
After the sensation of being slammed into the ground,
an eerie silence settled over everything.
...Something was wrong.
I could breathe.
I was fully conscious.
And yet...
my body wouldn't move.
At the edge of my vision,
something was impossibly far away.
My own legs.
It took a few moments before I understood.
I had been...
severed.
For anyone else,
this would have been the end.
The pain should have dragged me into darkness.
But I was still alive.
Fear clenched around my heart.
Nausea surged up inside me.
"...So it's true..."
"I really have become..."
Nanika.
No longer human.
The moment that thought crossed my mind,
countless slender tendrils began to extend
from the cleanly severed edges of my body,
moving as though they possessed a will of their own.
Countless threads.
Pulsing.
Searching for one another.
Drawing themselves together.
I didn't even have time to resist.
The tendrils intertwined,
weaving themselves together like living stitches,
until...
my body became whole once more.
It moved.
My fingers.
My legs.
They were unmistakably mine again.
People around me gasped in horror.
Someone was holding the child in their arms.
Safe.
Still alive,
crying uncontrollably,
but completely unharmed.
The moment I saw that,
something inside me quietly gave way.
Disgusting.
Terrifying.
You're not human.
All of that was true.
And yet...
with this power...
I had saved a life.
A train had torn my body apart.
Anyone else would have died without question.
Yet I was still here.
And because I was,
someone else's life...
someone else's future...
would continue.
As I let out a trembling breath,
I looked up at the sky.
The fear hadn't disappeared.
Neither had the disgust,
or the doubt.
They were all still there.
Even so...
"...I can use this."
The words never left my lips.
But I knew they were there.
This body.
This power.
Even this existence...
this thing that had become Nanika.
If it could be used to help people...
If I could stand
not on the side that spread fear,
but on the side that protected others from it...
Then perhaps...
this wasn't a curse.
For the first time,
I was able to accept—if only a little—
what I had become.

I picked up the remote and switched on the television almost absentmindedly.
The news was showing yet another war in some distant part of the world.
In a calm, measured voice,
the anchor described the situation
as casually as if delivering the weather forecast.
Explosions.
Screams.
Flames.
Human beings, driven by fear, were tearing one another apart.
And yet...
I couldn't believe what was unfolding on the screen had begun all at once.
It felt like the inevitable result
of something that had been building for a very long time.
Then, my grandfather's words came back to me.
"You know... that's what war really is."
"It begins with a fight started by children,
and because no one steps in to stop it,
people who had nothing to do with it end up getting hurt."
"War is nothing more than bullying the weak."
When I was a child,
I knew what it felt like to be bullied.
That was why...
I could never accept something like that.
Competition.
The word drifted into my mind.
Competition for wealth.
Competition between nations.
Competition over who was right.
Competition that should have existed to create what people needed and share it with one another
had somehow become a struggle to take from one another instead.
The television never explained why.
It simply amplified fear,
quietly...
relentlessly.
Watching it,
I couldn't help seeing echoes
of the way Nanika had behaved in the mountains.
If nothing changes...
the same thing will happen again.
As I stared at the images on the screen,
I remembered.
Nanika began to mutate in response to fear.
If fear gave birth to conflict...
And if bearing that fear myself could protect someone else...
"...Then maybe...
there's something I can do."
If I could become anything...
perhaps I could even stop a war.
I wasn't going to be
the person who did nothing but live in fear anymore.
"...I have to keep living."

I remembered what had happened at that mountain lodge ten years ago.
When I reunited with Yohei in the old wing, he had said:
"But... have you already become one of those things?..."
"That's incredible..."
"I didn't know we could do something like this..."
As he spoke, both of his eyes slid toward the center of his face until he looked like a one-eyed goblin from a folktale.
Right...
I could mutate, too.
The thought crossed my mind.
Standing in front of the mirror in my room,
I stared intently at my hand.
A little fear still lingered inside me,
but I couldn't resist finding out what this body could do.
"Alright..."
"Let's give it a try."
I clenched my fist tightly.
"Observe carefully, ladies and gentlemen..."
"A dove is about to appear!"
A light, floating sensation ran through my fingertips.
And then, unbelievably,
my hand actually turned into a tiny dove.
Its wings fluttered softly.
When it pecked gently at my other hand,
I couldn't help laughing.
"Whoa... that's amazing!"
When I changed the dove back into my hand,
the feathers vanished instantly,
and everything returned to normal.
Next, I stared at images of presidents and world leaders on television,
then focused my thoughts on the mirror.
"I wonder if I can become that guy..."
In an instant,
my reflection transformed into the president.
Excitement bubbled up from deep inside my chest.
"This is ridiculously fun...!"
After that,
I spent hours trying all kinds of different forms.
A cat.
A dog.
And at one point,
I even split my face into two separate faces just to see if I could.

I kept in touch with the others who had become Nanika,
and together with the people from Hakuba Grand Lodge,
we transformed ourselves and appeared wherever conflict erupted around the world.
"My Lord... why do You allow such cruelty to exist?"
"Can this truly be what You desire?"
At first, Tsuruoka-san had shown little enthusiasm for joining us.
But after witnessing the devastation of the battlefield with his own eyes,
those were the words that escaped his lips.
Tsuruoka-san had once been so devoted to Banrikyo that he joked his paycheck would grow once war broke out and the company profited from it.
Yet, despite everything,
it seemed he still possessed a heart capable of feeling for others.
"That's not what God wants," I said.
"It's simply people who can't understand another person's pain,
using God's name to satisfy their own selfish desires."
If God truly existed,
I couldn't believe such a being would favor one person over another.
A God who was truly just could never willingly permit something like this.
Or at least...
that was what I believed.
Because if God did exist
and truly wished for horrors like these,
then God, too,
would be a being ruled by selfishness and pride.
Even human beings are capable of holding back their selfishness and pride.
That's how ordinary people manage to live alongside their neighbors every day.
If there were something even a human being could do,
yet God could not,
then I saw no reason to worship such a god.
Perhaps the horrors of the battlefield had awakened that same fear inside Tsuruoka-san.
As always,
fear was the trigger.
The whites of his eyes rolled back.
His tongue stretched unnaturally long.
Countless tendrils burst from his back,
spreading outward in every direction.
His transformation made him look like a blazing sun.
The tendrils lashed outward,
searching desperately for prey.
Unfortunately...
there was nothing here to hunt.
Because every one of us...
had already become Nanika.
As I took the form of government officials in an attempt to prevent wars,
I began to glimpse the machinery that kept them turning.
If the threat posed by neighboring nations could be amplified,
governments gained a reason to expand their militaries.
The taxes collected from their citizens paid for weapons built by the defense industry,
and in return, money—and political support—flowed back.
The neighboring countries were caught in much the same cycle.
As long as public frustration could be directed toward an external enemy,
people were less likely to focus on the problems within their own borders.
Stoking a sense of crisis
seemed to serve both sides equally well.
A win-win arrangement.
So even at the negotiating table,
while everyone spoke eloquently about peace,
I couldn't shake the feeling that no one truly intended to bring the wars to an end.
Because if they did...
someone would stop profiting.

The real question was...
who was pulling the trigger.
Wars weren't started by presidents,
or corporations.
They were set in motion by ordinary people,
on both sides,
after fear had taken hold of them.
They were told, again and again,
that neighboring countries were dangerous.
They were encouraged to see enemies where there had once been neighbors.
And before long,
voices demanding, "Destroy them!"
swelled like a modern-day witch hunt.
The media amplified that fear.
Advertising money from corporations.
Its relationship with the government.
No media organization was ever completely free from those influences.
But that didn't mean every journalist was the same.
Even within public broadcasters and commercial networks,
there were people with the courage to stand by their convictions.
People who struggled to reveal truths that were inconvenient for governments or corporations.
If they couldn't report them openly,
they would weave them into a drama.
If prime time was impossible,
they would hide them in programs that aired long after midnight,
when hardly anyone was watching.
They understood
just how small their voices were.
Even so,
they refused to remain silent.
That was their way of resisting.
And it wasn't only the traditional media.
The new media of the Internet was no different.
In fact...
the way fear was ignited,
the way hatred spread,
the way outrage snowballed until it threatened to ignite war...
looked disturbingly similar
to the way frightened Nanika continued to mutate,
multiplying themselves,
and attacking people indiscriminately.
It seemed to me that governments scattered the first sparks because war served their interests.
The media and online voices fanned those sparks into flames.
And in the end,
the ones who truly set the fire ablaze were ordinary people on both sides,
frightened into panic by the fear that had been planted inside them.
The government struck the match.
The media and the Internet fed the flames.
And the people,
terrified,
confused,
and desperate,
danced to the fire they believed they were trying to escape.
It was no different in Japan.
"We must protect the lives and property of our people."
"Increase defense spending."
"Reconsider the Three Non-Nuclear Principles."
"Lift restrictions on arms exports."
"Strengthen our offensive capabilities."
Each phrase sounded noble.
Complicated.
Convincing.
And yet...
I couldn't shake the feeling
that somewhere behind those words,
the Prime Minister was quietly smiling to himself,
watching the seeds of fear he had planted begin to bear fruit.
The people...
seemed to be nothing more than fertilizer for that harvest.
As though they were livestock raised for slaughter.
Scatter the feed...
wait for them to gather...
and then lead them away.
Most people never even realized it.
Apparently,
politicians didn't have to become Nanika...
to speak with a forked tongue.

I remembered something my grandfather once told me.
"War, you see, can also be started simply to create demand.
When things stop selling, they're destroyed.
And to destroy them... people are killed."
"Even if a war breaks out somewhere far away,
and Japan isn't directly involved,
there are still people who profit from making weapons.
That's the kind of world we live in."
"International affairs, you know,
can sometimes feel like a carefully scripted match—
like professional wrestling—
where someone is always meant to come out ahead."
"If our birthrate is already falling,
what happens if young people start throwing their lives away?
All that does... is leave fewer people behind to cherish."

“Tell me—
which do you think truly cares about this country?
The ones who sacrifice their fellow citizens
to protect their own gain or pride
or the ones who try to get along with other countries,
and live by helping one another here at home?”

“You see, in competition…
people trip each other up,
push one another down,
and hurt each other—
and yet, they call that ‘bonds’… or ‘unity.’”
“…That’s something
I’ve never quite been able to understand.”
“I think… real bonds
are something you can stand in together,
without having to trample anyone else.”

“Humans aren’t creatures that destroy themselves
by fighting over resources.
We’re beings who can think—
who can hold back our selfish impulses and pride,
and choose to live together.”
“Sho, remember this.
Before being Japanese…
what matters is whether you can remain human.”

後年、歴史家たちはこの会談をこう記すことになるのかもしれない。

そうして実際に国と国の間に入り込んでみて、ようやく分かった。

競争の延長線上に、必然として置かれている。
生活に必要なものが、まだ十分に行き渡らない時代。
その頃の金儲けの競争には意味があった。
作れば売れる。
働けば生活は少しずつ良くなる。
だが――
生活に必要なものが、すでに十分に行き渡り、余るようになると話は変わる。
余ったものは売れない。
売れないものは価値を失う。
価値を失えば、金は回らない。
そこで必要になるのが、破壊だった。
そしてその破壊の手段として使われるのが”戦争”
かつておじいちゃんがそんな話をしてくれた時僕はこんな事を思ったのを覚えている。
戦争は、破壊だ。
発展じゃない。
発展は、前に進むことだ。
壊すことは、後ろに下がることだ。

そんな理屈が、本当に正しいのだろうか。
足りているなら、
みんなで分け合って使えばいい。
それだけの話じゃないか。

パレスチナの少年（完全版）
世界は平和になった。
僕たちナニカは自分がいろいろな姿に変身できる能力を使って各国首脳の姿になり世界を平和に導いたのだ。
僕たちが世界を平和に導いた時のことは、またいつか話すとして――
ひとつ思い出すのは、パレスチナで出会ったあの少年のことだ。
あの土地の空気は、乾いていて、焦げたような匂いを帯びていた。
昼は砂の光が目を焼き、夜は風が鉄の味を運んできた。
瓦礫の街を抜けるたび、崩れた壁の隙間から子どもたちの笑い声が聞こえた。
けれどその笑いは、どこか壊れた音色をしていた。
僕はその日、薄暗い市場を歩いていた。
物資は乏しく、露店のテーブルにはひび割れたパンや、埃をかぶった缶詰が並んでいる。
香辛料の匂いの中で、遠くから銃声のような音が響いた。
人々は一瞬だけ目を上げたが、すぐに日常へ戻っていった。
その時、軽い衝撃が腰に走った。
振り向くと、小さな影が人波の中に消えていく。
僕の財布がなかった。
気づいた瞬間、体が勝手に動いた。
人間の走る速さではなかった。
“ナニカ”が筋肉の奥で蠢き、視界の輪郭が流れる。
数秒後、路地の奥で少年を見つけた。
彼は壁に背を預け、ポケットの中の財布を睨んでいた。
年は十にも満たない。
髪は砂にまみれ、頬には古い傷跡。
眼だけが異様に澄んでいて、まっすぐ僕を見返した。
逃げるでもなく、謝るでもなく、
ただ「どうする？」とでも言いたげに。
僕は何も言わなかった。
代わりにポケットから小さな包みを取り出した。
マクビティ・ダイジェスティブ・ビスケット。
あの味だけは、なぜか手放せなかった。
包みを差し出すと、少年はためらいながら近づき、
奪うようにして一枚を取った。
そして、ぽきりと音を立てて――
半分を僕に差し出した。
その小さな手の震えが、今でも忘れられない。
人は飢えても、分け与えることを忘れない。
そのことを思い知らされた瞬間だった。
それから、僕たちは何度も会った。
彼は僕を見ると、少しはにかんだ顔をして手を振った。
僕はいつも食べ物を買い与えた。
彼は受け取るたびに必ず半分を僕に返した。
まるでそれが、彼なりの約束のようだった。
ある夕暮れ、僕たちは崩れた建物の影で座っていた。
少年はタバコを指で回しながら言った。
「これ、かっこいいんだろ？」
僕は笑って首を振った。
「それは大人のふりをする煙だよ」
彼は肩をすくめた。
「大人になれない子どもより、ましさ」
僕はその言葉に何も返せなかった。
戦争の中では、子どもは子どもでいることを許されない。
結局、僕はポケットマネーでタバコを買ってやった。
それが善か悪か、今でも分からない。
数日後、夜の路地で彼の腰に光るものを見た。
ナイフ。
錆びついていたが、刃は鋭かった。
僕は声をかけようとした。
「それで何を守るつもりだ？」
だが、彼は笑って答えた。
「自分の分を、取られる前に」
その夜、僕は長い間眠れなかった。
人を守るための刃は、いつか人を傷つける。
けれどその事実を、僕も少年も、もう知ってしまっていた。
そして翌日。
僕は彼を探した。
市場にも、廃墟にもいなかった。
誰に聞いても、知らないと言う。
夕暮れが赤から紫へ変わる頃、
狭い路地裏で、彼を見つけた。
倒れていた。
腹に刃が突き立っていた。
僕は膝をついた。
血はすでに冷たく、空気が重く沈んでいた。
彼の瞳がわずかに動いた。
僕を見た。
微笑んだ。
そして口の端で、何かを言おうとした。
けれど声にはならなかった。
その瞬間、僕の中の“ナニカ”が目を覚ました。
身体が震え、皮膚の下で黒い波が走った。
腕が変形し、影のように伸びた。
その触手が少年に触れると、血の流れが逆戻りするように体が動いた。
傷が閉じ、息が戻った。
けれど、それはもう「人の息」ではなかった。
彼は立ち上がった。
僕を見つめたまま、何も言わなかった。
その瞳の奥で、何かが生まれ、そして壊れた。
「これで君は死なない」
僕は呟いた。
「けれど、生きてもいけない」
夜風が吹き、空に砕けたビスケットのような星が散った。
彼はそれを見上げ、
まるで、まだ半分を分けようとするみたいに、
手を伸ばした。
世界が静かに変わりはじめた、あの夜の記憶を、
僕は今でも抱えたまま生きている。
ナニカに変わった僕たちは、
それぞれのやり方で、
人の心を取り戻そうとしていた。
あの少年も、きっとその一人だ。
どこかで、同じ空を見ている。
