In the Storm

After escaping from the underground storage room, I began lifting the rocks scattered nearby and piling them on top of the hatch, one after another, making sure nothing could climb out through the opening.
The wooden hatch would lift slightly every now and then, as though something beneath it was trying to push it open. Each time it did, the damp wood groaned under the strain.
Breathing hard, I added more weight.
My clothes clung heavily to my body, soaked through by the rain. Mud made my hands slippery.
Still, I kept stacking rocks.
Eventually, the wooden hatch stopped moving altogether.
Even the slightest give disappeared.
From directly below came shrill squeals and the sound of something striking the wood.
The noise seemed to stab at my spine more than my ears.
But I didn't look back.
As rain and wind lashed against me, I started moving forward with the building on my left.
The soaked T-shirt plastered to my body flapped and danced wildly in the storm.
Far off in both directions, I could see faint yellow lights.
But where I stood was swallowed completely by darkness, and every light was impossibly distant.
My feet were bare.
Each time a loose stone dug into the sole of my foot, the pain was sharp.
Yet at the same time, it was the only thing reminding me that I was still here.
To my left, through the rain-shrouded darkness, I could make out a long shape covered by a plastic tarp.
The sheet had been weighed down with rocks and trembled violently beneath the rain.
Long.
Layered.
Stacked.
*Probably construction lumber,* I guessed.
The wind sliced past my ears.
It tugged at my T-shirt, as if trying to tear it away.
The rain hammered down from above, pressing against me as though it wanted to crush me into the ground.
Water streamed down both my legs like waterfalls.
*Well... maybe it'll wash away what I did to myself back there.*
The thought came with a bitter trace of self-mockery.
Behind me, faintly, I could still hear something pounding against the hatch beneath the ground.
I found myself thinking:
***For now... let's just make it over there.***
Carefully easing my feet forward to lessen the pain from the uneven stones, I continued on.
Gripping the flapping plastic sheet beside me, little by little.
The wind felt strong enough to rip me away.
Without something to hold onto, I couldn't have moved forward at all.
The sounds from the underground hatch behind me had grown distant.
Or perhaps they had simply become lost beneath the storm.
My mind and body felt as though they had been stretched tight for so long that parts of them had gone numb.
So I walked.
Just forward.
The building remained on my left.
Darkness stretched endlessly to my right.
The distant yellow lights didn't seem any closer than before.
*"If I keep going, I'll reach them eventually."*
There was no evidence to support that belief.
Nothing at all.
And yet my feet continued moving.
Because I felt that if I stopped—
I might simply collapse.
I couldn't even tell whether my ragged breathing came from exhaustion or from the cold.
All I knew was that I kept walking.

The rain hammering against the roof continued without pause, loud enough to split my ears.
Above, the wind howled across the sky like some gigantic beast, its deep, rumbling roar rolling through the darkness.
Batatatatata...
The plastic sheets covering the stacked construction materials beside me snapped violently in the gale. Rain slammed against them, sending sprays of water back into my face.
The rain pounded down as though trying to crush my skull, while the wind twisted at my body with enough force to wrench me off balance.
Hunching over, clinging to the plastic sheeting for support, I forced myself forward one step at a time—literally crawling my way ahead.
Everyone's been taken...
Uchida-san, the doctor...
Morishita Aya-san, who had told us she was studying environmental science...
And Takizawa-san, the man who had brought all of us here...
Takizawa-san's GranAce was probably still sitting in the Sarukura parking lot.
Its owner would never climb into it again.
They'd been right there in front of me.
Maybe I could have saved them—
But I hadn't done anything.
Not one thing.
If Mari-senpai was still alive...
If she was suffering somewhere...
If she was waiting for someone to help her...
Then I—at the very least—I had to save her.
Where should I go...?
Where could I possibly find Mari-senpai?
The first floor was already a nest of monsters.
The old building had been overrun as well.
There wasn't a single safe place left anymore.
The second floor...
The man in his sixties—the one who had fallen from the attic beams, threatened me with a knife, and locked me inside the storage room—had gone downstairs with the glasses-and-jeans guy and one of the women in her twenties—the one who kept talking about that actor—attached to his head.
Now that I thought about it, the people who had become those things seemed to gravitate toward the first floor.
If that was true, then maybe—just maybe—the second floor was still safe.
Maybe Mari-senpai was hiding somewhere up there too.
For now, I should follow that possibility and head to the second floor.
After all, the first floor was crawling with more than twenty monsters already.
That was what I decided.

My entire body was soaked through by the rain.
Water streamed over my skin like a waterfall, soaking not only my clothes but every inch of me, and my body trembled uncontrollably.
It wasn't just fear anymore.
The cold was draining away my body heat.
The soles of my bare feet throbbed from constantly stepping on sharp stones scattered across the ground.
Above the plastic sheeting on my left stood a window boarded shut with wooden planks.
Any light that should have leaked through had long since lost its color, leaving only a faint gray outline.
It belonged to what should have been the empty dining hall.
Inside, Uchida-san and Morishita-san had been taken.
The room had been overflowing with the transformed victims.
Keeping it in the corner of my eye, I forced myself onward.
I was getting close to the covered walkway connecting the old building and the new one.
To my left, the building stepped back, forming an alcove-like space filled with twisted sheets of metal, scrap lumber, broken pottery, and discarded ceramic toilets.
It looked like a dumping ground for things nobody wanted anymore.
Somewhere deep within that darkness, something shifted with a faint creeeak as the wind caught it.
My heart nearly jumped out of my chest.
Then, ahead of me—
A yellow glow spilled faintly into the storm.
The new building.
The nearest room was a storage room, and beyond it lay the Japanese-style recreation room.
Something was moving within the light spilling from the windows.
A silhouette.
But it wasn't shaped like a human being.
The roaring rain and screaming wind swallowed my footsteps and breathing.
That was my only saving grace.
Rain hammered against my face.
Water ran into my mouth, and I spat it out.
It hurt just to keep my eyes open.
Still, I had to keep moving.
Ahead lay the covered passage connecting the hut to the washroom building.
Beyond that was the walkway running between the hut and the washroom block.
Once I reached it, there would be a corrugated roof overhead.
Shelter from the rain.
Wooden slats covered the floor there.
If I could just make it that far...
Maybe the pain in my feet would ease, if only a little.
Off to my left, water dripped from a roof into a bucket sitting beside a pile of ceramic containers.
Clang...
Clang...
Clang...
Clang...
The small metallic sound repeated over and over with eerie regularity.
Breathing hard, I pushed forward through that world of mingling sounds—
the rain, the wind, the distant creaks, the dripping water—
moving only forward.
Forward.

The path ran alongside the new building.
On my left should have been the storage room—the one that had been locked when we'd first arrived, the one I hadn't been able to see inside.
This was where Tanaka-san had been.
Tanaka-san—the woman who had been taken by the black fetus-like creature in the washroom.
Tanaka-san—the woman whose face had melted after she cut away the tendril attached to Naoki-kun and was sprayed with the liquid the black fetus-like creature spat out.
This was also where Tanaka-san—the former high school geography teacher—had been standing at the shop counter.
...I had no idea who might be hiding where, waiting to attack.
I had to be careful.
Stacks of lumber sat under plastic sheets weighed down with stones.
The sheets snapped and flapped violently in the wind.
Water roared from the gutters.
Somewhere below, I could hear the deep rushing sound of water flowing underground.
Ahead and to my left, the yellow-lit windows of the recreation room drew closer.
In the yellow light spilling from the windows to my right, silhouettes swayed and shifted.
What were they doing in there?
I couldn't risk taking a look.
If they spotted me, it would be over.
Keeping low beneath the windows, I crept forward.
It felt as though someone might peer out at any moment and see me.
My heart was pounding so hard I thought it might burst right out of my mouth.
My chest ached, and it felt as though my stomach and back were being pulled toward each other from the inside, as if my upper and lower body might tear apart at the waist.
The stacked lumber ended beside a rotting washbasin that looked ready to collapse at any moment.
Beyond it lay the covered walkway connecting the main building to the washroom wing.
I had made it past the recreation room windows.
So far, so good.
I hadn't been spotted.
Ahead of me, the entrance to the main building stood open.
Open.
If I got inside and moved a little farther, there should be a staircase.
Could I reach the second floor from there?
But inside were the creatures Morishita-san had called the Inhumans.
They looked human.
And they attacked with terrifying speed.
If one of them spotted me, I'd be cornered before I had a chance to escape.
Going inside felt dangerous.
...Was there another way to reach the second floor?
If a fire broke out and that staircase became unusable, there would be no escape route left.
It was hard to imagine a building like this having only one staircase leading upstairs.
There was probably another one farther ahead, somewhere beyond the corner to the left.
If I could reach that staircase, maybe I could get to the second floor without running into any of those things.
The problem was getting there.
To continue on, I would have to pass directly in front of that open doorway.
And passing in front of it meant exposing myself, even if only for a second.
Worse, the entrance to the washroom wing on my right was open as well.
If one of those things was hiding in either doorway and suddenly rushed out at me, it would be over.
I couldn't watch both sides at once.
My heart hammered against my ribs.
Rain pounded down from above.
I wanted desperately to get under a roof.
But no matter what happened...
I could not let myself be seen.
To my right was the corner of the washroom wing.
First, I'll check whether there's anyone inside.
I moved toward it through the driving rain and howling wind.
Reaching the corner, I pressed myself against the wall and listened for any sound from within the washroom wing.
The downspout directly in front of me droned and vibrated as torrents of rainwater rushed through it.
I heard nothing from inside.
Nothing at all.
I had no choice but to go.
I knew it in my head, yet my legs felt like they belonged to someone else.
The moment I stepped forward, I felt certain something would seize me and drag me away.
The thought alone made every pore in my body tighten.
But if I wanted to find Mari-senpai, I had to keep moving.
If I stopped here... then Senpai... Senpai would surely—
"It's okay. There's still time. There has to be."
My lips moved on their own, as if trying to convince myself.
My teeth chattered so hard they barely met, the clicking echoing in my ears louder than the rain.
The first floor had already become a nest of the Inhumans.
The old building had fallen as well.
There was no safe place left anywhere.
The second floor might still be safe.
That possibility alone was keeping me on my feet.
If I rounded the corner ahead, there might be an exterior staircase leading to the second floor.
If I could use that staircase...!
First, I had to get past this covered walkway connecting the washroom wing.
If I couldn't get past it, nothing else mattered.
Summoning every ounce of courage I had left, I carefully took a step forward—

From behind the shadow of the half-collapsed washbasin, I cautiously peeked into the lodge, exposing no more than the tip of my face.
—There they were.
Ayako Uchida was walking toward the intersection from the direction of the lounge.
Uchida Ayako-san, who had been human only moments ago.
Dr. Uchida, who had desperately tried to save Naoki-kun after the tendrils attached themselves to him.
Uchida Ayako-san, who had risked herself trying to protect Morishita Aya-san.
The pastel-green rainwear she wore was smeared with slick, reddish mucus.
Her hair clung to her forehead, plastered there by the slime.
Her pace was slow, yet perfectly regular.
At fixed intervals, the soles of her shoes tapped softly against the floor.
Appearing from the darkness on the dining hall side was Takamura-san.
Former nurse Takamura-san.
Takamura-san, who had lost Akane-san and still tried to help Uchida-san treat Naoki-kun.
Takamura-san, who had said at Hakuba-jiri, “But what we’re really looking forward to is eating the meals we make ourselves up on the mountain, and watching the stars.”
Her head hung low.
The shadows across her face were too deep to make out any expression.
Dried mucus clung to the backs of her hands, hardened as though a slug had crawled across them, frozen in the shape of fingers grasping at something.
The two passed each other in silence.
No conversation.
No glance.
No emotion.
Nothing.
I jerked my head back and held my breath.
Rain hammered against the corrugated plastic roof just behind me, booming like giant drums.
Yet for that brief instant, it felt as though every sound had receded into the distance.
When I cautiously looked again, Tsuruoka-san was walking through the corridor.
And right behind him, almost glued to his back, was the male half of the young couple whose relationship had always seemed to be led by the woman.
Tsuruoka-san kept his eyes fixed on Uchida-san ahead of him.
He would drift too close, then ease back slightly, then move closer again—
And I realized, with bitter irony, that even after becoming one of those things, Tsuruoka-san's weakness for women still remained.
It was the worst possible sight.
I fixed my eyes on the open doorway leading into the washroom wing and waited for my chance.
The presence of the four figures drifted farther and farther away in the opposite direction—
The intersection connecting the entrance hall, lounge, washrooms, and dining hall was about to be empty.
“Now...”
Keeping low, I stepped onto the wooden walkway.
The feel of the slats beneath my bare feet was undeniably gentler than the jagged stones outside.
But rain blew in sideways, drenching the wood.
The surface was as slick as glass coated with soap.
My foot slipped slightly.
I nearly lost my balance.
Splash!
The sound of water striking the soaked wood echoed through the storm.
I froze where I stood.
Keeping my face pointed toward the shadowed lodge ahead, I glanced sideways into the building without turning my head.
!!!
Oh no. Oh no, oh no, oh no!
Tanaka-san was beginning to emerge into the intersection.
Don't look this way.
Don't look this way.
Don't look this way.
I pleaded desperately in my mind.
Then—
The white light spilling from the washroom behind me suddenly flickered.
My heart lurched violently.
“...!”
At the exact instant my shoulders twitched in reflex,
Tanaka-san stopped dead in her tracks.
Slowly, her head began turning in my direction.
As though she were about to look straight at me.
Every muscle in my body locked up.
I dropped instantly into the darkness beyond the washroom entrance.
My vision went black.
Only the thunderous pounding of my heartbeat remained.
(Don't see me...
Don't see me...
Don't see me...)
I turned and looked into the washroom.
Nothing.
There was nothing there at all.
The fluorescent light had simply flickered.
Careful not to slip, I moved forward on my tiptoes along the wet wooden walkway.
The sound of footsteps crossing the lodge floor drifted through the storm.
Drawing closer.
Moving away.
Then returning again.
The rhythm was too precise.
Too regular.
So devoid of humanity that it felt insane.
And then—
From the darkness deeper within the washroom wing, I heard someone humming.
It wasn't cheerful.
It wasn't sad.
Just a hollow tune repeating meaningless notes without emotion.
I thought I could make out another figure far ahead in the dim building, hidden somewhere beyond the shadows.
And at the same time, I couldn't shake the feeling that someone was approaching from behind me as well.
As though I were slowly being surrounded.
Maybe it was only my imagination.
But if something really was getting closer—
This would be the end.
The typhoon winds suddenly intensified.
BAAM!
The corrugated plastic roofing thundered overhead.
My heart nearly stopped as it jolted violently inside my chest.
Before I realized it, tears had begun gathering in my eyes.
Not because I was afraid.
Not because I was cold.
But because the simple desire to **live** had stretched my mind to its breaking point.
My throat trembled as I drew a slow breath.
—Without letting anyone notice me.
—Absolutely no one.
—For Mari-senpai's sake.
The emotionless humming was still going.
But now it seemed to be coming not from the washroom itself, but from inside one of the toilet stalls.
I had no idea what whoever was inside was doing.
Still, if they were in the restroom, they probably wouldn't be able to attack me immediately.

The moment I stepped onto the covered walkway that connected the lodge and washroom wing to the corridor leading toward the drying room, the wet wooden slats beneath my feet let out an ominous creak.
"Giiish..."
Above me, the vinyl roofing was being battered by the violence of the rain.
Ratatatatatat!
The relentless impacts echoed overhead in a furious barrage.
Whenever the wind caught the edge of the soaked roofing sheets,
Bweeeeen... Bweeeen...
a strange metallic hum drifted through the storm, sounding half like machinery and half like some mournful instrument.
From the long windows of the washroom wing on my right,
soft yellow light spilled out in narrow bands.
To my left were the windows of the staff office.
Dark glass.
And then—
something black crossed through the glow behind them.
A shadow.
Slightly shorter than a person.
Yet the rhythm of its movement wasn't human.
Not a shuffle.
Not a hurried walk.
Something unnatural.
My body stiffened on instinct.
(What was that...? Who was that?)
No answer came.
Only the wind.
Its howl seemed almost to push against my back, urging me forward.
Was it just the shadow of something moving?
No.
There weren't any trees here.
Then what was it?
I held my breath and kept moving.
The next window belonged to the employees' room.
Rain distorted the glass,
yet somehow the shape moving behind it stood out with unsettling clarity.
A long arm.
No...
Maybe it only looked like an arm.
Maybe my imagination was filling in the gaps.
But my brain was already screaming danger.
(Don't look... don't look at it... if you look, it'll notice you...)
Some instinct deeper than reason was sounding an alarm inside my head.
The wet wooden slats beneath my feet were slippery.
Again and again my footing failed, and I nearly fell.
Each time, it felt as though my heart flipped inside my chest.
Then—
Behind me, somewhere inside the washroom wing, the fluorescent lights flickered once more.
Flick... flick...
For a moment, the world stopped.
I couldn't turn around.
What if, the instant I looked back,
someone—or something—was standing right behind me?
What if a pitch-black face with no eyes and no nose was pressed against the glass?
Fear crawled up my spine.
The rain suddenly intensified, hammering against the metal roofing overhead,
and a gust of wind tore through the walkway, slapping cold rain across my face.
Only then did I finally manage to breathe again.
(I have to keep moving...)
(...there's no other choice...)
Farther ahead.
A window that probably belonged to the lodge owner's room.
Nothing seemed to be moving inside.
At least, that's how it looked.
And yet...
In the deepest corner of the darkness beyond the glass,
I could feel the unmistakable presence of someone facing me.
I couldn't see anyone.
But I could feel their gaze.
Only that pressure reached me.
Grinding my teeth together,
I forced myself forward,
one step,
then another,
the wooden slats creaking softly beneath my feet.
Beyond the drying room on my right stood a large water tank.
Its beige FRP walls were framed by gray steel supports, forming a massive cube that sat beyond the reach of the walkway's light, swallowed by darkness.
Across the passage stood a gray steel door.
Behind it should have been the power room where Suzuki Sho-san and I had built the electric spear.
At last I reached the door.
The small window of the power room was completely dark.
But the rain, driven sideways by the wind, bent and scattered the light.
For a split second, it created the illusion that something was standing inside.
I caught my breath.
(No...)
(It's nothing...)
(Just a shadow...)
I kept repeating it to myself.
Yet the skin on my back refused to settle.
Ever since entering this corridor,
I had avoided looking behind me.
Because I was certain that if I did,
something would be standing there.

Beyond the gray steel door was the corner of the lodge.
I slipped into the shelter of the corner, pressing myself against the wall as I tried to steady my breathing beneath the cover of the rain.
Thank goodness.
Just as I'd hoped, there was an exterior staircase leading up to the second floor.
The covered walkway behind me was the place that had felt alive with unseen presences only moments ago.
That shifting shadow...
It hadn't moved like an ordinary person.
Just remembering it sent a chill down my spine.
Wind curled around the corner of the building, whipping my soaked T-shirt mercilessly against my body.
It felt as though the storm itself was trying to betray my hiding place.
I quickly pressed a hand against my chest to keep the fabric from flapping.
A gust howled past my ear, stealing what little warmth I had left.
Carefully, I leaned out and looked around the corner.
Far down the walkway where I had been hiding moments before—near the exit of the main lodge building, lit by the orange glow from within—
the shadow moved again.
Or at least, I thought it did.
It seemed to have stopped.
Maybe it was looking this way.
I immediately pulled back behind the corner.
My heart pounded so loudly that even the droplets falling from the tips of my wet hair sounded like footsteps giving away my position.
Keeping close to the wall, I moved forward through the rain.
The lodge's exterior was soaked black, making it look like the skin of some enormous creature.
Ahead of me, the underside of the staircase emerged faintly from the darkness.
The shadows beneath it were even deeper.
Rain struck the steel framework overhead.
Clang. Clang.
Irregular metallic notes echoed through the storm.
Each gust of wind changed their rhythm,
making it sound disturbingly as though someone were walking above me.
I swallowed hard and crept toward the base of the stairs.
To climb them, I would have to step out into the open and turn back at the landing.
Once I reached that point, the path to the second floor would be right in front of me.
But then—
From somewhere beyond the turn in the staircase came a faint sound.
Rattle... rattle...
As though something were rolling stones across the ground.
Was it the wind?
A loose branch?
Or...
the footsteps of something waiting here?
The sound drifted in and out beneath the rain,
never clear enough to identify.
That uncertainty only fed my imagination,
doubling the fear.
My throat burned.
(I have to go.)
Staying here was more dangerous.
Repeating that to myself, I slipped out from beneath the staircase and carefully moved around to the front.

I carefully gripped the wet handrail.
Whenever the wind shook the corrugated metal roof above the staircase, sheets of rainwater spilled down all at once.
The sound resembled approaching footsteps, and I instinctively shrank back.
A deep breath.
All right.
I told myself that and began climbing, one step at a time.
The metal steps were fitted with anti-slip ridges, but rain swept in from the side and poured down them like miniature waterfalls.
Even with bare feet, I felt as though I could slip at any moment.
If I lost my footing, I might cry out without meaning to.
That thought forced me to move with extreme care.
The faint sensation of my bare soles touching the cold metal.
It felt as though putting even the slightest weight on a step might make it creak beneath me.
Water streamed down the stairs and washed over the tops of my feet, as if trying to sweep them away.
I couldn't afford to let my guard down.
Those things could be hiding anywhere.
And...
something had attacked from above before.
The moment I remembered that, a cold sensation crawled slowly up my back.
An image flashed into my mind—
that thing clinging to the roof over the staircase.
My neck prickled instantly.
"What if... it's up there again...?"
Unable to endure the fear any longer, I looked up.
The metal roof above was still being hammered by the rain.
White droplets splashed and dripped from its edges.
Fortunately, there was no sign of anything lurking there.
But the rain was so loud that I couldn't be certain.
I couldn't prove that nothing was there.
And somehow, that made it even worse.
One step.
Then another.
Holding my breath,
making myself as small as possible,
I continued climbing.
