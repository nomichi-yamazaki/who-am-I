Disturbance

Yohei and I stepped out into the washroom and washed our hands. As we opened the door, a restless murmur reached us from the right, mixed with the sound of rain.
I hadn’t paid it much attention before, caught up in our conversation, but now that I thought about it, the voices gathering in the drying room—where our thing was—had a strangely focused intensity.
I lightly tapped Yohei on the shoulder and signaled with my eyes.
“…What do you think that is?” I whispered.
“Could it be… the thing we found?” Yohei frowned.
We crept closer to the drying room and peered inside through the open entrance.
What jumped into view was the two men from earlier—the jeans-and-glasses guy and the athletic one—spreading out a silver thermal sheet and eagerly taking photos with their phones.
Beside them were the two young women from the lounge—the same ones who had come down the stairs chatting about a movie while we were checking in—the father and child who had just been brushing their teeth at the washroom, and the three energetic guys who had been joking around with their “I can do it” talk—all leaning in, watching with keen interest.
So that’s what they were talking about earlier…Idiot, don’t worry about it, … I thought.
“H-hey… wait a second…” My voice rose unintentionally.
My heart pounded, my breath catching in my throat.
“…Photos? That’s… that’s it, isn’t it…?” Yohei’s eyes were fixed on something deep inside the room, still covered by the thermal sheet.
We exchanged glances, at a loss for words. Don’t tell me… they opened it on their own…?
“We gotta tell the others!” Yohei said.
I nodded, and we broke into a run, heading back along the passage between the washroom and the lodge.
In the damp air, we ran, breathless, our slippers clattering against the wooden slats beneath our feet.
At the end of the passage, we turned right, flung open the lodge’s sliding door, rushed inside, and slammed it shut behind us before sprinting toward the stairs on the right.
We charged up the steps, turned right at the top, and ran down the corridor.
The sensation of running synced with the pounding of my heart, amplifying the urgency.
When we reached our room, we turned left and slid open the door to Hinode.
Inside, Takizawa-san, Tsuruoka-san, Mari-senpai, and Yabe-san were sitting on the tatami, talking.
They looked up as we burst in mid-conversation.
“They’re making a scene in the drying room!” Yohei reported, out of breath.
I nodded behind him, struggling to catch mine.
“They’ve spread out the thermal sheet… they’re taking pictures,” I added.
Tsuruoka-san reacted immediately.
He stood up and quickly scanned the room, flustered.
Takizawa-san and the others followed, rising one after another, then rushing out as if moving in sync.
Tsuruoka-san, Takizawa-san, Mari-senpai, and Yabe-san headed down the corridor toward the stairs.
Yohei and I followed, tension and urgency thick in the air as we moved.
The lodge felt like it was about to grow just a little more chaotic than usual.
Tsuruoka-san, Takizawa-san, Mari-senpai, Yabe-san, Yohei—and I—started down the stairs.
“Who’s gathered there?” Tsuruoka-san asked in a low voice.
“The athletic guy we saw at reception—and the one in jeans,” Yohei answered, and I nodded, adding,
“And the girls from the lounge… plus the father and child who were cooking earlier.”
Tsuruoka-san frowned for a moment, his gaze fixed toward the drying room. “Those idiots…” he muttered.
We went down the stairs and turned left, the lodge’s exit coming into view.
Tsuruoka-san yanked the door open with a loud rattle.
There was anger in the way he threw it open.
As the door slid aside, damp, oddly warm air wrapped around us.
Thunder rumbled overhead.
Dim yellow light flickered in the darkness.
Rain rattled against the plastic corrugated roofing, water spilling through the gutters.
Stepping outside and looking left, the entrance to the drying room—and the restless crowd—came into view at once.
The moment he reached the front of the drying room, Tsuruoka-san’s eyes sharpened.
“What the hell do you think you’re doing?!”
His voice carried a raw, pride-fueled anger.
I arrived at the entrance of the drying room as well.
Inside the drying room, some people were eagerly pointing their smartphones at it, others leaned forward to get a closer look, and a few exchanged excited whispers—those voices and movements only amplified the tension.
Tsuruoka-san’s voice rang out in front of the drying room.
“Hey—this is important research material for a university! Don’t take photos without permission!”
The athletic man and the guy in jeans and glasses widened their eyes, clearly caught off guard.
The two women in their twenties stiffened and quickly stepped back.
“S-sorry…” they murmured under their breath as they moved away.
The child from the family clung tightly to his father’s arm, and the man—likely his father—gently ushered him outside, saying, “Let’s wait out here for a bit.”
“S-sorry, sorry!” “They told us there was something interesting to see…” the three “I-can-do-it” guys joked nervously as they backed away.
The athletic man in his thirties, sweating, shot back defensively.
“E-even so… you should’ve put up a sign saying not to touch it or something!”
Tsuruoka-san’s eyes flashed.
“Sign or no sign—you saw this at the reception, didn’t you? You knew what it was and still did this!”
One of the women in her twenties gasped softly.
The “I-can-do-it” trio all started apologizing at once. “S-sorry…”
Tsuruoka-san’s anger didn’t subside. He locked eyes with the athletic man, and between them hung an unspoken challenge—you think you can get away with this?
“Everyone, please calm down. I’m sorry, but we can’t have people handling this without permission.” Mari-senpai said, trying to soothe the situation while clearly siding with Tsuruoka-san as she addressed the two men.
“Sorry… I did try to stop him…” the guy in jeans and glasses said.
“What the hell, are you trying to make me the bad guy here?” the athletic man snapped at him.
“Well, you were the one who—” the guy in glasses shot back.
“Tsuru-san, they’ve apologized. Let’s leave it at that,” Takizawa-san said, trying to calm him down.
Tsuruoka-san’s eyes were still sharply fixed.
“Then they need to delete those photos from their memory. Right now.”
He stepped closer, pulling the aluminum sheet back over it to conceal whatever lay exposed beneath.
“Wait, we have to delete them?” the athletic man in his thirties frowned slightly.
“If those get posted on social media, it’ll be a problem—for the university and for us,” Tsuruoka-san said, his voice edged not just with anger, but urgency.
“If you’re willing to put it in writing that you won’t make them public, then fine…” he added.
“Is that really necessary?” Yabe-san said quietly.
“Tsuru-san, maybe a promise not to post them is enough,” Takizawa-san suggested.
“Yeah, we’re not gonna post them. No way. Right?” the guy with glasses said.
“…Yeah, we won’t,” the athletic man added reluctantly.
“If you break that promise, we’ll have to take legal action… got it? Absolutely no showing anyone, no posting anywhere. Understood?” Tsuruoka-san pressed, his eyes flashing.
Everyone swallowed.
The athletic man, the two women in their twenties, and the three college guys—all nodded deeply.
At last, the tension began to ease, and Yabe-san let out a small sigh.
“He probably never imagined he’d be treated like such a spectacle… but in the end, everyone’s only thinking about themselves. He’s just being used as a prop.” Yabe-san said.
Her words hung in the air, silencing everyone gathered there.
That single remark—just being used as a prop—made me think of something larger: the countless ordinary people used as fuel for profit by a unified web of government, corporations, religion, the entertainment industry, organized crime, the media, and even corporate unions.
After a moment, people began to drift away—one by one.
A gust of wind rushed through the passageway, the wooden pillars lining both sides creaking with a sharp crack, and somewhere a panel banged loudly.
The rain seemed lighter now, reduced to a steady, whispering hiss.
