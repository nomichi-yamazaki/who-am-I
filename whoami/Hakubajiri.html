Hakuba-Jiri

*Shaaa…* *Trickle, trickle, trickle…*
To my right, I could hear the sound of stream water flowing.
“Oh, there it is at last. Hakuba-jiri Hut.
Let’s take a little break here.”
Ahead, where Takizawa-san pointed, the roof of Hakuba-jiri Hut appeared through the trees.
Its flat blue metal roof stood out against the sky, looking almost like an oasis.
—Yes… a break at last.
A small cheer burst inside my chest.
Relief slowly spread from the bottom of my heart,
and with it, all the fatigue I’d been holding back came rushing in at once.
Each time I stepped onto the stone-paved stairs,
the cold, rough texture rising through the soles of my boots made it feel even more real that I was truly climbing this mountain.
To my left, a gigantic triangular boulder came into view,
towering with the solemn presence of a guardian deity of the mountain itself.
Across its rock face, in crisp white letters, were the words:
*Otsukaresan,Youkoso Daisekkei.*-* Good work. Welcome to the Great Snow Valley.*
The lettering, slightly worn and faded by wind and snow,
carried the weight of passing years,
as if it were offering a quiet greeting to every traveler who arrived here.

Behind us stood the mountain hut itself,
built with a frame of wooden pillars and beams,
topped by a flat blue metal roof,
its walls clad in corrugated panels of a color somewhere between ocher and brown,
with dark brown aluminum-framed windows set into them.
There were two buildings, one in front and another farther back,
with the one in the rear rising higher.
The front hut had the feel of a souvenir shop or a small dining lodge,
and beside its entrance stood a bench,
where climbers sat wiping away their sweat and laughing together.
The building farther back was much larger,
its first and second floors lined with windows stretching in a single unbroken row.
Its triangular roof seemed almost pressed into the mountainside,
and somehow it reminded me of a school building.

So this was Hakuba-jiri Hut…
The triangular boulder loomed larger and larger until we finally reached it.
The wooden bench in front of it was worn with age,
its surface marked here and there by cracks carved by years of rain and wind.
I dropped onto it as if collapsing and let out a long breath.
The strength drained from my whole body,
and I sank into the bench as though I had just cast off a suit of heavy armor.
Seeing me like that, natural smiles spread across my companions’ faces.
A cold wind brushed my cheeks and lightly stirred my hair.
“Oi, you know the Great Snow Valley’s next.
If you’re already in that kind of shape, I’m worried about what’s ahead,” Tsuruoka-san said as he set his pack down nearby.
“*I’m worried for what lies ahead,*” Yabe-san murmured, still standing with one hand on her shoulder strap.
“What’s the difference? Means about the same thing,” Tsuruoka-san shot back, having apparently caught her correction.
“So Sakuma-kun getting a little tired is enough to trouble you that much…” Yabe-san said, turning toward him.
At that, Takizawa-san, Mari-senpai, and Yohei all let out quiet laughs.
Listening to their exchange, I let my gaze wander around us.
Mt. Shirouma towered magnificently before us,
and every now and then the wind stirred the trees into a restless rustle.
Riding that breeze came the scent of damp earth and moss, tickling my nose.
When I exhaled, my white breath dissolved into the cold air.
*Shaaa… Trickle, trickle, trickle…*
I could hear water running through the rocks.
“Oi, look over there! That’s the Great Snow Valley!
Come on, you all better get your photos now!”
At Takizawa-san’s voice, I looked up to see him still standing, arm stretched out as far as it could go, pointing ahead.
—Let’s see…
Forcing my tired body upright, I stood and looked toward Mt. Shirouma looming ahead.
A white vertical streak cut down across it.
“Wow… beautiful! It really is pure white!” Mari-senpai said.
“No way… we’re really gonna walk there!?
Whoa—this is awesome! I’m getting pumped!” Yohei said too.
—So that’s the Great Snow Valley…
Amazing.
I whispered it only in my mind, still too out of breath to say it aloud.
“The name Shirouma comes from the snow pattern left on the Great Snow Valley.
In spring, it looks just like a white horse, and that’s how the mountain got its name,” Takizawa-san explained.
—Huh… so that’s why.
I’d like to see it when it really takes the shape of a horse.
*Click… click, click… click…*
Everyone pulled out their cameras and started taking photos however they pleased.
—Should I take one too…?
My hand reached toward my camera,
but more than taking a picture, what I really wanted was to sit back down.

From a few steps up the stone stairs in front of the triangular rock where I had been, there was a square concrete slab on the right, and everyone seemed to be sitting there.
So I dragged my aching legs up the steps and made my way over to them.
Thud, thud—one after another, the sound of packs being dropped onto the concrete echoed around me.
Yabe-san and Yohei each laid out the small cushions they had brought and sat down with a quiet sigh of relief.
As I reached them and was about to sit directly on the concrete, Mari-senpai smiled and said,
“Here, Saku-chan, sit on this,”
pointing to a blue, metallic-looking mat with black trim—the one she was sitting on.
“Ah… th-thank you…”
I said, carefully lowering myself onto it.
“Alright then, I’ll make some coffee. Let’s all have a cup,”
Takizawa-san said as he opened the top lid of his pack and started rummaging inside.
“If we take a long break right at the start, it’ll make us not want to walk again,”
Yabe-san said to him.
“Well, we’ve got Saku-chan with us,”
Takizawa-san replied lightly.
“And this is about the only place we can properly rest anyway. It’ll be fine.”
“…I guess,”
Yabe-san said shortly.

“I look pretty worn out myself.
No wonder—there was trouble at the factory the other day, and I barely got any sleep for three days,”
Tsuruoka-san said, sitting on the concrete while looking at a photo he had taken on his phone.
“You know, once our production line stops, we’re talking tens of millions gone just like that.
The kind of pressure we’re under is on a whole different level.
Do you have any idea how much responsibility I’m carrying?” he went on.
“There he goes again, talking about work…”
Mari-senpai whispered beside me, glancing toward Yabe-san.
“And yet he still leaves right on time,”
I heard Yabe Laser mutter under her breath.
“And when this guy messed up the other day, you know what happened?
Right up against the deadline, he nearly shut the whole line down,
and I had to go apologize to the client myself,”
Tsuruoka-san said, turning to me and throwing the comment my way.
—Here we go again…
They say rumors fade after seventy-five days, but that was already three months ago…
I had just put my pack down and felt lighter,
and now it was like the weight had shifted straight onto my chest.
—Lately, I think I’ve been doing pretty well at work, though…
Having a junior join made things easier…
At first, I even had to make tea for everyone, and that was rough…
“Yeah… it kind of made me lose confidence…”
What came out of my mouth was the exact opposite of what I was thinking.
“Hm… well, I’ve heard something different.
That Saku-chan’s diligent and always keeps people updated.
I like those kinds of rumors better.
And I’ve heard you’ve got quite a few certifications too—
you’re a licensed electrician, right?”
Mari-senpai said, stepping in to back me up.
It’s true—I have a Class 2 electrician’s license,
a hazardous materials handler (Class B),
and a second-class boiler engineer certification.
But the electrician’s license was something I got through school.
“I mean… I got the electrician’s license at school…”
I said.
“Certificates don’t do the work for you,”
Tsuruoka-san said, pulling out a lighter from his pocket.
Click.
With a soft flick, he lit his cigarette.
The small flame briefly illuminated his face.
Exhaling a slow stream of smoke,
he gave a slight shrug, a trace of his usual self-satisfaction showing through.
Almost immediately, the air filled with the smell of tobacco.
Watching the smoke curl upward,
Mari-senpai frowned.
“Hey… the smoke…”
Yabe-san also fell silent, her expression tightening.
—I earned the other certifications myself, you know…
I thought.

“…By the way, Takizawa-san’s Toyota GranAce really made things easy.
It carried all the gear without any trouble, and the ride up here was super comfortable,”
Yohei said—maybe trying to change the subject, or maybe just jumping to a new topic like he always did.
“Yeah, no kidding.
It’s parked over at Sarukura, right?
The ride quality was exactly what you’d expect.
Which dealer did you get it from?
Must’ve talked them into a pretty nice discount, huh?”
Tsuruoka-san said teasingly, exhaling smoke as he looked at Takizawa-san.
When he talked, smoke even drifted out through his nose.
—Same as always… Tsuruoka-san really never thinks about other people, does he?
That’s what I found myself thinking as he polluted the fresh mountain air.
“From Miyamoto Toyota in Nagoya.
I pushed a little on the price, and they knocked quite a bit off for me.
I’ve been going there for years, so they take good care of me,”
Takizawa-san replied, pulling out a gold-colored cylindrical portable stove.
“So how much did you get them down?
Five hundred thousand?
Six hundred?
You really squeezed them hard, didn’t you?”
Tsuruoka-san pressed.
“Well, the Toyota GranAce was 6.5 million yen to start with.
After a bit of back-and-forth, I got it down to 6 million.
And they even threw in the power sliding doors on both sides and the navigation system as extras.
Well, I know the dealer pretty well,”
Takizawa-san said proudly.
—Huh… so Takizawa-san brags sometimes too.

—Six million…
A number like that rolled lightly into the mountain air, as if it weighed nothing at all.
As I listened to Takizawa-san and Tsuruoka-san trading boasts,
for some reason, my thoughts drifted back to my grandfather.
“Sho, you don’t have to put it in complicated terms.
You see, when it comes to taxes in this world,
there are ones that are easy to collect from anyone,
and ones where you have to choose carefully who to take from.”
Clink.
The small sound of a teacup being set down.
“Consumption tax, for example—
whether you have money or not,
you pay the same when you buy bread,
or when you buy shoes.
It’s a tax that’s hard to avoid.”
“You get taxed when you earn money,
and then taxed again when you spend it.
It kind of feels like…
they’re taking from the same money twice.”

After a short pause,
Grandpa looked out the window.
“So, you see, the money that’s collected,
it’s being used to reduce company taxes.
When you hear that kind of system exists…
Shou, what do you think?”
“More than whether it’s right or wrong,
Grandpa thinks about
who benefits and who ends up struggling.
That’s what he focuses on.”
“The money quietly collected from people in weaker positions
makes the powerful even more advantaged.
If that’s what people call ‘growth,’
Grandpa finds it a bit hard to understand.”

When I heard those words,
for some reason, a small flutter stirred deep in my chest.
Memories from my childhood, when I was trapped with nowhere to run,
and was blamed in the same way,
suddenly overlapped.
The consumption tax,
makes both the weak and the strong pay equally.
But the weight of the hardship,
is not the same.
—The reason I feel it that way,
is probably
because I was a bullied child.
Before reason can catch up,
my body reacts to a system
that leaves no way to escape.

Grandpa slowly swirled his teacup and then spoke quietly.
“The way the world works,
the words you see on the surface and the forces moving behind the scenes,
aren’t always the same.”
“Politics,
is closely connected to companies and those who hold large amounts of money, isn’t it?”
“That’s why,
without even realizing it,
the world can sometimes be arranged in a ‘convenient way’.”
He continued quietly, looking out the window.
“As a result,
even people who work properly
might end up struggling just to eat…
and that’s not entirely their fault.”
“It’s the system
that’s failing to support people properly.”

Grandpa smiled faintly and looked out the window.
“That’s why, Shou.
Before you take the words from the news at face value,
try to think once about who is able to breathe easier,
and who is having a harder time breathing behind the scenes.”
“The answer isn’t something
someone else will just give you.
I think the act of continuing to think about it
is itself part of being human.”
Looking up at the white snow valley,
I thought.
Even though we live in the same country,
there are people here worrying about the price of cars in the mountains,
while others are worried about today’s meal.
That gap felt
like a mountain far taller than the one in front of me, Hakuba-dake.

Seeing him brag like that, I wondered if Takizawa-san was really particular about cars.
…Come to think of it, the GranAce is sold overseas under the name HiAce, isn’t it?
I wonder if everyone knows that. If I said it now, they might be surprised… I thought.

“Can they really knock that much off the price?” even Tsuruoka-san said, sounding a little surprised.
—Should I say something? Maybe I should tell them? Or will they just say everyone knows that?
“Well, yeah, but that was because I had a trade-in.” Takizawa-san said.

—But maybe they’d be surprised that they didn’t know.
“Man, the ride really was comfortable. Even after a long drive, I didn’t feel tired at all. That’s the GranAce for you.” Youhei joined in.

—Should I say it? …Yeah, it’s now or never. I’ll say it the next time the conversation pauses.

“Isn’t it a bit too huge for coming to the mountains?” Tsuruoka-san said as he put out his cigarette in a portable ashtray, and somehow it sounded like sour grapes to me.
—Thank goodness. Finally free from that awful smell… I thought as he extinguished it.

—Alright, now maybe?
“Did you kno—” I started to open my mouth.
“Well, sure, bigger cars are better, but the ones carrying the really big responsibility are us. If a factory line stops even once, that’s tens of millions in losses. Every day’s a battle against pressure.” Even as Tsuruoka-san spoke, the stale smell of tobacco still seemed to pour from his mouth.
—I missed the timing… Great, it’s turned into work talk again. It’d be weird to bring up the HiAce thing now… Besides, didn’t he say the exact same thing earlier? I thought.
…Is he about to steer the conversation back to my mistake again?
“You should’ve brought your work responsibilities with you too…” Yabe-san said dryly.

A small silver pot was set on top of the brass cylindrical gas stove, and after pressing something like a syringe a few times,Takizawa-san inserted a small key-shaped control wrench and turned it.
As soon as a soft hiss began, he lit it.
The sound swelled into a steady whoosh.
Takizawa-san then pulled out enough instant coffee packets for everyone from his pack and handed them out one by one.
“Thank youu,” Mari-senpai said as she took hers from him.
“Thanks,” I said too, lowering my head as I accepted mine.
“Thanks a lot,” Youhei said.
“Thanks,” said Tsuruoka-san.
Yabe-san only gave a small nod and said nothing.
Each little word of thanks overlapped with the next, and for a moment, a gentle warmth softened the tired air around us.
I unclipped the stainless steel cup hanging from the side of my pack with a carabiner and set it down on the ground with a small clink.
I tore open the packet and took out the drip filter, hooking the paper tabs that stuck out like little ears over the rim of the cup.
Even though it was only a tiny act of preparation, it somehow made my heart settle down.

“I’m going to the bathroom,” Yabe-san said suddenly as she stood up and spoke to Mari-senpai.
“Wait, I’ll go too.” Following after Yabe-san, Mari-senpai got to her feet as well.
Yabe-san started ahead first, the sound of stones crunching sharply beneath her boots.
“I’m just going to the bathroom too,” Mari-senpai said, turning back for a moment.
The two of them walked off toward the space between the lodge in front that looked like a souvenir shop and the building behind it that reminded me of a schoolhouse.
I could hear the gritty jari-jari of gravel scattering under their steps as the backs of Mari-senpai’s pale pink T-shirt with its white arm covers and Yabe-san’s gray long-sleeved shirt grew smaller, swaying as they went.
Looking between the souvenir-shop-like lodge in front and the school-like building behind it, I saw a set of stone steps leading up to a slightly raised area, almost like a small terrace built of stacked stones, and there stood a long rectangular hut covered in ocher-colored wooden boards.
—So that’s where the bathroom is.
That was what I found myself thinking as I watched the two of them walk away.

The moment the two women stepped away, Tsuruoka-san—though I didn’t want him touching me—placed a hand on my shoulder and leaned in close, bringing his face right to my ear.
—What the hell… let go of me.
That was what I was thinking when…
“Hey, Shou… what kind of crap do you think Mari would dump out if someone gave her an enema? Bet that’d be something to see, huh?” Tsuruoka-san whispered, lowering his voice just a little.
—What…!? Wh-what is this guy even saying…?
My heart gave a violent thud, and heat rushed into my face.
But if I glared back at him here, it would ruin the mood.
And if something this vulgar ever reached Mari-senpai’s ears, I couldn’t imagine how disgusted she would feel.
—This bastard… he’s the worst. How can he laugh while saying something like that!?
Anger flared in me, as if Mari-senpai herself had been insulted.
Making dirty jokes with a real person’s name in them—what kind of human being does that?
That was what I thought.
And yet, even so, I forced out a polite, hollow smile.
The moment I did, a wave of self-loathing tightened painfully in my chest.
Secretly, I clenched my teeth in frustration.
“Senpai, that’s kinda messed up,” Youhei said, half-joking as if trying to keep things light.
It seemed Takizawa-san had heard it too, because he was grinning to himself.
Maybe he just didn’t want to ruin the atmosphere either, but… laughing while Mari-senpai was being talked about like that…
I never thought he was that kind of person.
I felt a little disappointed.
Like some of the trust I’d had in him was quietly starting to shake.

Hissss…
The stove heating the water began to make a sharper sound.
“Hey, Takky, that stove… is that an Optimus? You’ve been using that old thing for ages, huh?” Tsuruoka-san said, narrowing his eyes at the now-hissing burner.
—Huh… they call that a stove, not a gas burner?
For some reason, I suddenly felt like I’d wandered into a different world where only I didn’t know the language, and an awkward sense of being out of place crept over me.
“Yep, that’s right. Back in the day it was sold as the Svea 123R.
Simple, tough, and still going strong.”
“Whoa, they still sell those?” Youhei leaned forward with interest.
“The model’s changed now, but this one’s been with me for twenty years at least.
Take care of it, and it’ll last forever,” Takizawa-san said, lightly tapping the brass tank with his finger. It gave off a dry clang.
“That’s nice.
Stuff like that.” I said.
It was an honest reaction—something between cool and stylish—but at the same time I felt disgusted with myself.
What the hell am I doing? Mari-senpai was just insulted, and here I am sucking up to Takizawa-san when he laughed along with it.
“Just like you, Takky—you really know how to hold onto your gear.
Still, my Jetboil would get that water boiling way faster,” Tsuruoka-san said with a sly grin, adding the kind of unnecessary comment he just couldn’t help making.
“Fast isn’t everything.
Whether it’s freezing or the wind’s howling, this thing never lets me down,” Takizawa-san replied with a wry smile, not giving an inch.
The hissing grew louder, and tiny bubbles began to form along the bottom of the pot.
Little by little they multiplied, trembling finely against the metal.
In another moment, those bubbles would rise to the surface and begin to burst.

“...And then the department manager came in wearing the same tie again.” Mari-senpai’s voice drifted closer.
When I looked over, Mari-senpai and Yabe-san were walking back from the restroom, chatting as they approached.
—Mari-senpai works in HR, so when she says department manager, she probably means Suzaki-san, right?
For a moment, I felt like telling her that Tsuruoka and the others had been talking badly about her.
But if she asked what they said, there was no way I could bring myself to repeat something that awful out loud.
“Third day in a row.
And I’m pretty sure it’s the same shirt too...” Yabe-san replied as they came closer.
“The hasn’t-washed-it theory?” Mari-senpai said as she walked.
“They call him pickled plum because he smells sour,” Yabe-san answered.
“Oh stop, that makes it feel like the smell’s going to stick between my nose and throat,” Mari-senpai said with a laugh.
—Guess everyone likes gossiping about other people after all...
The thought made me almost smile, and before I knew it, I’d nearly forgotten how angry Tsuruoka had made me.
It was also a little surprising—and strangely nice—to hear usually quiet Yabe-san talking like this.

The bubbles clinging to the bottom of the pot swelled larger, and the surface of the water began to burst with a lively gobble-gobble, the sharp hissing sound fading away.
When Takizawa-san twisted the little wrench-like key on the stove, the flame went out, and white steam rose softly from the pot.
“Alright, one by one...” Takizawa-san said as he began pouring hot water over the instant drip pouch set on Tsuruoka-san’s cup.
“Welcome back,” I said to Mari-senpai and Yabe-san as they returned.
“I’m back~,” Mari-senpai answered brightly.
“Mm.” Yabe-san only gave a small nod.
“Welcome back,” Youhei said.
“Welcome back,” Takizawa-san said as he poured hot water into the drip pouch over my cup.
“Thank you,” I said to him.
“Welcooome baaack~,” Tsuruoka-san chimed in with a teasing, overly playful tone.
—The hell do you mean, welcome back...? After saying something that awful just a minute ago...
The irritation was still there, simmering inside me.

As Takizawa-san poured the hot water, steam rose from the drip pouch, and the rich aroma of coffee began to spread through the air.
It felt as though the irritation I’d been carrying toward Tsuruoka was slowly being soothed away.
The clear mountain air.
The cold wind rushing down from the snow valley.
And in the middle of it all, the coffee sending up steam, its fragrance wrapping around us.
Before I knew it, the anger from earlier had drifted somewhere far away, and all I could think was, Ah… this kind of moment is nice.
“Good timing getting back.
Another minute and the water would’ve cooled off on us,” Takizawa-san said as he began pouring hot water over the drip pouch set on Mari-senpai’s cup, which rested on the blue mat.

Mari-senpai set her pack on her knees and slid the zipper open with a soft shrrrip.
“Oh, wow—this pack opens all the way from the front?” I said, a little surprised.
“Nice, right?
It’s really convenient when you need to grab something quickly,” Mari-senpai said, then leaned forward slightly and peered inside, reaching in as if searching for something.
“When it comes to packs, my Deuter’s the one to beat.
Just look at this classy green! Carries way better too!” Maybe because I’d complimented Mari-senpai’s pack, Tsuruoka-san suddenly cut in like that.
Ignoring him completely, Mari-senpai kept rustling around inside her bag, and then pulled out a clear plastic package.
“Ta-da—Baumkuchen.
Let’s all have some,” she said.
Inside the bag were several individually wrapped slices of Baumkuchen, each one cut from the ring-shaped cake.
Mari-senpai opened the package seal and, smiling warmly, began handing them out one by one.
—Oh right.
I brought potato chips too.
I pulled my own pack closer.
“Yabecchi,” Mari-senpai said as she handed one to Yabe-san.
“Thanks,” Yabe-san replied shortly.
“Here you go, Saku-chan,” Mari-senpai said, offering one to me just as I was about to open my own pack.
“Thank you,” I answered, letting go of my pack with one hand to take the Baumkuchen she gave me, setting it gently beside me.
For some reason, being the second one she handed it to made me quietly happy.
“Here you go,” Mari-senpai said as she passed a wrapped slice to Youhei.
“Thaanks! Sweet stuff is the best!” Youhei said.

“Anyone want milk and sugar?” Takizawa-san asked, holding out the individually wrapped creamers and sugar sticks from a plastic bag.
“I’m good,” Yabe-san said to him.
I opened the top flap of my pack and reached inside, feeling around through the contents.
“I’m fine too.
...Here, Tsuruoka-san,” Mari-senpai said, declining Takizawa-san’s milk and sugar before handing the Baumkuchen to Tsuruoka.
—You don’t have to give one to a guy like him.
He was saying horrible things about you.
My hands stopped as the thought flashed through my mind.
“Thanks, Mari-chan.
See, women really know how to be thoughtful,” Tsuruoka said.

“What about you, You-chan?” Takizawa-san asked Youhei.
“I’m good too.
The Baumkuchen’s sweet enough for me,” Youhei said.
“Saku-chan?” Takizawa-san asked me.
I’d never had coffee without milk and sugar before, and honestly I wasn’t great with drinks that were just plain bitter.
But with everyone else turning it down, it felt embarrassing—and somehow selfish—for me to be the only one to ask for some.
“Ah, I’m okay too,” I said, turning it down before I could stop myself.
Maybe I should have taken it after all.

“Here, Takizawa-san.” Mari-senpai handed him a slice of Baumkuchen.
“I’ll take it.
Thanks,” Takizawa-san said, rustling through his own pack as he accepted it.

“Takkī, give me some of that. If I don’t put this stuff in, it ain’t coffee—it’s just bitter mud water.”
Tsuruoka-san said, asking Takizawa-san for sugar and milk.

“How many sticks?” Takizawa-san asked.

“Four creamers, four sugars.”

“That sweet?”
Takizawa-san said as he pulled several sticks of sugar and creamer from the plastic bag and handed them over.

“It ain’t coffee unless you put this much in…”
Tsuruoka-san said as he took the sticks from Takizawa-san.
Then his eyes landed on my pack.

“Shō, is that a Millet pack?”
Tsuruoka-san said, squinting at my backpack.
Then he smirked.

“Carry that thing long enough and you start earning millet-age or what?”
He burst out laughing at his own joke.
—Deciding it isn’t coffee unless it has sugar and milk in it…
That somehow felt narrow-minded to me.

A little while ago, he’d been going on about how the secret of good iced coffee was the difference in the brewing method.
But if he dumped in that much sugar and milk, wouldn’t every difference in extraction just disappear?
That was what I found myself thinking.

“My pack?”
“Y-yeah,” I answered, still rummaging through my bag for the potato chips.

A strangely flat silence drifted through the group.
The conversation had just… died.
What was that about?

“Hey…”
Tsuruoka-san nudged my arm.

“Yeah?”
I stopped searching through my bag and looked over at him.

“It’s Millet, so I was saying you rack up millet-age.”
He repeated the joke slowly, like I was supposed to get it this time.

“Oh—ah, ahaha… ahahaha…”
The way he said it, it felt like he’d keep going until someone laughed, so I gave in and forced out a laugh.

“From me too—just a little sugar boost.”
As if trying to rescue the mood, Takizawa-san pulled out a handful of Black Thunder bars from his pack pocket and began passing them around.

“Our department manager’s always making dad jokes too,”
Mari-senpai said to Yabe-san.

“Maybe they’re nice in summer… they keep things cool,”
Yabe-san murmured.
The two of them laughed.

Come to think of it, the wind blowing down from the snow valley really did have a sharp chill to it—cold enough to make me shiver.

Were Mari-senpai and Yabe-san gossiping about Suzaki-san from earlier?
When I pictured Suzaki-san, I saw a thin, somewhat tall man in a gray suit, balding, with an intimidating sort of presence…

I’d only really seen him during my job interview and the occasional time around the office.
I’d barely ever spoken to him.

—That guy tells jokes?
On top of being called pickled plum, too…

Takizawa-san was still handing out the Black Thunder bars.

“Thank you.”

—Was that the same trouble I’d been involved in too? I wondered.
“Man, that’s rough. With the labor shortage too, everyone on the floor’s hanging on by a thread,” Takizawa-san said, his voice growing louder as they approached.
“Yeah, but the people upstairs only look at the numbers—they don’t understand what’s happening on the ground. The way they pile on the pressure is brutal,” Tsuruoka-san said.
“Exactly. Lately all management ever talks about is efficiency. Honestly, I’m worn out too,” Takizawa-san said.
“Well, that’s exactly why we came to the mountains—to forget all that and just enjoy ourselves. At least for today, let’s take it easy,” Tsuruoka-san said.
I hadn’t forgiven him for everything else, but on this one point alone, I had to agree with him.
