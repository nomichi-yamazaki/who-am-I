Ascent

Standing at the entrance to the forest,
a mountain trail stretched out ahead of us,
its edges firmly reinforced with wooden boards to hold the soil in place.

The path, a mix of gravel and earth,
was packed down solid beneath the damp forest air,
giving it a quiet sense of reliability.

Soft sunlight filtered through the canopy,
the shifting light weaving between trunks and leaves,
casting a gentle rhythm across the ground at our feet.

A faint scent of fallen leaves brushed past my nose,
while the rustling of branches in the breeze
settled into the ears as the quiet murmur of the forest.

Takizawa-san stepped forward at the front,
moving at a steady pace,
his back—marked by years of experience—swaying gently with each step.

The large pack on his shoulders seemed to speak for itself,
carrying not just weight,
but a quiet reliability that felt as if it could carve a path ahead without yielding.

Behind him,
Mari-senpai followed with calm, measured steps.
Her mint-green jacket blended softly with the morning forest,
while the small accessories she wore swayed lightly,
adding a subtle rhythm to the trail.

In third place,
Yabe-san walked on in silence.
Yet the faint wear on her black pack
and the firmly fastened hiking boots she wore
spoke of a confidence that clearly did not belong to a beginner.

Each step sank into the gravel with a crunch,
the slightly damp earth pressing back against the soles of my boots.

Until just moments ago,
my tension had drifted somewhere out of reach—
but as the trail began to climb,
that uneasiness slowly lifted its head again.

“Hey… for trails like this,
is there some kind of trick to climbing them?
I’m not really confident in my stamina…”
I said,
lifting my feet and stepping up one level at a time.

“Yeah, there is.
The trick is… keep your steps small,
your center of gravity in the middle,
don’t let your core sway,
and keep your breathing steady—like, in through your nose…”
Takizawa-san replied,
glancing sideways as he stepped over the uneven ground.

—Wait…?
Small steps… center of gravity…
Where’s my core supposed to be?
What does ‘in the middle’ even mean…?

I had no idea what he was talking about.

“Just think of it as a walk.”
Yabe-san said,
barely turning her head as if only pretending to look back.

When she spoke to someone behind her,
she always turned less than most people would.

—I see… yeah, that makes sense.
It’s just walking.
…I’m here to enjoy this, aren’t I…
I thought.

…She looks experienced.
Maybe I should just follow her so I don’t fall behind…
I told myself.

I kept my eyes on the ground,
steadily making my way up the trail.

The slope wasn’t particularly steep.
It felt less like a climb
and more like a long, uneven staircase stretching on and on.

But that gentleness didn’t make it easy.

I lifted one foot onto the next step,
pushed my body up with it,
then pulled the other leg up and set it down on the higher ground.

The movement itself was simple,
but the height of each step was inconsistent,
throwing off my rhythm.

Some steps matched my stride perfectly,
while others were just awkwardly short—
making me stumble—
or unexpectedly high,
forcing me to lift my leg more than I wanted.

Sometimes I had to take several steps on a single level
before the next rise appeared.

Each time,
the pack on my back tried to pull my balance away.

The tight straps dug slowly into my shoulders and hips,
and one spot on my back had already begun to dampen with sweat.

In front of me,
Yabe-san’s black pack—covered with a mesh pocket—
swayed gently in a steady rhythm.

With each movement,
small items inside flickered into view through the netting,
and now and then,
the faint sound of fabric brushing in the wind reached my ears.

Her fluid movements made the pack seem like a natural extension of her body,
and somehow,
that quiet rhythm brought a strange sense of reassurance to the mountain path.

Just think of it as a walk.

Yabe-san’s voice echoed in my mind—
but I didn’t quite have the spare energy to agree with it.

A soft, rushing sound—like water slipping over stone—reached my ears.

The stream sounded cool and refreshing,
yet in reality,
the heat inside my body kept building, little by little.

I couldn’t fall behind.
Trying not to lose sight of Yabe-san—who walked so effortlessly—
I pushed myself to keep up.

Hah… hah…
My breathing was starting to grow rough.

Sweat gathered at my temples,
then, unable to cling any longer,
ran down along my cheek—
the sensation oddly like a small insect crawling across my skin.
I wiped it away without thinking.

The straps of my heavy pack dug into my shoulders,
and with each slow step,
I could feel my strength being drained, bit by bit.

Takizawa-san’s voice echoed back to me—
*Drink water often.*

He was right.
I’d been so focused on climbing
I hadn’t even noticed how thirsty I was.

I reached for the side pocket of my pack—
fumbled a little—
and finally pulled out my bottle.
Twisting off the cap,
I took a sip.

“…Ahh…”
The sound slipped out before I could stop it.

Cold water slid across my tongue
and sank into my heated throat.

In that moment,
it felt like something deep inside me had just woken up.

—Was water always this good?

The clear air,
the sweat along my neck,
the weight on my back,
and this steady, gentle climb—

all of it made that single sip feel extraordinary.

It was as if
this water existed just to remind me
that I was truly alive.

But while I was drinking,
the distance between me and Yabe-san had grown.

Her black pack—wrapped in that swaying mesh—
was already farther ahead than I’d expected,
nearly disappearing beyond the bend in the trail.

Startled,
I shoved the bottle back into my pack,
nearly losing my balance,
and hurried forward with longer strides.

Something inside my pack thudded softly.

With rough breaths scraping in my throat,
I climbed—one step, then another—
until at last,
Yabe-san’s back came into view again.

Without thinking,
I let out a quiet breath of relief.

Her back remained unchanged—
silent,
steady,
facing forward—

as if it were the rhythm of this mountain itself.

It was hot.

The fleece I had put on for the chilly air now felt as heavy as a sauna suit.
I wanted to take it off,
but if I stopped now,
I’d end up making everyone wait.

Looking up at the backs of the others ahead of me,
I searched for the right moment.
But the line never broke,
continuing upward in the same steady rhythm.

—If I’m going to take it off,
it has to be in an instant…
No, wait—
I’d have to undo the straps on my pack,
take off the fleece,
then fasten everything all over again…

Just imagining it,
I could already picture myself being left behind.

I could even clearly see myself in my mind,
frantically spilling the contents of my pack everywhere
and scrambling to chase after them.

—Maybe I should just put up with it a little longer.

Sweat slowly gathered at the back of my neck,
and I could feel it running down inside my T-shirt.

The heat clinging to my cheeks,
and the frustration of not being able to take the fleece off,
left irritation and unease smoldering deep in my chest.

—Aren’t they hot too?

No one said a word.
They just kept moving their feet.

I was the only one worrying about pointless things,
constantly adjusting myself to everyone else’s pace.

The fact that I couldn’t even bring myself to say,
“Hey, give me a second—I need to take off my fleece,”
made me dislike myself just a little more.

The green hills rising on both sides were round and gentle, their slopes wearing an almost peaceful expression.
Yet the mountain trail threading through the valley was strewn with rocks of every size, leaving almost no such thing as level ground beneath our feet.
The slightest tilt of a stone underfoot threatened to twist my ankle, making it hard to find any steady rhythm.
It felt as if the ground itself might betray me at any moment.
And yet, whenever I lifted my face, the sharply defined triangular summit of Mount Shirouma stood clear against the blue sky, piercing upward like a blade.
Drawn onward by that distant shape, I kept moving forward.
Partway up, water spilled down from the rocky slope to our left, weaving between massive boulders before cutting across the trail.
A rough bridge of timber beams had been laid across it so our boots wouldn’t have to sink into the water.
I crossed it carefully, placing each step with deliberate caution.
Everyone had made such a commotion at the larger bridge over the stream earlier, but this crossing was smaller, and perhaps because the greenery around it pressed in thicker here, dimmer and more overgrown, no one said a word.

Was it anxiety that made my thoughts drift this way?
Whenever I walked like this, my mind would slip away on its own, dragged back to the recent news and the endless stream of words that had poured across my screen.
Here in Japan, resentment toward immigrants and neighboring countries had been spreading like sparks carried on the wind.
The moment someone hurled out a harsh phrase, voices convinced it was justice would gather into a swarm, swelling all at once into another blaze of outrage.
Emotion always surged ahead of reason, and the eyes of those who seemed to need an “enemy” were forever turned outward, searching beyond themselves.

A little while ago, the Prime Minister had said,
“If one of our allied nations is attacked, Japan too faces an existential threat.”
From the moment those words were spoken, the atmosphere with the neighboring countries had visibly changed.
At the civilian level, tourism and business should have been things built on mutual dependence, each side supporting the other.
And yet artists’ performances were canceled,
threats like “We’ll cut your heads off,”
or “Then we won’t lend you any more pandas,”
started flying back and forth in raw, naked bursts of emotion.
Even within Japan, insults aimed at the people of the other country spread with frightening speed.
—How is this any different from children fighting?
Because the other side provoked us, we answer with the same attitude.
If we can still puff out our chests and call that justice, then in the end we’re only standing at the very same level.
Shouldn’t it be different?
Shouldn’t reason be what reins in pride and selfishness,
with consideration, cooperation, humility, and reflection—
all those things placed carefully between us as we learn to live alongside the person next to us?
And yet the leaders of nations across the world…
do they truly possess none of those brakes that make us human?
To sacrifice someone else for one’s own gain—
if that is what an intelligent being chooses to do,
then how are we any different from creatures with no intelligence at all?

I’d talked about that once when I went to visit my grandfather’s house.
After a thoughtful pause, he spoke slowly.
“Sho, the beginning of war isn’t all that different from a children’s quarrel.”
He narrowed his eyes a little and continued, still holding his teacup in both hands.
“At first, it’s never anything that big. Someone gets hurt, someone else feels like their pride’s been crushed, and then neither side can back down.”
“People around them start saying, ‘Hit them back,’ and they themselves get it into their heads that staying quiet means losing.”
He let the words settle before going on.
“With kids fighting, too, both sides usually start out believing they’re the one who’s right.”
“War’s the same. If you only look at the beginning, there’s hardly any way to tell the difference.”
Here, the tone of his voice shifted just slightly.
“…But.”
My grandfather continued, choosing each word with care.
“War is what happens when there’s no adult to stop the children’s fight, and the adults just keep it going themselves.”
“And the ones getting hit aren’t even the people who started the fight.”
Then, almost to himself, he murmured,
“Grown adults start fighting like children, and the people who get hurt are the ones who weren’t even fighting.
Don’t you think there’s something terribly unfair about that?”
The immigration issue followed the very same pattern.
Words of exclusion flew back and forth, and people on the right would say, It’s your fault for being here, while lashing out at those on the left who did little more than insist that discrimination was wrong.
But the truth was far simpler than that.
The increase in immigrants hadn’t been caused by the people on the left who were now taking the blame, but by those who had long supported the pursuit of cheap labor and the competition for profit.
Because it was all in the name of competition, wages were driven lower, hours stretched longer, and the number of disposable non-regular workers kept rising.
The result was that younger generations could no longer build stable lives, and fewer children were born.
Labor shortages followed, and foreign workers were brought in to fill the gaps.
In other words—
what was happening now was the consequence of their own choices.
And yet they pushed those choices conveniently out of sight, while desperately searching for someone else to blame.
As I turned those thoughts over in my mind,
my grandfather’s voice suddenly rose again from somewhere deep in my chest.

“That’s the thing about human beings,” he had said.
“We’re not creatures doomed to destroy ourselves fighting over scraps of food.
Because we can think,
we can hold back our selfishness and our pride,
and choose to live together.”
“Sho.
Before being Japanese,
the real question is whether you can remain human.”

Those words felt as though they were quietly—yet unmistakably—casting their light on the place I was about to step into,
and on the reality I would soon have to face.

When the endless rocky climb finally gave way, the view opened a little, and we entered an area where thick alpine shrubs crowded both sides of the trail.
Then, without warning, a wooden boardwalk appeared beneath our feet.
I stepped onto it behind Takizawa-san, Mari-senpai, and Yabe-san.
A dry gish sounded, and the solid feel of the planks answered through the soles of my boots.
Kash… Kash… Kash… Kash…
Kash, kash, kash, kash…
The sound of hiking boots striking the wooden path echoed ahead and behind me.
With the ground beneath us flat, it was so much easier to walk.
Freed from the endless uneven steps, all I had to do was move my legs straight forward.
There was no fear of a rock rolling underfoot, no worry about missing a step and losing my balance.
Only moments ago, every foothold had demanded my full attention, my breathing growing ragged with the strain, but now the steady sensation beneath my boots brought a deep sense of relief.
Just having the trembling uncertainty of the ground replaced by something orderly made even the scenery seem calmer somehow.
Kash… Kash… Kash… Kash…
Kash, kash, kash, kash…
The scrape of my boots against the boardwalk blended with everyone else’s, the sounds layering together into a strangely soothing rhythm.

“Sho, you suddenly picked up the pace. What’s up with that? Sprout wings out of your back or something?” Yohei called from behind me, teasing.
“Shut up,” I said with a laugh, still walking as I slipped my right hand from the shoulder strap of my pack, twisting only my upper body to the side and pretending to throw a punch at Yohei’s soft stomach.
“Sho, I hear when you’re tired, you’re supposed to chant *panty*,” Tsuruoka-san said from somewhere behind Yohei.
“…It’s just that walking got easier.” Apparently she’d heard us; Yabe-san muttered it like a quiet aside without even turning around as she kept facing forward.
It sounded like she was making a deadpan correction to how far Tsuruoka-san’s comment had drifted off-topic.
—She stays quiet, but she really does hear everything… Yabe laser.
Trying not to laugh, I thought to myself.
—Yabe-san gives off this don’t-get-too-close feeling, but she’s actually kind of funny…
“Huh? Senpai, what’s this *panty* thing? Now I really gotta know,” Yohei said behind me.
From the shape of his voice, I could tell he was half-turning as he spoke.
—Better not get involved. I faced forward again.
“Oh, it’s kinda like one of those sacred chants you say during yoga or something,” Tsuruoka-san said proudly from farther back.
“You mean *shanti*… sexual harassment,” Yabe-san said, sounding almost exasperated, giving a faint nod as she kept walking without looking back.
Mari-senpai, walking just ahead of her, let out a soft laugh.
I nearly got pulled into laughing too and had to hold it in.
—Maybe when he first heard *panty*, even Yabe-san had no idea what they were talking about… Thinking that made it even funnier.
“Senpai, apparently it’s not *panty*, it’s *shanti*. Like some yoga spell or something,” Yohei called after hearing that.
“Yeah, but chanting *panty* sounds way more effective for curing fatigue, doesn’t it?” Tsuruoka-san said from the rear.
—Is he saying that to make people laugh, or does he honestly mean it? But the way he says everything so seriously makes it sound like he genuinely believes it.
“There it goes off the rails again,” I heard Yabe-san murmur up ahead.
“Stop it… my stomach’s gonna twist from laughing,” Mari-senpai said, glancing back over her shoulder.
Her face was bright with laughter, and somehow it made my chest feel warm.
Mari-senpai’s smile had a way of soothing me.
“Huh? You could hear that? Crap, seriously…” Yohei said from behind me.
The leaves of the low trees lining both sides of the path swayed gently in the sunlight.
It was only the smallest change, but that quiet peacefulness made me feel, just a little, as though I’d been saved.

The boardwalk ended, giving way to a soft, easy trail that stretched between the grass, gentle underfoot and mercifully free of large rocks.
My breathing gradually began to steady, and with it came the smallest bit of room in my mind to relax.
After following that smooth path for a while, wooden steps reinforced with planks to hold back the soil began to appear.
There was depth between each rise, enough that we had to walk several paces before reaching the next step.
Just as the climb began to demand a little more from my legs again, Takizawa-san, who had been leading ahead, stopped and turned around.
“Let’s peel a layer off here for a bit. It’s gettin’ too damn hot,” he said, and inwardly I felt a rush of relief.
At last, I finally had the perfect excuse to take off the fleece I’d been wanting to shed for some time now.
Everyone began lowering their packs, each of us starting to strip off an outer layer.
I slid my pack from my shoulders and, watching what everyone else was doing, decided it would fit perfectly tucked beneath the top lid.
I opened the pack’s lid and folded the fleece I’d been wearing, slipping it neatly into place.
“Oh, Tsuru-san, that’s a nice one you’ve got there. Mammut, is it?” Takizawa-san said, pointing at Tsuruoka-san’s jacket.
“Nice, right? Picked it up as an older model a while back, but it’s still going strong. Light as anything, tough too, and a little rain won’t faze it in the slightest,” Tsuruoka-san said, sounding faintly proud.
“Heh, nice. Mine’s this one—I found it at a shop in Nagoya called Campal. Bit of a minor brand, but the breathability’s spot-on,” Takizawa-san replied.
“Campal? What’s that, sounds like some kinda frog oughta hop out of it,” Tsuruoka-san said.
“What’re you talkin’ about, Tsuru-san? It’s a pretty well-known shop,” Takizawa-san answered.
As I listened to them go back and forth, my eyes drifted almost on their own toward the space diagonally ahead of me.
Mari-senpai was in the middle of taking off her mint-green jacket and stowing it in her pack.
Once she had it off, her white arm covers and pale pink T-shirt came into view, the fabric clinging slightly where it had absorbed sweat, hinting at the faint outline of the bra beneath.
—Crap. If she catches me staring, who knows what she’ll say…
I hurriedly looked away, my heart giving a quick, nervous flutter.
Are those arm covers for sun protection? Don’t they make her hotter? I tried to steer my thoughts elsewhere.
“But hey, Sho, those pants of yours are awfully plain. No-brand?”
Suddenly Tsuruoka-san called out to me.
—Did he notice I was looking at Mari-senpai? The thought made me uneasy.
“Uh, um… I think they’re just ones I bought at Uniqlo...” I answered.
“Uniqlo? What, you plannin’ to become some kind of mountain fashion leader?” Tsuruoka-san said.
Everyone laughed, and a warm, easy mood spread through the group.

“Alright then, let’s get movin’ again,” Takizawa-san called as he swung his pack back onto his shoulders, and everyone began adjusting their gear before setting off once more.
—Chatting about nothing important like this, laughing, getting a little flustered, and then climbing again.
Somehow, just that alone makes my heart feel a little lighter.
I thought that as I started walking again myself.
Everyone began climbing the staircase carved into the slope with wooden planks holding back the soil.
Takizawa-san took the lead, followed by Mari-senpai, then Yabe-san, and I fell into step behind them.
I found myself thinking how surprising it was that even such a short rest could make things feel so much easier.
Up one step, walk a few paces, then climb the next… over and over again.
Behind me, the little cowbell hanging from Yohei’s pack began to ring out with a bright clink-clink.
—Are there really bears in a mountain like this? I wondered suddenly.
From farther back came the tapping sound of Tsuruoka-san’s trekking pole striking the ground.
For some reason, it made me feel as though I were being driven forward from behind.
I can’t fall behind.
I stayed close behind Yabe-san as I walked.
Right in front of me, the black pack on Yabe-san’s back, covered in mesh, swayed with each step.
The magnificent ridgeline of Mount Shirouma towering ahead somehow felt as if it were easing my fatigue.
But the sound of Yohei’s cowbell and the steady tap of Tsuruoka-san’s pole kept catching at my nerves.
It really did feel as if something were urging me onward from behind.
That same feeling had come over me once before, when Tsuruoka-san had tried to draw me into his religion.

That sound brought back the memory of when Tsuruoka-san had invited me before.
It happened when the two of us were alone during lunch break. Even though I hadn’t wanted him touching me, he’d slung an arm around my shoulders and said something like, Hey, how about going to Toyota Stadium this Sunday? About fifty thousand people gather there and sing all at once.
The energy’s incredible. C’mon, let’s go.
Even now, I could still remember the exact words he’d used.
“Oy, Sho! How ’bout Toyota Stadium this Sunday? Gonna be like fifty thousand people there, all singin’ together. The atmosphere’s outta this world. C’mon, let’s go… hey, c’mon, go with me!”
Around the company, there were quiet rumors that Tsuruoka-san was a follower of Banri-kyo, and I’d heard those whispers myself.
I knew a few people at work who belonged to Banri-kyo too, and somehow they all felt a little different from ordinary people—many of them seemed narrow in perspective, self-centered, and stubborn, as if the whole world revolved around themselves.
The image I had of Tsuruoka-san was no exception.

At the company where I worked, there were supposedly quite a few followers of Banri-kyo, but I’d also heard that some of them kept their faith hidden, which meant there was no real way of knowing who belonged to the sect and who didn’t.
I’d often heard stories about someone approaching in a friendly way, only for it to turn out later that they were a follower, and once you’d grown close, refusing their invitation became painfully difficult.
Mari-senpai had always been kind to me, so what if she was one too…?
The moment that thought crossed my mind, I found myself imagining Mari-senpai suddenly changing, trying to persuade me into Banri-kyo, and the image alone sent a chill through me.
If, just hypothetically, Mari-senpai really were a follower of Banri-kyo and tried to recruit me…
Would I even be able to say no?
If Mari-senpai asked me gently, could I really bring myself to refuse?
…No. Not Mari-senpai. She’d never do something like that.
Even imagining such a thing made me feel guilty.


Banri-kyo was a new religious movement that fused Christianity and Buddhism together.
At first glance, those two faiths might seem unrelated, but the sect taught that they shared the common concepts of heaven and hell.
It claimed that the Buddha was a messenger of the Lord, identified Jehovah with Maitreya, and even went so far as to say that the Amida in *Namu Amida Butsu* referred not to the Buddha at all, but to Amminadab—the man said to have kept the Ark of the Covenant, like the one that appeared in *Indiana Jones*.
Its followers carried prayer beads and chanted *Namu Amminadab* as part of their devotion.
I’d heard somewhere that *namu* supposedly meant “I devote myself,” but that in Sanskrit, *nam* could also refer to a name.
So, in that sense, maybe *Namu Amminadab* was basically the same as repeating, *Amminadab-sama, Amminadab-sama* over and over.


There were ugly rumors surrounding Banri-kyo.
People said the way it gathered money was ruthless, that if someone in a family fell ill, followers would surround the house and pray, and that anyone who left the sect could end up socially isolated—or even find human waste scattered in front of their home.
There were even darker stories, the kind you couldn’t tell whether they were lies or truth: whispers that a city council member involved in helping victims who had lost their fortunes through offerings had been murdered through yakuza connections.
On top of that, there were rumors that Banri-kyo was deeply entangled with the ruling political party.
Some even claimed that vast sums collected through spiritual sales and other schemes were being funneled, through channels never spoken of publicly, to a nation cast as an enemy.
What made it even stranger was the rumor that this so-called enemy nation always seemed to launch missiles at the exact moments when the ruling party’s approval ratings began to fall—and immediately afterward, those ratings would recover.
But no matter how I thought about it, that made no sense.
If it were truly an enemy state, the weakening of the other country’s government should be the perfect opportunity.
There would be no reason to deliberately take actions that helped restore its support.
Seen that way, even the very relationship of “hostility” itself began to feel like some kind of manufactured arrangement.
As if fear was being stirred up, conflict staged, and behind it all someone was profiting from the mechanism.
That line of thought brought back something my grandfather had once said: that international relations were like professional wrestling, with the script already decided in advance so that someone, somewhere, would come out ahead.

Maybe that was why I felt so strongly that I never wanted anything to do with Banri-kyo.
To begin with, I didn’t believe in gods or anything like that.
Religion was something people in the distant past had come up with, nothing more.
I had never heard the voice of any god.
Some people claimed they could hear such voices, while I couldn’t.
If that were true, then it would mean that God was unfair—unequal.
And if God were unequal, then that would make such a being ruled by pride and selfishness.
But there were people who could restrain their own pride and selfishness and live in harmony with those around them—
which would make them, in a way, greater than any god.
There was no reason to worship something inferior to human beings.
That was how I saw it.
And yet, whenever someone pressed me strongly, I could never quite bring myself to refuse.
Even then, as much as I wanted to say no, the part of me that rejected it and the part that feared what might happen if I didn’t accept—*Would I be punished? Would it be safer to go along with it?*—fought inside my chest.
“Senpai… I’m sorry… I really can’t… it’s not for me… I’m sorry…”
I remember repeating myself over and over, my voice on the verge of breaking, until I finally managed to refuse.
Perhaps Tsuruoka-san understood that pushing too hard might only provoke resentment from those outside the sect, because after that he stopped pressing me any further.
Even so, whenever election season came around, he would still tell me to vote for the candidates supported by the group.
Being around Tsuruoka-san always carried that kind of tension, leaving me with a vague, lingering sense of unease.

Once we cleared the staircase reinforced with wooden planks, the ground beneath our feet changed completely.
Rocks of every size lay scattered irregularly between the grass on both sides, rolling loosely underfoot.
It was the kind of path where even the smallest stone would shift when stepped on, making every stride awkward.
I moved forward carefully, checking each step one by one.
The instability beneath my boots forced me to spend constant energy keeping my balance, but the majestic form of Mount Sirouma towering before me soothed my mind.
Its summit, where the lingering snowfields glittered against the blue sky, seemed to fill my tired heart with a quiet kind of strength.
Then, from somewhere behind me, a light *clink-clink* began to draw closer.
The cowbell hanging from Yohei’s waist rang out with every step, as if marking a rhythm from behind.
Then came the *tonk-tonk* of Tsuruoka-san’s pole striking the rocks, and together the sounds created the strange sensation that the two of them were somehow driving me onward.
The ringing of Yohei’s bell and the tapping of Tsuruoka-san’s trekking pole were both rhythmic, yet their patterns never stayed perfectly aligned—they slipped out of sync, then faintly overtook one another.
At times, the *clink* and the *tap* landed together with the perfect timing of the alternating beats in a mochi-pounding rhythm, while at others they suddenly fell into the offbeat, throwing off my sense of pace as if the trail itself had lost its step.
Even as I became aware of the odd tension lurking inside those sounds, the vastness of the wilderness before me gave my heart a small place to rest.

The unease I felt around Tsuruoka-san wasn’t only because of the religion.
There had been something else too.
Something like this had happened once.
At the workshop, Tsuruoka-san had accidentally kicked over a can, spilling machine oil all across the floor, and with a casual *“Sorry, sorry—do me a favor, will ya?”* he’d left me to clean it up.
The humiliation of that moment still remained inside me even now.
Even now, the memory came back with painful clarity.
The black machine oil gleaming across the floor.
The sticky sensation of it clinging to the soles of my boots had felt as though it were dragging me down into the cold concrete itself.
“Sorry, sorry. Help me out here.” Tsuruoka-san’s light, easy voice.
The faintly condescending smile folded into those words settled heavily in the pit of my chest.
—Again?
I thought bitterly to myself.
Quiet, unnoticed me, once again cast as the one cleaning up after someone else.
Humiliation, resentment, and self-loathing swirled together inside me.
Amid the murmur of the people around us and the relentless noise of the machines, my thoughts sank into darkness.
—Damn it, I can’t keep being like this… but what the hell am I supposed to do?
I bent down, crouching low despite the strain in my back, and wiped the floor with a rag.
When that was done, I soaked the rag in cleaning gasoline and wiped it down again.
The smell of gasoline and machine oil clung to the back of my nose and throat as if it would never leave.

What fed my sense of inferiority wasn’t just my inability to say what I really thought—it was the major mistakes I’d made at work.
Even now, three of them still came back to me.
Two of those involved Tsuruoka-san.
The first was when, in my very first assignment, I got the delivery date wrong by a full day, delaying the shipment to the client.
My supervisor told me he’d cover for me, but the client flew into a rage, and Tsuruoka-san—who had to deal with them—ended up lashing out at me, and I was publicly called out in an internal meeting.
The second time, it had actually been Tsuruoka-san who made the operational mistake, but since I was standing nearby, I found myself saying that I’d been the one who checked it last, and I ended up taking the blame for him.
“Sorry about that,” he’d said, even thanking me, but in the end, all the responsibility fell on me.
The third mistake didn’t involve Tsuruoka-san, but it was worse in a different way—
a junior I’d been training ended up suffering a minor electric shock because I’d been too slow to call out a warning.
The company said it was an issue with the system, but ever since then, I’d grown afraid of dealing with people at all.
Part of me still wanted to be useful, to make up for things, to start over—
but those thoughts were always drowned out by the feeling that I couldn’t do anything right, that my judgment was flawed, that my relationships never worked out, that no matter what I did, it would only end in failure.

Large rocks lay scattered among the grass, making the footing unstable again.
Each time the soles of my hiking boots struck stone, they gave off a dry *click*, throwing off my rhythm.
Behind me, Yohei’s cowbell rang out in bright *clink-clink*s, while Tsuruoka-san’s trekking pole tapped the ground with a steady *tok, tok*.
The two sounds pressed at my ears as if urging me onward, filling me with a restless impatience.
Up ahead, Takizawa-san was struggling to find secure footing in front of a massive boulder embedded in the trail.
Mari-senpai and Yabe-san caught up, and I joined the line behind them.
Getting stopped just when I’d found a good pace made the climb feel even more tiring.
One by one, we took our turn climbing over the rock.
After Takizawa-san, Mari-senpai climbed up and moved on, then Yabe-san in front of me began making her way over it.
And then me…
My fingertips scraped against the rough stone surface as I gripped it, and I pulled myself upward with all the strength in my arms.
“Whew…”
The breath slipped out before I knew it.
By the time I got over the rock, there was already a gap between me and Yabe-san ahead.
—It’ll be bad if I get left behind.
Panic tightened in my chest, and I hurried to quicken my pace.
I pushed myself to catch up to Yabe-san, then stayed close behind her, almost glued to her back so I wouldn’t be left behind again.
The trail still stretched endlessly on.
When we came to a small rocky step, Yabe-san, right in front of me, hesitated for just a moment as she worked her way over it, and I had no choice but to stop short, nearly stumbling into her.
I climbed over the step right after her, but by then she was already moving ahead again, and the gap between us had opened once more.
“Ah…”
The breath slipped from me before I could stop it.
Being halted so suddenly, then forced to hurry and catch up again, over and over, was slowly draining my strength.
Behind me, the *clink-clink* and *tok-tok* still followed relentlessly.
Inside that inescapable rhythm of sound, all I could do was keep climbing without stopping, trying to catch up to Yabe-san ahead.
*Good, I caught up.*
I had just settled into walking close behind her again when it happened.
Yabe-san suddenly turned around.
“Too close,” she said.
—W-what the hell, that’s scary.
The words made my heart jolt.
Had I really been walking *that* close?
Was I not supposed to close the distance that much?
“S-sorry,” I blurted out in a panic.
I quickly put some space between us, but inside, a small spark of irritation flared.
She didn’t have to complain like that.
Everyone was tired. I thought she could’ve had a little more patience.
What a stuck-up woman…
I was only following because I didn’t want to fall behind…
While those thoughts swirled in my head, Yabe-san, still facing forward, muttered quietly,
“There’s no need to apologize.”
I couldn’t make sense of it, and the confusion only deepened.
Why had she sounded angry at first?
Did I do something weird?
Maybe my footsteps had made her feel like she was being driven from behind.
…I thought she was on my side after seeing her push back at Tsuruoka-san…
Yabe-san really did have something about her that made her hard to approach.
Even so, she was strange—there was no denying that.
She rarely spoke as it was, and moments like this only made me think about her more.

Yabe-san worked in my company’s overseas business division, and she was probably about the same age as Mari-senpai—maybe even a little younger.
Her hair was glossy black, cut in a boyish short style, and with her slender frame and upright posture, she gave the impression of someone completely at home in the mountains.
At the company, she was known as an eccentric, the kind of woman people said could outwork most men, but she was also quiet and carried an air that made her hard to approach.
I had hardly spoken with Yabe-san—no, more than that, I’d actually never really talked with her at all before this.
So the truth was, I didn’t really know what kind of person she was.
Still, because I’d heard she’d even pushed back against Tsuruoka-san, somewhere in my heart I’d started to see her as a dependable ally.
But from Yabe-san’s point of view, it seemed even I might be nothing more than a nuisance.

The trail remained a narrow path scattered with rough white stones winding between pale green grass.
The slope still climbed at a steady angle.
My calves and thighs were really starting to ache.
Each time my pack shifted, the weight digging into my shoulders seemed to build little by little.
And yet, once I started leaving a bit more space between us, the climb strangely became easier.
Before, I’d been right on Yabe-san’s heels, so every time she stopped in front of a larger rock, I’d have to stop abruptly too.
But now, even when she paused to step up over a bigger stone, I had enough room that I didn’t need to stop at all.
—Did Yabe-san maybe tell me that for this reason?
No, no. She could’ve said it in a nicer way than that.
Maybe she really had been annoyed after all.
Thinking that, I kept following behind her.
Now that I’d put some distance between us, instead of climbing with my eyes fixed on her pack like before, my gaze naturally fell lower.
Just ahead of me, the movement of Yabe-san’s hips rose and shifted with each step.
Every time her legs, wrapped in those charcoal pants with their muted sheen, moved forward in turn, the rounded lines beneath the fabric subtly shifted from side to side, the cloth tightening and loosening, sometimes emphasizing the curve, sometimes hiding it again.
For someone so slender, the shape was unexpectedly firm and rounded.
—Whoa, what the hell am I even thinking?
I shook my head in a hurry.
No, no, don’t think about stuff like that…
I told myself firmly.

As I found myself thinking about those rumors—that Banri-kyo might even use organized crime—I was reminded of a conversation I’d once had with my grandfather.
At dusk, I had been staring out the living room window, trying to sort through the haze in my thoughts.
Stories about organized crime I’d heard at school, incidents involving delinquent youths I’d seen in the news, the behind-the-scenes dealings of politicians and corporations mentioned on television… one question after another had begun to surface.
—If groups like that don’t exist just to do bad things, then why have they been around for so long…?
Is there some kind of connection between people struggling to get by, delinquency, and organized crime…?
And why would politicians or corporations make use of organizations like that…?
Those were the kinds of things I’d been turning over in my mind.

Letting out an unconscious breath, I made my way over to my grandfather.
“Grandpa, there’s something I want to ask you. Why do groups like organized crime even exist? They’re not *just* there to do bad things, right?” I asked.
“Oh, that’s a good question, Sho,” my grandfather said.
“Well, you see, organizations like that haven’t always existed simply for wrongdoing.
Back in the Edo period, there were groups that helped ordinary people protect themselves and support each other when trouble came up.
They were almost like handymen for the community.”
He spoke with a small smile.
“I see… so a long time ago they were kind of like community fixers…
But is it also true that people who have a hard life, or who grow up without enough love at home, can end up drifting into delinquency?” I asked after thinking for a moment.
“Yeah, that happens a lot,” he said with a slow nod.
“When life gets difficult, people can end up lacking love and a sense of safety.
And when that happens, some young people start drifting into trouble.
Organizations like those pull kids like that in and end up filling the cracks left by society.
What they do may be wrong, but sometimes it’s the structure of society itself that pushes things in that direction.”
“But Grandpa, why would politicians or companies ever make use of groups like that?” I asked, tilting my head.
“That’s where it gets even more complicated,” he replied.
“Politicians and corporations sometimes use those organizations behind the scenes for competition or vested interests.
They might rely on them for help during elections, or to gain an edge over rivals.
That’s why it’s not just that organized crime itself is bad—it survives by getting tangled up in the cracks of society and in people’s interests.”
He folded his hands, his expression turning more serious.
“I get it… so it’s not as simple as them just being evil. They’ve ended up taking on a role inside the way society works…
Yeah… that’s kind of complicated.”
Even as the thought surprised me, I felt like I could somehow understand what he meant.

At that moment,
a scene from school suddenly came back to me.
People who had shattered classroom windows,
showing off their strength.
People who were always shouting,
living as if they had to threaten someone just to exist.
The teachers had called them “dangerous,” but
when I thought about it now,
maybe those people…
had simply never been protected by anyone.
People who hadn’t received love from their parents,
who had no place where they could feel safe,
who had no choice but to protect themselves.
People who couldn’t survive unless they appeared strong.
People like that gathering together,
forming organizations like organized crime.
Society,
uses them when it’s convenient.
But most of the time,
treats them like something unwanted.
And yet,
being treated as unwanted
is exactly what sustains their role.
By being feared,
hated,
and pushed away,
they become the ones who take on the “work in the shadows.”
“But you know, Sho…
even those delinquents don’t have the courage to stand up to the yakuza,
and the yakuza themselves…
when it comes to something bigger—
power…
that’s not something they can easily defy.”

When really,
the people who need human warmth the most
are the very ones pushed farthest away from others,
and before they even realize it,
they end up helping create
the very kind of loveless society
that once shut them out.
That struck me as
something deeply ironic.
It isn’t that the world turns bad simply because there are bad people.
It’s the mechanism that makes people turn bad
that keeps creating new villains.
And in that way,
spinning round and round,
the world itself
might slowly,
little by little,
be changing into something colder.

“That’s right. The world isn’t so simple.
…Funny thing is, people can make use of something themselves,
and still feel uneasy about it at the same time.
It’s the same with politicians and corporations—
they rely on groups like that because they’re convenient,
but on the surface, they still see them as something bad.
The human heart can carry contradictions like that and keep moving forward anyway.
And among the people inside those organizations,
there are some who, deep down, think, *‘I’m causing trouble for society…’*
They’re not so different from us—just people, with human feelings.
Even when they do wrong, it’s not always born from pure malice.”
My grandfather said this with a gentle smile.
“I see… I guess everyone really is carrying their own circumstances and feelings,” I said, returning a small smile.
“That’s exactly it, Sho. Wrongdoing, necessity, vested interests, and inner conflict—they all get tangled together.
Even just trying to understand that can change the way you see the world.”
He nodded kindly as he spoke.
“Yeah… thanks, Grandpa. I feel like things make a little more sense now,” I said with a faint smile.

“Also… this is something I heard at school, and it was kind of hard to believe…
is it true that the entertainment industry has deep ties with organized crime?” I asked.
“Sho, these days the entertainment world is run by TV networks and agencies, right?
But it wasn’t always like that.
There was a time when the yakuza themselves were the ones managing entertainment.
To run traveling shows—plays, sumo, music tours—you needed people who could secure venues, gather crowds, and handle trouble when it came up.
So the closeness between entertainment and the underworld wasn’t anything unusual.
It just happened that way over time.”
“It’s no surprise, then, that the same people who’ve been moving money and crowds in that world would later become involved with politics and corporations.
Entertainment, the underworld, politics… in the end, they’re all connected somewhere by the same mechanism—gathering people and moving money.
…And Sho, there’s another reason why entertainers and athletes often become politicians.
People like that already have their faces and names known to the public, right?
In elections, the hardest part is simply getting people to know you.
So for political parties, someone who’s already well-known is easy to use as a figure to gather votes.”
“So… they get chosen because they’re famous?” I asked.
“That’s part of it.
Before whether they’re smart or not,
sometimes what matters is simply whether they look like they can win.
Politics, in the end, is a world of competition too.
Trying to use pieces that can win…
in that sense, it’s not so different from business,” my grandfather replied.

“Sho, the world may look like everything is moving separately,
but more often than not, it’s all connected.”
My grandfather continued.
“Politics, corporations, television, celebrities, the yakuza, organized crime…
they all seem like different worlds, don’t they?
But through the flow of money and the ties between people,
they often end up facing the same direction.”
“And when that happens…
it’s not so much that someone is deliberately lying.
It’s that everyone starts believing the *same story*.”
“The people who believe it don’t even think they’re deceiving anyone.
But when only one story is all you ever hear,
it begins to feel like the truth.”
“So, Sho…
the scariest thing in this world
might not be bad people at all,
but the moment people stop questioning.”
“So that means everyone’s being deceived?” I asked.
“Rather than being deceived…
it’s that people become *so connected they can no longer see*.
The world outside the frame, I mean.”
“So remember this—
never believe only one story.
Listen properly to different ones too.
That’s the surest way not to be fooled.”
