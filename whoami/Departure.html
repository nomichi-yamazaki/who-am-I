Departure

“Alright—let’s take it easy today. No pushing too hard.”
Takizawa-san’s voice was calm, but carried a firm core as it drifted up into the open sky.

At that single remark, the air around us tightened for a brief moment.
A few seconds of silence followed.

Without thinking, I tightened my grip on the straps of my pack.

Takizawa, at the front, began to move slowly toward the left side of Sarukura-so.

Crunch. Crunch.
The sound of his boots pressing into the gravel broke the silence.
His steps were steady—without hesitation.

Each time he checked his compass, it gave me a quiet sense of reassurance.

Right behind him, Mari-senpai followed with light, easy steps.
Crunch, crunch, crunch…
As her footsteps blended with his, the rhythm softened slightly.

The brim of her hat swayed, catching the morning light for just a moment.
The Gregory logo on her pack shifted gently from side to side—I could almost hear the rhythm of her breathing.

Third was Yabe-san.

She said nothing, her hood pulled low over her face—yet something about her presence made it hard to look away.
Her feet brushed through the grass, her black boots pushing quietly against the ground.

Crunch…
Crunch…

Then—me.

I took a step forward.
The sole of my boot met the ground as if it were sticking to it, my weight settling slowly into place.
My pack shifted slightly on my back, and in the cold air, my own breath seemed to linger around me.

Crunch. Crunch. Crunch. Crunch…

The ground still held traces of morning mist, and each step through the grass made a faint, damp sound.

Ahead of me, the backs of my companions stretched into a loose line.

“Alright, let’s go! Let’s go!” Yohei’s bright voice rang out just behind me.

A small bear bell rattled cheerfully, softening the air just a little.

Crunch, crunch, crunch…
Even as he kicked pebbles along the way, his steps remained light.

At the very back was Tsuruoka-san.
The steady tap of his trekking pole striking the ground lingered in my ears.

Even without turning around, I knew he was there.
…And somehow, that felt a little reassuring.

And so, the six of us began our climb of Mt. Shirouma.


Takizawa-san stepped onto the path beside Sarukura-sanso.

A weathered white arrow-shaped sign, tied to a tree on the left side of the lodge, caught my eye.
In black letters, it read: “Mt. Shirouma Trailhead.” Below that: “Hakuba Daisekkei – approx. 1 hour.” A red line was drawn beneath it.

Takizawa, carrying a large black backpack and wearing a dark gray jacket that reflected the light dully…
Mari-senpai, with a purple pack and a mint-colored jacket…
Yabe-san, with a minimal black pack and subdued black-and-gray clothing that seemed to wrap around her entirely…

I followed them as they turned in beside the lodge.

The sunlight was blocked by the building, and the area grew dim.

A flat path covered in gravel stretched ahead.

To my right, Sarukura-sanso stood solid and unmoving.

Dark brown planks were laid horizontally, giving it the familiar face of a mountain lodge.

Beneath a small lean-to attached to its side, pale gray propane tanks stood in a row.

Their rugged appearance seemed to quietly speak of the preparations needed to survive in the mountains.

Near the end of the lodge, I could see two signs standing side by side on the left.

The one in front was a low, horizontal board made of dark brown wood.

The one behind it was white—also horizontal, but taller, supported by two posts.

Beyond them, the trail stretched out silently.

The path bore clear signs of human hands.

Where the ground rose unevenly, square-cut wooden beams had been laid across and embedded into the soil, forming rough steps.

The wood had darkened with age, and small stones scattered for traction caught the morning light with a faint, dull gleam.

It didn’t feel like a trail so much as a path inviting us in.

At the front, Takizawa-san adjusted his GPS as he passed beside the signs and began to climb the gentle slope.

The two signs ahead on the left slowly drew closer.

The lower, dark brown sign in front marked the Sarukura trailhead.
Across the top, a green band stretched almost the full width, with the words “Sarukura Trailhead” written in white.
Below it, in a white section, red letters warned: “Proper climbing equipment is required beyond this point.”
Beneath that were additional notes in white text, along with what appeared to be translations in English, Korean, and Chinese.

The taller white sign behind it read “Northern Alps Wildlife Protection Area Map” in black letters.
Below it was a map, with the protected area outlined in red.

Birdsong echoed from all around.
Short, sharp chirps from high branches…
Low, hidden calls from deep within the brush.

It should have sounded lively—yet strangely, none of it lingered in my ears, as if the forest itself were absorbing the sound.

As we climbed, the slope gradually grew steeper, and the beech forest began to close in around us like a wall.

Loose stones scattered underfoot, making each step unstable.

At times, tree roots jutted sideways across the trail, blocking the path ahead.

The sky was swallowed by overlapping branches, and the light filtering through the trees was faint.
Morning dew still clung to the ground beneath our feet.

On both sides of the trail, multiple faint paths branched off, as if avoiding broken branches and patches of mud.
But they always converged back into a single trail, which was gradually swallowed by the depths of the forest.

—But this is supposed to be a company hiking club, right…?
I always thought hiking was something more casual… but this feels like a full-on mountain climb.
The complaint drifted through my mind.

“Saku-chan, you gotta take small sips of water regularly, alright?
That’s the best way to avoid heatstroke.
Back in the day, they used to tell us not to drink at all—but now it’s the opposite. They keep telling you to drink, drink, drink.”

Had my frustration shown on my face?
Takizawa-san, walking ahead, glanced back over his shoulder as he spoke.

“Really? You weren’t supposed to drink water back then?” I asked, looking up.

“Yeah.
Sweating? Endure it.
Thirsty? Endure it.
That was the kind of time we lived in.
Looking back now… it was pretty ridiculous.”
He said it as he continued up the trail at an unhurried pace.

“My dad told me the same thing.
He said even in the middle of summer, they’d get yelled at if they drank water during practice.
…And things like wall sits and bunny hops—they had to do them every day.
Even though they’re banned now.”
Mari-senpai said this while pulling a plastic bottle from the side of her pack, just ahead of Yabe-san.

“…It must’ve been nothing but unreasonable rules… back then…”
Yabe-san murmured, still facing forward as she climbed.

“Exactly.
Back then, it was all about guts and grit.
Kids these days can actually learn the logic behind things—makes it a better time, if you ask me.”
Farther up the trail, Takizawa said this without turning around, only his head angled slightly to the side.

“My middle school was pretty strict too… but hearing stuff like that, I guess it wasn’t so bad after all,” I said.
I said it to go along with everyone—but that kind of harshness wasn’t what I had experienced.

It wasn’t the strict training or anything like that…
It was the bullying.

I never even belonged to a particularly strict club.

My parents were never especially harsh with me, either.


I was born to a father from Aomori and a mother from Fujisawa, Kanagawa.
When I was a child, we moved from Yokohama to Nagoya because of my father’s job.
It was a big change for me—but aside from that, I’d grown up without much hardship, living comfortably in a proper home, morning and night.
No one ever said that was a bad thing, but…

I guess I’ve never really struggled with anything.

Maybe that’s why I hesitate over the smallest things… why I get scared so easily… why my feet just stop.

When I get tired, I immediately want to stop walking.
The moment I feel my pack is heavy, I start thinking I can’t do this.

Even though I’m walking with everyone else, it feels like I’m the only one drifting… like I’m falling behind somehow.
That feeling slowly spread through my chest.

And yet, sometimes, when I’m at work, I find myself wondering—

Working long hours for low pay… coming home exhausted… only to repeat the same thing the next day.

In a world where that’s called “normal”… what are people really living for?

It’s not just the company.

When I look around at society as a whole, it’s the same everywhere.

Things no one even knows are truly necessary are pushed onto people just to be sold.
Forests are cut down, the environment is damaged—
and at those very sites, low wages and long working hours are treated as the norm.

Living in a society like that…
maybe it’s only natural that people stop having the space to think about marriage or children.

Before we can even talk about the future, just getting through today takes everything we have.

And yet… I’ve been fortunate.

Even so, I still feel this tightness in my chest.

—Then what about the people who are forced to keep working like this?
How heavy must the weight they carry be?

“These days, they talk about having the courage not to push yourself… about teaching people not to overdo it.
Back then, they probably would’ve just called it being soft.”
Mari-senpai said as she walked along the trail.

—She’s right… I really am soft…

I didn’t say it out loud.
Instead, I lowered my gaze to my feet and took a slow breath to steady myself.

I work at Sansei Electronics Seiki Co., Ltd., in Midori Ward, Nagoya, Aichi Prefecture.
It’s been six years since I was assigned to the electrical maintenance department.

I’ve mostly gotten the hang of handling on-site issues and starting up machinery.

But when it comes to relationships at work… and the distance between me and the senior staff, I still feel tense.

Especially when it comes to Tsuruoka-san—I have complicated feelings about him.

Once, during a lunch break, he started talking to me about religion with an intense, almost feverish look in his eyes.
To be honest… it scared me a little.

Since then, I’ve found myself avoiding him without really meaning to.
And in the past, I’ve made three major mistakes—two of them involved him.

About him… honestly, he scares me a bit.
…No, not just a bit. I’m really uncomfortable around him.

In the middle of all that, Takizawa-san half-pushed me into it—“Hey, Saku-chan, why don’t you join us?”
And Mari-senpai added, “It’ll be a good way to refresh.”
That’s how I ended up joining this climb.

To be honest, part of me just went along with it, swept up by the atmosphere at work.

But with Yohei and Mari-senpai there… I figured somehow it would work out.
At least, that’s what I told myself.

…Still, the truth is, I’m just tagging along.

I don’t really have the route memorized, and it’s not like I’ve studied the map properly.

If I remember right, we start from Sarukura and climb up to the Hakuba Grand Lodge for the first night.
The next day, we traverse from Mt. Shirouma to Mt. Hakubayari, then stay at Yarionsen Lodge.
After that, we descend and head home… that was the plan, I think.

Things like course times… I have no idea.

—Can I really make it all the way to the summit?

When I heard “hiking,” I thought it’d be something like walking up a small hill with a rice ball in hand.
…Not a full-scale mountain climb like this.

Even so, there’s no turning back now.

Dry leaves underfoot crunched—zaku… zaku…

The ground was still firm, and the soles of my boots bit into it solidly.

Gripping the straps of my backpack tightly, I looked up at the backs of the group walking ahead of me.

—I’ll be fine. I’m just here for fun.

I muttered that to myself, then ran my finger along the space between my brows.

My slightly damp fingertips brushed against my cool forehead and slid across it.

Since I was a child—
ever since the moment I was born—I’ve never been able to accept the idea that “this is just how the world is.”

Isn’t that something someone else came up with?
If that’s the case, then I should have the right to think for myself… to create something of my own.
That’s how I’ve tried to live.

My name is Sho Sakuma.
I’m 26 years old, working in the electrical maintenance department.

I’m 169 centimeters tall, with an average build.
If you want to put it positively, I’m “ordinary.” If you want to put it bluntly… I’m probably unremarkable.
At the company, I’m not the type who stands out. Or rather… I don’t want to stand out.

I’ve been like this for as long as I can remember.
Even back in the industrial high school I graduated from in Aichi’s coastal area, whenever I tried to say something, my mouth would often freeze, unable to move.

During the time I was at school, the windows were often broken.

The ones who broke the glass were always the so-called troublemakers.
Back then, everyone seemed to find those kids frightening—and I was no exception.
But somewhere in the back of my mind, I found myself thinking:
if someone is truly strong, do they really need to show it off like that?

The troublemakers around me would say things like “I don’t even care about you,” and never even look my way.
Meanwhile, the students who were afraid of being targeted by them would use me as a shield to bully others, trying to show off their own strength.

But bullying someone like me only amounted to picking on the weak—it didn’t prove strength at all…

I still remember, every now and then, being laughed at and told,
“Hey, why do you talk like that?”

The backpack I’m carrying is a Millet one I found by searching online for “beginner recommended hiking gear.”

It’s navy blue.
Somehow, I thought it was a safe, reasonable choice.

I also bought my fleece from Montbell, but when I put the whole outfit together, the colors and balance felt a bit off. I stood in front of the mirror for a while, unsure.

Yohei, who walks behind me, casually says, “Looks good, doesn’t it?”—but I can’t tell whether he really means it or not.

Up ahead of Yabe-san is Mari-senpai, someone who has always been kind to me, but I just can’t seem to make eye contact with her.

She’s so kind, and yet why is it that I always end up unable to say anything?
The words, “Senpai, did you change your gloves?”—that one simple sentence—keeps circling endlessly in the back of my throat.

…No, it’s more like… I end up like this whenever I’m in front of Mari-senpai. Even I don’t understand myself. I’m left regretting the things I blurted out without thinking.

No one is listening, yet I feel embarrassed and quietly grip the strap of my shoulder again.

There is no wind. Soft morning light filters through the trees.
The backs of the three people walking ahead feel close, and yet distant.
It felt as if I alone belonged to an entirely different group of people.

This kind of feeling isn’t something I’ve experienced for the first time.

When I was in elementary school, I was what you might call a target of bullying.
I still don’t really understand the reason even now.

It was things like the way I spoke, or where I happened to be standing—
minor differences like those were enough for others to tease me or laugh at me.

Ever since then, I developed a habit of asking myself why.
Why do people go out of their way to put someone else down?
Why do they fight, argue, and end up hating one another?

Wouldn’t it be far easier if everyone just got along?

Perhaps because I’ve lived while holding on to that thought,
I’ve become unusually good at gauging the distance between myself and others.

“Sho, how is it? Feels good, doesn’t it? In the greenery there’s something called piton chips… If you’re exposed to piton chips, it’s supposed to cure even a cold.”

Tsuruga-san, from behind, spoke as if he knew—or maybe didn’t know—that I was keeping my distance.

—Piton chips… that sounds kind of like potato chips.
I thought to myself. I still didn’t really understand what “feels good” was supposed to mean.

“Yes… it feels good,” I replied, forcing my answer to go against what I truly felt.

“Phytoncide…” came Yabe-san’s quiet voice from up ahead.

—Huh? So it’s not “piton chips,” but “phytoncide”? I wondered.

Tsuruga-san continued from behind, “the other day, someone who had lost consciousness in the mountains came to again.”

“Did the piton chips or whatever help him wake up, then?” Yohei, walking behind me, asked Tsuruga-san. From his tone, I could tell he had turned around while speaking.

“No, he just regained consciousness because he happened to fall.”
Tsuruga-san replied from behind.

“You were talking about phytoncide, weren’t you…”
Without turning around, Yabe-san, walking in front, interjected. Hearing that, I almost laughed.

—Is Tsuruga-san serious about this?
Or is he saying it on purpose to make people laugh?

…Still, Yabe-san’s retorts are sharp, like a laser beam cutting through everything.
…Yabe Laser… I’ll start calling her that in my head.

But it’s a good position I’ve ended up in—able to hear both the conversation behind me and the one in front.

“I guess he fell, got covered in piton chips, and that’s what woke him up, huh.”
Yohei said from behind me, and Mari-senpai and Yabe-san in front let out a laugh.

“No, piton chips have nothing to do with it.”
Tsuruga-san said.

“You were talking about piton chips and all that, weren’t you.”
Yabe-san added one more time, delivering the final blow.

—Sharp. Yabe Laser…
Trying not to burst out laughing, that’s what I found myself thinking.
