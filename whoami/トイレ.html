Toilet

For a moment, I thought, Oh right… I should use the restroom before heading back to the room.
My pace slowed slightly as I sorted through my thoughts, walking a bit more leisurely.
Yeah… I’ll go to the restroom, then head back.
“I’m gonna hit the restroom,” I said.
Yohei paused for a beat, then grinned. “Well then… guess I’ll go too!”
The corridor was wide, divided down the middle by a row of square wooden pillars.
If you went straight along the left side of the pillars, it led toward the stairs.
If you followed the right side, it connected to the washroom area at the far end.
The others headed left toward the stairs, while Yohei and I went right.
On the wall to our right was a fire hydrant cabinet.
Inside a tall rectangular frame were two compartments—a horizontal one above and a slightly taller vertical one below.
At the top sat a dome-shaped red lamp, and beneath it a round red alarm unit with a transparent circular plate, through which a black push-button switch could be seen.
At the upper right of the lower compartment, a metallic label read FIRE HYDRANT in raised silver letters.
If there’s a hydrant, there must be a fire pump somewhere, I thought.
As we walked on, my eyes were drawn to a hand-drawn map posted on the wall.
It showed hiking trails, mountain huts, even little illustrations of ptarmigans and flowers.
“Hey, there’s a map. Where are we right now? …Oh, here it is. This is where we are,” I said, finding the Hakuba Grand Lodge marked on it.
“Ohh—this is our battlefield!” Yohei said, pointing at Mount Hakuba on the map. “Not a battlefield—a butterfly.” I said, pointing at the small butterfly drawn nearby. I can’t talk this naturally with most people… so why is it so easy with Yohei? I thought as we chatted.
“With this typhoon coming, who knows what’ll happen—but conquering Mount Shakushi and Mount Yari… man, it feels like heavy metal,” Yohei said, standing in front of the map.
“Yeah, we’ll conquer the mountains with our ’80s heavy metal spirit,” I joked. “I’d like to check out Hakuba Oike or Tsugaike someday too—but first, we deal with this weather.”
As I said that, we naturally started walking again toward the washroom area.
Yohei and I walked slowly down the corridor, casually drifting into old memories.
“Oh yeah, I listened to that 80s heavy metal compilation the other day,” I said as we headed toward the washroom.
Our reflections in the glass door ahead grew larger with each step.
“Oh? Which one? Megadeth? Metallica?” Yohei asked.
“No, not those. It had stuff like Skid Row and Twisted Sister. But listening to it now, the lyrics are surprisingly positive. Even when they’re screaming, it kinda pumps you up.”
I slid open the door at the end of the lodge with a rattling sound.
Even though we’d been told to bring warm clothes, the air felt strangely warm—humid, almost suffocating. Maybe it was the approaching storm.
The sound of rain poured down in sheets, mixed with the trickling of water running through the gutters.
Somewhere above, the wind howled faintly.
In the darkness, I could make out the long, low building of the toilets stretching to the left.
The orange light along the passage between the lodge and the building, and the white glow from inside the washroom, spilled out through the glass.
The ground between them dipped slightly, with a wooden ramp leading down.
“Yeah… Back then, riding our scooters through the hills at night, talking about our dreams with those songs blasting,” Yohei said as we stepped onto the ramp.
“You remember saying we’d make it big as a band under that sky full of stars?” I said, glancing back as I pulled the door shut behind me.
I turned and started down the ramp after Yohei.
“Of course I do. Now we’re stuck in sales and maintenance, but yeah… I’ll never forget that feeling.”
Our feet clattered against the wooden slats laid over the concrete.
Each step made the boards rattle softly beneath us.
Off to the left, the door to the drying room loomed faintly in the dim light.
The two guys from earlier—the one in jeans with glasses and the more athletic one—were there, along with the two young women we’d seen in the lounge.
—So they did go talk to them after all…
Figures.
“But hey… what if that thing in the drying room is actually alive?” Yohei said, glancing toward it as he headed for the washroom entrance.
“Well, if it showed up at one of our gigs, it’d definitely get the crowd going,” I said with a laugh, waiting for him to open the door.
“‘The Thing, Sho, and Yohei’s Heavy Metal Show’—sounds like a hit to me!”
Yohei slid the door open with a rattling sound.
A faint heaviness hung in the air, mixed with the scent of deodorizer and old wood.
A father and child who had been cooking in the kitchenette were now at the sink.
Wooden walls, a wooden ceiling, and a slatted floor laid over concrete.
The fluorescent lights above cast a pale glow, and a frosted glass window on the far wall reflected the dim interior, softly revealing the worn tones of the wood and the thick plywood flooring.
Parts of the wooden slats were slightly warped from decay, their edges darkened, carrying a damp smell faintly blended with the deodorizer.
Along the right wall stretched a long wooden sink with a stainless-steel top.
Beneath it, a blue plastic bucket held a gray, worn rag, and a long white plastic water tank rested beside it.
In front of the sink, four tall rectangular mirrors were lined up side by side.
On the narrow ledge below them sat a translucent blue plastic cup holding a toothbrush and a tube of toothpaste, alongside a soap dish with a flesh-colored bar of soap.
The toothbrush and toothpaste probably belonged to the father.
The cup… maybe his too, or maybe it was just there for anyone to use.
The faucet handles were old-fashioned—silver, triangular knobs with rounded edges, each with a small blue screw gleaming at the center.
A sign on the wall read: “Save water.”
The child brushed his teeth while the father watched over him quietly.
On the left were two wooden sliding doors, each with a frosted glass window set in a lattice frame—one near, one farther back.
The nearer door was marked “Men,” the one beyond it “Women.”
The aged wood bore faded colors and scratches, as if telling the story of years of use.
Between the doors hung a large, faded poster of a mountain landscape.
“Hakuba Three Peaks,” it read in red letters, with Mount Yarigatake, Mount Shakushi, and Mount Hakuba labeled beneath in black.
The surface of the poster was rippled with age, its edges frayed and torn in places.

“Then maybe we should ask it to play drums—we don’t have a drummer,” I said to Yohei.
“Yeah… it’s cute, so it’d definitely be a hit,” Yohei replied.
Laughing, we stepped into the washroom and slid open the men’s door with a clatter.
A wave of stale air mixed with deodorizer hit my nose.
Above us, rain hammered loudly against the metal roof.
On the left were four cloudy white urinals lined up close together, with a single narrow frosted window above them.
On the right, four old wooden stall doors stood in a row, their paint peeling in places, the metal handles dulled from years of use.
The green rubber mat beneath our feet curled up at the edges, damp and slightly sticky.
I stepped up to a urinal, unzipping my pants.
“Since it’s small, we’d have to get a custom drum set made too…” I said.
“Yeah… the sound would be kinda pathetic. Normally it’s like DO-DO-TA-DO-TTA, but this would be more like to-to-ka-tot-ka…” Yohei replied.
“Sounds like a toccata,” I laughed, moving to the farthest urinal.
It had shrunk completely.
“Then it wouldn’t be heavy metal… more like light metal,” Yohei said, standing one urinal over from me.
“Pathetic…” I muttered. Light metal was pathetic, but so was mine.
I relaxed a little. Bright yellow…
“Aluminum… instead of Iron Maiden, maybe Aluminum Maiden?” Yohei said, leaning back slightly.
Not much came out. Maybe it had all turned into sweat.
“Haha…” I chuckled, shaking it a bit as I laughed at what he said.
“Titanium’s light too, right? Titanium Maiden?” Yohei added, rocking his hips slightly.
“Titanium Maiden… that might actually work?” I said, zipping up.
For a moment, I remembered how Tsuruoka-san had caught himself in his zipper back at Hakuba-jiri, and I almost burst out laughing.

Rattle…
I slid the toilet door open.
On the wall in front of me were four mirrors, with a long sink stretching beneath them.
The father and child from earlier were gone.
Shhh… Shaaah… shhh…
The sound of rain striking the metal roof rose and fell as the wind carried it.
