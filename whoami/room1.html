Guest Room

“Well then, let’s head back, grab our stuff, and go to the room,” Takizawa-san said.
The five of us stepped out of the drying room, slid the door shut behind us, and walked along the wooden slats before climbing back up the wooden ramp toward the entrance.
We moved down the corridor, heading toward the front, passing the staircase on our right.
Up ahead, I could see Mari-senpai and Yabe-san standing beside the backpacks gathered together, talking to each other.
“Well then, see you,” Kobayashi Ren-san said at the intersection, then turned and headed off to the right.
The corridor to the right had wooden floors and a wooden ceiling. On the left side were sliding doors—wooden lattice frames with glass panels set into them—one near the front and another farther back, with matching glass-paneled lattice windows lined up between them.
On the right side, there were several plain doors without windows.
And straight ahead stood a gray-painted metal door—something that felt strangely out of place in a mountain lodge.

We reached where Mari-senpai and Yabe-san were waiting, picked up our own backpacks, and headed back down the corridor.
The staircase came into view—wooden steps with open gaps between them, letting you see through to the other side—and we climbed up to the second floor.
The two men were there on the left.
The athletic one and the guy with glasses, both probably in their thirties.
So when they said they were heading back to their room, it wasn’t a private room… more like a shared sleeping space, I thought.
That large room was on the left side of the stairs, made up of four raised sections stretching toward the back.
The two in the middle were connected, with a partition wall running between them toward the rear.
There were narrow walkways running toward the back between the rightmost section and the one next to it, and between the leftmost section and its neighbor.
On each raised platform, stacks of folded futons and pillows were lined up neatly, with shelves built above them for storing backpacks.
Along the wall above the shelves were long, narrow windows set at regular intervals, letting in soft light, while below the shelves were simple partitions dividing each person’s sleeping space.
Those partitions probably helped support the shelves as well.
The two men in their thirties were lying at the very front of the rightmost section, talking to each other.
Our room was on the right side of the stairs, so after reaching the top, we turned right and doubled back along the corridor.
To the left, I could see another hallway stretching inward, with four wood-grain sliding doors lined up, all without windows.
We stepped in briefly to check, and above each door hung a small plaque bearing names—Hoshikage, Minegiri, Komorebi, and Kazahana.
We’d been told to use rooms called Hinode and Setsuhō, so this wasn’t it.
We stepped back out and continued farther in.
Another hallway appeared on the left, again stretching inward, with four doors lined up on each side.

At the far end on the left stood a middle-aged man and woman.
The woman wore a gentle smile, and the man beside her had a kind expression as well.
They weren’t standing close enough to be holding hands, yet they naturally adjusted each other’s belongings and shared small items between them, and somehow, just watching them put me at ease.
But something felt… off.
Neither of them wore a wedding ring, and they weren’t leaning into each other or exchanging soft, playful smiles.
Their appearances and ages didn’t quite match either, and they didn’t look like siblings.
And yet, the rhythm of their conversation and the way they glanced at each other felt more intimate than even long-time companions.
In my mind, a small question mark flickered—Huh…?
Lovers? Siblings? No… they didn’t quite fit either.
Still, the way they naturally looked out for each other created a strangely comfortable atmosphere.
Could they be having an affair?
I found myself stopping in my tracks, my eyes following their quiet exchange, feeling inexplicably drawn in.

We stepped into that hallway and checked the names of the rooms one by one.
The nearest room on the left was Unkai (Sea of Clouds), the one on the right was Gekkō (Moonlight), and further down were the two rooms we’d be staying in—Hinode (Sunrise) and Seppō (Snow Peak).
“So, which one are we taking?” Takizawa-san asked.
The receptionist—the guy who complained to me—said the women should take Seppō… I thought, but didn’t say it out loud.
“If the weather were nice, you could probably see Tsurugi from Seppō. The view might be better,” Mari-senpai said with a smile.
“With weather like this, does it really matter?” Yabe-san replied.
“Well, either one’s fine by me,” Tsuruoka-san added casually.
“Alright then, the women can take Seppō, and we’ll take Hinode,” Takizawa-san said, settling it.
When we slid the door open, four futons were neatly laid out on the tatami floor.
The room was about six tatami mats in size, with only a narrow strip of space left between the futons—there wasn’t much room for luggage except along the edges.
There were no windows, and the air felt a little heavy with humidity and the scent of tatami.
Small shelves and platforms for belongings sat in the corners, and a compact heater was placed near the wall opposite the entrance.
The ceiling was low, with exposed beams faintly visible overhead.
The air carried a mix of cool mountain freshness and the aged scent of wood, creating a strangely calming atmosphere.
After setting down our bags, the four of us men finally had a moment to relax.
Wiping the sweat from his face, Takizawa-san said, “Man, I’m drenched—I’m gonna change,” and immediately started taking off his clothes.
“Ah, I’m good,” Yohei said, sounding half-bothered, staying seated on his black Springdale pack with the rainbow-striped tape.
“Yeah, I’ll stay like this too,” Tsuruoka-san said flatly.
“…Yeah, same here. I’ll just stay like this,” I managed to say.
