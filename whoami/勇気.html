引き返す勇気

…I had no idea how much time had passed.
Maybe it had been hours.
Or maybe only a few seconds.
My side and left arm hurt.
I had probably injured them when I fell through the ceiling opening…
Holding my breath, I stayed perfectly still in the darkness, listening only to the sound of my own heartbeat.
And just as that pounding finally began to calm down—
I suddenly noticed something echoing faintly in the distance.
At first, I thought it was only my imagination.
But little by little, the sound became unmistakably real.
The roaring howl of the wind.
Rain hammering against the walls.
The typhoon.
That’s right… the storm had been closing in.
Until that moment, I hadn’t heard any of it at all.
It was as if the raging storm outside had vanished completely from the world.
The thing that had stolen away all sound was fear.
Not logic.
It wasn’t my ears that had shut down—it was my mind.
With trembling hands, I reached out and touched the damp wall.
The vibrations of the wind outside traveled faintly through my fingertips.
“…I’m alive…”
Little by little, the feeling that I was still in the real world began to return.

The storm outside had drawn even closer than before.
The wind gouged at the mountainside, and somewhere beneath the eaves, something rolled across the ground and came to a stop.
—? …Footsteps.
Outside the storage room.
Coming this way.
I held my breath.
The footsteps stopped just outside the door.
Then, in the next instant—metal scraped softly against metal.
Someone was touching the padlock from the outside.
Clack… clack…
Slowly. Carefully.
As if testing for a reaction from whoever might be inside.
At that moment, my heart jolted violently, and it felt as though countless stars had exploded across the darkness inside my head. For an instant, I couldn’t think at all.
Instinctively, I backed away from the door.
In reality, it may have lasted only a split second.
But after what felt like an impossibly long stretch of frozen time, my thoughts suddenly came rushing back all at once.
(Could it be… another survivor? No… that can’t be…)
My mind tore itself apart between hope and despair.
Beyond the kitchen—I remembered the dining hall.
The slime smeared across the walls.
The arms and legs jutting out from the mass, impossible to tell whose they had once belonged to.
And the sound that thing had made when the “mass” began to move.
Clack.
The padlock came undone.
All the blood drained from my body.
Every beat of my heart sent strange ringing deep into my ears.
Out in the darkness beyond the door, the sliding door rattled against its frame.
(Run… run…!)
A voice screamed inside my head.
But there was nowhere to run—except up.
The opening in the ceiling.
The same hole where that “lodge owner” had stretched his neck up and peered down into the storage room.
Holding my breath, I looked up at the ceiling.
—What do I do!? What do I do!?
The darkness outside was breathing.
Rain hammered against the walls, wind howled through the night, and once again something rolled beneath the eaves.
Each time it happened, the walls of the storage room trembled slightly, and the nails gave faint little creaks.
(What do I do…? What am I supposed to do…?)
The storage room door.
Someone was trying to open it from the outside.
Clack… clack…
The sound felt distant, yet impossibly close at the same time.
There was nowhere to escape—except upward.
I raised my eyes toward the ceiling once again.
That opening.
I had fallen through it only moments ago.
And from there, that “neck” had peered down at me.
The face of the lodge owner—stretching, creaking, smiling.
My throat burned with pain.
My fingertips trembled on their own.
That thing might still be up there in the crawlspace.
But if I stayed here, then whatever was outside the door would definitely come in.
(Above… or outside… either way, it’s hell.)
Grinding my teeth, I forced myself to stand.
The wooden floor creaked beneath my feet.
That sound alone nearly made my heart stop.
Clatter… clatter…
Someone was trying to force the door open.
Holding my breath, I stood beneath the ceiling hatch.
I looked up.
The dark wooden panel.
Once I opened it, there would be no turning back.
If that thing was waiting above me, my life was over.
If it wasn’t—then maybe I could still survive.
I raised my right foot and planted it on the shelf.
Reaching up, I pulled myself higher.
Then the other foot… climbing the shelving little by little.
The air near the ceiling felt warmer.
The flathead screwdriver still lay atop the cardboard box whose flaps had been twisted together like a Möbius strip to keep it shut.
With trembling fingers, I reached out and grabbed the screwdriver.
The cold feel of the screwdriver brought me back to reality.
The afterimage of the lodge owner’s face—staring from inside the opening—flashed through my mind.
It felt as though the moment I opened the hatch, his face would already be there waiting for me.

—The crawlspace.
That was the only path left to me now.
But up there…
that “thing” that had peered down at me before might still be waiting in the darkness.
If I opened it, there could be a monster.
If I didn’t, “something” would come through the door.
There was nowhere else to run.
And yet—
did I really have the courage to open that hatch?
When someone tells you to obey the Japanese flag—
would you be able to think about what lies behind those words?
When everyone around you begins spreading fear and panic,
would you be able to stop,
and ask yourself,
“Is that really true?”
When the internet erupts in outrage.
When voices begin shouting to drive immigrants out.
When people choose someone to brand as a “witch” and start tearing them apart.
In moments like that,
could you stop yourself
from simply being swept along with the crowd?
When someone threatens you into doing something wrong.
When they tell you,
“Everyone else is doing it.”
Even then—
would you still have the courage to refuse?
When failing to lower your weapon would turn you into a killer and destroy your life.
When refusing to lower it could make you a mass murderer—or even bring an end to human history itself—could you still put away the weapon you drew to protect yourself?
Is that progress really safe for humanity?
Right now,
standing before this dark opening,
I felt as though I was being confronted with that very same question.
This wasn’t just about whether I would survive.
It felt more like a test
of whether I could remain human.

(What do I do? Am I really going to open it?)
My breathing grew shallow. Voices screamed inside my head.
“Don’t.” “Go.” “You’re out of time.”
None of them felt like my own anymore.
Then—
a voice came from beyond the door outside.
“...Bon curry. I’ll bring it out now.”
It was the lodge owner’s voice.
That voice, unmistakably.
That calm tone, carrying the faintest trace of a smile.
Something inside me snapped.
Fear and hope exploded at the same time, mixing together in the depths of my mind.
(The owner…? Then does that mean… there’s nothing up there?)
As I stared at the hatch above me, the afterimage of the owner’s face peering down from inside it flashed across my mind.
Even though I could hear his voice outside the door, I still felt as though the moment I opened the hatch, his face would be waiting there.
(You don’t know that. They can change shape however they want.)
And besides, someone else could be waiting up there.
Life or death… it was nothing but a gamble.
If the owner—or anyone—was inside that crawlspace, I was dead.
But there was no time left to think.
I had to be prepared to die. …God, it was terrifying.
Holding my breath, I jammed the flathead screwdriver into the latch, twisted it free, and yanked the ceiling hatch open in one motion.
The wood creaked.
Warm air drifted down from above, stirring the dust into the air.
Beyond the opening—
darkness.
Nothing was there.

I sensed someone entering through the door.
—Hurry! Hurry! Hurry! my mind screamed.
I thrust my head into the opening and grabbed the hatch lid suspended by four metal rods, pulling myself upward.
Creeeak...
The opening shook with a groaning sound.
Bracing my waist against the edge of the hatch, I dragged myself up with both arms.
—Hurry! Hurry! Hurry!
That thing might stretch its neck up here again.
It might even force its way inside.
But there was no time to close the hatch.
I hauled myself forward, crawling desperately until I managed to climb onto the beam crossing directly in front of me.
The ceiling groaned and crackled around me.
Creeeak... crack... crack...
Each second seemed frozen in place, while at the same time my thoughts spun at terrifying speed.
The light pouring in from the hole in the dining hall ceiling and from the hatch I had just climbed through kept the attic from being completely dark.
I could see better now than before.
Rows of pillars. Beams crisscrossing ahead.
Where I was, the space above the beams was high enough for a person to crawl across them.
But farther to the left, the ceiling dropped lower, leaving almost no space above the beams at all.
The layout of the second floor surfaced in my mind.
The sleeping areas had been built slightly higher, while the corridor running between them was lower.
That meant the sections where there was no space above the beams must be directly beneath the hallway.
And if I could somehow make it across beneath that section, I might reach the crawlspace above the sleeping area on the other side—
and beneath that should be the office and the staff rooms.
If I could get there...
and if there was another inspection hatch somewhere over there...
then maybe I could climb down.
Maybe I could still escape.
I moved toward the left, where a pillar stood at the intersection of the beams, then stretched my right hand past it and started crawling into the section where there was no space above the beams.
To my left was a concrete wall.
Beyond it lay the Power Room where the generator and fire pump had been kept.
It felt like the lodge owner’s neck was coming after me again from behind.
Like it was right there, just inches behind my back.
And from the hole in the dining hall ceiling off to my rear right, it felt as though the Things that had twisted themselves into monstrous shapes were crawling up into the crawlspace as well.
If they caught me, it was over. I would disappear from this world.
Desperately, I forced my arms and legs to keep moving as I crawled along the beam toward the section where there was almost no space above it.
I could feel the gritty dust piled along the beam against my palms and knees.
Far away, I could hear rain and wind hammering against the walls.
The dusty smell in the air was mixed with something scorched.
Ahead of me, the section where there was barely any clearance above the beam drew closer and closer.
—Can I really squeeze through a space that narrow and make it to the other side?
The anxiety kept building.
There was barely enough room between the beam and the thin wooden lattice supporting the ceiling panels below for a human head to fit through.
To get across, I would have to crawl over the suspended lattice itself.
The same lattice I had nearly punched through when my foot slipped earlier.
It was only hanging there, suspended by metal rods. I had no idea whether it could even support a person’s weight.
Carefully, keeping most of my weight on the beam, I lowered my right foot onto the lattice.
As close to one of the hanging metal rods as possible.
Then, while still leaving most of my weight on the left side, I moved my right hand onto the lattice too.
I shifted my left foot over as well, trying to distribute my weight evenly across all four limbs.
The lattice groaned and cracked beneath me.
The rods suspending it swayed unsteadily.
The dust made my throat rasp and itch.
My nose twitched too.
But I didn’t have the luxury of worrying about that.
The lodge owner—or one of those twisted things—might catch up to me at any moment.
And if they did, it was over.
I’d be as good as dead.
And not quickly, either.
I’d end up writhing in agony like the people I had seen in the dining hall.
My eyes, my hair—everything would probably be dissolved and consumed by the Things until I became one of those horrors too.
I moved my left hand onto the lattice, and my full weight finally shifted completely onto the framework supporting the ceiling panels.
If too much weight concentrated in a single spot, the lattice below would give way instantly under me.
Lying flat on my stomach atop the lattice, I inched forward little by little, moving my limbs with extreme care.
Whenever I moved one arm or leg, I made sure to move only that one, keeping my weight distributed across at least three points at all times.
The ceiling panels creaked and groaned beneath me.
Far away, wind and rain battered against the walls.
Ahead of me, the beam drew closer and closer.
If I lifted my chin even a little, I'd hit the beam above me.
I turned my head sideways.
…If I did that, it looked like my head might barely fit between the beam and the lattice.
Little by little, I moved my hands and feet forward.
Rustle… rustle…
The sound of my body crawling echoed through the crawlspace.
Cough—cough—cough…!
My own breath stirred up the dust piled around me, and I inhaled it, breaking into a fit of coughing.
It hurt.
But I couldn’t afford to care about that.
Slowly, desperately, I kept moving forward.
My head slipped beneath the beam.
I kept inching ahead.
Then my shoulders struck against the underside of it.
Using both arms stretched out ahead of me, I dragged my body farther underneath.
Creeeak… creak…
The ceiling below shuddered noisily beneath my weight.
Once my head cleared the far side of the beam, it became slightly easier to move.
I pulled myself forward with both hands.
My shoulders finally slid out from beneath the beam.
Rustle… rustle…
The sound of my body crawling echoed through the crawlspace.
My hips bumped against the underside of the beam.
I spread my legs wider, trying to distribute my weight.
Using both arms, I pulled myself farther through.
My hips slid beneath the beam.
Little by little, I kept moving forward, inching ahead with both hands and feet.
At last, my lower body cleared the beam completely.
Still keeping my weight spread out, I continued crawling forward.
—Thank God… I made it through.
So far, nothing had attacked me.
Even if one of the Things had been chasing me, maybe squeezing through a space this tight wouldn’t be easy for it either.
—What if the lodge owner’s face is right nearby?
—What if one of those grotesque Things is right beside me?
The thought terrified me.
Summoning my courage, I turned around to look.
Shadows of pillars and beams stretched through the darkness.
Far behind me, I could see light spilling in through the hole in the dining hall ceiling.
There was nothing there.
A small wave of relief escaped me.
—Please let Mari-senpai be safe…
Please let Mari-senpai and the others be safe.
